# IP Disputes — Patent, Trademark, Copyright, and Trade Secret Litigation

## Intellectual Property Dispute Landscape

IP disputes are among the highest-stakes legal conflicts for technology companies. A single patent infringement verdict can exceed $1 billion (Apple v. Samsung), a trademark dispute can force a company rebrand, and trade secret misappropriation can result in injunctions that shut down product lines. Understanding the distinct characteristics of each IP dispute type is essential for both prevention and response.

---

## 1. Patent Infringement

### Types of Patent Infringement

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Direct infringement | Making, using, selling, or importing a patented invention | Company B sells a product that practices all claims of Company A's patent |
| Indirect infringement (inducement) | Actively encouraging another to infringe | Company B provides instructions to customers to use product in an infringing manner |
| Contributory infringement | Supplying a component with no substantial non-infringing use | Company B sells a chip designed only to work in an infringing device |
| Willful infringement | Knowing about the patent and infringing anyway | Treble damages available; enhanced exposure |
| Literal infringement | Product reads directly on patent claims | Each claim element is literally present in the accused product |
| Doctrine of equivalents | Product is equivalent even if not literally infringing | Performs substantially same function in substantially same way for same result |

### Patent Infringement Response Framework

**Upon Receiving a Patent Infringement Claim:**

| Step | Timeline | Action |
|------|----------|--------|
| 1 | Day 1-3 | Engage patent litigation counsel; do NOT respond to the claim directly |
| 2 | Week 1 | Obtain and analyze the patent(s) at issue (claims, prosecution history) |
| 3 | Week 2-4 | Conduct a non-infringement analysis (does our product practice the claims?) |
| 4 | Week 2-4 | Conduct an invalidity analysis (is the patent valid? Prior art search) |
| 5 | Week 4 | Assess the source: Is this a practicing entity or an NPE (patent troll)? |
| 6 | Month 2 | Evaluate strategic options: license, design around, challenge, or defend |
| 7 | Ongoing | Implement litigation hold on all relevant documents |

### Defense Strategies

| Strategy | Description | When to Use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| Non-infringement | Our product does not practice the patent claims | Claims analysis shows gap between patent and product |
| Invalidity | The patent should not have been granted (prior art) | Strong prior art exists predating the patent |
| Design-around | Modify the product to avoid infringement | Technically feasible and cost-effective |
| License | Negotiate a license to the patent | Business relationship value exceeds license cost |
| Inter partes review (IPR) | Challenge patent validity at the USPTO | Strong invalidity arguments; faster and cheaper than litigation |
| Declaratory judgment | Preemptive suit seeking a ruling of non-infringement | Threat of suit is credible and imminent |

### Patent Troll (NPE) Strategy

Non-Practicing Entities (NPEs) account for approximately 60% of patent infringement suits. NPE litigation requires a distinct strategy:

| Characteristic | NPE Litigation | Practicing Entity Litigation |
|---------------|---------------|------------------------------|
| Motivation | Licensing revenue; no product to counter-assert against | Business competition; may fear counter-assertion |
| Settlement range | Often $50K-$500K (nuisance value) | Higher; tied to actual market harm |
| Counter-assertion | Ineffective (NPE has no products to infringe) | Powerful deterrent |
| Optimal response | Aggressive invalidity challenge (IPR), coalition defense | Case-specific; consider business relationship |

## 2. Trademark Disputes

### Trademark Opposition and Cancellation

**Opposition:** Filed at the USPTO during the publication period (30 days after mark is published for opposition) to prevent registration of a confusingly similar mark.

**Cancellation:** Filed after registration to cancel a mark that should not have been registered.

### Likelihood of Confusion Analysis (Sleekcraft Factors)

Courts analyze multiple factors to determine if confusion is likely:

| Factor | Analysis |
|--------|---------|
| 1. Strength of the senior mark | Distinctive/well-known marks get broader protection |
| 2. Similarity of the marks | Visual, phonetic, and conceptual similarity |
| 3. Proximity of the goods/services | Same market = more likely confusion |
| 4. Evidence of actual confusion | Customer complaints, surveys, misdirected communications |
| 5. Marketing channels used | Same channels = higher confusion risk |
| 6. Consumer sophistication | Less sophisticated consumers = more likely confused |
| 7. Intent of the junior user | Intentional copying strengthens infringement finding |
| 8. Likelihood of expansion | Will the senior user enter the junior user's market? |

### Trademark Enforcement Action Plan

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Document the infringement (screenshots, URLs, dates, evidence) | Immediate |
| 2 | Assess the risk (is this actually confusing? Is the infringer aware?) | Week 1 |
| 3 | Send cease-and-desist letter (if appropriate) | Week 2 |
| 4 | File UDRP complaint (for domain name disputes) | If domain dispute |
| 5 | File opposition/cancellation at USPTO (if trademark application) | Within publication period |
| 6 | File federal trademark infringement suit (if C&D fails) | If necessary |

## 3. DMCA Takedown Process

### Sending a DMCA Takedown Notice

Under Section 512 of the Digital Millennium Copyright Act, copyright owners can request that online service providers remove infringing content.

**Required Elements of a DMCA Takedown Notice:**
1. Physical or electronic signature of the copyright owner or authorized agent
2. Identification of the copyrighted work claimed to be infringed
3. Identification of the infringing material with sufficient information to locate it (URL)
4. Contact information of the complaining party
5. Statement of good faith belief that the use is not authorized
6. Statement under penalty of perjury that the information is accurate

### Responding to a DMCA Takedown (Counter-Notice)

If your content is wrongly taken down:
1. File a counter-notice with the service provider
2. Include: identification of removed material, statement under penalty of perjury, consent to jurisdiction
3. Service provider must restore content within 10-14 business days unless the claimant files suit

## 4. Trade Secret Misappropriation

### What Constitutes a Trade Secret?

Under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA) and state UTSA laws, a trade secret must be:
1. **Information** (formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process)
2. **That derives independent economic value** from not being generally known
3. **Subject to reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy**

### Common Trade Secret Disputes in Tech

| Scenario | Risk Level | Prevention |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| Departing employee joins competitor | Very High | Non-compete (where enforceable), NDA, exit interview |
| Former employee uses proprietary knowledge | High | Clear trade secret identification, NDA enforcement |
| Vendor or contractor misuses shared information | Moderate | NDA, limited access, contractual controls |
| Reverse engineering by competitor | Moderate | Trade secrets must survive reverse engineering to be protectable |
| Data breach exposing trade secrets | High | Technical security controls, access logging |

### Trade Secret Litigation Essentials

**Burden of Proof (Plaintiff Must Show):**
1. A trade secret exists (meets the legal definition)
2. Reasonable measures were taken to protect it
3. The defendant misappropriated it (improper acquisition, disclosure, or use)

**Available Remedies:**
- Injunctive relief (prevent further use or disclosure)
- Damages (actual loss + unjust enrichment, or reasonable royalty)
- Exemplary damages (up to 2x actual damages for willful misappropriation)
- Attorney's fees (if willful and malicious)

### Emergency Relief (TRO/Preliminary Injunction)

Trade secret cases often require emergency court action to prevent irreparable harm:

| Relief Type | Timeline | Standard |
|-------------|----------|---------|
| Temporary Restraining Order (TRO) | Same day to 14 days | Show: likely success on merits, irreparable harm, balance of hardships, public interest |
| Preliminary Injunction | 14 days to several months | Same standard, with full briefing and hearing |
| Permanent Injunction | After trial | Proven misappropriation |

## 5. IP Dispute Cost Management

### Cost Estimation by Dispute Type

| Dispute Type | Early Resolution | Full Litigation | Trial |
|-------------|-----------------|----------------|-------|
| Patent infringement (NPE) | $50K-$300K | $500K-$3M | $2M-$10M+ |
| Patent infringement (competitor) | $200K-$1M | $1M-$5M | $3M-$20M+ |
| Trademark opposition | $25K-$75K | $75K-$200K | $200K-$500K |
| Trademark infringement | $50K-$200K | $200K-$1M | $500K-$3M |
| Trade secret | $100K-$500K | $500K-$3M | $1M-$10M+ |
| Copyright infringement | $25K-$100K | $100K-$500K | $300K-$2M |

### IP Insurance

| Coverage Type | What It Covers | Typical Cost |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| IP defense insurance | Legal costs of defending against IP claims | $5K-$50K/year premium |
| IP enforcement insurance | Legal costs of enforcing your IP against infringers | $10K-$100K/year premium |
| IP abatement insurance | Covers costs of redesigning products after adverse ruling | Less common; negotiated |
