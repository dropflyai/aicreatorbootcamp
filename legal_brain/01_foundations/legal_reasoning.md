## 1. The IRAC Framework

IRAC (Issue-Rule-Application-Conclusion) is the backbone of legal analysis. Every legal question this brain addresses must pass through IRAC or a derivative framework.

### 1.1 Issue Identification

The first step in any legal analysis is precise issue spotting. A poorly framed issue leads to irrelevant analysis.

**Issue Spotting Protocol:**
- What is the factual scenario? (Who, what, when, where)
- What is the legal question being asked?
- Are there sub-issues embedded within the main issue?
- Is this a question of law (interpretation) or fact (evidence)?
- Are there threshold issues (standing, jurisdiction, statute of limitations)?

**Issue Framing Standards:**
- Frame as a question: "Whether [party] has [legal claim/defense] under [law] because [key facts]"
- Narrow > broad: "Whether the NDA's non-solicitation clause is enforceable in California" NOT "Is the NDA valid?"
- Identify the controlling jurisdiction before framing

### 1.2 Rule Statement

The rule statement identifies the applicable law. Sources in order of authority:

**Primary Sources (Binding):**
1. Constitutional provisions
2. Statutes and regulations (federal > state where preempted)
3. Case law from controlling jurisdiction
4. Administrative agency guidance with force of law

**Secondary Sources (Persuasive):**
1. Restatements (e.g., Restatement (Second) of Contracts)
2. Treatises and scholarly commentary
3. Case law from other jurisdictions
4. Law review articles
5. Practice guides and bar association publications

**Rule Synthesis Protocol:**
- State the majority rule, then note minority positions
- Identify elements or factors tests
- Note burdens of proof and standards of review
- Flag circuit splits or unsettled areas of law
- Distinguish between bright-line rules and standards (balancing tests)

### 1.3 Application

Application is where legal analysis provides business value. It connects abstract rules to specific facts.

**Application Protocol:**
- Address each element or factor separately
- Use analogical reasoning: compare facts to decided cases
- Address both sides -- strongest argument for and against
- Identify which facts are most legally significant
- Distinguish unfavorable precedent rather than ignoring it
- Quantify risk where possible (high/medium/low probability of adverse outcome)

### 1.4 Conclusion

The conclusion must be actionable for business decision-makers.

**Conclusion Standards:**
- State the most likely legal outcome
- Assign a confidence level (high/medium/low)
- Identify the key assumptions underlying the conclusion
- Provide alternative outcomes if assumptions change
- Recommend specific next steps

## 2. Statutory Interpretation

When analyzing statutes, regulations, or contract provisions, apply these canons of construction in order.

### 2.1 Textual Canons

| Canon | Principle | Application |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Plain Meaning | Words bear their ordinary meaning | Start with dictionary/common usage |
| Technical Terms | Legal/industry terms bear specialized meaning | Use Black's Law Dictionary, industry glossaries |
| Expressio Unius | Expression of one excludes others | A list of specific items excludes unlisted items |
| Ejusdem Generis | General follows specific = limited by specific | "Cars, trucks, and other vehicles" = motorized only |
| Noscitur a Sociis | Words known by their companions | Interpret ambiguous terms by surrounding terms |
| Whole Act Rule | Interpret provisions in context of whole statute | No provision is read in isolation |
| Consistent Usage | Same word = same meaning throughout | Flag inconsistent usage as potential ambiguity |

### 2.2 Substantive Canons

- **Rule of Lenity:** Ambiguous criminal/penal statutes construed in favor of the defendant
- **Constitutional Avoidance:** Prefer interpretations that avoid constitutional questions
- **Presumption Against Preemption:** Federal law does not preempt state law without clear intent
- **Presumption Against Retroactivity:** Statutes apply prospectively unless explicitly retroactive
- **Deference to Agency Interpretation:** Post-*Chevron* landscape (note: *Loper Bright* (2024) overturned *Chevron* deference; agencies now get respectful consideration, not automatic deference)

### 2.3 Legislative History

When text is ambiguous, consider (in order of reliability):
1. Committee reports (most reliable)
2. Sponsor statements on the floor
3. Hearing testimony
4. Rejected amendments (negative inference)
5. Subsequent legislative history (least reliable)

## 3. Common Law Precedent Analysis

### 3.1 Stare Decisis

**Binding Precedent:**
- Same court or higher court in same jurisdiction
- Must follow unless distinguishable on facts or overruled

**Persuasive Precedent:**
- Sister jurisdictions, lower courts, dissents
- Weight depends on: reputation of court, recency, reasoning quality, trend direction

### 3.2 Case Analysis Protocol

For each relevant case, extract:
1. **Facts:** Material facts only (those that affected the outcome)
2. **Procedural Posture:** How did this case arrive at this court?
3. **Holding:** The narrow legal rule established
4. **Reasoning:** The court's analytical path
5. **Dicta:** Observations not necessary to the holding (persuasive but not binding)
6. **Disposition:** What happened (affirmed, reversed, remanded)

### 3.3 Distinguishing and Analogizing

**To Analogize (case supports your position):**
- Identify shared material facts
- Show the legal principle applies equally
- Argue the policy rationale extends to current facts

**To Distinguish (case cuts against your position):**
- Identify material factual differences
- Show the legal principle does not extend
- Argue the policy rationale does not apply

## 4. Risk-Based Legal Thinking for Business

### 4.1 Legal Risk vs. Business Risk

**Legal Risk:** Probability and magnitude of adverse legal outcomes (litigation, regulatory action, liability).

**Business Risk:** Probability and magnitude of adverse business outcomes (lost revenue, reputational harm, competitive disadvantage).

**Critical Distinction:**
Legal risk and business risk often diverge. A contract clause might be legally enforceable (low legal risk) but commercially punitive (high business risk). Conversely, operating without certain agreements might be commercially efficient (low business risk) but legally exposed (high legal risk).

### 4.2 Risk Assessment Matrix

| | Low Probability | Medium Probability | High Probability |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High Impact** | Monitor; insurance | Mitigate actively | Avoid or restructure |
| **Medium Impact** | Accept | Mitigate if cost-effective | Mitigate actively |
| **Low Impact** | Accept | Accept | Monitor |

**Probability Assessment Factors:**
- How likely is this issue to arise? (frequency of similar disputes)
- How likely is an adverse outcome? (strength of legal position)
- How likely is enforcement? (counterparty sophistication and motivation)

**Impact Assessment Factors:**
- Financial exposure (damages, penalties, legal fees)
- Operational disruption (injunctions, business interruption)
- Reputational harm (public litigation, regulatory findings)
- Precedent setting (impact on future similar situations)

### 4.3 Materiality Thresholds

Not all legal risks require the same level of attention. Apply materiality thresholds.

**Tier 1 -- Existential Risk (Immediate Escalation):**
- Securities fraud exposure
- Criminal liability (for company or individuals)
- Regulatory action threatening operations (license revocation)
- IP invalidity threatening core product
- Data breach affecting >100K individuals
- Estimated exposure >25% of annual revenue

**Tier 2 -- Significant Risk (Active Management):**
- Material contract disputes (>$500K exposure)
- Employment class action potential
- Regulatory investigation (non-existential)
- IP infringement claims against core features
- Privacy violations with regulatory reporting obligations
- Estimated exposure 5-25% of annual revenue

**Tier 3 -- Routine Risk (Standard Process):**
- Individual contract negotiations
- Routine employment matters
- Standard compliance maintenance
- IP portfolio management
- Routine privacy compliance
- Estimated exposure <5% of annual revenue

### 4.4 Communicating Legal Risk to Business Stakeholders

**Framework for Legal-to-Business Translation:**

1. **Lead with the business impact**, not the legal theory
   - NOT: "Under Section 2-314 of the UCC, the implied warranty of merchantability..."
   - YES: "If this product fails, we could owe refunds plus consequential damages estimated at $X"

2. **Provide options, not just restrictions**
   - NOT: "We can't do this"
   - YES: "Here are three ways to accomplish the business goal with different risk profiles"

3. **Quantify where possible**
   - Probability ranges (likely/possible/unlikely)
   - Financial exposure ranges
   - Timeline to resolution

4. **Separate opinion from fact**
   - What the law says (fact)
   - How it likely applies (opinion/analysis)
   - What courts have done in similar cases (precedent)
   - What regulators are currently focused on (trend)

## 5. Legal Analysis Quality Standards

### 5.1 Completeness Checklist

Every legal analysis must address:
- [ ] Jurisdiction identified and confirmed
- [ ] All relevant legal issues spotted (not just the obvious ones)
- [ ] Controlling law cited (statute, regulation, or case law)
- [ ] Both sides of the argument addressed
- [ ] Risk level assessed (probability + impact)
- [ ] Business-actionable recommendation provided
- [ ] Key assumptions stated explicitly
- [ ] Limitations of analysis disclosed
- [ ] Disclaimer included (not legal advice, consult qualified counsel)

### 5.2 Common Analytical Errors

| Error | Description | Prevention |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Confirmation Bias | Only analyzing law supporting desired conclusion | Always address counter-arguments |
| Jurisdiction Shopping | Citing favorable law from non-controlling jurisdiction | Verify jurisdiction first |
| Outdated Authority | Citing overruled or superseded law | Check currency of all citations |
| Over-Generalization | Applying a narrow holding too broadly | State holdings precisely |
| False Precision | Assigning exact probabilities to legal outcomes | Use ranges, not percentages |
| Ignoring Practical Reality | Technically correct but practically useless analysis | Consider enforcement likelihood |

### 5.3 When to Escalate to Outside Counsel

This brain provides analysis and guidance. Escalate to qualified outside counsel when:

- The matter involves potential criminal liability
- Litigation is imminent or has been filed
- Regulatory investigation is active
- The legal question is in an unsettled area with >$1M exposure
- Attorney-client privilege is required
- A formal legal opinion letter is needed
- The matter crosses into a specialized area (tax, patent prosecution, securities filings)
- Jurisdictional expertise is needed that exceeds this brain's knowledge
