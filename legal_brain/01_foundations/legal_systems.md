## 1. Common Law vs. Civil Law Systems

### 1.1 Common Law Systems

Common law systems (United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, India) derive legal principles primarily from judicial decisions (case law) supplemented by statutes.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Stare Decisis:** Courts are bound by prior decisions of equal or higher courts in the same jurisdiction
- **Adversarial System:** Parties present competing arguments; judge serves as neutral arbiter
- **Jury Trials:** Available in many civil and criminal matters (constitutional right in US)
- **Case-Driven Development:** Law evolves incrementally through judicial interpretation
- **Equity Jurisdiction:** Courts can fashion equitable remedies (injunctions, specific performance) beyond monetary damages
- **Discovery:** Extensive pre-trial discovery process (depositions, document production, interrogatories)

**Practical Implications for Business:**
- Contract interpretation emphasizes party intent, construed against the drafter
- Broader discovery obligations increase litigation cost and exposure
- Precedent creates more predictable outcomes but slower legal evolution
- Forum selection matters enormously (judge vs. jury, conservative vs. plaintiff-friendly jurisdictions)

### 1.2 Civil Law Systems

Civil law systems (Continental Europe, Latin America, Japan, South Korea, much of Asia and Africa) derive legal principles primarily from comprehensive statutory codes.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Code-Based:** Comprehensive civil codes (e.g., French Code Civil, German BGB) are the primary source of law
- **Inquisitorial System:** Judge plays active role in investigating facts
- **Limited Discovery:** No equivalent to US-style discovery; courts control evidence gathering
- **No Binding Precedent:** Prior decisions are persuasive but not binding (though practically very influential)
- **Codified Principles:** General principles of good faith, fairness encoded in statute
- **Specialized Courts:** Separate courts for commercial, labor, administrative, constitutional matters

**Practical Implications for Business:**
- Contracts can be shorter; code provides gap-filling rules
- Good faith obligations are implied by law (even in commercial contracts)
- Litigation is generally less expensive (no discovery, no juries)
- Penalty clauses may be enforceable (unlike US common law rule against penalties)
- Specific performance is the default remedy (damages are secondary)

### 1.3 Mixed and Hybrid Systems

| Jurisdiction | System | Notes |
|-------------|--------|-------|
| Louisiana | Civil law (state) + Common law (federal) | French/Spanish civil code heritage |
| Quebec | Civil law (provincial) + Common law (federal) | French civil code heritage |
| Scotland | Mixed civil/common | Unique blend |
| South Africa | Roman-Dutch + English common law | Complex hybrid |
| Israel | Mixed | Ottoman, British mandate, modern Israeli law |
| China | Civil law + socialist legal tradition | Rapidly evolving commercial law |
| Japan | Civil law (German-influenced) + significant US influence post-WWII | Strong administrative guidance tradition |

## 2. United States Jurisdictional Framework

### 2.1 Federal vs. State Jurisdiction

**Federal Jurisdiction Arises When:**
1. **Federal Question:** Claim arises under US Constitution, federal statute, or federal treaty (28 USC Section 1331)
2. **Diversity Jurisdiction:** Parties are citizens of different states AND amount in controversy exceeds $75,000 (28 USC Section 1332)
3. **Supplemental Jurisdiction:** State claims closely related to federal claims (28 USC Section 1367)
4. **Removal:** Defendant removes state-filed case meeting federal jurisdiction requirements (28 USC Section 1441)

**State Jurisdiction Applies When:**
- General jurisdiction over all matters not exclusively federal
- Most contract disputes, tort claims, employment matters, real estate
- State courts can hear federal claims (concurrent jurisdiction) unless Congress specified exclusive federal jurisdiction

**Exclusive Federal Jurisdiction:**
- Bankruptcy (Title 11)
- Patent and copyright infringement (28 USC Section 1338)
- Securities fraud (Securities Exchange Act Section 27)
- Antitrust (Clayton Act Section 12)
- Immigration
- Federal tax disputes (Tax Court)
- Admiralty (maritime)

### 2.2 State Law Variation -- Critical Business Areas

| Area | Variation Level | Key Differences |
|------|----------------|-----------------|
| Non-compete enforcement | Extreme | CA: banned entirely; most states: reasonableness test |
| Employment at-will exceptions | High | Montana: not at-will; implied contract exceptions vary |
| Privacy laws | High | CA (CCPA/CPRA), VA, CO, CT, TX, OR, MT all different |
| Corporate law | Moderate | Delaware dominates (>65% of Fortune 500) |
| Contract law | Moderate | Most adopted UCC Article 2 for goods |
| Trade secret protection | Low-Moderate | All states adopted UTSA (except NY used to be an exception); DTSA provides federal option |
| LLC law | High | Charging order protections, series LLCs, operating agreement freedom vary significantly |

### 2.3 Choice of Law and Forum Selection

**Choice of Law Clause:**
- Determines which state's substantive law governs the contract
- Generally enforceable if the chosen state has a reasonable relationship to the transaction
- Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws Section 187: respects party choice unless it violates fundamental public policy of a state with materially greater interest
- Cannot override mandatory consumer protection statutes in consumer's home state

**Forum Selection Clause:**
- Determines where disputes are litigated or arbitrated
- Presumptively valid and enforceable (*M/S Bremen v. Zapata Off-Shore Co.*, 407 U.S. 1 (1972))
- Can specify: state court, federal court, or arbitration (and specific arbitral institution)
- May be unenforceable if: procured by fraud, enforcement would be unreasonable, clause is part of adhesion contract in consumer context

**Strategic Considerations:**
- Delaware Chancery Court: Specialized, expert judges, no juries, fast (preferred for corporate disputes)
- Northern District of California: Tech-savvy, but plaintiff-friendly for employment claims
- Eastern District of Texas: Historically plaintiff-friendly for patent cases (though Patent Venue Act (2017) narrowed venue options)
- Arbitration: Private, faster, limited appeal, but discovery and class action waivers may be challenged

## 3. Regulatory Agencies -- Key Federal Bodies

### 3.1 Agencies Most Relevant to Startups and Tech

| Agency | Jurisdiction | Key Enforcement Areas |
|--------|-------------|----------------------|
| **FTC** | Consumer protection, competition | Unfair/deceptive practices, privacy enforcement, merger review |
| **SEC** | Securities regulation | Securities offerings, public company reporting, insider trading |
| **DOJ** | Federal criminal law, antitrust | Criminal fraud, antitrust (with FTC), FCPA |
| **USPTO** | Patent and trademark registration | Patent examination, trademark registration |
| **Copyright Office** | Copyright registration | Registration (prerequisite to statutory damages) |
| **EEOC** | Employment discrimination | Title VII, ADA, ADEA, GINA enforcement |
| **DOL** | Labor standards | FLSA (wage/hour), OSHA, ERISA |
| **CFPB** | Consumer financial protection | Fintech, lending, payments, credit reporting |
| **FDA** | Food, drugs, medical devices | Healthtech, digital therapeutics, SaMD |
| **FCC** | Communications, spectrum | TCPA (robocalls/texts), CAN-SPAM (with FTC), net neutrality |
| **OFAC** | Sanctions, embargoes | Prohibited countries/persons lists, sanctions compliance |
| **FinCEN** | Anti-money laundering | BSA/AML compliance, money transmission |
| **NIST** | Standards (voluntary) | Cybersecurity Framework, AI Risk Management Framework |

### 3.2 Administrative Law Basics

**Rulemaking Process (APA Section 553):**
1. Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) -- published in Federal Register
2. Public comment period (typically 30-90 days)
3. Agency considers comments and issues final rule
4. Final rule published; effective date (usually 30+ days after publication)
5. Congressional Review Act: Congress can disapprove final rules

**Agency Enforcement:**
- Investigation (subpoenas, civil investigative demands)
- Administrative proceedings (ALJs, administrative hearings)
- Consent decrees / consent orders (settlement without admission)
- Civil penalties (monetary fines, often per-violation)
- Referral to DOJ for criminal prosecution (rare, serious cases)

**Judicial Review of Agency Action:**
- Post-*Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo* (2024): No more *Chevron* deference
- Courts exercise independent judgment on questions of law
- Agency factual findings reviewed under "substantial evidence" or "arbitrary and capricious" standard
- Exhaustion of administrative remedies generally required before judicial review
- Statute of limitations for challenging rules: typically 6 years (28 USC Section 2401)

### 3.3 State Regulatory Bodies

- **State Attorneys General:** Consumer protection, state antitrust, privacy enforcement (increasingly active)
- **State Insurance Commissioners:** Insurance regulation (McCarran-Ferguson Act gives states primary authority)
- **State Banking Regulators:** State-chartered banks, money transmitter licensing
- **State Labor Boards:** State employment law enforcement
- **State Privacy Regulators:** California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA), others emerging

## 4. International Law for Business

### 4.1 International Contract Law

**UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG):**
- Applies automatically to commercial sales of goods between parties in signatory states (unless excluded)
- US is a signatory; important to explicitly opt out in contracts if US domestic law is preferred
- Does not cover services, IP licensing, or employment
- Common practice: "This agreement shall be governed by the laws of [State], excluding the CISG"

**UNIDROIT Principles of International Commercial Contracts:**
- Soft law (not automatically binding)
- Used for gap-filling in international contracts
- Recognized by international arbitral tribunals

### 4.2 International Dispute Resolution

**International Arbitration:**
- New York Convention (1958): Enables enforcement of arbitral awards in 170+ countries
- Major institutions: ICC (Paris), LCIA (London), SIAC (Singapore), HKIAC (Hong Kong), AAA/ICDR (US)
- Preferred for cross-border disputes: enforceable, neutral forum, expert arbitrators
- Seat of arbitration determines procedural law; governing law of contract determines substantive law

**Foreign Judgments:**
- No comprehensive US treaty for enforcement of foreign judgments
- State-by-state enforcement under Uniform Foreign-Country Money Judgments Recognition Act
- Defenses: lack of due process, fraud, public policy, lack of jurisdiction

### 4.3 International Regulatory Frameworks

**Anti-Corruption:**
- US Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA): Prohibits bribing foreign officials; applies to US companies worldwide and foreign companies listed on US exchanges
- UK Bribery Act (2010): Broader than FCPA; covers commercial bribery; strict liability for failure to prevent
- Both have extraterritorial reach

**Export Controls:**
- Export Administration Regulations (EAR) -- administered by BIS (Department of Commerce)
- International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR) -- administered by DDTC (State Department)
- Software and encryption exports require classification and may need licenses
- "Deemed export" rule: Sharing controlled technology with foreign nationals in the US counts as an export

**Sanctions:**
- OFAC Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List: Cannot transact with listed persons/entities
- Country-based sanctions programs (comprehensive vs. targeted)
- Secondary sanctions: US sanctions can apply to non-US companies dealing with sanctioned parties
- Screening obligations: Must screen customers, vendors, and transactions

### 4.4 International IP Protection

- Patents: Territorial; must file in each country (or use PCT for streamlined international filing)
- Trademarks: Territorial; Madrid Protocol enables multi-country filing through one application
- Copyright: Berne Convention provides automatic protection in 180+ signatory countries (no registration required for existence of rights; registration required in US for statutory damages)
- Trade Secrets: Protection varies; TRIPS Agreement sets minimum standards

## 5. Practical Application Framework

### 5.1 Jurisdiction Identification Checklist

For any legal question, determine:

1. **Where is the company incorporated?** (Corporate law jurisdiction)
2. **Where does the company operate?** (Regulatory jurisdiction)
3. **Where are the employees/contractors?** (Employment law jurisdiction)
4. **Where are the customers/users?** (Consumer protection, privacy jurisdiction)
5. **What does the contract say?** (Choice of law, forum selection)
6. **Are there international elements?** (CISG, GDPR, FCPA, export controls)

### 5.2 Multi-Jurisdictional Compliance

When operating across jurisdictions, comply with the most restrictive applicable standard unless doing so would violate another jurisdiction's mandatory rules. This is the "highest common denominator" approach.

**Exceptions where jurisdiction-specific compliance is necessary:**
- Tax law (cannot apply one jurisdiction's tax regime to another)
- Employment law (must comply with each employee's jurisdiction)
- Privacy law (when laws conflict rather than merely differ in strictness)
- Securities law (offering structure must comply with each jurisdiction's requirements)
