## 1. Term Sheet Negotiation

### 1.1 What Is a Term Sheet?

A term sheet is a non-binding document that outlines the key terms of a proposed investment. It serves as the basis for drafting the definitive legal documents.

**Binding vs. Non-Binding Provisions:**
| Typically Binding | Typically Non-Binding |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Confidentiality | Valuation and economics |
| Exclusivity (no-shop period, usually 30-60 days) | Board composition |
| Governing law | Protective provisions |
| Expenses (investor legal fees paid by company, typically capped at $25K-50K) | Registration rights |

### 1.2 Key Economic Terms

**Pre-Money Valuation:**
- The value of the company BEFORE the investment
- Post-money = Pre-money + Investment
- Investor ownership = Investment / Post-money

**Price Per Share:**
- Pre-money valuation / Fully-diluted shares (including option pool)
- This is the price the investor pays per share of preferred stock

**Option Pool:**
- Investors typically require the option pool to be created or expanded BEFORE the investment (pre-money)
- This means existing stockholders bear the dilution of the option pool, not the new investors
- Negotiate: Size the pool based on actual hiring plan (12-18 months), not arbitrary percentages
- Standard: 10-20% of fully-diluted shares (15% most common for Series A)

**Liquidation Preference:**
- Amount preferred stockholders receive before common in a liquidation event
- Standard: 1x non-participating preferred (investor gets back 1x investment OR converts to common and shares pro rata, whichever is greater)
- Avoid: Participating preferred (double-dip), multiples (>1x), accruing dividends that increase the preference

**Anti-Dilution Protection:**
- Protects investors if the company raises a future round at a lower price (down round)
- **Weighted Average (Standard):** Adjusts conversion price based on the weighted average of old and new prices. Broad-based weighted average (includes all outstanding shares + options in denominator) is market standard and fairest.
- **Full Ratchet (Investor-Aggressive):** Adjusts conversion price to the lowest price in a future round, regardless of amount sold. Heavily penalizes founders; avoid unless in a desperate situation.
- **Pay-to-Play:** Requires investors to participate in down rounds to maintain anti-dilution protection. Founder-friendly; encourages investor support.

### 1.3 Key Control Terms

**Board Composition:**
- Negotiate to maintain founder/common control as long as possible
- Standard Series A: 2 common + 1 preferred + 0-2 independent (5-seat board)
- Odd number avoids deadlock
- Independent directors should be truly independent (mutually agreed)

**Protective Provisions:**
- Veto rights for preferred stockholders on specified corporate actions
- Standard: new equity issuance, change of control, incurring debt, changing charter/bylaws, changing board size, liquidation
- Negotiate: Limit scope; add materiality thresholds (e.g., debt >$250K, not all debt)
- Resist: Veto over budget, hiring, day-to-day operations

**Drag-Along:**
- Right of a specified majority (typically majority of common + majority of preferred) to force all stockholders to approve a sale
- Purpose: Prevents minority holdouts from blocking a sale
- Standard: Included in Voting Agreement; activated by board approval + specified stockholder majority

### 1.4 Term Sheet Red Flags

| Red Flag | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| Participating preferred with no cap | Investors get both liquidation preference AND pro rata share of all remaining proceeds |
| Full ratchet anti-dilution | Devastating to founders in a down round |
| Multiple liquidation preference (>1x) | More money must go to investors before common sees anything |
| Cumulative dividends | Increases liquidation preference over time |
| Founder vesting restart | Founders forced to re-vest shares already earned |
| Redemption rights | Investors can force company to buy back their shares after 5-7 years |
| Super pro rata rights | Investors can invest more than their pro rata share in future rounds |
| Excessive protective provisions | Investor veto over routine business decisions |
| Founder non-compete without carve-outs | Overly restrictive post-departure limitations |
| Board control by investors | Investors appoint majority of directors |

## 2. Definitive Legal Documents

### 2.1 Stock Purchase Agreement (SPA)

The SPA is the agreement by which investors purchase preferred stock.

**Key Provisions:**
- **Shares and Price:** Number of shares, price per share, total investment
- **Representations and Warranties (Company):** Organization, capitalization, financial statements, IP ownership, material contracts, litigation, compliance, no conflicts, taxes, employee matters
- **Representations and Warranties (Investor):** Accredited investor status, investment intent (not for resale), ability to bear risk of loss
- **Conditions to Closing:** Accuracy of representations, legal opinion, no MAC, completion of due diligence, third-party consents
- **Indemnification:** Company (and sometimes founders) indemnify investors for breach of reps; survival period (typically 12-24 months)

### 2.2 Investors' Rights Agreement (IRA)

**Key Provisions:**
- **Registration Rights:** Demand registration (investor can require company to file registration statement), piggyback registration (investor can include shares in company's IPO), S-3 registration (short-form registration once eligible)
- **Information Rights:** Monthly/quarterly/annual financial statements, annual budget, cap table updates
- **Pro Rata Rights (Preemptive Rights):** Right to participate in future financing rounds to maintain ownership percentage
- **Board Observer Rights:** Non-voting attendance at board meetings
- **Key Person Insurance:** Company must maintain key person life insurance
- **ROFR on Founder Shares:** Company (or investors) have right of first refusal if founders want to sell shares
- **Lock-Up:** Stockholders agree to lock-up in connection with IPO (typically 180 days)

### 2.3 Right of First Refusal and Co-Sale Agreement (ROFR/Co-Sale)

- **ROFR:** Before any stockholder can sell shares to a third party, the company has first right to purchase, then investors have the right
- **Co-Sale (Tag-Along):** If a founder sells shares, investors can sell a proportional number alongside the founder
- **Exceptions:** Transfers to family members, trusts, affiliates; estate planning transfers
- **Purpose:** Prevents founders from cashing out while investors remain locked in

### 2.4 Voting Agreement

- **Board Composition:** All parties agree to vote for designated board nominees
- **Drag-Along:** Specified majority can force all stockholders to approve a sale
- **Founder Voting Commitments:** Founders agree to vote shares as specified
- **Termination:** Upon IPO, dissolution, or mutual consent

### 2.5 Certificate of Incorporation Amendment

- Amends the Certificate of Incorporation to authorize the new series of preferred stock
- Sets forth the rights, preferences, and privileges of the preferred stock:
  - Dividends (if any)
  - Liquidation preference and participation
  - Conversion rights (optional and mandatory conversion)
  - Anti-dilution protection
  - Voting rights (as-converted basis)
  - Protective provisions
  - Redemption (if any)

## 3. Legal Opinion Letters

### 3.1 When Required

Investors in priced rounds typically require company counsel to deliver a legal opinion letter at closing.

**Standard Opinion Topics:**
- Company is duly organized and validly existing under Delaware law
- Company has authority to execute the transaction documents
- Transaction documents are valid and enforceable against the company
- Shares are duly authorized, validly issued, fully paid, and non-assessable
- No conflicts with charter, bylaws, or material agreements
- Securities exemption (Reg D) is available for the offering
- No litigation that would have a material adverse effect

### 3.2 Practical Considerations

- Opinions are issued by company counsel (not investor counsel)
- Company counsel charges $5K-15K for the opinion letter
- Opinions are qualified by standard assumptions, exceptions, and limitations
- Opinions should match the scope of representations in the SPA (not broader)

## 4. Securities Filings

### 4.1 Federal Filings

**Form D:**
- Filed with SEC within 15 days of first sale of securities
- Required for Regulation D offerings (Rule 504, 506(b), 506(c))
- Filed electronically via EDGAR
- Public document (anyone can search EDGAR and find your Form D)
- Amendment required for material changes (additional sales, additional investors)
- Failure to file does not invalidate the exemption but may trigger state consequences

### 4.2 State Filings (Blue Sky)

- Rule 506 offerings are preempted from state registration (NSMIA)
- BUT: States can (and do) require notice filings and collect fees
- File in each state where a purchaser resides
- Timing: Within 15 days of first sale (varies by state)
- Cost: $100-500 per state filing
- Track which states require filings and what their specific requirements are
- Some states (e.g., California, New York) are particularly aggressive about enforcement

## 5. Closing Mechanics

### 5.1 Pre-Closing Checklist

- [ ] All transaction documents in agreed final form
- [ ] Board resolutions authorizing the transaction
- [ ] Certificate of Incorporation amendment filed with Delaware Secretary of State
- [ ] 409A valuation current (or new valuation in process)
- [ ] Cap table reconciled and current
- [ ] All conditions to closing satisfied or waived
- [ ] Funds wire instructions provided
- [ ] Legal opinion letter prepared by company counsel
- [ ] Officers' certificate prepared (certifying accuracy of representations)
- [ ] Good standing certificates obtained (Delaware + operating states)
- [ ] Stock certificates (or book entries) prepared for issuance
- [ ] Accredited investor questionnaires collected (for 506(c))
- [ ] Management rights letter prepared (for ERISA compliance of VC funds)

### 5.2 Signing and Closing

**Simultaneous Signing and Closing (Most Common for VC):**
- All documents signed and funds wired on the same day
- Company delivers: signed documents, certificates, legal opinion
- Investors deliver: signed documents, funds (wire transfer)
- Closing confirmed when all documents are signed and funds are received

**Split Signing and Closing (Less Common):**
- Documents signed on one day; conditions satisfied later; closing occurs when all conditions met
- Used when: regulatory approval required, third-party consent needed, complex conditions

### 5.3 Post-Closing Actions

- [ ] Update cap table to reflect new investment
- [ ] File Form D with SEC (within 15 days)
- [ ] File state blue sky notice filings
- [ ] Issue stock certificates or electronic entries
- [ ] Update board composition per Voting Agreement
- [ ] Schedule new board meeting
- [ ] Commence 409A valuation for post-round fair market value
- [ ] Update D&O insurance coverage
- [ ] Press release / public announcement (if desired, coordinated with investors)
- [ ] Set up information rights reporting cadence

## 6. Special Situations

### 6.1 Bridge Financing

When the company needs capital between rounds:
- Convertible note or SAFE with conversion into the next round
- May include warrant coverage (additional equity sweetener for bridge investors)
- Existing investors may provide bridge with favorable terms
- Risk: Bridge at punitive terms (high discount, low cap) dilutes founders significantly

### 6.2 Down Rounds

When the new round is at a lower valuation than the previous round:
- Anti-dilution provisions are triggered (existing investors' conversion price adjusts downward)
- Pay-to-play: Investors must participate or lose anti-dilution protection
- May require recapitalization (restructuring existing preferred)
- Significant dilution to common stockholders
- Consider: Is the capital necessary? Are the terms the best available? What is the alternative?

### 6.3 Inside Rounds

When existing investors lead the new round (no new lead investor):
- Heightened fiduciary duty concerns (investors on both sides of the transaction)
- Board should consider: independent valuation, independent committee of disinterested directors, market check (did you try to find outside lead?)
- Document process carefully to protect against breach of fiduciary duty claims
