## 1. IP Portfolio Strategy Overview

Intellectual property is often the most valuable asset of a technology company. A coherent IP strategy aligns protection mechanisms with business objectives, competitive positioning, and available resources.

### 1.1 The Four Pillars of IP Protection

| Pillar | What It Protects | Duration | Registration | Cost |
|--------|-----------------|----------|-------------|------|
| **Patents** | Inventions (processes, methods, systems, compositions) | 20 years from filing | Required (USPTO) | $15K-50K+ per patent |
| **Trademarks** | Brand identifiers (names, logos, slogans, trade dress) | Indefinite (with renewal) | Recommended (USPTO) | $2K-5K per mark |
| **Copyrights** | Original works of authorship (code, content, designs) | Life + 70 years (or 95 years for work-for-hire) | Recommended (Copyright Office) | $500-1K per registration |
| **Trade Secrets** | Confidential business information (algorithms, data, processes) | Indefinite (while secret) | None (protection through secrecy) | Ongoing operational cost |

### 1.2 Strategic Decision Framework

**Step 1: Inventory IP Assets**
- Core technology (algorithms, architectures, data models)
- Product features and user experience innovations
- Brand elements (name, logo, tagline, domain names)
- Content (documentation, marketing materials, training materials)
- Data assets (proprietary datasets, customer insights, models)
- Processes (business methods, operational workflows)

**Step 2: Classify by Strategic Value**
| Classification | Definition | Protection Strategy |
|---------------|------------|-------------------|
| Crown Jewels | Core competitive advantage; loss would be existential | Maximum protection (patent + trade secret + contractual) |
| Important | Significant competitive value; loss would be costly | Strong protection (selective patents + trade secret) |
| Routine | Standard technology; competitive value is low | Basic protection (trade secret + copyright) |
| Commoditized | Widely known; no competitive advantage | No active protection; consider open-sourcing strategically |

**Step 3: Select Protection Mechanisms**
Protection mechanisms are not mutually exclusive. A single innovation may be protected by multiple mechanisms simultaneously (e.g., patented method + trade secret for implementation details + copyright for code expression + trademark for product name).

## 2. Patent Strategy

### 2.1 When to Patent

**Patent When:**
- The invention is independently discoverable (reverse-engineering is feasible)
- You need offensive capability (assert against competitors or infringers)
- The patent would create a meaningful barrier to entry
- You are building a portfolio for licensing revenue
- Investors or acquirers expect a patent portfolio (common in deep tech, biotech, hardware)
- You need to establish priority over a competitor working on similar technology

**Do NOT Patent When:**
- The invention is better protected as a trade secret (not independently discoverable)
- The cost of prosecution exceeds the commercial value of the patent
- The technology will be obsolete before the patent issues (2-4 year prosecution timeline)
- Publishing the patent specification would educate competitors more than it protects you
- Enforcement would be impractical (difficult to detect infringement)

### 2.2 Defensive vs. Offensive Patent Strategy

**Defensive Strategy:**
- Goal: Deter patent infringement lawsuits against you
- Method: Build a portfolio that creates mutual assured destruction (MAD) with competitors
- Mechanism: Cross-licensing agreements; freedom to operate (FTO) analysis
- Cost-effective: Defensive publications (publish inventions to create prior art, preventing others from patenting)
- Organizations: Open Invention Network (OIN) for Linux/open source; LOT Network for reducing patent troll risk

**Offensive Strategy:**
- Goal: Assert patents against infringers for licensing revenue or injunctive relief
- Method: Prosecute high-quality patents with broad claims; actively monitor market for infringement
- Risk: Patent assertion invites counter-assertion and reputational concerns
- ROI: Can be significant (Qualcomm, Dolby, ARM business models are licensing-based)

### 2.3 Provisional vs. Utility Patents

**Provisional Patent Application:**
- Establishes priority date (critical: first-to-file system in US since AIA 2013)
- Lower cost ($1,500-5,000 including attorney fees)
- No formal claims required (but should include detailed description)
- Expires after 12 months (must file utility application to maintain priority)
- Not examined by USPTO; never becomes a patent on its own
- Strategic use: File provisionals early and often to establish priority; decide whether to convert to utility within 12 months based on business value

**Utility Patent Application:**
- Formal application with claims, specification, drawings
- Examined by USPTO (2-4 years average prosecution time)
- Cost: $15K-50K+ through issuance (including attorney fees, filing fees, office action responses)
- Maintenance fees required at 3.5, 7.5, and 11.5 years after issuance
- 20-year term from earliest effective filing date

### 2.4 Patent Prosecution Process

1. **Prior Art Search:** Identify existing patents and publications in the field ($2K-5K)
2. **Provisional Filing:** Establish priority date with detailed technical disclosure
3. **Utility Filing:** Within 12 months of provisional; includes claims defining scope
4. **Examination:** USPTO examiner reviews for novelty, non-obviousness, utility, enablement
5. **Office Actions:** Examiner rejections; attorney responds with arguments and claim amendments (typically 2-3 rounds)
6. **Allowance/Issuance:** Patent granted after all rejections overcome
7. **Maintenance:** Pay maintenance fees to keep patent in force
8. **International Filing:** PCT application within 12 months of priority date for international protection

### 2.5 Software Patents -- Special Considerations

Post-*Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank* (2014), software patents face heightened scrutiny:
- Abstract ideas are not patentable (Section 101)
- Two-part *Alice* test: (1) Is the claim directed to an abstract idea? (2) If yes, does the claim recite an "inventive concept" that transforms it into patent-eligible subject matter?
- Survival strategies: Claim specific technical improvements, not generic computer implementation of abstract concepts
- Focus claims on: data structures, specific algorithms, hardware-software interaction, improvements to computer functionality itself
- Avoid: Claims that merely automate manual processes or recite "do it on a computer"

## 3. Trademark Strategy

### 3.1 Trademark Registration Process

**Step 1: Clearance Search**
- Conduct comprehensive search (USPTO TESS database + state registrations + common law use + domain names)
- Assess risk of confusion with existing marks (likelihood of confusion factors: *Polaroid* or *Sleekcraft* test)
- Consider hiring a search firm for important marks ($500-1,500 per search)

**Step 2: Application Filing**
- File with USPTO (TEAS Plus: $250/class; TEAS Standard: $350/class)
- Basis: Use in commerce (Section 1(a)) or intent to use (Section 1(b))
- Identify goods/services and international classes accurately
- Consider filing in key international markets simultaneously (Madrid Protocol)

**Step 3: Examination**
- USPTO examining attorney reviews for registrability
- Common refusals: likelihood of confusion (Section 2(d)), descriptiveness (Section 2(e)(1)), generic term
- Response period: 3 months for office actions (extendable)

**Step 4: Publication**
- Published in Official Gazette for 30-day opposition period
- Third parties can oppose registration if they believe they would be damaged

**Step 5: Registration (or Statement of Use)**
- If filed under Section 1(a): Registration issues
- If filed under Section 1(b): Must file Statement of Use before registration (up to 3 years of extensions)

### 3.2 Trademark Strength Spectrum

| Category | Strength | Example | Registrability |
|----------|----------|---------|---------------|
| Fanciful | Strongest | Xerox, Kodak, Google | Easy to register |
| Arbitrary | Strong | Apple (for computers), Amazon (for retail) | Easy to register |
| Suggestive | Moderate | Copilot (for AI), Slack (for communication) | Generally registrable |
| Descriptive | Weak | "Best Buy" (requires secondary meaning) | Registrable only with proof of secondary meaning |
| Generic | None | "Computer Store" | Never registrable |

### 3.3 International Classes for Tech Companies

Common classes for technology companies:
- **Class 9:** Software, apps, downloadable content, computer hardware
- **Class 35:** Advertising services, business management, SaaS business tools
- **Class 38:** Telecommunications, internet access, data transmission
- **Class 41:** Education, training, entertainment services
- **Class 42:** SaaS, cloud computing, software development, IT consulting

## 4. Copyright Strategy

### 4.1 Copyright in Software

**What Is Protected:**
- Source code and object code (literary works)
- User interface elements (visual works -- but limited after *Oracle v. Google* (2021) for APIs)
- Documentation, specifications, manuals
- Website content, marketing materials
- Database compilations (selection and arrangement, not underlying facts)

**What Is NOT Protected:**
- Ideas, methods, systems, processes (these may be patentable)
- Facts and data (though compilations may be protected)
- Functional elements dictated by external factors (merger doctrine)
- APIs as functional specifications (*Google LLC v. Oracle America* -- fair use for reimplementation)

### 4.2 Copyright Registration

**Why Register (Even Though Copyright Exists Automatically):**
- Required for filing infringement lawsuit in US (for US works)
- Statutory damages ($750-$30,000 per work; up to $150,000 for willful infringement) -- only available if registered before infringement or within 3 months of publication
- Attorney's fees recoverable only with timely registration
- Prima facie evidence of ownership if registered within 5 years of publication

### 4.3 Work-for-Hire Doctrine

**Automatic Work-for-Hire (Employee):**
- Works created by employees within scope of employment are automatically owned by employer
- No assignment agreement needed (but best practice to include in employment agreement for certainty)

**Work-for-Hire by Agreement (Independent Contractor):**
- Must be in writing, signed before work begins
- Only applies to nine enumerated categories (Section 101 of Copyright Act)
- Software does not cleanly fit any category -- work-for-hire agreements for software by contractors may NOT be effective
- **Critical:** Always include a belt-and-suspenders IP assignment clause in contractor agreements alongside any work-for-hire provision

## 5. IP Assignment Agreements

### 5.1 Why IP Assignment Is Critical for Startups

Failure to properly assign IP to the company is the #1 IP-related due diligence finding that kills or delays fundraising and M&A transactions.

**Who Must Sign IP Assignment:**
- All founders (including those who contributed IP before incorporation)
- All employees (CIIA -- Confidential Information and Inventions Assignment agreement)
- All contractors and consultants (IP assignment in contractor agreement)
- Any third party who contributed to the technology

### 5.2 Key Provisions in IP Assignment

**Present Assignment Language:**
- Use "hereby assigns" (present tense, self-executing)
- NOT "agrees to assign" (future promise, requires separate execution)
- "Employee hereby assigns to Company all right, title, and interest in and to all Inventions..."

**Scope of Assignment:**
- All inventions, works of authorship, developments, improvements, discoveries
- Made during employment/engagement AND related to company business OR made using company resources
- Include moral rights waiver (where applicable and enforceable)

**Exceptions (Where Required by Law):**
- California Labor Code Section 2870: Cannot require assignment of inventions made entirely on employee's own time, without company equipment, unrelated to company business
- Similar statutes in: Delaware, Illinois, Kansas, Minnesota, North Carolina, Washington
- Best practice: Include statutory notice verbatim in the CIIA

## 6. IP Due Diligence Checklist

For fundraising or M&A readiness:

- [ ] All founders signed IP assignment agreements (including pre-incorporation IP)
- [ ] All employees signed CIIAs with IP assignment provisions
- [ ] All contractors signed agreements with IP assignment provisions
- [ ] No gaps in assignment chain (every contributor covered)
- [ ] Open source audit completed; no copyleft contamination of proprietary code
- [ ] Patent portfolio documented (provisional, pending, issued, with prosecution status)
- [ ] Trademark registrations current (applications, registrations, renewals)
- [ ] Copyright registrations filed for key software (optional but recommended)
- [ ] Trade secret protection measures documented and in place
- [ ] No known IP claims, disputes, or infringement risks
- [ ] Freedom to operate analysis completed for key technology areas
- [ ] Third-party IP licenses inventoried and compliant
