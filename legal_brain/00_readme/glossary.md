# Legal Brain -- Glossary of Terms

> **DISCLAIMER:** This content is for educational and informational purposes only. It does not constitute legal advice. Consult qualified legal counsel for specific legal matters.

---

## Purpose

This glossary defines key legal terms used throughout the Legal Brain modules. Definitions are drawn from authoritative legal sources including Black's Law Dictionary, the Restatement (Second) of Contracts, the Uniform Commercial Code (UCC), and relevant statutes and regulations. Terms are organized by legal domain.

---

## General Legal Terms

**Adjudication** -- The formal pronouncement of a judgment or decree in a legal proceeding; the process by which a court or tribunal resolves a dispute.

**Arbitration** -- A private dispute resolution mechanism in which parties submit their controversy to one or more impartial arbitrators who render a binding (or sometimes non-binding) decision. Governed in the US by the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C. Sections 1-16).

**Burden of Proof** -- The obligation of a party to establish a contested fact to a required degree of certainty. In civil cases, typically "preponderance of the evidence" (more likely than not); in criminal cases, "beyond a reasonable doubt."

**Cause of Action** -- A set of facts sufficient to justify a legal right to sue; the factual and legal basis for a claim for judicial relief.

**Common Law** -- Law developed by courts through judicial decisions, as distinguished from statutory law enacted by legislatures. The US (except Louisiana) and UK legal systems are common law systems.

**De Minimis** -- Too trivial or minor to merit legal consideration. From the Latin maxim "de minimis non curat lex" (the law does not concern itself with trifles).

**Discovery** -- The pre-trial phase in litigation during which parties exchange relevant information and evidence. Includes interrogatories, document requests, depositions, and requests for admission.

**Due Diligence** -- The investigation or audit of a potential investment, business, or transaction to confirm material facts, identify risks, and inform decision-making. In M&A, the comprehensive review of a target company's legal, financial, and operational status.

**Fiduciary Duty** -- The highest standard of care recognized in law. A fiduciary must act in the best interests of the beneficiary, with duties of care, loyalty, and good faith. Directors and officers of corporations owe fiduciary duties to stockholders.

**Force Majeure** -- An unforeseeable event beyond the control of parties that prevents contractual performance. Common examples: natural disasters, war, pandemic, government action. Must typically be specifically enumerated in the contract to be invoked.

**Forum Selection** -- A contractual clause designating the court or jurisdiction where disputes must be litigated. Upheld under the US Supreme Court's ruling in M/S Bremen v. Zapata Off-Shore Co., 407 U.S. 1 (1972).

**Indemnification** -- A contractual obligation by which one party agrees to compensate another for specified losses or damages. Distinguished from a warranty (a promise that a fact is true) and a guarantee (a promise to answer for another's debt).

**Injunction** -- A court order requiring a party to do or refrain from doing a specific act. Preliminary injunctions are issued before trial; permanent injunctions after final judgment.

**Jurisdiction** -- The authority of a court to hear and decide a case. Personal jurisdiction concerns authority over the parties; subject matter jurisdiction concerns authority over the type of dispute.

**Liability** -- Legal responsibility for one's acts or omissions. Can be civil (damages, injunction) or criminal (fines, imprisonment). "Strict liability" imposes responsibility without fault.

**Mediation** -- A voluntary, non-binding dispute resolution process in which a neutral mediator facilitates negotiation between parties. Unlike arbitration, the mediator does not impose a decision.

**Negligence** -- Failure to exercise the standard of care that a reasonably prudent person would exercise in similar circumstances. Elements: duty, breach, causation, damages.

**Precedent (Stare Decisis)** -- The legal principle that courts should follow the rulings of prior cases when deciding similar issues. Binding precedent comes from higher courts within the same jurisdiction; persuasive precedent from other jurisdictions.

**Privilege (Attorney-Client)** -- The legal protection of confidential communications between a client and their attorney made for the purpose of seeking or providing legal advice. Waivable only by the client.

**Pro Rata** -- In proportion to a share, interest, or ownership percentage. Commonly used in equity distribution, liquidation preferences, and insurance allocation.

**Statute of Frauds** -- A legal doctrine requiring certain types of contracts to be in writing to be enforceable. Under UCC Section 2-201, contracts for the sale of goods over $500 must be in writing. Under common law, contracts that cannot be performed within one year, transfers of real property interests, and suretyship agreements require writing.

**Statute of Limitations** -- The time period within which a legal action must be commenced. Varies by claim type and jurisdiction. Contract claims: typically 4-6 years; tort claims: typically 2-3 years; UCC sale of goods: 4 years (Section 2-725).

**Venue** -- The geographic location (county, district) where a case is heard. Distinguished from jurisdiction (the court's legal authority to hear the case).

**Waiver** -- The voluntary, intentional relinquishment of a known right. Can be express (written statement) or implied (conduct inconsistent with assertion of the right). Under UCC Section 1-306, a waiver of one breach does not waive future breaches.

## Contract Law Terms

**Acceptance** -- Manifestation of assent to the terms of an offer, creating a binding contract. Under the common law "mirror image rule," acceptance must match the offer exactly. Under UCC Section 2-207, additional or different terms in acceptance do not prevent contract formation between merchants.

**Breach** -- Failure, without legal excuse, to perform any promise which forms the whole or part of a contract. Material breach excuses the non-breaching party's performance; immaterial breach does not. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Sections 235-243.

**Condition** -- An event, not certain to occur, which must occur before a party's performance becomes due (condition precedent), or the occurrence of which extinguishes an existing duty (condition subsequent). See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Section 224.

**Consideration** -- Something of value (promise, act, forbearance) exchanged between parties to a contract. Necessary for contract formation under common law. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Section 71. Adequacy of consideration is generally not examined by courts.

**Covenant** -- A binding promise in a contract to do or refrain from doing something. Distinguished from a condition (which qualifies a duty) and a representation (which states a fact). Affirmative covenants require action; negative covenants prohibit action.

**Damages (Expectation)** -- The standard measure of contract damages, intended to put the non-breaching party in the position they would have occupied had the contract been fully performed. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Section 347.

**Damages (Consequential)** -- Losses flowing from the breach that are not direct but are foreseeable at the time of contracting. Under Hadley v. Baxendale (1854), recoverable only if reasonably foreseeable. Frequently excluded or capped in commercial contracts.

**Damages (Liquidated)** -- A sum specified in the contract as damages in the event of breach. Enforceable if it is a reasonable estimate of anticipated harm and actual damages would be difficult to calculate. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Section 356; UCC Section 2-718.

**Entire Agreement (Integration Clause)** -- A provision stating that the written contract contains the complete agreement between the parties and supersedes all prior negotiations, representations, and agreements. Invokes the parol evidence rule. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Sections 209-218.

**Novation** -- The substitution of a new obligation for an existing one, or the substitution of a new party for an original party, with the consent of all parties. Extinguishes the prior obligation.

**Offer** -- A manifestation of willingness to enter into a bargain, creating in the offeree the power of acceptance. Must be sufficiently definite and communicated to the offeree. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Section 24.

**Parol Evidence Rule** -- When a contract is fully integrated (a complete and final expression of the agreement), evidence of prior or contemporaneous agreements or negotiations that contradict or vary the writing is inadmissible. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Section 213.

**Representation** -- A statement of past or present fact made by one party to induce the other to enter into a contract. Distinguished from a warranty (which creates a contractual obligation). Misrepresentation may void the contract.

**Specific Performance** -- An equitable remedy requiring the breaching party to perform the contract as promised. Available when monetary damages are inadequate, typically for unique goods or real property. See Restatement (Second) of Contracts, Section 359; UCC Section 2-716.

**Warranty** -- A contractual promise that a statement of fact is or will be true. Express warranties arise from specific representations; implied warranties arise by operation of law. Under UCC, implied warranty of merchantability (Section 2-314) and fitness for a particular purpose (Section 2-315).

## Intellectual Property Terms

**Copyright** -- Legal protection for original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium of expression. Grants exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, perform, display, and create derivative works. Duration: life of author plus 70 years (17 U.S.C. Section 302).

**Copyleft** -- A licensing approach (originating with the GNU GPL) that requires derivative works to be distributed under the same or compatible license terms. Creates a "viral" licensing effect.

**Fair Use** -- A defense to copyright infringement permitting limited use of copyrighted material without permission for purposes such as criticism, comment, news reporting, teaching, scholarship, or research. Analyzed under the four-factor test of 17 U.S.C. Section 107.

**Patent** -- A government-granted exclusive right to an inventor for a novel, non-obvious, and useful invention. Utility patents last 20 years from filing date; design patents last 15 years from grant date. Governed by 35 U.S.C.

**Prior Art** -- Any evidence that an invention was already known, used, published, or patented before the filing date of a patent application. Used to challenge novelty and non-obviousness requirements.

**Trade Dress** -- The visual appearance of a product or its packaging that serves as a source identifier. Protectable under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Section 1125(a)) when distinctive and non-functional.

**Trade Secret** -- Information that derives economic value from not being generally known and is subject to reasonable efforts to maintain its secrecy. Protected under the Defend Trade Secrets Act (18 U.S.C. Section 1836) and state UTSA adoptions.

**Trademark** -- A word, phrase, symbol, design, or combination thereof that identifies and distinguishes the source of goods or services. Registered with the USPTO under the Lanham Act (15 U.S.C. Sections 1051-1141). Rights can also arise through common law use.

**Work for Hire** -- Under copyright law, a work created by an employee within the scope of employment, or a work specially ordered or commissioned in certain categories with a written agreement. The employer or commissioning party is the author. See 17 U.S.C. Section 101.

## Privacy and Data Protection Terms

**Controller** -- Under GDPR Article 4(7), the entity that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data. Analogous to "business" under CCPA.

**Data Minimization** -- The GDPR principle (Article 5(1)(c)) that personal data collected must be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary for the stated processing purposes.

**Data Processing Agreement (DPA)** -- A contract between a data controller and data processor (or business and service provider under CCPA) governing the processing of personal data. Required under GDPR Article 28.

**Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)** -- A risk assessment required under GDPR Article 35 before processing that is likely to result in high risk to data subjects' rights and freedoms. Must describe processing, assess necessity and proportionality, and identify mitigating measures.

**Data Subject** -- Under GDPR, an identified or identifiable natural person whose personal data is processed. Has rights including access, rectification, erasure, portability, and objection.

**Lawful Basis** -- Under GDPR Article 6, one of six legal grounds that must exist for processing personal data: consent, contractual necessity, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, or legitimate interests.

**Personal Data** -- Under GDPR Article 4(1), any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person. Under CCPA Section 1798.140(v), information that identifies, relates to, describes, or could reasonably be linked to a particular consumer or household.

**Processor** -- Under GDPR Article 4(8), an entity that processes personal data on behalf of the controller. Analogous to "service provider" under CCPA.

**Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** -- Pre-approved contractual terms adopted by the European Commission for transferring personal data from the EU/EEA to third countries without an adequacy decision. Current version adopted June 2021.

## Corporate and Securities Terms

**83(b) Election** -- A tax election under IRC Section 83(b) allowing a recipient of restricted stock to recognize income at the time of grant rather than at vesting, based on the fair market value at grant. Must be filed with the IRS within 30 days of the stock grant.

**Accredited Investor** -- Under SEC Rule 501 of Regulation D, an individual with income exceeding $200,000 ($300,000 jointly) in each of the two most recent years, or net worth exceeding $1 million (excluding primary residence), or a qualifying entity.

**Cap Table** -- A capitalization table listing all securities issued by a company, including common stock, preferred stock, options, warrants, and convertible instruments, along with their holders and relevant terms.

**Fiduciary Duty of Care** -- The obligation of directors and officers to act with the care that an ordinarily prudent person in a like position would exercise under similar circumstances. Protected by the business judgment rule in Delaware.

**Fiduciary Duty of Loyalty** -- The obligation of directors and officers to act in good faith and in the best interests of the corporation and its stockholders, not in their own self-interest. Prohibits self-dealing, corporate opportunities doctrine.

**Liquidation Preference** -- A provision in preferred stock terms specifying the amount preferred stockholders receive before common stockholders in a liquidation event. "1x non-participating" means return of investment; "participating" means return of investment plus pro rata share of remaining proceeds.

**QSBS (Qualified Small Business Stock)** -- Under IRC Section 1202, stock in a qualifying C corporation that, if held for more than 5 years, may be eligible for exclusion of up to 100% of capital gains (up to $10 million or 10x basis).

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)** -- An investment instrument developed by Y Combinator in which an investor provides capital in exchange for the right to receive equity in a future priced round. Not debt; no maturity date or interest.

## Dispute Resolution Terms

**Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR)** -- Methods of resolving disputes outside of traditional litigation, including mediation, arbitration, and negotiation. Often required contractually before litigation may be commenced.

**E-Discovery** -- The discovery of electronically stored information (ESI) in litigation or regulatory proceedings. Governed by Federal Rules of Civil Procedure Rules 26 and 34, and the Sedona Principles.

**Legal Hold (Litigation Hold)** -- The obligation to preserve potentially relevant documents and ESI when litigation is reasonably anticipated. Failure to implement a legal hold may result in spoliation sanctions.

**Settlement** -- An agreement between parties to resolve a dispute without a full trial. Typically documented in a settlement agreement and release, which may include confidentiality provisions.

**Spoliation** -- The intentional or negligent destruction of evidence relevant to litigation. May result in adverse inference instructions, monetary sanctions, or default judgment.
