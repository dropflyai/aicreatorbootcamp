## 1. Board of Directors

### 1.1 Board Composition

**Early Stage (Pre-Series A):**
- Typically 1-3 directors
- Founders serve as directors
- No outside directors required (but advisable to have at least one independent voice)
- Board approval required for major decisions (equity issuance, debt, acquisitions, officer compensation)

**Post-Series A:**
- Typically 3-5 directors
- Common composition: 2 founders/common directors + 1 investor director + 1-2 independent directors
- Investor directors nominated by preferred stockholders (per Voting Agreement)
- Independent directors mutually agreed by common and preferred stockholders

**Growth Stage (Series B+):**
- Typically 5-7 directors
- Additional investor directors (one per lead investor in each round, typically)
- Additional independent directors
- Board composition becomes a significant negotiation point in each round

**Pre-IPO / Public:**
- Majority independent directors required (NYSE/NASDAQ listing standards)
- Committee requirements: Audit (all independent), Compensation (all independent), Nominating/Governance (all independent)
- Lead independent director or independent board chair

### 1.2 Board Composition Negotiation

| Seat Type | Nominated By | Typical Number |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| Common | Founders / common stockholders | 1-2 |
| Preferred (Series A) | Series A lead investor | 1 |
| Preferred (Series B) | Series B lead investor | 0-1 |
| Independent | Mutual agreement of Common + Preferred | 1-2 |

**Key Negotiation Principles:**
- Founders should resist losing board control as long as possible
- Odd number of directors avoids deadlocks
- Independent directors should be truly independent (no financial relationship with company or investors)
- Board observer seats (non-voting) are a compromise when investors want representation but a board seat is not warranted

## 2. Fiduciary Duties

### 2.1 Duty of Care

Directors must act with the care that an ordinarily prudent person would exercise in a like position under similar circumstances.

**What This Requires:**
- Attend board meetings and be prepared (review materials in advance)
- Stay informed about the company's business and financial condition
- Exercise independent judgment (do not rubber-stamp management proposals)
- Make informed decisions (consider all material information reasonably available)
- Ask questions and seek expert advice when needed (legal, financial, technical)

**Practical Standards:**
- Read board packages before meetings
- Review financial statements and understand key metrics
- Understand major contracts, litigation, and regulatory matters
- Visit company facilities and engage with management between meetings
- Document decision-making process in board minutes

### 2.2 Duty of Loyalty

Directors must act in good faith and in the best interests of the corporation and its stockholders, rather than in their own interest or the interest of third parties.

**What This Requires:**
- No self-dealing (transactions in which the director has a personal interest)
- No usurpation of corporate opportunities (cannot take business opportunities that belong to the company)
- No competition with the company
- Disclose all material conflicts of interest
- Recuse from votes where conflicted

**Conflict of Interest Protocol:**
1. Director identifies potential conflict (financial interest, relationship, competing obligation)
2. Director discloses conflict to the full board
3. Director recuses from discussion and vote on the conflicted matter
4. Remaining disinterested directors deliberate and decide
5. Document the conflict disclosure, recusal, and independent deliberation in board minutes

**Common Conflicts in Startup Context:**
- VC director's fund is a party to the transaction (e.g., follow-on investment, bridge loan)
- Founder-director is negotiating their own compensation or severance
- Director has a financial interest in a vendor or customer
- Director serves on the board of a company that could be a competitor or partner

### 2.3 Business Judgment Rule

The business judgment rule is a presumption that directors acted on an informed basis, in good faith, and in the honest belief that the action taken was in the best interest of the company.

**When It Applies:**
- Director was disinterested and independent with respect to the decision
- Director was informed (made a reasonable effort to obtain material information)
- Director acted in good faith
- Director was not grossly negligent

**When It Does NOT Apply:**
- Self-dealing or conflict of interest (entire fairness review applies)
- Waste of corporate assets (no rational business purpose)
- Bad faith (intentional failure to act, conscious disregard of duties)
- Decisions made without adequate information (uninformed decisions)

**Standard of Review When Business Judgment Rule Does Not Apply:**
- **Entire Fairness:** The most exacting standard. Burden on defendants to prove both fair dealing (process) and fair price (substance). Applied to interested transactions.
- **Enhanced Scrutiny (Revlon/Unocal):** Applied in change-of-control transactions. Board must show reasonable grounds for believing a threat existed (Unocal) or that it sought the best price reasonably available (Revlon).

## 3. Board Meetings

### 3.1 Meeting Requirements

**Notice:**
- Written notice required (bylaws specify notice period, typically 2-10 days)
- Notice must include date, time, place (or conference call information)
- Waiver of notice: Director can waive notice in writing (before or after meeting)
- Attendance without objection constitutes waiver of notice

**Quorum:**
- Majority of authorized directors (unless bylaws specify otherwise)
- Each director has one vote
- Actions require majority of directors present at a duly constituted meeting
- If quorum is lost during meeting, no further action can be taken

**Written Consent in Lieu of Meeting:**
- DGCL Section 141(f): Board may act by unanimous written consent without a meeting
- Must be signed by ALL directors (not just a majority)
- Commonly used for routine actions (approving option grants, ratifying officer actions)
- Best practice: Use written consent for non-controversial matters; hold meetings for substantive discussions

### 3.2 Board Minutes

**What to Include:**
- Date, time, location (or that meeting was held by conference call/video)
- Directors present and absent (note quorum)
- Officers, counsel, and other attendees
- Summary of matters discussed (sufficient detail to show informed deliberation)
- Resolutions approved (verbatim text)
- Recusals and conflicts noted
- Approval by vote (unanimous, majority, or specific vote count)
- Executive session (note that it occurred; do not minute the discussion content)

**What NOT to Include:**
- Verbatim transcript of discussion (creates discovery risk)
- Individual director's positions or dissents (unless director specifically requests inclusion)
- Legal advice in detail (waives privilege; note "counsel advised the board regarding [topic]")
- Speculative or forward-looking statements that could be taken out of context

### 3.3 Board Materials

**Standard Board Package:**
1. Agenda
2. Prior meeting minutes (for approval)
3. Financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow, runway)
4. Key metrics dashboard (ARR, MRR, churn, CAC, LTV, headcount)
5. CEO report (strategic updates, key wins, challenges)
6. Department reports (as relevant)
7. Action items requiring board approval (resolutions)
8. Legal/compliance updates (litigation, regulatory, material contracts)

**Distribution:** 5-7 days before the meeting (give directors time to review)

## 4. Board Committees

### 4.1 Audit Committee

**Purpose:** Oversee financial reporting, internal controls, external audit, and risk management.

**Key Responsibilities:**
- Select and oversee external auditor
- Review financial statements and SEC filings (public company)
- Oversee internal audit function
- Review related party transactions
- Oversee whistleblower/ethics hotline
- Review cybersecurity and data privacy risk

**Composition:** All independent directors; at least one financial expert (public company requirement)

**When to Establish:** Series C+ or pre-IPO (or when board exceeds 5 members)

### 4.2 Compensation Committee

**Purpose:** Set executive compensation and oversee equity incentive programs.

**Key Responsibilities:**
- Set CEO compensation (base, bonus, equity)
- Approve executive compensation packages
- Administer stock option/equity incentive plans
- Approve option grants and equity awards
- Review compensation philosophy and peer benchmarking
- Review and approve employment agreements and severance arrangements

**Composition:** All independent directors (no company executives)

**When to Establish:** Series B+ or when executive compensation becomes complex

### 4.3 Nominating and Governance Committee

**Purpose:** Identify and recommend director candidates; oversee corporate governance practices.

**Key Responsibilities:**
- Identify and evaluate board candidates
- Recommend director nominees
- Oversee board evaluation process
- Review and recommend governance guidelines
- Oversee director onboarding and education

**When to Establish:** Pre-IPO (required for public companies)

## 5. D&O Insurance

### 5.1 Why D&O Insurance Is Essential

Directors and Officers (D&O) insurance protects individual directors and officers from personal liability for claims arising from their service.

**Who It Protects:**
- Directors (board members)
- Officers (CEO, CFO, CTO, VP-level and above)
- The company itself (for indemnification obligations and securities claims)

**Types of Claims Covered:**
- Securities fraud allegations (investor lawsuits)
- Breach of fiduciary duty claims
- Employment practices liability (discrimination, wrongful termination)
- Regulatory investigations and proceedings
- Derivative lawsuits (stockholder suing on behalf of the company)
- M&A-related claims (alleged inadequate sale price)

### 5.2 D&O Policy Structure

| Coverage Part | What It Covers | Who It Protects |
|--------------|---------------|----------------|
| Side A | Loss for which the company does not (or cannot) indemnify directors/officers | Individual directors/officers |
| Side B | Reimburses the company for indemnification payments to directors/officers | The company |
| Side C (Entity Coverage) | Claims against the company itself (securities claims for public companies) | The company |

### 5.3 D&O Insurance Procurement

**When to Purchase:**
- Before first board meeting with outside directors
- Required before any fundraising round (investors will insist; directors will insist)
- Must be in place before directors agree to serve

**Typical Costs:**
| Stage | Annual Premium | Coverage Limit |
|-------|---------------|---------------|
| Seed | $2,000-5,000 | $1M-3M |
| Series A | $5,000-15,000 | $3M-5M |
| Series B | $15,000-50,000 | $5M-10M |
| Series C+ | $50,000-200,000 | $10M-25M |
| Pre-IPO / Public | $200,000-1M+ | $25M-100M+ |

## 6. Stockholder Agreements

### 6.1 Voting Agreement

**Purpose:** Governs how certain stockholders vote their shares, particularly regarding board composition.

**Key Provisions:**
- Board composition: Specifies who nominates each director seat
- Voting commitments: Stockholders agree to vote for nominated directors
- Drag-along: If specified majority approves a sale, all stockholders must vote in favor
- Termination: Usually upon IPO, dissolution, or mutual agreement

### 6.2 Investors' Rights Agreement (IRA)

**Key Provisions:**
- **Registration rights:** Right to have shares registered for public sale (demand registration, piggyback registration, S-3 registration)
- **Information rights:** Right to receive financial statements (monthly, quarterly, annual), budgets, and cap table
- **Pro rata rights:** Right to participate in future financing rounds to maintain ownership percentage
- **Board observer rights:** Right to attend board meetings as non-voting observer
- **Key person provisions:** Company must maintain key person(s) or notify investors

### 6.3 Right of First Refusal and Co-Sale Agreement (ROFR)

**Key Provisions:**
- **ROFR:** If a stockholder wants to sell shares, the company has first right to purchase, then investors have right to purchase
- **Co-sale (tag-along):** If a founder sells shares, investors can sell a proportional amount alongside (prevents founders from cashing out while investors are locked in)
- **Exceptions:** Typically excludes estate planning transfers, transfers to family members, transfers to affiliates

### 6.4 Protective Provisions

Rights given to preferred stockholders to veto certain corporate actions:

**Typical Protective Provisions (Require Preferred Stockholder Approval):**
- Issuance of new equity (dilution protection)
- Change in authorized shares
- Declaration of dividends
- Change in board size
- Creation of debt above a threshold
- Change of control (merger, acquisition, asset sale)
- Amendment to charter or bylaws that adversely affects preferred
- Liquidation or dissolution
- Creation of a new series of stock senior to or pari passu with existing preferred
