# Career Development -- Ladders, Competencies, Succession, and Mobility

## Purpose

This module defines the framework for employee career development. Career growth is consistently ranked as a top driver of engagement and retention. Gallup research shows that 87% of millennials rate professional development opportunities as important in a job, and CEB/Gartner finds that employees who perceive career opportunities are 2.5x more engaged. The HR Brain mandates structured career development systems that are transparent, equitable, and aligned to organizational needs.

---

## 1. Career Ladders

### 1.1 Definition and Design Principles

A career ladder defines the progression path within a job family, from entry-level through the most senior individual contributor or management roles. Well-designed career ladders provide:

- **Clarity**: Employees understand what is expected at each level
- **Fairness**: Promotion criteria are objective and consistently applied
- **Motivation**: Visible path forward drives engagement and effort
- **Retention**: Internal growth opportunities reduce external job-seeking

### 1.2 Dual-Track Career Architecture

Most knowledge-work organizations require both individual contributor (IC) and management tracks to avoid forcing top ICs into management roles they are not suited for:

```
                    INDIVIDUAL CONTRIBUTOR TRACK    │    MANAGEMENT TRACK
                                                    │
Level 7:    Distinguished/Fellow Engineer           │    VP of Engineering
Level 6:    Principal Engineer                      │    Senior Director
Level 5:    Staff Engineer                          │    Director
            ────────────────────── BRIDGE ──────────│──────────────────
Level 4:    Senior Engineer                         │    Engineering Manager
Level 3:    Engineer                                │
Level 2:    Junior Engineer                         │
Level 1:    Associate Engineer                      │

Key principles:
- IC and management tracks are PARALLEL in seniority and compensation
- Staff Engineer = Director in scope, impact, and pay
- Moving from IC to management (or back) is a LATERAL move, not promotion
- Bridge point: Level 4-5 is where the tracks diverge
- IC track requires increasing technical breadth and organizational impact
- Management track requires increasing people leadership and strategic scope
```

### 1.3 Level Expectations Matrix

Each level must be clearly defined across multiple dimensions:

| Dimension | Junior (L1-2) | Mid (L3) | Senior (L4) | Staff (L5) | Principal (L6) |
|-----------|--------------|----------|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Scope** | Individual tasks | Features/projects | Multi-team projects | Org-wide initiatives | Company-wide strategy |
| **Autonomy** | Guided | Independent on defined problems | Defines approach | Self-directed | Shapes direction |
| **Complexity** | Well-defined | Moderate ambiguity | Significant ambiguity | Highly ambiguous | Novel/unprecedented |
| **Impact** | Team | Team/multi-team | Multi-team | Organization | Company/industry |
| **Influence** | Within team | Across team | Across teams | Across organization | Across company |
| **Technical depth** | Learning | Solid | Deep | Broad and deep | Industry-leading |
| **Mentorship** | Receives | Gives informally | Mentors juniors | Mentors seniors | Mentors staff+ |
| **Communication** | Reports status | Explains decisions | Influences decisions | Shapes strategy | External thought leadership |

### 1.4 Promotion Criteria and Process

**Promotion is NOT:**
- A reward for tenure ("they've been here 3 years")
- A retention tool ("they'll leave if we don't promote them")
- Based on potential alone ("they could do the next level")

**Promotion IS:**
- Evidence that the employee is ALREADY performing at the next level consistently
- Based on documented demonstration of next-level competencies
- Validated through calibration with peers at the target level

**Promotion process:**
```
1. NOMINATION (Manager or self-nomination)
   - Written promotion case: specific examples of next-level performance
   - Duration requirement: performing at next level for 3-6 months minimum
   - Sponsor support: at least one peer-level or above advocate

2. EVIDENCE REVIEW (HRBP + skip-level manager)
   - Review promotion case against level expectations matrix
   - Check for consistent next-level performance (not one-time events)
   - Verify compensation alignment with new level band

3. CALIBRATION (Promotion committee)
   - Cross-team calibration to ensure consistent standards
   - Diverse committee membership
   - Document rationale for approval or denial

4. COMMUNICATION
   - If approved: Manager delivers news; effective next cycle or immediately
   - If denied: Manager provides specific feedback on gaps with development plan
   - Denied promotions MUST include a path to close gaps
```

## 2. Competency Models

### 2.1 Definition

A competency model is a structured collection of competencies (knowledge, skills, abilities, and behaviors) that define what is required for effective performance in a role, function, or level. Competency models are the backbone of career development, hiring, performance management, and learning.

### 2.2 Competency Model Architecture

**Core competencies (apply to all employees):**
- Communication
- Collaboration
- Accountability
- Learning agility
- Inclusion and belonging

**Functional competencies (specific to job family):**
- Engineering: System design, code quality, debugging, technical communication
- Product: User research, prioritization, stakeholder management, data analysis
- Sales: Discovery, negotiation, pipeline management, relationship building

**Leadership competencies (apply to all people managers):**
- People development
- Decision-making
- Strategic thinking
- Change management
- Team building

### 2.3 Competency Proficiency Levels

Each competency should be defined at multiple proficiency levels:

```
COMPETENCY: COLLABORATION

Level 1 - FOUNDATIONAL
  Behaviors: Participates constructively in team discussions. Shares
  information when asked. Completes commitments to team members.

Level 2 - DEVELOPING
  Behaviors: Proactively shares relevant information. Seeks input from
  others before making decisions that affect them. Resolves minor
  conflicts directly with colleagues.

Level 3 - PROFICIENT
  Behaviors: Facilitates effective collaboration across team boundaries.
  Builds relationships with key stakeholders. Navigates competing
  priorities to find mutually beneficial solutions.

Level 4 - ADVANCED
  Behaviors: Designs and implements collaborative processes that improve
  cross-functional effectiveness. Mentors others in stakeholder management.
  Resolves complex multi-party conflicts.

Level 5 - MASTER
  Behaviors: Shapes organizational collaboration culture. Creates
  frameworks and systems that enable collaboration at scale. Influences
  industry practices in collaborative work.
```

## 3. Individual Development Plans (IDPs)

### 3.1 Purpose and Research

IDPs formalize the employee's development goals, gap analysis, and action plan. Research from the Corporate Leadership Council (2004) found that employees with strong development plans are 3.5x more likely to be engaged and 2x more likely to stay.

### 3.2 IDP Framework

```
INDIVIDUAL DEVELOPMENT PLAN

Employee: _______________     Manager: _______________
Date: _______________         Review date: _______________

SECTION 1: CAREER ASPIRATIONS (1-3 year horizon)
  Where do you want to be in your career?
  - Short-term (12 months): _______________
  - Medium-term (2-3 years): _______________
  - Long-term direction (3-5 years): _______________

SECTION 2: CURRENT STRENGTHS
  What competencies are you strongest in?
  1. _______________  (Evidence: _______________)
  2. _______________  (Evidence: _______________)
  3. _______________  (Evidence: _______________)

SECTION 3: DEVELOPMENT AREAS
  What competencies need growth to reach your next career goal?
  1. _______________  (Current level: ___ / Target level: ___)
  2. _______________  (Current level: ___ / Target level: ___)
  3. _______________  (Current level: ___ / Target level: ___)

SECTION 4: DEVELOPMENT ACTIONS (70-20-10 model)

  70% ON-THE-JOB LEARNING:
  - Stretch assignment: _______________
  - Cross-functional project: _______________
  - Job shadowing: _______________
  - Timeline: _______________

  20% LEARNING FROM OTHERS:
  - Mentor: _______________ (meeting frequency: ___)
  - Peer learning group: _______________
  - Coaching sessions: _______________
  - Timeline: _______________

  10% FORMAL LEARNING:
  - Course/certification: _______________
  - Conference: _______________
  - Book/resource: _______________
  - Timeline: _______________

SECTION 5: SUPPORT NEEDED
  - From manager: _______________
  - From organization: _______________
  - Budget required: _______________

SECTION 6: PROGRESS TRACKING
  Quarterly check-in dates: Q1: ___ Q2: ___ Q3: ___ Q4: ___
```

### 3.3 The 70-20-10 Development Model (Lombardo & Eichinger)

| Source | % | Examples |
|--------|:---:|---------|
| **On-the-job experience** | 70% | Stretch assignments, new projects, increased scope, lateral moves, cross-functional work, problem-solving |
| **Learning from others** | 20% | Mentoring, coaching, peer feedback, shadowing, teaching others, communities of practice |
| **Formal learning** | 10% | Courses, certifications, conferences, books, workshops, e-learning |

**Key insight**: Most development happens through challenging work experiences, not classroom training. Managers must deliberately create developmental assignments.

## 4. Mentoring Programs

### 4.1 Formal Mentoring Program Design

| Element | Best Practice |
|---------|-------------|
| **Matching** | Skills/interest-based matching (not random); allow employee input; cross-functional pairs preferred |
| **Duration** | 6-12 months per cycle; renewable by mutual agreement |
| **Meeting frequency** | Monthly minimum (60 minutes); employee drives agenda |
| **Training** | Brief orientation for both mentors and mentees (expectations, confidentiality, communication) |
| **Structure** | Provide discussion guides and goal templates; do not over-script |
| **Executive sponsorship** | Senior leader visibly sponsors the program; participates as mentor |
| **Measurement** | Pre/post survey on career clarity, network breadth, skill development; retention comparison |

### 4.2 Mentoring vs. Sponsorship

| Dimension | Mentor | Sponsor |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| Relationship | Advisory; shares wisdom | Advocacy; uses political capital |
| Action | Talks WITH you | Talks ABOUT you (in rooms you're not in) |
| Focus | Development and learning | Advancement and opportunity |
| Power dynamic | Can be any level above | Must be senior leader with influence |
| DEIB impact | Moderate | High (sponsors create access for underrepresented groups) |

**HR Brain mandate**: Formal sponsorship programs should be established for underrepresented groups at the senior IC and management levels, as sponsorship -- not mentoring -- is what closes the advancement gap (Hewlett, 2013).

## 5. Succession Planning

### 5.1 Definition

Succession planning is the systematic process of identifying and developing internal candidates to fill critical roles. It ensures organizational continuity, reduces key-person risk, and creates visible advancement pathways.

### 5.2 Succession Planning Framework

```
STEP 1: IDENTIFY CRITICAL ROLES
  - Roles where vacancy would significantly impact operations
  - Roles requiring rare or hard-to-replace expertise
  - Leadership positions at director level and above
  - Roles with single points of failure (only one person can do this)

STEP 2: ASSESS CURRENT BENCH STRENGTH
  For each critical role, identify:
  - Ready now: Could step in within 30 days (1-2 candidates minimum)
  - Ready in 1 year: With development, could fill the role in 12 months
  - Ready in 2-3 years: High-potential talent with longer development timeline
  - Emergency successor: Could temporarily cover if incumbent departed suddenly

STEP 3: TALENT ASSESSMENT (9-Box Grid)

                    HIGH POTENTIAL
                         │
  ┌──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┐
  │                      │                      │
  │   Inconsistent       │     Star             │
  │   Talent             │     (Promote/         │
  │   (Develop or        │      Stretch)         │
  │    reassess)         │                      │
  │                      │                      │
  ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┤
  │                      │                      │
  │   Misplaced          │     Core             │
  │   Talent             │     Contributor       │
  │   (Move to better    │     (Develop in role) │
  │    fit role)         │                      │
  │                      │                      │
  ├──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┤
  │                      │                      │
  │   Under-             │     Solid            │
  │   performer          │     Professional      │
  │   (PIP or exit)      │     (Retain; valued) │
  │                      │                      │
  └──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┘
                         │
                   HIGH PERFORMANCE

STEP 4: DEVELOPMENT ACTION PLANS
  For each successor candidate:
  - Gap analysis (current competencies vs. target role requirements)
  - IDP with specific development actions
  - Stretch assignments aligned to role preparation
  - Mentoring/sponsorship by current role incumbent or peer
  - Timeline for readiness

STEP 5: ANNUAL REVIEW
  - Update succession plans annually (minimum)
  - Review after any critical role vacancy or departure
  - Track pipeline diversity (if succession bench is homogeneous, intervene)
  - Board-level review for CEO and C-suite succession
```

### 5.3 Succession Planning Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Bench strength | % of critical roles with 1+ ready-now successor | >80% |
| Successor diversity | % of successor candidates from underrepresented groups | Meet or exceed current representation |
| Internal fill rate | % of critical role vacancies filled internally | >60% |
| Successor readiness | % of "ready in 1 year" candidates who are actually promoted within 18 months | >50% |
| Key person risk | # of critical roles with zero successors identified | 0 |

## 6. Internal Mobility

### 6.1 Why Internal Mobility Matters

Internal mobility -- the movement of employees between roles, teams, or functions -- is a primary driver of retention and organizational agility. LinkedIn data (2023) shows that employees who make an internal move have 75% higher retention at 2 years compared to those who do not.

### 6.2 Internal Mobility Program Design

**Internal job board:**
- All open positions posted internally before or simultaneously with external posting
- Minimum 5-day internal posting period before external advertising
- Employees can apply without manager approval (manager is notified, not gatekeeper)
- Clear process for internal candidates (modified interview, not full external process)

**Internal transfer guidelines:**

| Element | Policy |
|---------|--------|
| Eligibility | Minimum 12 months in current role (exceptions for high performers with manager approval) |
| Manager role | Supportive, not blocking (managers who block internal transfers are addressed) |
| Timeline | 2-4 week transition period; current manager and new manager negotiate start date |
| Performance requirement | Must be in good standing (meets expectations or above) |
| Compensation | Adjusted to new role's band; lateral moves may or may not include increase |
| Relocation | If applicable, standard relocation policy applies |
| Knowledge transfer | Mandatory transition plan before departure from current role |

### 6.3 Rotation and Stretch Programs

| Program | Duration | Target Audience | Purpose |
|---------|----------|----------------|---------|
| Job rotation | 6-12 months | High-potential talent, early-career | Breadth of experience; cross-functional understanding |
| Short-term assignment | 1-3 months | Any employee | Skill building; project staffing; explore interests |
| Shadowing program | 1-2 weeks | Employees exploring career options | Low-commitment career exploration |
| Internal gig marketplace | Variable | All employees | Project-based staffing; skill exposure |
| Leadership rotation | 12-18 months per rotation | Future leaders | Preparation for senior management |

## 7. Learning and Development Investment

### 7.1 L&D Budget Benchmarks

| Category | Small (<500 emp.) | Medium (500-5,000) | Large (5,000+) |
|----------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Annual L&D spend per employee | $500-1,500 | $1,000-2,500 | $1,500-3,000 |
| L&D as % of payroll | 1-2% | 2-3% | 2-4% |
| Annual learning hours per employee | 20-30 | 30-40 | 40-60 |

Source: ATD (Association for Talent Development) State of the Industry Report

### 7.2 Development Program Evaluation (Kirkpatrick Model)

| Level | Question | Method | When |
|:---:|---------|--------|------|
| 1 - Reaction | Did participants like the program? | Post-training survey | Immediately after |
| 2 - Learning | Did participants learn the content? | Pre/post assessments | Before and after |
| 3 - Behavior | Did participants change their behavior? | 360 feedback, manager observation | 3-6 months after |
| 4 - Results | Did the behavior change produce business results? | Business metrics correlation | 6-12 months after |

**HR Brain mandate**: Evaluate all significant L&D investments at Levels 1-3 minimum. Level 4 for programs exceeding $50K total investment.
