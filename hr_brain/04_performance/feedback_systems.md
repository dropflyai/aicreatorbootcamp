# Feedback Systems -- 360 Feedback, Coaching, and Difficult Conversations

## Purpose

This module defines the framework for multi-directional feedback, manager coaching capability, and navigating difficult performance conversations. Feedback is the primary mechanism by which employees learn, grow, and align their behavior with organizational expectations. Yet most organizations deliver feedback poorly -- too infrequently, too vaguely, and too focused on judgment rather than development. The HR Brain mandates feedback systems that are frequent, specific, evidence-based, and psychologically informed.

---

## 1. 360-Degree Feedback

### 1.1 Definition and Research Base

360-degree feedback collects performance data from multiple perspectives: self, manager, direct reports, peers, and sometimes external stakeholders (clients, cross-functional partners).

**Research summary:**
- 360 feedback for development purposes shows positive effects on behavior change (Smither, London, & Reilly, 2005: meta-analysis found modest but significant improvement over time)
- 360 feedback used for administrative decisions (pay, promotion) tends to produce rating inflation and political gaming (Bracken, Timmreck, & Church, 2001)
- Self-manager rating discrepancy is a powerful diagnostic: self-raters who rate themselves much higher than others rate them are often lower performers who lack self-awareness (Atwater & Yammarino, 1992)

### 1.2 When to Use 360 Feedback

| Use Case | Appropriate? | Rationale |
|----------|:---:|-----------|
| Leadership development | Yes | Rich developmental data from multiple perspectives |
| Manager effectiveness | Yes | Direct reports are best positioned to assess management quality |
| Team effectiveness diagnosis | Yes | Reveals interpersonal dynamics and communication patterns |
| Annual performance rating | Caution | Can supplement but should not replace manager evaluation |
| Compensation decisions | No | Creates perverse incentives; politicizes feedback |
| Promotion decisions | Limited | Can inform but risks popularity contest dynamics |
| Pre/post training evaluation | Yes | Measures behavioral change from training investment |

### 1.3 360 Feedback Process Design

```
PHASE 1: PREPARATION (2 weeks before launch)
  - Communicate purpose, process, and confidentiality to all participants
  - Select raters (employee nominates; manager approves; minimum 3 per category)
  - Rater categories: Self, Manager, Direct Reports (if applicable), Peers (3-5)
  - Ensure anonymity for all rater categories except manager
  - Minimum 3 respondents per category for anonymity protection

PHASE 2: DATA COLLECTION (2-week window)
  - Online survey instrument (validated competency-based questions)
  - Likert scale ratings (1-5) PLUS open-ended narrative questions
  - Response rate target: 80%+ per individual
  - Reminder at 1 week; escalation to manager if below 50% at day 10

PHASE 3: REPORT GENERATION
  - Individual report showing:
    - Self vs. others comparison (by rater category)
    - Strengths (highest-rated competencies)
    - Development areas (lowest-rated competencies)
    - Hidden strengths (others rate higher than self)
    - Blind spots (self rates higher than others)
    - Verbatim comments (aggregated by category, anonymous)
  - No individual rater scores visible (only category averages)

PHASE 4: DEBRIEF AND ACTION PLANNING (within 2 weeks of report)
  - Trained facilitator or executive coach reviews results with participant
  - Focus on patterns, not individual data points
  - Identify 2-3 development priorities
  - Create action plan with specific behaviors and timeline
  - Follow-up check-in at 90 days

PHASE 5: FOLLOW-UP (6-12 months)
  - Repeat 360 to measure progress
  - Compare longitudinal data
  - Adjust development plan based on progress
```

### 1.4 360 Feedback Questionnaire Design

**Competency-based items (example set for people managers):**

| Competency | Sample Item | Scale |
|------------|-----------|-------|
| Communication | "Communicates expectations clearly and ensures understanding" | 1 (Strongly Disagree) - 5 (Strongly Agree) |
| Feedback | "Provides timely, specific, and actionable feedback" | 1-5 |
| Development | "Actively supports my professional growth and career aspirations" | 1-5 |
| Decision-making | "Makes decisions in a timely manner with appropriate input" | 1-5 |
| Inclusiveness | "Creates an environment where all team members feel valued and heard" | 1-5 |
| Accountability | "Holds self and others accountable for commitments and results" | 1-5 |
| Strategic thinking | "Connects team work to broader organizational goals" | 1-5 |
| Trust | "I trust this person to act with integrity and in the team's best interest" | 1-5 |

**Open-ended questions (required):**
1. "What is this person's greatest strength as a [leader/colleague]?"
2. "What one thing, if changed, would have the biggest positive impact?"
3. "Is there anything else you would like this person to know?"

### 1.5 360 Feedback Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Risk | Prevention |
|-------------|------|-----------|
| Too many raters | Survey fatigue; low quality responses | Cap at 8-12 total raters per individual |
| No debrief | Data without coaching has minimal impact | Mandatory debrief with trained facilitator |
| Using for punitive purposes | Destroys trust in the feedback system | Separate developmental 360 from administrative processes |
| Rating retaliation | Raters punish colleagues for honest feedback | Strict anonymity; minimum 3 per category |
| Survey too long | Low completion rates; superficial responses | Cap at 25-30 items + 3 open-ended questions (15-20 minutes) |

## 2. Radical Candor Framework

### 2.1 Core Model (Scott, 2017)

Kim Scott's Radical Candor framework provides a practical model for giving feedback that is both caring and direct:

```
                    CARE PERSONALLY
                         │
                         │
    Ruinous Empathy      │      Radical Candor
    (Care but don't      │      (Care AND challenge
     challenge)          │       directly)
                         │
  ───────────────────────┼─────────────────────────
                         │
    Manipulative         │      Obnoxious Aggression
    Insincerity          │      (Challenge without
    (Neither care        │       caring)
     nor challenge)      │
                         │
                CHALLENGE DIRECTLY
```

### 2.2 Operationalizing Radical Candor

**Radical Candor (target quadrant):**
- "I care about your success, which is why I need to share this with you..."
- Specific, timely, and focused on behavior (not character)
- Delivered privately for constructive feedback; publicly for praise
- Includes both the observation and why it matters

**Ruinous Empathy (most common failure mode):**
- Avoiding hard conversations because you care about the person's feelings
- Giving vague praise instead of specific constructive feedback
- Not addressing performance issues because you don't want to hurt someone
- Result: The person never improves; eventually faces worse consequences

**Obnoxious Aggression:**
- Being direct without demonstrating care
- Publicly criticizing or humiliating
- Result: Fear-based culture; people hide problems instead of surfacing them

**Manipulative Insincerity:**
- Neither caring nor being direct
- Backstabbing, passive-aggression, political manipulation
- Result: Toxic culture; no trust; no development

### 2.3 Feedback Frequency Guidelines

| Feedback Type | Frequency | Medium | Documentation |
|--------------|-----------|--------|---------------|
| Reinforcing (praise) | Daily/weekly | In-person, Slack, public meeting | Optional (recognition system) |
| Constructive (minor) | As needed, within 48 hours | Private conversation | Manager notes (1:1 doc) |
| Constructive (significant) | Within 24 hours | Private 1:1 meeting | Documented in performance system |
| Formal performance feedback | Quarterly minimum | Scheduled meeting | Performance management platform |
| Developmental feedback (360) | Annually or semi-annually | Written report + debrief | 360 system |

## 3. Difficult Conversations

### 3.1 When Difficult Conversations Are Required

Managers must initiate difficult conversations when:
- Performance consistently falls below expectations despite informal feedback
- Behavior violates company values or policies
- Interpersonal conflict disrupts team effectiveness
- An employee's attitude or behavior is negatively affecting others
- Compensation or career expectations cannot be met
- Organizational changes affect the employee's role, reporting, or job security
- Termination is necessary

### 3.2 DEAR Framework for Difficult Conversations

**D - Describe** the specific situation or behavior (facts only):
- "In the last three sprints, you have missed your committed deliverables by an average of 30%."

**E - Express** the impact and why it matters:
- "This has caused downstream delays for the QA team and has twice required other engineers to pick up unfinished work, which impacts team trust and sprint predictability."

**A - Ask** for the employee's perspective:
- "Help me understand what's happening from your side. Are there obstacles I'm not seeing? Is there something about the work or environment that's affecting your output?"

**R - Resolve** with a specific forward plan:
- "Let's agree on a plan for the next sprint. I'd like to see you commit to a reduced scope that you can reliably deliver, and we'll check in mid-sprint to assess progress."

### 3.3 Conversation Preparation Checklist

Before any difficult conversation, the manager should:

- [ ] Clarify the specific issue (behavioral, factual, documented)
- [ ] Review relevant documentation (performance data, prior feedback, attendance records)
- [ ] Check for bias: Would I have the same conversation with any employee exhibiting this behavior?
- [ ] Consult with HRBP (for significant issues; mandatory for PIP, demotion, or termination conversations)
- [ ] Plan the conversation structure (open, share, listen, agree on next steps)
- [ ] Choose an appropriate private setting
- [ ] Prepare for emotional reactions (anger, crying, defensiveness, silence)
- [ ] Have a clear desired outcome in mind
- [ ] Plan follow-up documentation

### 3.4 Managing Emotional Reactions

| Reaction | Response Approach |
|----------|------------------|
| **Anger/defensiveness** | Acknowledge the emotion ("I can see this is frustrating"); pause; refocus on facts and behaviors, not character |
| **Crying** | Offer a moment to compose; express empathy without backtracking on the message; offer to continue later if needed |
| **Silence/withdrawal** | Ask open-ended questions; give time to process; schedule a follow-up if the person needs time |
| **Deflection/blame** | Redirect to the specific behavior discussed; "I understand there may be other factors, but right now I need to focus on [specific issue]" |
| **Denial** | Reference specific documentation; "Here is what I observed on [date]..." |
| **Threats (legal, resignation)** | Remain calm; do not match escalation; "I understand you're upset. Let's focus on finding a path forward." Document and consult HRBP |

## 4. Manager Coaching

### 4.1 Coaching vs. Managing vs. Mentoring

| Mode | Definition | When to Use | Approach |
|------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Managing** | Directing work, setting expectations, holding accountable | Clear performance standards; new employees; urgent situations | Tell and monitor |
| **Coaching** | Developing capability through questions, reflection, and guided practice | Employee has potential but needs skill development; growth opportunity | Ask and develop |
| **Mentoring** | Sharing experience, wisdom, and career guidance | Career development; organizational navigation; senior relationship | Share and advise |

### 4.2 The GROW Coaching Model (Whitmore, 1992)

The most widely used coaching framework:

| Step | Purpose | Sample Questions |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| **Goal** | Establish what the employee wants to achieve | "What would success look like?" "What do you want to accomplish?" |
| **Reality** | Explore the current situation | "Where are you now?" "What have you tried?" "What's working/not working?" |
| **Options** | Generate possible actions | "What could you do?" "What else?" "If there were no constraints, what would you try?" |
| **Will/Way Forward** | Commit to specific next steps | "What will you do?" "When will you do it?" "How will I know?" |

### 4.3 Manager Coaching Capability Development

**Training requirements for all people managers:**

| Topic | Content | Duration | Frequency |
|-------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Feedback fundamentals | SBI model, STAR, reinforcing vs. constructive | 4 hours | At promotion to management + annual refresh |
| Coaching skills | GROW model, active listening, powerful questions | 8 hours | Annual |
| Difficult conversations | DEAR model, emotional management, documentation | 4 hours | Annual |
| Inclusive leadership | Bias awareness, equitable feedback, psychological safety | 4 hours | Annual |
| Performance management | Goal setting, calibration, PIPs, development planning | 4 hours | Annual |
| Employment law basics | What managers can/cannot say; documentation requirements | 2 hours | Annual |

### 4.4 Manager Effectiveness Metrics

| Metric | Source | Target | Alert Threshold |
|--------|--------|--------|-----------------|
| Team engagement score | Annual/pulse surveys | Above org average | Below org average for 2+ cycles |
| Team turnover rate | HRIS | Below org average | >1.5x org average |
| 1:1 consistency | Calendar/platform data | Weekly/biweekly | >2 missed in a quarter |
| Feedback frequency | Performance platform | Monthly minimum | No feedback logged in 60+ days |
| Direct report development | IDP progress, promotion rate | Positive trend | No promotions in 2+ years (team >5) |
| Upward feedback score (360/survey) | Direct report feedback | >3.5/5.0 | <3.0/5.0 |

## 5. Recognition Systems

### 5.1 Recognition Research

Recognition is a powerful motivator when done correctly. Bersin (2012) found that organizations with strategic recognition programs have 31% lower voluntary turnover.

**Effective recognition characteristics (Brun & Dugas, 2008):**
- **Timely**: Close to the behavior being recognized (within days, not months)
- **Specific**: References the exact behavior and its impact (not generic "great job")
- **Sincere**: Authentic and genuine (scripted recognition feels transactional)
- **Aligned to values**: Connects the behavior to organizational values
- **Appropriate in type**: Matches the significance of the contribution (don't over-inflate or under-recognize)

### 5.2 Recognition Program Design

| Type | Description | Frequency | Cost |
|------|-----------|-----------|------|
| Verbal praise (manager) | 1:1 or team meeting acknowledgment | Daily/weekly | $0 |
| Peer recognition (platform) | Colleagues recognize each other publicly | Ongoing | $10-50/employee/month (platform + points) |
| Spot bonuses | Small cash/gift awards for exceptional contributions | Monthly | $50-500 per award |
| Team celebrations | Team event to mark milestone achievement | Quarterly | $50-100/person |
| Annual awards | Company-wide recognition for outstanding performance | Annual | $500-2,000 per award |
| Service milestones | Anniversary recognition at key tenure milestones | At milestones | $100-1,000 per milestone |

### 5.3 Recognition Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| Recognizing only results, not effort | Discourages risk-taking and learning from failure | Also recognize process, learning, and growth |
| Recognizing the same people repeatedly | Creates perception of favoritism; disengages others | Track recognition distribution; ensure breadth |
| Delayed recognition | Loses impact; employee forgets the context | Recognize within 48 hours of the behavior |
| Public recognition for private person | Can embarrass introverted employees | Ask preferences; offer private alternatives |
| Mandated recognition ("tell 3 people something nice today") | Feels forced and inauthentic | Make recognition easy and natural, not mandatory |
