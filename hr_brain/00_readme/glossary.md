# HR Brain — Glossary of Terms

## Purpose

This glossary defines key terms used throughout the HR Brain modules. All definitions align with SHRM standards, academic literature, and industry practice. Terms are grouped by domain and cross-referenced where relevant.

---

## Talent Acquisition

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Active Candidate** | A job seeker currently looking for employment and applying to open positions. Contrast with passive candidate. |
| **ATS (Applicant Tracking System)** | Software that manages the recruiting pipeline: job postings, applications, screening, scheduling, and offer management. Examples: Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby. |
| **Boolean Search** | A sourcing technique using AND, OR, NOT operators to find candidate profiles on LinkedIn, GitHub, or other platforms. |
| **Candidate Experience** | The holistic perception a candidate forms about an organization throughout the recruiting process, from job posting to offer/rejection. |
| **Competency Model** | A framework defining the knowledge, skills, abilities, and behaviors required for success in a role or level. |
| **Culture Add** | Evaluation of what unique perspectives and strengths a candidate brings, replacing the problematic "culture fit" concept which can perpetuate homogeneity. |
| **Employer Brand** | The organization's reputation and value proposition as perceived by current and potential employees. Distinct from consumer brand. |
| **EVP (Employee Value Proposition)** | The complete set of offerings an employer provides in exchange for employee contribution: compensation, benefits, culture, growth, purpose. |
| **Hiring Manager** | The individual who owns the open role and will manage the new hire. Responsible for final hiring decision in collaboration with HR. |
| **Job Architecture** | The systematic framework of job families, functions, levels, and titles that organizes all roles within an organization. |
| **Passive Candidate** | A professional not actively job searching but potentially open to the right opportunity. Typically higher quality but harder to engage. |
| **Requisition (Req)** | A formal, approved request to fill a position, including budget, level, title, and justification. |
| **Structured Interview** | An interview method where all candidates are asked the same predetermined questions, evaluated against consistent rubrics. Reduces bias and increases predictive validity (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998). |
| **Time to Fill** | The number of days from requisition opening to offer acceptance. Industry median: 36-44 days depending on role level. |
| **Time to Productive** | The duration from start date until a new hire reaches expected productivity. More meaningful than time to fill for measuring hiring effectiveness. |

## Compensation and Benefits

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Base Salary** | Fixed cash compensation paid on a regular schedule, excluding bonuses, equity, and benefits. |
| **Compa-Ratio** | An employee's salary divided by the midpoint of their pay band. A compa-ratio of 1.0 means the employee is at the midpoint. Below 0.8 or above 1.2 typically signals a pay equity issue. |
| **Compensation Philosophy** | An organization's stated approach to pay: lead market (pay above median), match market (pay at median), or lag market (pay below median, offset by other value). |
| **Equity Compensation** | Non-cash compensation granting ownership stake: stock options (ISOs/NSOs), RSUs (Restricted Stock Units), or ESOP shares. |
| **409A Valuation** | An independent appraisal of a private company's common stock fair market value, required by IRC Section 409A for setting stock option strike prices. |
| **FLSA (Fair Labor Standards Act)** | Federal law establishing minimum wage, overtime eligibility, recordkeeping, and child labor standards. Defines exempt vs. non-exempt classification. |
| **Job Leveling** | The process of assigning roles to levels within a career architecture based on scope, complexity, impact, and required expertise. |
| **Market Pricing** | The practice of comparing internal roles to external salary survey data to ensure competitive positioning. Sources: Radford, Mercer, Pave, Levels.fyi. |
| **Pay Band (Salary Range)** | The minimum, midpoint, and maximum salary for a given job level. Typical band width: 40-60% from minimum to maximum. |
| **Pay Equity** | The principle and practice of ensuring employees are compensated fairly regardless of gender, race, ethnicity, or other protected characteristics, when performing substantially similar work. |
| **RSU (Restricted Stock Unit)** | A promise to deliver shares of company stock upon vesting. No exercise price required. Taxed as ordinary income at vesting. |
| **Stock Option** | The right to purchase company shares at a fixed price (strike price) set at the grant date. ISOs (Incentive Stock Options) receive preferential tax treatment; NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options) do not. |
| **Total Rewards** | The complete value of employment: base salary + variable pay + equity + benefits + perks + development + culture + purpose. |
| **Vesting Schedule** | The timeline over which equity compensation becomes owned by the employee. Standard: 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff. |

## Performance Management

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Calibration** | A process where managers collectively review and adjust performance ratings to ensure consistency, reduce bias, and maintain fair distribution across teams. |
| **Career Ladder** | A defined progression path showing the competencies, responsibilities, and expectations at each level within a job family. |
| **Continuous Feedback** | An ongoing, real-time approach to performance communication replacing or supplementing annual reviews. Grounded in research showing recency bias undermines annual-only feedback (Murphy & Cleveland, 1995). |
| **IDP (Individual Development Plan)** | A structured plan co-created by employee and manager outlining development goals, skills to build, learning activities, and timelines. |
| **OKR (Objectives and Key Results)** | A goal-setting framework (Doerr, 2018) where qualitative Objectives define what to achieve, and measurable Key Results define how success is measured. Typically set quarterly. |
| **PIP (Performance Improvement Plan)** | A formal, documented plan outlining specific performance deficiencies, measurable improvement targets, support resources, and a timeline (typically 30-90 days). Both a support mechanism and a legal documentation tool. |
| **Promotion** | Advancement to a higher level within the career architecture, involving expanded scope, increased compensation, and new title. Distinguished from a title change without level increase. |
| **Skip-Level Meeting** | A meeting between an employee and their manager's manager, providing upward visibility and an additional feedback channel. |
| **SMART Goals** | Goals that are Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. A foundational goal-setting framework from Doran (1981). |
| **Succession Planning** | The systematic identification and development of internal talent to fill critical roles when they become vacant. Reduces key-person risk. |

## Culture and Engagement

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **eNPS (Employee Net Promoter Score)** | An engagement metric based on responses to "How likely are you to recommend this company as a place to work?" Score range: -100 to +100. Above +20 is generally considered good; above +50 is excellent. |
| **Employee Engagement** | The emotional commitment and discretionary effort an employee gives to their work and organization. Distinct from satisfaction (which lacks the behavioral component). Gallup defines it through 12 workplace elements (Q12). |
| **Gallup Q12** | Twelve validated survey questions that measure the core elements of employee engagement. Developed through meta-analysis of millions of employee responses. Predictive of productivity, retention, profitability, and safety outcomes. |
| **Organizational Culture** | The shared assumptions, values, and artifacts that shape behavior within an organization (Schein, 2010). Culture is descriptive (what people actually do), not aspirational (what leadership wishes they did). |
| **Psychological Safety** | The shared belief that a team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — asking questions, admitting mistakes, proposing ideas without fear of punishment (Edmondson, 1999). Google's Project Aristotle identified it as the #1 predictor of team effectiveness. |
| **Pulse Survey** | A brief, frequent survey (weekly/bi-weekly/monthly) measuring real-time employee sentiment on specific topics. Complements comprehensive annual surveys. |
| **Recognition Program** | A systematic approach to acknowledging employee contributions, aligned with organizational values. Research shows recognition is among the strongest engagement drivers (Gallup). |
| **Schein's Three Levels** | Edgar Schein's culture model: (1) Artifacts — visible structures and processes; (2) Espoused Values — strategies, goals, philosophies; (3) Basic Underlying Assumptions — unconscious, taken-for-granted beliefs. |
| **Values Operationalization** | The process of translating abstract values statements into observable, measurable behaviors that can be assessed in hiring, performance reviews, and promotion decisions. |

## Organizational Design

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **ADKAR Model** | A change management framework (Prosci): Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement. Focuses on individual-level adoption of change. |
| **Flat Organization** | A structure with few or no hierarchical levels between leadership and individual contributors. Enables speed but creates coordination challenges at scale. |
| **Galbraith Star Model** | An organizational design framework with five components: Strategy, Structure, Processes, Rewards, and People. All five must be aligned for effective organization. |
| **Kotter's 8-Step Model** | A change management framework: (1) Create urgency, (2) Build coalition, (3) Form vision, (4) Communicate vision, (5) Empower action, (6) Generate wins, (7) Consolidate gains, (8) Anchor in culture. |
| **Matrix Organization** | A structure where employees report to both a functional manager and a project/product manager. Enables cross-functional collaboration but creates dual-authority complexity. |
| **Mintzberg's Configurations** | Henry Mintzberg's five organizational forms: Simple Structure, Machine Bureaucracy, Professional Bureaucracy, Divisionalized Form, and Adhocracy. Each suited to different environments and strategies. |
| **Span of Control** | The number of direct reports a manager oversees. Optimal range: 5-9 for knowledge work (Graicunas, 1937; modern research suggests wider spans with strong systems). |
| **Team Topology** | A framework (Skelton & Pais, 2019) defining four team types: Stream-aligned, Enabling, Complicated Subsystem, and Platform. Optimizes team structure for fast flow. |
| **Two-Pizza Team** | Amazon's principle that teams should be small enough to feed with two pizzas (typically 6-8 people). Reduces coordination overhead and increases ownership. |

## DEIB (Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, Belonging)

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Belonging** | The feeling of being accepted, valued, and included as a full member of a group. The emotional outcome that diversity + inclusion aims to create. Distinct from mere inclusion (being present) or fitting in (changing oneself to conform). |
| **Bias Interruption** | Systematic methods to reduce the impact of unconscious bias on decisions: structured interviews, blind resume review, diverse panels, standardized rubrics. More effective than bias training alone (Dobbin & Kalev, 2016). |
| **DEIB** | Diversity (representation of different identities), Equity (fair treatment and access adjusted for systemic barriers), Inclusion (active welcoming and valuing of differences), Belonging (feeling of being an integral, valued member). |
| **ERG (Employee Resource Group)** | Voluntary, employee-led groups organized around shared identity or experience (e.g., Women in Tech, Black Professionals Network, LGBTQ+ Alliance). Provide community, mentorship, and organizational feedback. |
| **Inclusive Hiring** | Recruiting practices designed to attract diverse candidates and reduce bias in assessment: inclusive job descriptions, diverse sourcing, structured interviews, diverse panels, standardized evaluation criteria. |
| **Microaggression** | Brief, everyday exchanges that communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative messages to members of marginalized groups, often unintentionally (Sue, 2010). |
| **Psychological Safety** | See Culture and Engagement section. Critical enabler of inclusion — without psychological safety, diversity does not produce its benefits. |
| **Representation** | The demographic composition of an organization's workforce compared to relevant labor market benchmarks. Measured at hiring, promotion, and retention stages. |

## HR Operations and Compliance

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **At-Will Employment** | The legal doctrine (applicable in most US states) that either employer or employee can terminate the employment relationship at any time, for any lawful reason, with or without cause or notice. Subject to exceptions: implied contract, public policy, covenant of good faith. |
| **COBRA** | Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act. Requires employers with 20+ employees to offer continuation of health insurance coverage after qualifying events (termination, reduction in hours). |
| **Exempt vs. Non-Exempt** | FLSA classification. Exempt employees (typically salaried, meeting duties tests) are not entitled to overtime pay. Non-exempt employees must receive overtime (1.5x) for hours worked beyond 40 per week. Misclassification carries significant legal risk. |
| **FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act)** | Federal law requiring employers with 50+ employees to provide up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave for qualifying family and medical reasons. |
| **HRIS (Human Resource Information System)** | Core HR technology managing employee records, payroll, benefits administration, time tracking, and compliance. Examples: Workday, BambooHR, Rippling, Gusto. |
| **I-9 Verification** | Federal requirement to verify the identity and employment authorization of all employees hired in the United States. Must be completed within 3 business days of start date. |
| **Offboarding** | The structured process of managing an employee's departure: knowledge transfer, access revocation, equipment return, exit interview, benefits transition, final pay compliance. |
| **Progressive Discipline** | A graduated approach to managing performance or conduct issues: verbal warning, written warning, final written warning, termination. Creates documentation trail and demonstrates fairness. |
| **Title VII** | Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Prohibits employment discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, and national origin. Applies to employers with 15+ employees. Enforced by the EEOC. |
| **Workers' Compensation** | A state-mandated insurance program providing benefits to employees who suffer work-related injuries or illnesses. No-fault system: employees receive benefits regardless of who caused the injury. |

## People Analytics

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Attrition Rate** | The percentage of employees who leave the organization over a period. Voluntary attrition excludes terminations and layoffs. Regrettable attrition specifically measures the departure of high performers or critical talent. |
| **Cost Per Hire** | Total recruiting costs (internal + external) divided by total hires in a period. SHRM/ANSI standard: includes advertising, agency fees, recruiter salary, technology, travel, signing bonuses. |
| **Flight Risk** | Predictive assessment of an employee's likelihood of voluntary departure, typically based on tenure, compensation positioning, engagement scores, manager relationship, and career progression. |
| **ONA (Organizational Network Analysis)** | Analysis of communication and collaboration patterns to identify informal influencers, information bottlenecks, and isolated individuals. Uses email, Slack, calendar, and collaboration tool metadata. |
| **People Analytics** | The application of statistical analysis and data science to workforce data to inform people decisions. Distinct from HR reporting (descriptive metrics) by its emphasis on prediction and causal inference. |
| **Quality of Hire** | A composite metric assessing the value a new hire brings, typically measured through performance ratings, time to productivity, hiring manager satisfaction, and retention at 12-18 months. |
| **Regrettable Turnover** | Voluntary departures of employees the organization wanted to retain — typically high performers or holders of critical skills. The most meaningful turnover metric for strategic workforce planning. |
| **Span of Control Ratio** | Managers divided by total employees, or average number of direct reports per manager. Used to assess organizational flatness and managerial capacity. |
