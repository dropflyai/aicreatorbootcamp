# Compensation Strategy -- Total Rewards Philosophy and Design

## Purpose

This module defines the strategic framework for compensation design. Compensation is the single largest operating expense for most knowledge-work organizations (typically 60-80% of operating costs) and the most direct mechanism for attracting, retaining, and motivating talent. Compensation decisions must be grounded in market data, internal equity analysis, and the organization's strategic positioning. The HR Brain prohibits compensation decisions made without data.

---

## 1. Total Rewards Framework

### 1.1 Definition

Total rewards encompasses everything of value that the organization provides to employees in exchange for their contribution. WorldatWork defines five elements:

| Element | Components | Strategic Function |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Compensation** | Base salary, variable pay, equity | Attract, retain, motivate |
| **Benefits** | Health, retirement, insurance, PTO | Attract, retain, security |
| **Work-life effectiveness** | Flexibility, remote work, wellness, childcare | Retain, engage, well-being |
| **Recognition** | Formal and informal recognition, awards | Motivate, engage, reinforce values |
| **Development** | Training, education assistance, career pathing | Retain, grow, future capability |

### 1.2 Compensation Philosophy Design

Every organization must articulate a compensation philosophy that guides all pay decisions. Key dimensions:

**Market positioning:**
```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│  Lead (75th+):    Compete on cash. Premium employer.    │
│                   Higher fixed costs. Attracts broadly.  │
│                                                         │
│  Match (50th):    Market-competitive. Balanced approach. │
│                   Most common strategy.                  │
│                                                         │
│  Lag (25th):      Below market in cash. Must compensate  │
│                   with equity, mission, flexibility, or  │
│                   development. Startup model.            │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Pay mix (fixed vs. variable):**
```
Role type determines appropriate mix:

Individual contributor:  Base 80-90%  |  Variable 10-20%
Manager:                 Base 75-85%  |  Variable 15-25%
Director:                Base 70-80%  |  Variable 20-30%
VP:                      Base 60-70%  |  Variable 30-40%
C-suite:                 Base 40-60%  |  Variable 40-60%
Sales:                   Base 50-60%  |  Variable 40-50%
```

**Philosophy statement template:**
```
[Company] targets the [Nth percentile] of the [market definition] for total
compensation. We emphasize [fixed/variable/equity] compensation and differentiate
pay based on [performance/skills/market demand/role criticality]. We are committed
to pay equity and transparency, and we review our compensation structure [annually/
semi-annually] against market data.
```

## 2. Market-Based vs. Internal Equity

### 2.1 Market-Based Pay (External Equity)

Market-based compensation sets pay based on what the external labor market pays for comparable roles. This is the dominant approach in technology, finance, and knowledge work.

**Advantages:**
- Directly competitive for talent acquisition
- Grounded in real labor market data
- Easier to defend to candidates and employees
- Responsive to supply-demand dynamics

**Disadvantages:**
- Can create internal inequity (same-level employees paid very differently based on market timing)
- Market data has lag (surveys are 6-12 months old)
- Job matching is imprecise (titles and duties vary across organizations)
- Can disadvantage roles in historically undervalued fields (often correlated with gender)

### 2.2 Internal Equity

Internal equity ensures that employees within the organization are paid fairly relative to each other based on the value of their contributions, skills, and experience.

**Job evaluation methods for internal equity:**

| Method | Description | Complexity | Best For |
|--------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| **Ranking** | Rank all jobs from highest to lowest value | Low | Small organizations (<50 roles) |
| **Classification** | Assign jobs to pre-defined grade levels | Medium | Government, large bureaucracies |
| **Point-factor** | Score jobs on compensable factors (skill, effort, responsibility, conditions) | High | Large organizations, pay equity defense |
| **Market pricing** | Price each job to market data; derive internal structure from market | Medium-high | Knowledge work, tech, competitive markets |

### 2.3 Resolving Market vs. Internal Equity Tensions

When market rates for a specific role significantly exceed the internal equity structure, the HR Brain applies this decision framework:

```
Market premium >20% above internal peers at same level?
├── Is this a persistent market condition (not a bubble)?
│   ├── Yes → Create a market adjustment premium (documented, reviewed annually)
│   │         Flag for internal equity review; consider re-leveling
│   └── No → Use signing bonus to bridge gap (one-time, not structural)
├── Are internal peers likely to discover the disparity?
│   ├── Yes → Proactively address through compensation cycle or retention adjustments
│   └── No → Document rationale; monitor for equity claims
└── Does the disparity correlate with a protected characteristic?
    └── Yes → STOP. Conduct pay equity analysis before proceeding
```

## 3. Pay Bands and Salary Structures

### 3.1 Pay Band Design

Pay bands define the minimum, midpoint, and maximum compensation for each job level. Well-designed bands provide flexibility for individual positioning while maintaining organizational consistency.

**Band construction:**
```
Band Width = (Maximum - Minimum) / Minimum x 100

Midpoint = (Maximum + Minimum) / 2

Compa-ratio = Employee salary / Band midpoint x 100
  < 80%:  Significantly below range (investigate)
  80-90%: Below midpoint (growth opportunity)
  90-110%: At midpoint (appropriately positioned)
  110-120%: Above midpoint (high tenure or performance)
  > 120%: Above range (red-circled; freeze until range catches up)

Range penetration = (Salary - Minimum) / (Maximum - Minimum) x 100
  More granular than compa-ratio; shows position within band
```

### 3.2 Band Overlap and Progression

Adjacent bands should overlap by 20-30% to allow for development within level without requiring promotion:

```
Level 3:  ├────────[====MIDPOINT====]────────┤
Level 4:          ├────────[====MIDPOINT====]────────┤
Level 5:                  ├────────[====MIDPOINT====]────────┤

Midpoint differential (promotion increase): 10-15% between adjacent levels
Overlap: 20-30% between adjacent bands
```

### 3.3 Salary Structure Maintenance

- **Annual market update**: Adjust bands based on new survey data (target completion Q1)
- **Structure movement**: Increase all band minimums, midpoints, and maximums based on market movement (typically 3-5% annually)
- **Green-circled employees**: Below band minimum; bring to minimum immediately (within 90 days)
- **Red-circled employees**: Above band maximum; freeze base salary until band catches up (lump-sum bonus in lieu of raise if performance merits)
- **Compression monitoring**: Check that experienced employees are not paid less than new hires at the same level

## 4. Geographic Differentials

### 4.1 Geographic Pay Strategy

For distributed organizations, geographic differentials adjust compensation based on cost of labor (not cost of living) in the employee's location.

**Approaches:**

| Approach | Description | Pros | Cons |
|----------|-----------|------|------|
| **National rate** | Same pay regardless of location | Simple, transparent, equitable feel | Overpays low-cost areas, underpays high-cost areas |
| **Zone-based** | 3-5 geographic tiers with % adjustments | Balanced complexity and fairness | Zone boundaries are arbitrary; employees near borders feel unfair |
| **Metro-area pricing** | Pay based on specific metro area market data | Most accurate to local market | Complex to administer; creates many different rates |
| **Headquarters-based** | Pay based on HQ location with remote discount | Common in early remote transitions | Feels punitive to remote workers; talent attraction issues |

### 4.2 Zone-Based Model (Recommended)

```
Zone 1 (100%): San Francisco, New York, Seattle
Zone 2 (90%):  Los Angeles, Boston, Washington DC, Austin
Zone 3 (80%):  Denver, Chicago, Atlanta, Portland
Zone 4 (70%):  Smaller metros, suburban areas
Zone 5 (60%):  Rural areas, lowest cost-of-labor markets

Note: Zones based on cost of LABOR data (what employers pay for talent),
not cost of LIVING (what employees spend). Use Radford, Mercer, or
ERI geographic differentials data.
```

### 4.3 Relocation and Zone Changes

- Employees who relocate to a lower-cost zone: Adjust compensation at next review cycle (not immediately, to avoid punitive perception). Some organizations grandfather current rate.
- Employees who relocate to a higher-cost zone: Adjust within 30 days (retention risk if delayed).
- Policy must be clearly documented and communicated before implementation.

## 5. Variable Compensation

### 5.1 Annual Bonus Plans

**Design elements:**

| Element | Decision | Considerations |
|---------|----------|---------------|
| Target | % of base salary | By level; funded by company performance |
| Funding mechanism | Company performance threshold | Revenue, EBITDA, or OKR-based |
| Individual modifier | Performance rating multiplier | 0x to 2x based on individual performance |
| Payout timing | When paid | Annual (most common), semi-annual, quarterly |
| Discretionary vs. formulaic | How calculated | Formulaic preferred for transparency and legal clarity |

**Bonus calculation:**
```
Individual bonus = Base salary x Target % x Company modifier x Individual modifier

Example:
Base salary: $150,000
Target bonus: 15%
Company modifier: 1.1 (exceeded targets)
Individual modifier: 1.2 (exceeds expectations rating)

Bonus = $150,000 x 0.15 x 1.1 x 1.2 = $29,700
```

### 5.2 Sales Compensation

Sales compensation requires specialized design. Key principles from the Alexander Group and SHRM:

- **On-target earnings (OTE)**: Total expected compensation when hitting 100% of quota
- **Pay mix**: Typically 50/50 to 60/40 base/variable for quota-carrying roles
- **Quota setting**: Must be achievable by 60-70% of salespeople (if <50% hit quota, quotas are too aggressive)
- **Accelerators**: Above-quota earnings should accelerate (1.5-3x rate) to reward top performers
- **Decelerators**: Below-threshold earnings may decelerate or have a floor (prevents demoralization)
- **SPIFs**: Short-term incentive funds for specific behaviors (new product launch, end-of-quarter push)
- **Clawback provisions**: Commission recovery for churned customers (typically 6-12 month lookback)

### 5.3 Retention Bonuses

Used to retain critical talent during high-risk periods (M&A, leadership transitions, competitive market):

- Amount: Typically 15-50% of annual base salary
- Vesting: Paid at end of retention period (cliff) or in installments
- Clawback: Required if employee departs before retention period ends
- Documentation: Must clearly state conditions, payment schedule, and clawback terms
- Tax treatment: Ordinary income; withhold at supplemental rate

## 6. Annual Compensation Review Cycle

### 6.1 Timeline

```
Month 1 (October):  Market data refresh; band adjustment analysis
Month 2 (November): Budget allocation; merit increase guidelines approved
Month 3 (December): Manager merit increase recommendations submitted
Month 4 (January):  Calibration sessions; pay equity analysis
Month 5 (February): Final approvals; communication prep
Month 6 (March):    Effective date; employee communications

Note: Separate from performance review cycle (stagger by 1-2 months
to avoid conflating feedback with compensation conversations)
```

### 6.2 Merit Increase Guidelines

| Performance Rating | Typical Merit % | Range |
|-------------------|----------------|-------|
| Exceptional (top 5%) | 6-10% | Above maximum for strong retention case |
| Exceeds expectations (top 25%) | 4-6% | Above budget average |
| Meets expectations (middle 50%) | 2-4% | At or near budget average |
| Needs improvement (bottom 15%) | 0-2% | Below average; development-contingent |
| Unsatisfactory (bottom 5%) | 0% | No increase; on PIP or exiting |

**Budget management:**
- Total merit budget: Typically 3-5% of total payroll
- Differentiation ratio: Top performers should receive 2-3x the increase of average performers
- Compa-ratio targeting: Use merit increases to move employees toward appropriate position in range

### 6.3 Promotion Increases

Promotions warrant increases separate from annual merit:
- Typical promotion increase: 10-15% (to position appropriately in new band)
- Must bring employee to at least the minimum of the new band
- Target: 25th-50th percentile of new band for promotions
- Off-cycle promotions: Process independently from annual cycle but use same guidelines

## 7. Pay Transparency

### 7.1 Regulatory Landscape

Pay transparency laws are expanding rapidly across the United States:

| Jurisdiction | Requirement | Effective |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Colorado | Salary range in all postings | 2021 |
| New York City | Salary range in postings for NYC roles | 2022 |
| California | Salary range in postings; pay scale to current employees on request | 2023 |
| Washington State | Salary range in postings | 2023 |
| Additional states | Expanding; check current legislation | Ongoing |

### 7.2 Voluntary Transparency Strategy

Beyond legal requirements, proactive pay transparency builds trust and reduces perceived unfairness:

| Level | What is Shared | With Whom |
|-------|---------------|-----------|
| **Level 1** | Compensation philosophy and approach | All employees |
| **Level 2** | Pay band ranges for each level | All employees |
| **Level 3** | Individual position within range (compa-ratio) | Individual employee + manager |
| **Level 4** | Full compensation data for all employees | All employees (rare; Buffer model) |

**Recommended minimum**: Level 2 for all organizations. Level 3 for organizations with mature compensation infrastructure.

## 8. Compensation Communication

### 8.1 Manager Preparation

Before compensation conversations, equip managers with:
- Employee's current compensation and position within band
- Market data supporting the compensation decision
- Total compensation value (base + bonus + equity + benefits value)
- Talking points for explaining the decision
- Answers to anticipated questions
- Escalation path for unresolved concerns

### 8.2 Total Compensation Statement

Provide annually (at minimum) to every employee showing the full value of their compensation:

```
YOUR TOTAL COMPENSATION SUMMARY

Base Salary:                      $150,000
Annual Bonus (at target):          $22,500
Equity (annualized value):         $50,000
Health Insurance (employer share): $18,000
401(k) Match:                       $9,000
PTO Value (20 days):               $11,538
Other Benefits:                     $5,000
─────────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL COMPENSATION VALUE:         $266,038
```
