# Hiring Process -- Job Architecture to Onboarding

## Purpose

This module defines the end-to-end hiring process from role definition through the first 90 days of employment. Hiring is a chain of interdependent decisions where quality at each stage compounds. A poorly defined job description attracts wrong-fit candidates, leading to wasted interview time, poor hiring decisions, and early turnover. The HR Brain enforces rigor at every link in this chain.

---

## 1. Job Architecture

### 1.1 Definition and Purpose

Job architecture is the systematic framework that organizes all roles within the organization into a coherent structure. It provides the foundation for compensation, career pathing, workforce planning, and compliance.

**Components of job architecture:**

| Component | Definition | Example |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Job family** | Broad grouping of related roles | Engineering, Product, Marketing |
| **Job sub-family** | Specialized area within a family | Frontend Engineering, Backend Engineering, DevOps |
| **Job level** | Seniority tier within a sub-family | Junior (L1), Mid (L2), Senior (L3), Staff (L4), Principal (L5) |
| **Job title** | Specific designation | Senior Frontend Engineer |
| **Job code** | Unique identifier for the role | ENG-FE-L3 |
| **FLSA classification** | Exempt or non-exempt status | Exempt (salary basis + duties test) |
| **EEO category** | Federal reporting classification | Professionals (EEO-1 Category 2) |

### 1.2 Level Descriptors

Each level must have clear, differentiated descriptors across core dimensions:

```
LEVEL FRAMEWORK (example: Engineering)

L1 — Junior Engineer
  Scope: Individual tasks and small features
  Autonomy: Works with guidance; follows established patterns
  Impact: Team-level; executes defined work
  Technical: Developing fundamentals; learning codebase
  Communication: Communicates within team
  Experience: 0-2 years

L2 — Engineer
  Scope: Complete features and projects
  Autonomy: Works independently on well-defined problems
  Impact: Team-level; contributes to team goals
  Technical: Solid fundamentals; productive contributor
  Communication: Communicates across team boundaries
  Experience: 2-5 years

L3 — Senior Engineer
  Scope: Large features, cross-team projects
  Autonomy: Defines approach; handles ambiguity
  Impact: Multi-team; influences technical direction
  Technical: Deep expertise; designs systems
  Communication: Influences technical decisions
  Experience: 5-8 years

L4 — Staff Engineer
  Scope: Organization-wide technical strategy
  Autonomy: Identifies and drives strategic initiatives
  Impact: Organization-wide; sets technical direction
  Technical: Broad and deep; recognized expert
  Communication: Influences engineering culture
  Experience: 8-12 years

L5 — Principal Engineer
  Scope: Company-wide, industry-level contribution
  Autonomy: Self-directed; shapes company strategy
  Impact: Company-wide; industry influence
  Technical: Creates new approaches; advances the field
  Communication: External thought leadership
  Experience: 12+ years
```

### 1.3 Job Description Standards

Every job description must include:

1. **Job title**: Standard title from job architecture (avoid inflated or creative titles that obscure level)
2. **Department and reporting line**: Clear organizational placement
3. **Job summary**: 2-3 sentences describing the role's purpose and impact
4. **Essential functions**: 5-8 key responsibilities (these define ADA "essential functions")
5. **Required qualifications**: True minimum requirements only (skills, experience, credentials)
6. **Preferred qualifications**: Nice-to-have qualifications (clearly labeled as preferred)
7. **Compensation range**: Salary band minimum to maximum (required by law in many jurisdictions)
8. **Benefits summary**: Link to total rewards package
9. **EEO statement**: Standard language
10. **Accommodation statement**: ADA interactive process invitation

**Job description anti-patterns to avoid:**
- "Requirements" lists exceeding 15 items (discourages diverse applicants)
- Years of experience as a hard requirement (correlated with age discrimination claims)
- Degree requirements when skills-based alternatives exist
- Gendered language ("rockstar," "ninja," "aggressive")
- Jargon or company-specific terminology
- "Other duties as assigned" without defining essential functions

## 2. Compensation Benchmarking for Hiring

### 2.1 Market Pricing Methodology

Before opening any requisition, the role must have an approved compensation range based on market data.

**Data sources (prioritized by reliability):**
1. **Purchased survey data**: Radford/Aon, Mercer, Culpepper, Pave, Levels.fyi (tech)
2. **Compensation platforms**: Carta Total Comp, Option Impact, Salary.com
3. **Job posting data**: Aggregated from mandated salary disclosures
4. **SHRM compensation surveys**: Industry and geography-specific

**Market pricing process:**
```
1. Match role to survey benchmark (match on duties and level, not title)
2. Pull market data (25th, 50th, 75th percentile for base, bonus, equity)
3. Apply geographic differential if applicable
4. Determine target percentile based on compensation philosophy
5. Set range: Minimum (80% of midpoint) - Midpoint - Maximum (120% of midpoint)
6. Validate against internal equity (compare to similarly-leveled employees)
7. Obtain compensation approval before posting requisition
```

### 2.2 Compensation Range Width

| Level | Typical Range Width | Rationale |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|
| Entry-level | 30-35% | Narrow range; limited performance differentiation |
| Individual contributor | 40-50% | Moderate range; room for growth within level |
| Senior IC / Manager | 50-60% | Broader range; significant skill differentiation |
| Director+ | 60-70% | Wide range; substantial variable compensation |
| Executive | 80-100%+ | Very wide; significant equity and variable comp |

### 2.3 Offer Positioning Within Range

| Candidate Profile | Target Position | Justification |
|------------------|----------------|---------------|
| Meets minimum qualifications | 0-25th percentile of range | Room for growth; lower risk if underperforms |
| Meets all requirements, some preferred | 25th-50th percentile | Standard offer positioning |
| Exceeds requirements, strong interview | 50th-75th percentile | Premium for proven capability |
| Exceptional candidate, competitive market | 75th-100th percentile | Requires VP approval; must justify premium |
| Above range maximum | Escalate: re-level the role or decline | Never offer above range without re-leveling |

## 3. Offer Design

### 3.1 Total Compensation Package

An offer package includes all compensation components. Present as total compensation, not just base salary:

| Component | Typical Structure | Negotiability |
|-----------|------------------|---------------|
| **Base salary** | Annual fixed amount | Moderate (within approved range) |
| **Signing bonus** | One-time payment (often with clawback) | High (used to bridge gap or offset forfeitures) |
| **Annual bonus** | Target % of base, performance-dependent | Low (tied to level/role structure) |
| **Equity (RSU/Options)** | 4-year vesting, annual refresh | Moderate (within band for level) |
| **Relocation** | Lump sum or managed relocation | Moderate (policy-dependent) |
| **Benefits** | Health, retirement, PTO, perks | Low (standard package; rare to customize) |

### 3.2 Offer Approval Workflow

```
Recruiter prepares offer → Compensation team validates range/equity
→ Hiring manager approves → Department head approves (if above midpoint)
→ VP approves (if above 75th percentile or exception) → Offer extended

Timeline: 1-2 business days from debrief decision to verbal offer
```

### 3.3 Offer Negotiation Framework

**Principles:**
- Negotiate in good faith; the goal is a mutually satisfactory outcome
- Never penalize candidates for negotiating (especially important for DEIB -- research shows women are penalized for negotiating while men are rewarded; Bowles, Babcock, & Lai, 2007)
- Be transparent about constraints ("The range for this level is X to Y; here's where your offer falls and why")
- Use non-cash elements to bridge gaps when base salary is at ceiling (signing bonus, equity, start date flexibility, remote work, professional development budget)

**Negotiation response decision tree:**
```
Request within range?
├── Yes → Approve (document rationale)
├── Close to range ceiling → Counter with mix of cash and non-cash
├── Above range → Explain range constraints, offer alternatives
└── Significantly above range → Re-evaluate leveling; if correct level, decline gracefully
```

### 3.4 Offer Letter Requirements

Every offer letter must include:
- Position title and reporting relationship
- Start date and work location (or remote designation)
- Compensation: base salary, bonus target, equity grant
- Benefits eligibility date
- At-will employment statement (where applicable)
- Contingencies: background check, drug screen (if applicable), I-9 verification
- Expiration date for the offer (typically 5-7 business days)
- Signature lines for both parties

## 4. Pre-Employment Requirements

### 4.1 Background Check

Background checks must be conducted consistently for all candidates at the same level/role type to avoid discrimination claims.

**Scope by level:**

| Level | Criminal | Employment Verification | Education Verification | Credit | Professional License |
|-------|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Individual contributor | X | X | X | | If applicable |
| Manager | X | X | X | | If applicable |
| Director+ | X | X | X | X (if financial role) | If applicable |
| Executive / Finance | X | X | X | X | X |

**Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) compliance:**
1. Written disclosure and authorization (separate from application)
2. Pre-adverse action notice with copy of report and Summary of Rights
3. Reasonable waiting period (5 business days minimum)
4. Adverse action notice if offer is withdrawn

**Ban-the-box compliance:** In jurisdictions with fair chance laws, criminal history inquiry is deferred until after conditional offer.

### 4.2 I-9 Verification

- Form I-9 must be completed by end of third business day after start date
- Section 1 completed by employee on or before first day of work
- Section 2 completed by employer within 3 business days
- Document verification must be done in person (or via authorized alternative procedure)
- Retention: 3 years from hire date OR 1 year after termination, whichever is later

## 5. Onboarding -- The First 90 Days

### 5.1 Research Foundation

Effective onboarding reduces first-year turnover by 50% and accelerates time to productivity by 34% (Brandon Hall Group, 2015). Yet most organizations underinvest in onboarding, treating it as an administrative checkbox rather than a strategic integration process.

Bauer's (2010) "Four C's" onboarding framework:

| Level | Component | Description | Activities |
|-------|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | **Compliance** | Legal, regulatory, and policy requirements | I-9, tax forms, handbook acknowledgment, system access, security training |
| 2 | **Clarification** | Role expectations and performance criteria | Role overview, 30-60-90 plan, goal-setting, manager 1:1 |
| 3 | **Culture** | Organizational norms, values, and unwritten rules | Welcome events, team introductions, culture workshops, shadow sessions |
| 4 | **Connection** | Relationship building and social integration | Buddy assignment, skip-level meeting, cross-functional introductions, ERG invitations |

Most organizations only execute Level 1 (Compliance). The HR Brain requires all four levels.

### 5.2 Pre-Boarding (Offer Acceptance to Day 1)

The period between offer acceptance and start date is high-risk for reneging (especially in competitive markets). Pre-boarding maintains engagement:

**Week of acceptance:**
- Welcome email from hiring manager (personal, not template)
- IT equipment order initiated
- System access provisioned
- Buddy assigned and introduced via email

**2 weeks before start:**
- Pre-boarding packet: Company overview, team org chart, first-week schedule
- Benefits enrollment materials (allow time to review before Day 1)
- Any required reading or preparation materials
- Logistics confirmation: Office location/parking, remote setup instructions

**1 week before start:**
- Hiring manager confirms first-day agenda
- Team sends welcome message (Slack, email, video)
- All accounts and equipment confirmed ready

### 5.3 The 30-60-90 Day Plan

The 30-60-90 plan is the primary tool for structuring the new hire's first quarter. It must be jointly developed by the hiring manager and new employee during the first week.

```
DAYS 1-30: LEARN
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Objective: Absorb context, build relationships, understand the landscape

Key activities:
- Complete all compliance and administrative onboarding
- Meet all direct team members, key stakeholders, and cross-functional partners
- Attend team meetings and observe workflows
- Review documentation: strategy docs, product roadmaps, team OKRs
- Shadow experienced team members
- Complete required training modules
- Weekly 1:1 with manager (30 min minimum)

Success criteria:
- Can articulate team's mission, current priorities, and key metrics
- Has met all critical stakeholders
- Has identified 3-5 questions or observations about the work

DAYS 31-60: CONTRIBUTE
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Objective: Begin adding value through defined deliverables

Key activities:
- Own 1-2 small projects or workstreams independently
- Participate actively in team planning and retrospectives
- Begin developing domain-specific expertise
- Provide fresh-eyes observations and suggestions
- Buddy check-in (formal feedback exchange)
- 30-day onboarding survey completed

Success criteria:
- Has delivered at least one meaningful work product
- Is contributing independently to team discussions
- Has received feedback and incorporated it
- Manager confirms on-track trajectory

DAYS 61-90: DRIVE
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Objective: Operate at expected performance level for the role

Key activities:
- Own significant workstreams or projects
- Proactively identify improvement opportunities
- Begin mentoring newer team members (if senior role)
- Develop goals for next quarter (aligned to team OKRs)
- 90-day review with manager (formal check-in)

Success criteria:
- Performing at expected level for the role (per job level descriptors)
- Has built effective working relationships across the organization
- Can articulate personal goals and development areas
- Manager confirms successful completion of onboarding
```

### 5.4 Onboarding Metrics

| Metric | Measurement Method | Target | Frequency |
|--------|-------------------|--------|-----------|
| New hire satisfaction | Onboarding survey (30/60/90 days) | >4.0/5.0 | Per cohort |
| Time to productivity | Manager assessment at 30/60/90 days | <90 days for most roles | Per hire |
| First-year retention | % of cohort still employed at 12 months | >85% | Monthly cohort tracking |
| Manager satisfaction | Hiring manager survey at 90 days | >4.0/5.0 | Per hire |
| Compliance completion | % of required items completed on time | 100% | Weekly during first 30 days |
| Buddy program effectiveness | New hire + buddy satisfaction survey | >4.0/5.0 | At 30 and 90 days |

## 6. Hiring Decision Documentation

### 6.1 Requirements

Every hiring decision must be documented for legal compliance and organizational learning. Minimum documentation:

**For selected candidate:**
- Interview scorecards from all interviewers
- Aggregate scores and ranking
- Debrief notes with decision rationale
- Offer details and approval chain
- Background check results
- Any negotiation correspondence

**For non-selected candidates:**
- Disposition reason code at each funnel stage
- Interview scorecards (if interviewed)
- Non-discriminatory reason for non-selection
- Retained per EEOC record retention requirements (1 year minimum; 2 years for federal contractors)

### 6.2 Legal Defensibility Checklist

Before extending any offer, verify:

- [ ] Job description accurately reflects essential functions
- [ ] Evaluation criteria were established before interviews began
- [ ] All candidates for this role were asked the same core questions
- [ ] Scorecards completed independently by each interviewer
- [ ] Selection based on documented, job-related criteria
- [ ] No evidence of discriminatory considerations in any notes or communications
- [ ] Compensation within approved range
- [ ] Background check process complies with FCRA and applicable state law
- [ ] Offer letter includes all required elements

## 7. Hiring Process Continuous Improvement

### 7.1 Post-Hire Analysis (Quarterly)

- Correlate interview scores with 6-month and 12-month performance ratings
- Identify interviewers with highest and lowest predictive validity
- Analyze time-in-stage data to identify bottlenecks
- Review candidate experience survey results for process improvements
- Audit diversity metrics at each funnel stage

### 7.2 Hiring Retrospective (After Every Critical Hire)

For director-level and above, or any role that took >60 days to fill:

1. What worked well in this search?
2. What caused delays?
3. Was the job description accurate to what we needed?
4. Did we source from the right channels?
5. Were our interview questions effective?
6. What would we do differently?

Document findings and update process accordingly.
