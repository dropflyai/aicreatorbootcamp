# HR Benchmark Tests — Scenario-Based Evaluation (Authoritative)

This document contains benchmark scenarios for evaluating HR Brain output quality.
Each scenario must be evaluated against the HRScore.md dimensions.

These are not hypotheticals. These are realistic, high-stakes people situations.
The brain must produce actionable, expert-level responses to each scenario.

---

## HOW TO USE BENCHMARK TESTS

1. Present each scenario to the HR Brain
2. Evaluate the response against the scoring criteria provided
3. Score from 1-5 using the rubric for each scenario
4. A passing grade requires an average >= 4.0 across all scenarios
5. Any scenario scored < 3 requires the brain to be retrained on that domain

### Scoring Rubric (Applied to Each Scenario)
- **5** — Expert-level response. Actionable, legally sound, empathetic, considers
        second-order effects. Could be presented to a CHRO at a growth-stage company.
- **4** — Strong response. Mostly complete with minor gaps. Actionable and fair.
- **3** — Adequate response. Covers basics but misses nuance, legal risk, or human impact.
- **2** — Weak response. Generic advice that lacks specificity or legal awareness.
- **1** — Poor response. Incorrect, legally risky, or would harm employees/company.

## SCENARIO 1: Engineering Retention Crisis

**Context:**
Engineering team voluntary turnover jumped from 10% to 25% in one quarter.
Exit interviews reveal three themes:
- "Compensation is 20-30% below market"
- "No career growth or promotion path"
- "My manager is terrible but nothing changes"

The company is a 150-person startup in a competitive talent market. Budget is
constrained (Series B, 18 months of runway). Three senior engineers gave notice
this week, and the CTO is panicking.

**Task:**
Diagnose the root cause and create a 90-day retention plan. Deliver:
1. Root cause analysis (distinguish symptoms from causes)
2. Immediate actions (week 1-2) to stop the bleeding
3. Short-term plan (30-60 days) to address systemic issues
4. Medium-term plan (60-90 days) to rebuild trust and engagement
5. Budget-conscious recommendations (what has the highest ROI)
6. Metrics to track recovery

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does the diagnosis go beyond the exit interview themes to structural causes?
- Are immediate actions practical and executable this week?
- Does the plan address all three themes with specific interventions?
- Are budget constraints respected while still being meaningful?
- Is manager quality addressed directly (not avoided)?
- Are metrics leading indicators, not just lagging?

## SCENARIO 2: Compensation Philosophy Design

**Context:**
A 50-person startup has no formal compensation philosophy. It competes with
FAANG companies for senior engineers. Current state:
- Pay decisions are made by individual hiring managers
- No pay bands exist
- Wide variation in comp for similar roles (some 40% gaps)
- Engineers are discovering pay discrepancies through salary sharing
- Two female engineers identified a gender pay gap and escalated to the CEO
- The company cannot match FAANG total comp

**Task:**
Design a compensation philosophy and structure. Deliver:
1. Compensation philosophy statement (positioning, principles, transparency level)
2. Pay band structure with implementation approach
3. Pay equity remediation plan for identified gaps
4. Communication strategy for rolling out the new approach
5. How to compete with FAANG without matching their total comp
6. Manager training on compensation conversations

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the philosophy clear, honest, and defensible?
- Are pay bands structured correctly (range spread, midpoint progression)?
- Does the equity remediation plan address both the legal risk and the trust gap?
- Is the communication strategy transparent without creating panic?
- Are non-compensation levers identified for competing with FAANG?
- Does the manager training cover the most common difficult conversations?

## SCENARIO 3: Underperformance Intervention Design

**Context:**
A manager has 3 underperforming direct reports on a team of 8. The manager has:
- Given verbal feedback but has no written documentation
- Avoided difficult conversations for 6+ months
- Given all three "meets expectations" in the last review cycle
- One underperformer is on an H-1B visa
- One underperformer just returned from parental leave 3 weeks ago
- One underperformer has been with the company for 5 years and was previously high-performing

**Task:**
Design an intervention that is fair, documented, and legally defensible. Deliver:
1. Assessment of the manager's performance (the manager is part of the problem)
2. Legal risk analysis for each underperformer given their specific circumstances
3. Documentation remediation plan (building the written record)
4. Tailored intervention approach for each of the 3 employees
5. Manager coaching plan
6. Timeline with checkpoints
7. What NOT to do (common mistakes to avoid)

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the manager held accountable as part of the problem (not just the employees)?
- Are the H-1B visa, parental leave, and tenure implications correctly analyzed?
- Is the documentation plan specific (what to document, how, starting when)?
- Are the three interventions differentiated based on circumstances?
- Is the timeline realistic (not rushed, but not indefinite)?
- Are the "what NOT to do" items based on real legal risks?

## SCENARIO 4: Reduction in Force (RIF) Planning

**Context:**
The company needs to reduce headcount by 15% (30 of 200 employees) due to
a market downturn and missed revenue targets. The CEO wants it done in 2 weeks.
Considerations:
- Company operates in CA, NY, TX, and CO
- WARN Act may apply depending on how layoffs are structured
- The engineering team is 80% male; proposed cuts are 60% from non-engineering
- Several employees on approved FMLA leave
- 3 employees recently filed internal complaints about their managers
- The company has no severance policy

**Task:**
Design a legally compliant, compassionate RIF process. Deliver:
1. WARN Act analysis and compliance strategy
2. Selection criteria design (objective, defensible, non-discriminatory)
3. Adverse impact analysis methodology
4. Severance package recommendation with release structure
5. Communication plan (manager scripts, all-hands messaging, individual notifications)
6. Support for remaining employees (survivor guilt, workload, morale)
7. Timeline (is 2 weeks realistic? If not, what is?)

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the WARN Act analysis correct for the company size and structure?
- Are selection criteria objective and documented?
- Is adverse impact analysis properly structured (4/5ths rule)?
- Does the severance recommendation account for OWBPA requirements (if applicable)?
- Are the employees on FMLA and those who filed complaints specifically protected?
- Is the communication plan empathetic without being dishonest?
- Is the CEO's 2-week timeline challenged if unrealistic?

## SCENARIO 5: DEI Program Design

**Context:**
A 300-person tech company's workforce is:
- Engineering: 85% male, 5% Black, 3% Hispanic
- Leadership (VP+): 90% male, 95% white
- Overall voluntary turnover for women is 2x that of men
- Engagement survey: women and BIPOC employees report lower belonging scores
- Previous DEI training was a one-time unconscious bias workshop 2 years ago
- No ERGs, no DEI lead, no formal program

**Task:**
Design a comprehensive DEI strategy. Deliver:
1. Data analysis and root cause assessment
2. Systemic interventions (not just training and awareness)
3. Hiring pipeline diversification strategy
4. Inclusive leadership development program
5. Retention-focused initiatives for underrepresented groups
6. Accountability framework (who owns what, what gets measured)
7. Year 1 goals that are ambitious but achievable

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does the root cause analysis go beyond "pipeline problem"?
- Are interventions systemic (processes, policies, structures) not just programmatic?
- Is the hiring strategy specific (sourcing changes, process changes, assessment changes)?
- Does the leadership program address specific gaps (not generic awareness)?
- Are retention initiatives based on the actual reasons women and BIPOC employees leave?
- Is the accountability framework specific with named owners?
- Are Year 1 goals measurable and realistic?

## SCENARIO 6: Remote Work Policy Design

**Context:**
A company of 500 employees went fully remote during COVID. Now:
- CEO wants 3 days/week in-office for "collaboration and culture"
- 40% of employees were hired fully remote and live far from offices
- Engineering says they are more productive remote
- Sales leadership wants their team in-office
- Several employees have disabilities that make commuting difficult
- 15% of employees are caregivers who rely on remote flexibility
- Competitors are offering full remote; this is a retention risk

**Task:**
Design a flexible work policy. Deliver:
1. Policy framework that balances business needs and employee needs
2. Legal analysis (ADA accommodations, state law considerations, contractual obligations)
3. Approach for remote-hired employees (can you change their terms?)
4. Manager guidelines for hybrid team management
5. Cultural and collaboration solutions that do not require physical presence
6. Communication strategy for rollout
7. Exception request process

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does the framework avoid a one-size-fits-all approach?
- Is the legal analysis thorough (ADA, contractual terms, state laws)?
- Are remote-hired employees treated fairly (contract modification analysis)?
- Are manager guidelines practical and specific?
- Are collaboration solutions genuine (not just "pizza Fridays")?
- Does the communication strategy acknowledge the tension honestly?

## SCENARIO 7: Harassment Investigation

**Context:**
An employee reports that their VP-level manager has made repeated sexually
suggestive comments in 1-on-1 meetings over 3 months. The employee has:
- One text message exchange that is suggestive but ambiguous
- Verbal allegations with no witnesses
- Told one colleague informally (colleague can corroborate the disclosure)
- Not documented the incidents in writing until now

The VP is considered critical to the business (top revenue driver). The CEO
asks you to "handle this quietly."

**Task:**
Design the investigation process. Deliver:
1. Immediate protective measures for the complainant
2. Investigation plan (scope, investigator selection, timeline)
3. Interview plan (who, in what order, what questions)
4. Evidence collection strategy
5. Analysis framework (how to weigh he-said/she-said)
6. Response to CEO's "handle this quietly" request
7. Outcome options and proportionate responses
8. Anti-retaliation protections

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Are protective measures immediate and meaningful (not just "we'll look into it")?
- Is the investigation plan independent and thorough (not pre-judged)?
- Is the interview order correct (complainant first, witnesses, respondent last)?
- Does the evidence strategy address the lack of direct documentation?
- Is the response to the CEO's request firm and legally correct?
- Are outcome options proportionate (not automatically dismissal or automatically nothing)?
- Are anti-retaliation protections specific and monitored?

## SCENARIO 8: Onboarding Redesign

**Context:**
New hire 90-day retention is 78% (target: 95%). Exit survey data from early
departures shows:
- "I had no idea what I was supposed to do for the first month"
- "My manager was too busy to onboard me"
- "The tools and access took 2 weeks to set up"
- "I felt isolated as a remote employee"
- "The culture was nothing like what was described in interviews"

The company has 200 employees, 50% remote, growing 5+ new hires per month.

**Task:**
Redesign the onboarding program. Deliver:
1. Onboarding framework (pre-boarding, week 1, 30/60/90 day milestones)
2. Role clarity deliverables (what "success" looks like for each milestone)
3. Manager accountability in onboarding (checklist, training, consequences)
4. Remote-specific onboarding enhancements
5. Culture integration approach (authentic, not performative)
6. IT/access provisioning process fix
7. Success metrics and feedback loops

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does the framework start before day 1 (pre-boarding)?
- Are milestones specific and role-appropriate (not generic)?
- Is the manager held accountable with specific obligations?
- Are remote enhancements genuine (not just Zoom version of in-person)?
- Does the culture integration address the expectation gap from interviews?
- Is the IT fix a systemic solution (not just "be faster")?

## SCENARIO 9: Manager Effectiveness Program

**Context:**
Employee engagement survey reveals:
- "My manager does not give me regular feedback" — 55% agree
- "My manager does not support my career development" — 48% agree
- "I would not recommend my manager to others" — 35% agree

The company has 40 people managers. 25% have never received management training.
Skip-level interviews confirm the survey data. Three managers have been
flagged in multiple surveys.

**Task:**
Design a manager effectiveness program. Deliver:
1. Manager capability assessment framework
2. Foundational training curriculum (for all managers)
3. Intervention plan for the 3 flagged managers
4. Ongoing development program (not a one-time training)
5. Manager effectiveness metrics and accountability
6. Peer learning and manager community design
7. How to handle managers who do not improve

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the assessment framework comprehensive (not just one dimension)?
- Is the training curriculum practical (not theoretical)?
- Are the 3 flagged manager interventions specific and escalatory?
- Is the ongoing program sustainable (not just launch and forget)?
- Are metrics leading indicators of effectiveness (not just lagging)?
- Is there a clear consequence for managers who persistently underperform?

## SCENARIO 10: Employee Relations Crisis Management

**Context:**
An anonymous Glassdoor review goes viral on social media, alleging:
- Toxic work environment in the sales department
- Discrimination against older employees
- Retaliation against employees who raised concerns
- Pay inequity between men and women

The review has 500+ social media shares. Two reporters have reached out.
Internal Slack channels are buzzing. The CEO wants a response within 24 hours.

**Task:**
Design the crisis response. Deliver:
1. Immediate internal communication (within 4 hours)
2. Investigation plan for each allegation
3. External communication strategy (social media, press)
4. Employee support measures
5. Long-term remediation plan (even before investigation concludes)
6. Board communication (if applicable)
7. Legal risk assessment

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the internal communication honest without admitting liability?
- Is the investigation plan structured to address each specific allegation?
- Is the external communication measured (not defensive, not over-promising)?
- Are employee support measures meaningful?
- Does the long-term plan address systemic issues regardless of investigation outcome?
- Is the legal risk assessment practical?

## SCENARIO 11: Succession Planning

**Context:**
A 400-person company has:
- CEO is the founder, age 62, no stated retirement plans
- CTO has been hinting about starting their own company
- VP Sales has a non-compete that expires in 6 months (from prior employer)
- No identified successors for any C-suite role
- 5 other "single point of failure" roles with no backup
- Zero formal succession planning process

**Task:**
Design a succession planning program. Deliver:
1. Critical role identification methodology
2. Successor assessment framework (ready now, ready in 1 year, develop)
3. Development plans for high-potential successor candidates
4. Emergency succession plan (what if someone leaves tomorrow)
5. Board engagement on CEO succession
6. Knowledge transfer process for "single point of failure" roles
7. How to discuss succession without creating anxiety or entitlement

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the critical role methodology objective (not just seniority)?
- Is the assessment framework multi-dimensional (not just performance)?
- Are development plans specific to the gaps for each potential successor?
- Is the emergency plan actionable immediately?
- Is the board engagement approach appropriate for the CEO topic?
- Is the communication strategy sensitive to the politics involved?

## SCENARIO 12: International Expansion HR Setup

**Context:**
A US-based company is hiring its first 10 employees in the UK and Germany.
Currently:
- No entity in either country
- No knowledge of local employment law
- Planning to use EOR (Employer of Record) as interim solution
- Need to offer competitive local compensation
- Remote-first approach
- Need to maintain company culture across geographies

**Task:**
Design the international HR setup. Deliver:
1. EOR vs. entity analysis with decision framework
2. Country-specific employment law requirements (UK and Germany)
3. Compensation benchmarking approach for local markets
4. Benefits design that meets local requirements and expectations
5. Cultural integration strategy
6. Compliance monitoring framework
7. Timeline and budget estimate

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the EOR vs. entity analysis balanced with clear decision criteria?
- Are key employment law differences identified (at-will vs. notice periods, works councils, etc.)?
- Is the compensation approach market-competitive for both countries?
- Do benefits meet statutory requirements (pension, health, holiday)?
- Is the cultural integration strategy genuine?
- Is the compliance framework sustainable?

## SCENARIO 13: Employee Mental Health Program

Post-pandemic, the company is seeing:
- Burnout scores at all-time highs (self-reported)
- Short-term disability claims up 40% (mental health-related)
- Managers reporting that employees are disengaged

- Two employees have disclosed suicidal ideation to their managers

**Task:**
Design a comprehensive mental health support program. Deliver:
1. Assessment of current state and root causes
2. Manager training on mental health conversations and crisis response
3. Enhanced EAP and mental health benefits strategy
4. Organizational-level interventions (workload, boundaries, flexibility)
5. Crisis response protocol for acute situations
6. Stigma reduction strategy
7. Metrics to track program effectiveness

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does the assessment look at organizational causes (not just individual)?
- Is the manager training specific (not generic "be supportive")?
- Does the benefits strategy go beyond the existing underutilized EAP?
- Are organizational interventions concrete (workload caps, meeting-free times, etc.)?
- Is the crisis protocol clear and legally compliant?
- Is the stigma reduction strategy multi-faceted?

## SCENARIO 14: Performance-Based Compensation Restructure

**Context:**
The company wants to shift from tenure-based to performance-based compensation.
Current state: annual 3% "cost of living" increase for everyone. No performance
differentiation. Top performers are leaving because there is no upside. Low
performers have no incentive to improve. Budget for merit increases: 4% of
payroll.

**Task:**
Design the transition to performance-based compensation. Deliver:
1. Merit matrix design (linking performance rating to increase percentage)
2. Communication plan for a sensitive cultural change
3. Manager enablement (how to have the "you got 2% and your peer got 6%" conversation)
4. Handling the first cycle (when some get less than they expected)
5. Equity and fairness guardrails
6. Phase-in strategy (cold turkey vs. gradual)
7. Success metrics

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the merit matrix mathematically sound with the 4% budget constraint?
- Does the communication plan acknowledge this is a significant cultural shift?
- Are manager talking points specific for different scenarios?
- Are the first-cycle risks identified and mitigated?
- Do fairness guardrails prevent bias from distorting outcomes?
- Is the phase-in strategy appropriate for the organization's maturity?

## SCENARIO 15: Workplace Investigation Best Practices

**Context:**
The company receives three complaints in one month:
1. A team lead is accused of bullying by multiple team members
2. An expense report shows potential fraud by a sales executive
3. An employee claims they were denied a promotion due to pregnancy

Each requires investigation with different approaches, expertise, and urgency.

**Task:**
Design the investigation approach for all three complaints. Deliver:
1. Intake and initial assessment framework
2. Investigation plan for each complaint (scope, timeline, investigator)
3. Confidentiality and communication protocols
4. Evidence collection strategy per complaint type
5. Decision-making framework for each type of complaint
6. Documentation standards
7. Outcome and remediation options per complaint

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the intake framework consistent but adaptable to complaint type?
- Are investigation plans appropriately scoped (not over-investigating or under-investigating)?
- Is confidentiality balanced with the need for a thorough investigation?
- Are evidence strategies appropriate for each type (behavioral patterns vs. financial records vs. timeline/decisions)?
- Is the pregnancy discrimination complaint handled with particular care?
- Are outcome options proportionate and legally defensible?

## SCENARIO 16: Culture Integration Post-Merger

**Context:**
Two companies are merging (200 + 150 employees). Cultural assessment reveals:
- Company A: formal, process-driven, hierarchical, strong benefits, below-market pay
- Company B: informal, fast-moving, flat structure, above-market pay, minimal benefits
- Both companies have strong employee loyalty to their respective cultures
- Employees from both sides are anxious about job security
- Duplicate roles exist in HR, finance, and engineering
- Leadership team will be a mix from both companies

**Task:**
Design the culture integration strategy. Deliver:
1. Cultural assessment and gap analysis
2. "Best of both" integration framework (what to keep, change, or create new)
3. Compensation and benefits harmonization plan
4. Communication strategy for both workforces
5. Role clarity and reduction-in-force approach (if needed)
6. Integration timeline with milestones
7. Culture health monitoring during transition

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Is the cultural assessment objective (not favoring either company)?
- Does "best of both" actually integrate (not just pick one culture)?
- Is compensation harmonization fair and budgeted?
- Does communication address anxiety directly?
- Is the RIF approach (if needed) legally compliant and compassionate?
- Is the timeline realistic for cultural change (not too fast)?
