# Organizational Health -- Psychological Safety, Change Management, and Systems

## Purpose

This module defines the framework for diagnosing and improving organizational health -- the systemic conditions that enable sustained high performance. While engagement measures individual employee connection to work, organizational health measures the collective capability of the organization to align, execute, and renew itself. The HR Brain treats organizational health as the foundation upon which engagement, performance, and culture are built.

---

## 1. McKinsey Organizational Health Index (OHI)

### 1.1 Overview

The McKinsey OHI is the most comprehensive organizational health framework, based on research across 1,500+ organizations and 5 million+ survey responses. It demonstrates that healthy organizations generate total returns to shareholders 3x greater than unhealthy organizations.

### 1.2 Nine Dimensions of Organizational Health

| Dimension | Definition | Key Practices |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Direction** | Clear, compelling strategic direction understood by all | Shared vision, strategic clarity, cascaded goals |
| **Leadership** | Effective leadership at all levels | Authoritative, consultative, supportive, and challenging leadership styles |
| **Culture and climate** | Shared beliefs and quality of interactions | Trust, openness to challenge, internal competition vs. collaboration |
| **Accountability** | Clear ownership and consequences | Role clarity, consequence management, personal ownership |
| **Coordination and control** | Operational discipline and coordination | Financial management, professional standards, operational discipline |
| **Capabilities** | Skills, talent, and institutional knowledge | Talent acquisition, talent development, process-based capabilities |
| **Motivation** | Factors that inspire discretionary effort | Meaningful values, inspirational leaders, career opportunities, financial incentives |
| **Innovation and learning** | Ability to generate and implement new ideas | Top-down innovation, bottom-up innovation, knowledge sharing, external partnerships |
| **External orientation** | Awareness and responsiveness to external environment | Customer focus, competitive awareness, partnerships, government/regulatory relationships |

### 1.3 Health Archetypes

McKinsey identifies four archetypes (or "recipes") for organizational health. Organizations do not need to excel on all nine dimensions -- they need a coherent combination:

```
ARCHETYPE 1: LEADER-DRIVEN
  Strength: Strong central leadership driving direction and execution
  Emphasis: Direction, leadership, accountability
  Risk: Over-dependence on individual leaders; fragile succession

ARCHETYPE 2: MARKET-FOCUSED
  Strength: Deep customer orientation and external awareness
  Emphasis: External orientation, innovation, coordination
  Risk: May sacrifice internal alignment for customer responsiveness

ARCHETYPE 3: EXECUTION-EDGE
  Strength: Operational excellence and disciplined execution
  Emphasis: Accountability, coordination, capabilities
  Risk: May stifle innovation and adaptability

ARCHETYPE 4: TALENT/KNOWLEDGE CORE
  Strength: Top talent and deep expertise
  Emphasis: Capabilities, motivation, innovation
  Risk: May struggle with coordination and scaling
```

### 1.4 OHI Application

**Assessment process:**
1. Survey all employees on OHI dimensions (typically 60-80 items)
2. Benchmark against McKinsey's global database
3. Identify archetype alignment and gap areas
4. Design targeted interventions on lowest-scoring dimensions
5. Reassess annually to track progress

**HR Brain integration**: OHI results inform workforce planning, leadership development, organizational design, and culture strategy. Low-scoring dimensions trigger specific HR interventions from the relevant module.

## 2. Psychological Safety (Edmondson)

### 2.1 Definition

Amy Edmondson defines psychological safety as "a shared belief held by members of a team that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking." It is the most important predictor of team effectiveness, as demonstrated by Google's Project Aristotle research.

**Psychological safety is NOT:**
- Being nice or avoiding conflict
- Lowering performance standards
- Unconditional agreement or comfort
- The absence of accountability

**Psychological safety IS:**
- Feeling safe to admit mistakes without punishment
- Feeling safe to ask questions without looking ignorant
- Feeling safe to offer ideas without being humiliated
- Feeling safe to challenge the status quo without retaliation
- Candor + trust + accountability

### 2.2 Edmondson's Measurement

Edmondson's 7-item team psychological safety scale:

| # | Item | Construct |
|:---:|------|----------|
| 1 | If you make a mistake on this team, it is often held against you (R) | Mistake tolerance |
| 2 | Members of this team are able to bring up problems and tough issues | Problem surfacing |
| 3 | People on this team sometimes reject others for being different (R) | Inclusion |
| 4 | It is safe to take a risk on this team | Risk-taking |
| 5 | It is difficult to ask other members of this team for help (R) | Help-seeking |
| 6 | No one on this team would deliberately act to undermine my efforts | Trust |
| 7 | Working with members of this team, my unique skills and talents are valued | Valued contribution |

(R) = reverse-scored items

### 2.3 The Safety-Accountability Matrix

Edmondson identifies four zones based on the combination of psychological safety and accountability:

```
HIGH ACCOUNTABILITY
         │
         │   ANXIETY ZONE          │    LEARNING ZONE
         │   (High standards,      │    (High standards +
         │    low safety =         │     high safety =
         │    fear and stress)     │     high performance)
         │                         │
    ─────┼─────────────────────────┼────────────────────
         │                         │
         │   APATHY ZONE           │    COMFORT ZONE
         │   (Low standards,       │    (High safety,
         │    low safety =         │     low standards =
         │    disengagement)       │     complacency)
         │                         │
LOW ACCOUNTABILITY
         └─────────────────────────────────────────────
              LOW SAFETY                  HIGH SAFETY
```

**Target**: LEARNING ZONE (high psychological safety AND high accountability). This is where teams innovate, learn from failure, hold each other accountable, and achieve peak performance.

### 2.4 Building Psychological Safety

**Leader behaviors that build psychological safety:**

| Behavior | How to Practice |
|----------|----------------|
| Model vulnerability | Share your own mistakes and uncertainties ("I was wrong about X; here's what I learned") |
| Frame failure as learning | "What did we learn?" not "Who is responsible?" |
| Invite participation explicitly | "I want to hear from everyone. [Name], what do you think?" |
| Respond productively to bad news | Thank the messenger; focus on solving, not blaming |
| Acknowledge limits of your knowledge | "I don't know -- let's figure this out together" |
| Set up structures for safe input | Anonymous feedback channels; pre-meeting idea submission; round-robin |
| Address violations immediately | If someone is ridiculed or shut down, intervene in the moment |
| Reward candor | Publicly recognize when someone raised an uncomfortable truth that led to a better outcome |

### 2.5 Measuring Psychological Safety

- **Frequency**: Semi-annual minimum (annually at minimum; quarterly for teams in intervention)
- **Level of analysis**: Team level (psychological safety is a team-level phenomenon)
- **Alert threshold**: Any team scoring below 3.5/5.0 triggers manager coaching intervention
- **Tracking**: Longitudinal tracking by team; correlate with innovation, quality, and retention metrics

## 3. Google Project Aristotle

### 3.1 Research Summary

Google's Project Aristotle (2012-2015) studied 180 Google teams to identify what makes teams effective. The findings were surprising: team composition (who is on the team) mattered far less than team dynamics (how the team works together).

### 3.2 Five Dynamics of Effective Teams

Listed in order of importance:

| Rank | Dynamic | Definition | Measurement |
|:---:|---------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | **Psychological safety** | Team members feel safe to take risks and be vulnerable | Edmondson 7-item scale |
| 2 | **Dependability** | Team members reliably complete quality work on time | Peer assessment; delivery tracking |
| 3 | **Structure and clarity** | Clear roles, plans, and goals | Goal clarity survey items; RACI matrix usage |
| 4 | **Meaning** | Work is personally important to team members | Purpose alignment survey items |
| 5 | **Impact** | Team believes their work matters and creates change | Impact perception survey items; customer feedback |

For each dynamic, assess and intervene:

## 4. Kotter's 8-Step Change Model

### 4.1 The Model (Kotter, 1996)

John Kotter's change management framework remains the most widely used model for leading organizational transformation:

```
STEP 1: CREATE URGENCY
  Why change now? What happens if we don't?
  - Data-driven case for change
  - External threats or opportunities
  - Honest assessment of current state

STEP 2: BUILD A GUIDING COALITION
  Who will lead this change?
  - Senior leaders with authority and credibility
  - Influential informal leaders
  - Cross-functional representation
  - Diversity of perspectives

STEP 3: FORM A STRATEGIC VISION
  Where are we going?
  - Clear, compelling description of the future state
  - Aligned to organizational strategy
  - Simple enough to communicate in 5 minutes

STEP 4: ENLIST A VOLUNTEER ARMY
  How do we get broad buy-in?
  - Communicate the vision through every channel
  - Leaders model the desired behavior
  - Address concerns and resistance directly
  - Create opportunities for people to participate

STEP 5: ENABLE ACTION BY REMOVING BARRIERS
  What is preventing change?
  - Remove structural barriers (org design, processes)
  - Address skill gaps (training, coaching)
  - Empower people to act on the vision
  - Confront managers who undermine the change

STEP 6: GENERATE SHORT-TERM WINS
  How do we build momentum?
  - Plan for visible, unambiguous improvements
  - Achieve wins within 6-12 months
  - Recognize and celebrate contributors
  - Use wins to build credibility for the change effort

STEP 7: SUSTAIN ACCELERATION
  How do we keep going?
  - Use credibility from wins to tackle bigger changes
  - Hire, promote, and develop people who embody the change
  - Reinvigorate the process with new projects and themes
  - Do not declare victory too soon

STEP 8: INSTITUTE CHANGE
  How do we make it stick?
  - Anchor new approaches in the culture
  - Articulate connections between new behaviors and success
  - Ensure leadership succession supports the change
  - Create systems that sustain the new way of working
```

### 4.2 Why Change Efforts Fail

Kotter (1995) identified the most common failure points:

| Failure Point | Frequency | Root Cause |
|--------------|:---:|-----------|
| Not establishing enough urgency | #1 | Complacency; leaders underestimate resistance |
| Not creating a powerful guiding coalition | Common | Change led by too few people; insufficient authority |
| Lacking a vision | Common | Complex strategy without a clear, compelling narrative |
| Under-communicating the vision | Pervasive | 10x more communication needed than leaders typically provide |
| Not removing obstacles | Common | Old systems, structures, and resistant managers left in place |
| Not creating short-term wins | Common | Focus on long-term transformation without visible early progress |
| Declaring victory too soon | Common | Celebrating after first wins; new behaviors not yet anchored |
| Not anchoring changes in culture | Common | New practices abandoned when pressure eases |

## 5. PROSCI ADKAR Model

### 5.1 Individual Change Framework

While Kotter addresses organizational change, ADKAR (Prosci, Hiatt, 2006) addresses how individuals experience change:

| Stage | Definition | Key Question | Enablers |
|-------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Awareness** | Understanding why change is needed | "Why?" | Communication, data, stories |
| **Desire** | Personal motivation to participate | "What's in it for me?" | Incentives, consequences, personal connection |
| **Knowledge** | Understanding how to change | "How?" | Training, coaching, documentation |
| **Ability** | Demonstrated capability to implement | "Can I do it?" | Practice, support, time, resources |
| **Reinforcement** | Sustaining the change over time | "Will it stick?" | Recognition, measurement, accountability |

### 5.2 ADKAR Diagnostic

When change stalls, diagnose which ADKAR stage is the barrier point:

```
"Employees are not adopting the new system."

Awareness check: "Do they know WHY we are changing?"
  → If no: Communication problem. More "why" messaging needed.

Desire check: "Do they WANT to change?"
  → If no: Motivation problem. Address WIIFM; manage resistance.

Knowledge check: "Do they know HOW to use the new system?"
  → If no: Training problem. Provide education and documentation.

Ability check: "CAN they use the new system effectively?"
  → If no: Practice problem. Provide coaching, practice time, support.

Reinforcement check: "Are they CONTINUING to use the new system?"
  → If no: Sustainability problem. Measure, recognize, hold accountable.
```

## 7. Organizational Resilience

### 7.1 Resilience Factors

Organizational resilience is the ability to anticipate, prepare for, respond to, and adapt to disruptive events. Resilient organizations share:

- **Cognitive diversity**: Multiple perspectives prevent groupthink
- **Distributed decision-making**: Decisions can be made close to the problem
- **Psychological safety**: Problems are surfaced early, not hidden
- **Learning orientation**: Failures are studied, not punished
- **Redundancy**: No single point of failure in critical roles or processes
- **Strong social networks**: Informal connections enable rapid coordination
- **Adaptive capacity**: Ability to rapidly reconfigure resources and processes

### 7.2 Building Resilience Through HR Practices

| HR Practice | Resilience Contribution |
|------------|------------------------|
| Succession planning | Eliminates key-person risk |
| Cross-training and rotation | Builds redundancy and breadth |
| Psychological safety programs | Enables early problem detection |
| Diverse hiring | Increases cognitive diversity |
| Flexible work policies | Demonstrates adaptation capability |
| Post-incident reviews (blameless) | Builds learning orientation |
| Emergency communication protocols | Enables rapid coordination |
| Wellness and EAP | Supports individual resilience |
