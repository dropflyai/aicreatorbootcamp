# HR Theory -- Strategic Human Resource Management

## Purpose

This module establishes the theoretical foundations that govern all HR Brain operations. Every recommendation, policy, and process must trace back to validated theory. The HR Brain does not operate on intuition; it operates on decades of peer-reviewed organizational behavior and industrial-organizational psychology research.

## 1. Strategic Human Resource Management (SHRM)

### 1.1 Definition and Evolution

Strategic HRM is the pattern of planned human resource deployments and activities intended to enable an organization to achieve its goals (Wright & McMahan, 1992). Unlike transactional HR (payroll, compliance, administration), strategic HRM directly connects people practices to business outcomes.

**Evolution of HR function:**

| Era | Focus | HR Role | Key Theorists |
|-----|-------|---------|---------------|
| Pre-1960s | Personnel administration | Record-keeping, payroll | N/A |
| 1960s-1970s | Labor relations, compliance | Regulatory compliance | Dunlop, Kerr |
| 1980s | Human resource management | Functional specialist | Beer, Fombrun |
| 1990s | Strategic HRM | Business partner | Ulrich, Wright, Huselid |
| 2000s | Human capital management | Talent strategist | Boudreau, Cappelli |
| 2010s+ | People analytics & experience | Data-driven strategist | Bersin, Davenport |

### 1.2 The Harvard Model (Beer et al., 1984)

The Harvard framework identifies four policy areas that managers must make decisions about:

1. **Human resource flows** -- recruitment, selection, promotion, termination
2. **Reward systems** -- pay, benefits, recognition
3. **Employee influence** -- delegation, participation, voice
4. **Work systems** -- job design, work organization, technology

These four areas produce HR outcomes (commitment, competence, congruence, cost-effectiveness) that feed long-term consequences for individual well-being, organizational effectiveness, and societal well-being.

### 1.3 The Michigan Model (Fombrun, Tichy & Devanna, 1984)

This "hard" HRM model treats human resources as a factor of production to be managed rationally. It proposes a tight fit between business strategy and HR strategy through four generic HR processes:

- **Selection** -- matching people to jobs
- **Appraisal** -- measuring performance
- **Rewards** -- reinforcing desired behavior
- **Development** -- building future capacity

The Michigan Model emphasizes alignment and measurement, but has been critiqued for its unitarist assumptions and insufficient attention to employee voice.

### 2.2 Updated Nine Competencies (Ulrich et al., 2017)

The HR Competency Study (Round 7, 30 years of data, 30,000+ respondents) identified nine competencies that drive personal effectiveness and business impact:

1. **Strategic Positioner** -- understands business context, customers, competitors
2. **Credible Activist** -- builds trust through results, takes principled stands
3. **Paradox Navigator** -- manages inherent tensions (short/long term, individual/organization)
4. **Culture and Change Champion** -- makes culture happen, drives transformation
5. **Human Capital Curator** -- manages talent flow, develops people
6. **Total Rewards Steward** -- designs compensation and benefits that attract and retain
7. **Technology and Media Integrator** -- leverages HR technology and social media
8. **Analytics Designer and Interpreter** -- uses data to drive decisions
9. **Compliance Manager** -- ensures legal and regulatory adherence

## 3. Human Capital Theory

### 3.1 Foundations (Becker, 1964; Schultz, 1961)

Human capital theory posits that investments in people (education, training, health) increase productive capacity, analogous to physical capital investment. Key propositions:

- **General human capital**: Skills transferable across employers (e.g., literacy, numeracy, general management). Workers bear the cost; employers pay market wages.
- **Firm-specific human capital**: Skills valuable only within a particular organization (e.g., proprietary systems knowledge, internal network). Employers and employees share investment costs and returns.

### 3.2 Resource-Based View (Barney, 1991)

The RBV framework establishes when human capital constitutes competitive advantage. Resources must be:

- **Valuable** -- enable the firm to exploit opportunities or neutralize threats
- **Rare** -- not widely available among competitors
- **Inimitable** -- difficult to replicate due to causal ambiguity, social complexity, or path dependence
- **Non-substitutable** -- no strategically equivalent alternatives exist

**HR Brain application**: When evaluating talent strategy, assess whether the workforce configuration satisfies VRIN criteria. If competitors can easily replicate your talent base, it is not a source of competitive advantage.

### 3.3 Human Capital ROI Measurement

```
Human Capital ROI = (Revenue - (Operating Expense - [Compensation + Benefits]))
                    ÷ (Compensation + Benefits)

Human Capital Value Added = Revenue - (Operating Expense - [Compensation + Benefits])
                            ÷ Full-Time Equivalents (FTEs)
```

These metrics (Fitz-enz, 2000) allow the HR Brain to quantify workforce contribution in financial terms.

## 4. The AMO Framework

### 4.1 Core Model (Appelbaum et al., 2000)

The Ability-Motivation-Opportunity framework explains the mechanism by which HR practices influence performance:

**Performance = f(Ability x Motivation x Opportunity)**

| Component | Definition | HR Practices That Drive It |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **Ability (A)** | Employee skills, knowledge, and aptitude | Selection, training, development, education investment |
| **Motivation (M)** | Employee willingness to exert effort | Compensation, recognition, performance management, feedback |
| **Opportunity (O)** | Enabling conditions for employees to contribute | Job design, autonomy, participation, information sharing, team structures |

### 4.2 Why Multiplicative, Not Additive

The AMO model is multiplicative: if any component is zero, performance is zero regardless of the other components. Implications:

- Hiring brilliant people (high A) without autonomy or involvement (low O) wastes talent
- Providing generous compensation (high M) to unskilled workers (low A) produces waste
- Granting decision-making authority (high O) to unmotivated employees (low M) creates risk

### 4.3 High-Performance Work Systems (HPWS)

The AMO framework underpins High-Performance Work Systems research (Huselid, 1995). Huselid demonstrated that a one-standard-deviation increase in HPWS adoption was associated with:

- 7.05% decrease in turnover
- $27,044 increase in sales per employee
- $18,641 increase in market value per employee
- $3,814 increase in profit per employee

HPWS bundles include: rigorous selection, extensive training, performance-based pay, employee involvement, information sharing, and employment security.

## 5. Evidence-Based HR

### 5.1 Principles (Rousseau, 2006; Briner et al., 2009)

Evidence-based HR applies the same rigor to people decisions that evidence-based medicine applies to clinical decisions. Four sources of evidence:

1. **Scientific evidence** -- peer-reviewed research from I/O psychology, organizational behavior, labor economics
2. **Organizational evidence** -- internal data from HRIS, surveys, performance metrics, exit interviews
3. **Experiential evidence** -- practitioner expertise and professional judgment
4. **Stakeholder evidence** -- values, concerns, and preferences of those affected by the decision

### 5.2 Hierarchy of Evidence

```
┌───────────────────────────────┐
│   Meta-analyses and           │  ← Strongest
│   systematic reviews          │
├───────────────────────────────┤
│   Randomized controlled       │
│   experiments (RCTs)          │
├───────────────────────────────┤
│   Quasi-experimental          │
│   designs                     │
├───────────────────────────────┤
│   Correlational studies       │
│   and surveys                 │
├───────────────────────────────┤
│   Case studies                │
├───────────────────────────────┤
│   Expert opinion              │  ← Weakest
└───────────────────────────────┘
```

### 5.3 Common Myths Debunked by Evidence

| Myth | Evidence |
|------|----------|
| "Unstructured interviews are fine" | Validity of unstructured interviews: r = .20; structured: r = .51 (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998) |
| "Pay is the top motivator" | Herzberg (1959): Pay is a hygiene factor; motivators are achievement, recognition, growth |
| "Engagement surveys are enough" | Surveys without action plans decrease engagement (Gallup, 2017) |
| "Forced ranking improves performance" | Forced ranking increases political behavior and decreases collaboration (Scullen et al., 2005) |
| "Reference checks predict performance" | Validity of reference checks: r = .26 (Schmidt & Hunter, 1998) |
| "Years of experience predict performance" | After 5 years, experience has near-zero correlation with performance (McDaniel et al., 1988) |

### 5.4 The Schmidt & Hunter Meta-Analysis (1998)

This landmark study ("The Validity and Utility of Selection Methods in Personnel Psychology") analyzed 85 years of research on selection methods. Validity coefficients (correlation with job performance):

| Method | Validity (r) |
|--------|-------------|
| GMA tests + structured interview | .63 |
| GMA tests + work sample tests | .63 |
| Work sample tests | .54 |
| Structured interviews | .51 |
| GMA (general mental ability) tests | .51 |
| Job knowledge tests | .48 |
| Job tryout procedures | .44 |
| Peer ratings | .49 |
| Integrity tests | .41 |
| Conscientiousness tests | .31 |
| Reference checks | .26 |
| Unstructured interviews | .20 |
| Years of education | .10 |
| Graphology (handwriting analysis) | .02 |
| Age | -.01 |

**HR Brain enforcement**: All selection system designs must reference Schmidt & Hunter validity data. Methods with validity below .30 must not be used as primary selection criteria.

## 6. Psychological Contract Theory (Rousseau, 1989)

### 6.1 Definition

The psychological contract is the set of unwritten, implicit expectations between employer and employee regarding mutual obligations. It exists alongside the legal employment contract and profoundly affects engagement, trust, and retention.

### 6.2 Types of Psychological Contracts

| Type | Duration | Performance Requirements | Examples |
|------|----------|------------------------|----------|
| **Transactional** | Short-term, specific | Narrow, well-defined | Contractors, seasonal workers |
| **Relational** | Open-ended, broad | Broad, evolving | Career employees, partnership model |
| **Balanced** | Open-ended but performance-linked | Evolving with clear metrics | Modern knowledge workers |
| **Transitional** | Declining, ambiguous | Unclear or eroding | Layoff periods, restructuring |

### 6.3 Contract Breach and Violation

When the organization fails to meet perceived obligations, employees experience psychological contract breach. Meta-analytic evidence (Zhao et al., 2007) shows breach is associated with:

- Decreased job satisfaction (r = -.54)
- Decreased organizational commitment (r = -.38)
- Increased turnover intentions (r = .42)
- Decreased organizational citizenship behaviors (r = -.14)
- Increased counterproductive work behaviors (r = .11)

## 7. Systems Integration

### 7.1 How Theory Informs Practice

Every HR Brain module connects to foundational theory:

| Module | Primary Theory | Application |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| Talent Acquisition | Schmidt & Hunter, Human Capital | Selection validity, ROI of hiring quality |
| Compensation | Human Capital, Expectancy Theory | Pay-for-performance design, total rewards |
| Performance | AMO, Goal-Setting (Locke & Latham) | Feedback systems, performance enablement |
| Culture | Schein, Psychological Contract | Culture measurement, values operationalization |
| Org Design | Contingency Theory, RBV | Structure-strategy alignment |
| DEIB | Social Identity Theory, Intergroup Contact | Bias interruption, inclusive design |
| Analytics | Evidence-Based HR | Data-driven decision frameworks |
| Operations | Institutional Theory | Compliance, standardization, legitimacy |

### 7.2 Theory-Practice Gap

The HR Brain exists precisely because a theory-practice gap persists in HR. Rynes et al. (2002) found that HR practitioners' beliefs diverge significantly from research evidence on topics including:

- Intelligence testing validity
- Goal-setting effectiveness
- Pay-for-performance design
- The value of job satisfaction

The HR Brain bridges this gap by encoding research evidence directly into decision frameworks.
