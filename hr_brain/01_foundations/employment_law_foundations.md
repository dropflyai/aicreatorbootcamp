# Employment Law Foundations

## Purpose

This module codifies the legal framework governing the employment relationship in the United States. Every HR Brain recommendation must comply with applicable law. When legal requirements conflict with business preferences, legal requirements prevail without exception. This module does not constitute legal advice; it establishes the compliance baseline that triggers escalation to qualified legal counsel.

---

## 1. The Employment-At-Will Doctrine

### 1.1 General Rule

In the United States, employment is presumptively at-will. Either party -- employer or employee -- may terminate the relationship at any time, for any reason (or no reason), with or without notice. Montana is the only state that has modified this doctrine by statute (Montana Wrongful Discharge from Employment Act, 1987).

### 1.2 Exceptions to At-Will Employment

At-will employment is subject to significant exceptions that the HR Brain must track:

**Statutory exceptions:**
- Federal anti-discrimination statutes (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, etc.)
- Whistleblower protection statutes (SOX, Dodd-Frank, state equivalents)
- Retaliation prohibitions under virtually all employment statutes
- WARN Act (60-day notice for mass layoffs affecting 100+ employees)

**Common law exceptions:**
- **Public policy exception** (43 states): Cannot terminate for reasons that violate clear public policy (e.g., firing for jury duty, filing workers' comp claims)
- **Implied contract exception** (38 states): Employer statements in handbooks, offer letters, or verbal promises may create enforceable contract terms
- **Implied covenant of good faith** (11 states): Cannot terminate to avoid paying earned commissions, vested benefits, or in bad faith

### 1.3 Practical Implications

The at-will doctrine does not mean employers can terminate without risk. Documentation is essential because:
- Terminated employees may allege discriminatory motive
- Patterns of termination may reveal disparate impact
- Inconsistent application of policies creates liability
- Lack of documentation eliminates the employer's ability to demonstrate legitimate business reasons

## 2. Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964

### 2.1 Coverage and Protected Classes

Title VII applies to employers with 15 or more employees. It prohibits discrimination in all terms and conditions of employment based on:
- **Race** and color
- **Religion** (includes reasonable accommodation obligation)
- **Sex** (includes pregnancy per PDA 1978; sexual orientation and gender identity per Bostock v. Clayton County, 2020)
- **National origin**

### 2.2 Theories of Discrimination

**Disparate treatment (intentional discrimination):**
- Employee must establish prima facie case (McDonnell Douglas burden-shifting framework)
- Employer must articulate legitimate, non-discriminatory reason
- Employee must prove reason is pretextual
- Direct evidence of discriminatory intent (e.g., discriminatory comments) bypasses burden-shifting

**Disparate impact (unintentional discrimination):**
- Facially neutral policy or practice disproportionately affects protected group
- Employer must demonstrate business necessity and job-relatedness
- Employee may show less discriminatory alternative exists
- Four-fifths rule (EEOC Uniform Guidelines): Selection rate for protected group below 80% of majority group triggers investigation

**Harassment (hostile work environment):**
- Unwelcome conduct based on protected characteristic
- Severe or pervasive enough to alter conditions of employment
- Employer liability depends on harasser's relationship to victim (Faragher-Ellerth defense for supervisor harassment)

### 2.3 Enforcement

- **EEOC** (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission) administers Title VII
- **Charge filing**: 180 days from discriminatory act (300 days in deferral states)
- **Right to sue**: EEOC issues after investigation or upon request after 180 days
- **Remedies**: Back pay, front pay, compensatory and punitive damages (capped by employer size), injunctive relief, attorneys' fees

## 3. Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) of 1990

### 3.1 Core Requirements

The ADA prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities in all employment practices. Applies to employers with 15+ employees.

**Disability definition (as amended by ADAAA 2008):**
- Physical or mental impairment that substantially limits one or more major life activities
- Record of such impairment
- Being regarded as having such impairment

The ADAAA broadened the definition significantly, making it easier to establish disability status. The focus shifted from "is this person disabled?" to "was this person treated differently because of a disability?"

### 3.2 Reasonable Accommodation

Employers must provide reasonable accommodation to qualified individuals with disabilities unless doing so would cause undue hardship. The interactive process is mandatory:

1. Employee requests accommodation (no magic words required)
2. Employer engages in interactive process to identify limitations
3. Employer and employee identify potential accommodations
4. Employer selects and implements effective accommodation
5. Employer documents the process

**Common accommodations:**
- Modified work schedules or remote work arrangements
- Assistive technology (screen readers, voice recognition)
- Job restructuring (reallocating marginal functions)
- Leave of absence beyond FMLA entitlement
- Physical workspace modifications
- Reassignment to vacant position (accommodation of last resort)

### 3.3 Medical Inquiries and Examinations

| Stage | Permissible Inquiries |
|-------|----------------------|
| Pre-offer | Cannot ask about disability; can ask about ability to perform essential functions |
| Post-offer, pre-employment | May require medical examination if required of all entering employees in same job category |
| During employment | Must be job-related and consistent with business necessity |
| Fitness-for-duty | Only when reasonable belief employee cannot perform essential functions or poses direct threat |

## 4. Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA) of 1967

### 4.1 Scope

Protects individuals age 40 and older from employment discrimination. Applies to employers with 20+ employees. Key provisions:

- Prohibits mandatory retirement (with narrow exceptions for bona fide executives and public safety officers)
- Prohibits age-based disparate treatment and disparate impact
- Standard for disparate impact claims is higher than Title VII (Gross v. FBL Financial Services, 2009: age must be the "but-for" cause)
- Prohibits retaliation for filing ADEA complaints

### 4.2 Common ADEA Issues in Tech and Startups

- Job postings seeking "digital natives," "recent graduates," or specifying maximum years of experience
- RIF (reduction in force) decisions that disproportionately affect older workers
- Performance review language that references "energy," "cultural fit," or "adaptability" as proxies for age
- Benefits plans that reduce benefits for older workers (permissible only if cost-justified under the equal cost/equal benefit rule)

### 4.3 OWBPA (Older Workers Benefit Protection Act)

Governs waivers of ADEA claims in separation agreements:
- Must be written in plain language
- Must specifically reference ADEA rights
- Must advise consultation with attorney
- Individual termination: 21-day consideration period + 7-day revocation period
- Group termination (RIF): 45-day consideration period + 7-day revocation period + disclosure of decisional unit, eligibility criteria, and ages of selected/non-selected employees

## 5. Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) of 1993

### 5.1 Eligibility and Entitlement

Applies to employers with 50+ employees within 75 miles of worksite. Eligible employees must have worked 12 months and 1,250 hours in the preceding 12 months.

**Qualifying reasons for leave (up to 12 weeks unpaid):**
- Birth, adoption, or foster placement of a child
- Serious health condition of employee
- Serious health condition of spouse, child, or parent
- Qualifying exigency related to military service

**Military caregiver leave:** Up to 26 weeks for care of covered servicemember with serious injury or illness.

### 5.2 Employer Obligations

- Maintain group health insurance during leave at same level as if employee were actively working
- Restore employee to same or equivalent position upon return
- Cannot retaliate against employee for exercising FMLA rights
- Must post FMLA notice and include FMLA information in employee handbook
- Must provide eligibility notice within 5 business days of leave request
- Must provide designation notice within 5 business days of obtaining sufficient information

### 5.3 Intermittent Leave

Employees may take FMLA leave intermittently (in separate blocks of time) or on a reduced schedule when medically necessary. Employers may:
- Require medical certification
- Require recertification every 30 days (in most circumstances)
- Transfer employee to equivalent position better suited to intermittent leave
- Track intermittent leave in increments no larger than the smallest increment used for other leave types

## 6. Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)

### 6.1 Classification: Exempt vs. Non-Exempt

The FLSA establishes minimum wage, overtime pay, and recordkeeping requirements. Classification as exempt requires meeting both a salary basis test and a duties test:

**Salary basis test:** Employee must be paid a predetermined, fixed salary not subject to reduction based on quality or quantity of work. Current federal salary threshold: $35,568/year ($684/week). Many states have higher thresholds.

**Duties tests for common exemptions:**

| Exemption | Primary Duty | Key Requirements |
|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| Executive | Managing the enterprise or recognized department | Directs work of 2+ FTEs; authority to hire/fire or recommend |
| Administrative | Office/non-manual work related to management or business operations | Exercise of discretion and independent judgment on significant matters |
| Professional (Learned) | Work requiring advanced knowledge in a field of science or learning | Requires specialized academic training (law, medicine, engineering, etc.) |
| Professional (Creative) | Work requiring invention, imagination, originality | Writing, music, acting, graphic arts |
| Computer Employee | Systems analysis, programming, software engineering | Salary or hourly rate of at least $27.63/hour |
| Outside Sales | Making sales or obtaining orders away from employer's place of business | Customarily and regularly engaged away from office |
| Highly Compensated | Total annual compensation of $107,432+ | Performs at least one exempt duty |

### 6.2 Overtime Requirements

Non-exempt employees must receive overtime pay at 1.5x regular rate for all hours worked over 40 in a workweek. Key rules:
- Overtime is calculated per workweek (7 consecutive 24-hour periods), not per day (except California and a few other states)
- Employers cannot waive overtime even with employee consent
- Compensatory time in lieu of overtime is permitted only for public sector employers
- "Suffered or permitted to work" standard: If employer knows or should know employee is working, hours count

### 6.3 Misclassification Risk

Misclassification of non-exempt employees as exempt is one of the most common and costly employment law violations. The HR Brain must flag any classification that does not clearly satisfy both salary and duties tests. Penalties include:
- Back pay for unpaid overtime (up to 2 years, or 3 years for willful violations)
- Liquidated damages (equal to back pay)
- Attorneys' fees
- Class and collective action exposure

## 7. National Labor Relations Act (NLRA)

### 7.1 Scope

The NLRA protects employees' rights to organize, form unions, bargain collectively, and engage in concerted activity. Section 7 rights apply to most private-sector employees, whether or not they are unionized.

### 7.2 Protected Concerted Activity

Employees have the right to engage in "concerted activity" for mutual aid or protection, including:
- Discussing wages, benefits, and working conditions with coworkers
- Posting about working conditions on social media
- Complaining collectively to management about workplace issues
- Refusing to work in unsafe conditions (if group activity)

**HR Brain enforcement**: Policies that prohibit or chill employees from discussing pay, working conditions, or workplace concerns may violate the NLRA. Social media policies, confidentiality agreements, and non-disparagement clauses must be drafted narrowly to avoid NLRA violations.

### 7.3 Unfair Labor Practices

Employer unfair labor practices (Section 8(a)):
- Interfering with Section 7 rights
- Dominating or interfering with a labor organization
- Discriminating against employees for union activity
- Refusing to bargain in good faith with certified union

## 8. Documentation Standards

### 8.1 The Documentation Imperative

Documentation is the employer's primary defense in employment litigation. The HR Brain requires documentation for:

- All performance issues and coaching conversations
- Accommodation requests and interactive process steps
- Disciplinary actions (verbal warnings documented, written warnings, suspensions, terminations)
- FMLA eligibility determinations and leave tracking
- Classification decisions (exempt/non-exempt analysis)
- Hiring decisions (interview notes, scoring rubrics, selection rationale)
- Separation decisions (business justification, consideration of alternatives)

### 8.2 Documentation Best Practices

- Document contemporaneously (at or near the time of the event)
- Be factual, specific, and objective (avoid conclusions, characterizations, or emotional language)
- Include dates, times, witnesses, and specific behaviors or statements
- Maintain consistency (document similar situations similarly across employees)
- Store securely with appropriate access controls
- Retain per applicable retention schedules (EEOC: 1 year; FLSA: 3 years; FMLA: 3 years; OSHA: 5 years; ERISA: 6 years)

### 8.3 Common Documentation Failures

| Failure | Risk |
|---------|------|
| No documentation of performance issues before termination | Cannot establish legitimate business reason; inference of pretext |
| Inconsistent documentation across similarly-situated employees | Disparate treatment claim |
| Subjective language ("bad attitude," "not a culture fit") | Allegation of coded discriminatory language |
| Backdated documentation | Spoliation; credibility destruction |
| Over-documentation of protected-class employees only | Evidence of discriminatory surveillance |

## 9. State and Local Law Landscape

### 9.1 Areas Where State/Local Law Frequently Exceeds Federal

- **Minimum wage**: Many states and cities exceed federal minimum wage
- **Paid leave**: Paid sick leave (15+ states), paid family leave (9+ states), paid medical leave
- **Salary history bans**: 20+ states/cities prohibit asking about prior compensation
- **Ban-the-box**: 35+ states/cities restrict criminal history inquiries
- **Pay transparency**: Posting salary ranges in job postings (CO, CA, WA, NY, etc.)
- **Non-compete restrictions**: FTC proposed rule, CA ban, increasing state restrictions
- **Cannabis protections**: Growing number of states protect off-duty cannabis use
- **Predictive scheduling**: Fair workweek laws in several cities and states

### 9.2 Multi-State Compliance Framework

For organizations operating in multiple states, the HR Brain applies the most restrictive applicable standard and tracks state-specific requirements in a compliance matrix. The compliance calendar must include:
- Annual EEO-1 filing
- State new-hire reporting
- Benefits renewal deadlines
- Poster update requirements
- Training mandates (harassment prevention: CA, CT, DE, IL, ME, NY)

## 10. Compliance Escalation Protocol

The HR Brain does not practice law. When any of the following conditions arise, the Brain must escalate to qualified employment counsel:

1. Termination of employee who has recently filed a complaint, requested accommodation, or taken protected leave
2. Reduction in force affecting 10+ employees
3. Any allegation of harassment, discrimination, or retaliation
4. EEOC charge or demand letter received
5. Classification of ambiguous positions (exempt vs. non-exempt)
6. Drafting of restrictive covenant agreements
7. Any action involving an employee on active workers' compensation, FMLA, or ADA leave
8. Multi-state policy implementation
9. Union-related activity or organizing
10. Investigation of workplace violence threats
