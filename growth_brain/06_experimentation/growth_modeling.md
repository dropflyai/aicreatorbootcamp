# Growth Modeling — Growth Accounting, Growth Loops Modeling, K-Factor, Payback Modeling, Scenario Planning

## Overview

Growth modeling translates qualitative growth strategy into quantitative
frameworks that forecast outcomes, reveal constraints, and guide
resource allocation. Without models, growth teams operate on intuition
and react to results. With models, they predict outcomes, identify
leverage points, and make investment decisions with confidence. This
module covers five modeling disciplines: growth accounting, growth loop
modeling, viral coefficient (K-factor) calculation, CAC payback modeling,
and scenario planning for strategic decision-making.

## Section 1: Growth Accounting

### The Growth Accounting Framework

Growth accounting, developed by Jonathan Hsu at Tribe Capital,
decomposes net user growth into its component parts:

```
Net New Users = New Users + Resurrected Users - Churned Users

Net Growth Rate = (New + Resurrected - Churned) / Starting Users
```

### The Quick Ratio

The SaaS Quick Ratio measures the efficiency of growth:

```
Quick Ratio = (New MRR + Expansion MRR + Reactivation MRR)
              / (Churned MRR + Contraction MRR)
```

**Interpretation:**
- Quick Ratio > 4: Highly efficient growth (adding $4 for every $1 lost)
- Quick Ratio 2–4: Healthy growth
- Quick Ratio 1–2: Leaky growth (significant churn offsetting gains)
- Quick Ratio < 1: Shrinking (losing more than gaining)

**Benchmark by Stage:**
| Stage | Target Quick Ratio |
|-------|-------------------|
| Pre-PMF | > 1 (survival) |
| Early Growth | > 2 |
| Scaling | > 3 |
| Market Leader | > 4 |

### Growth Accounting Dashboard

Build a monthly growth accounting view:

```
Month: [YYYY-MM]
Starting MRR: $___

+ New MRR:           $___  (from new customers)
+ Expansion MRR:     $___  (upgrades + seat additions)
+ Reactivation MRR:  $___  (returning churned customers)
= Gross New MRR:     $___

- Churned MRR:       $___  (cancellations)
- Contraction MRR:   $___  (downgrades)
= Gross Lost MRR:    $___

= Net New MRR:       $___
= Ending MRR:        $___
= Quick Ratio:       ___

MoM Growth Rate:     ___%
Annualized Growth:   ___%
```

### Decomposing Growth Rates

When growth accelerates or decelerates, growth accounting reveals the
specific driver:

**Growth Acceleration Scenarios:**
- New MRR increasing + Churn stable = Acquisition improving
- New MRR stable + Churn decreasing = Retention improving
- Expansion MRR increasing = Monetization improving
- All three improving = Compounding growth (best case)

**Growth Deceleration Scenarios:**
- New MRR decreasing + Churn stable = Acquisition channels saturating
- New MRR stable + Churn increasing = Product-market fit degrading
- Expansion MRR decreasing = Upsell exhaustion or value delivery failure

## Section 2: Growth Loop Modeling

### Modeling a Growth Loop

Growth loops are self-reinforcing systems where the output of one cycle
becomes the input of the next. Modeling a loop quantifies its efficiency
and identifies optimization opportunities.

### Loop Components

Every growth loop model has:

**Input:** New users or actions entering the loop
**Steps:** Sequential actions within the loop (each with a conversion
rate)
**Output:** New users or actions generated by the loop
**Loop Efficiency:** Output / Input (must be > 0 to sustain, > 1 to
grow organically)

### Modeling a Referral Loop

```
Step 1: Active users (Input)
         → [% who refer] = Referral Rate
Step 2: Referrals sent
         → [Invites per referrer] = Branching Factor
Step 3: Total invites sent
         → [% who sign up] = Invite Acceptance Rate
Step 4: New sign-ups
         → [% who activate] = Activation Rate
Step 5: New active users (Output → feeds back to Step 1)

Loop Efficiency (K-factor):
K = Referral Rate x Branching Factor x Acceptance Rate x Activation Rate
```

**Example:**
```
1,000 active users
× 10% refer = 100 referrers
× 3 invites each = 300 invites sent
× 20% accept = 60 new sign-ups
× 80% activate = 48 new active users

K = 48 / 1,000 = 0.048 per user
```

This loop adds 4.8% more users per cycle. Each new user also refers,
creating compounding (geometric series):
```
Total users from 1,000 base = 1,000 / (1 - K) = 1,000 / 0.952 = 1,050
```

### Modeling a Content/SEO Loop

```
Step 1: Content published
         → [Avg organic traffic per piece] = Traffic Yield
Step 2: Organic visitors
         → [% who sign up] = Organic Conversion Rate
Step 3: New users
         → [% who create content] = Content Creation Rate
Step 4: User-generated content published (feeds back to Step 1)

Plus:
Step 3 alt: [% who become paying customers] = Revenue per user
         → Revenue funds more content creation (Investment Loop)
```

### Modeling a Paid Acquisition Loop

```
Step 1: Revenue from paying customers
         → [% reinvested in ads] = Reinvestment Rate
Step 2: Ad spend
         → [Customers per dollar] = 1/CAC
Step 3: New customers
         → [Revenue per customer per cycle] = ARPU x Retention
Step 4: Revenue from new customers (feeds back to Step 1)

Loop Efficiency:
Efficiency = (ARPU x Retention x Reinvestment Rate) / CAC
If > 1: Loop is self-sustaining
If < 1: Loop requires external capital
```

## Section 3: Viral Coefficient (K-Factor)

### K-Factor Calculation

```
K = i x c

Where:
i = number of invitations per user (branching factor)
c = conversion rate of each invitation (acceptance rate)
```

**Interpretation:**
- K > 1: Viral growth (each user brings more than one new user).
  Product grows exponentially without paid acquisition.
- K = 0.5–1.0: Viral amplification. Product does not grow purely
  through virality but each acquired user brings partial additional
  users, reducing effective CAC.
- K < 0.5: Low virality. Growth depends on paid or organic acquisition.
- K = 0: No virality. Zero organic referral.

### Viral Cycle Time

K-factor alone is insufficient—the speed of the viral cycle determines
growth velocity:

```
Effective viral growth = K^(t/cycle_time)

Shorter cycle time → Faster compounding → Faster growth
```

A product with K=0.5 and 1-day cycle time grows faster than a product
with K=0.8 and 30-day cycle time in the short run.

**Cycle Time Optimization:**
- Reduce time from sign-up to first invite sent
- Reduce time from invite received to sign-up
- Reduce time from sign-up to activation
- Automate invitation triggers (after value moment, not at sign-up)

### K-Factor by Product Category

| Category | Typical K-Factor | Exceptional K-Factor |
|----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Social Networks | 0.3–0.7 | > 1.0 (early Facebook, TikTok) |
| Messaging | 0.5–1.0 | > 1.0 (WhatsApp, Slack early) |
| Productivity SaaS | 0.1–0.3 | 0.3–0.5 |
| E-commerce | 0.05–0.15 | 0.15–0.3 |
| B2B Enterprise | 0.01–0.05 | 0.05–0.1 |

## Section 4: CAC Payback Modeling

### The Payback Period

CAC payback period measures how many months of revenue are required to
recover the cost of acquiring a customer:

```
Payback Period (months) = CAC / (ARPU x Gross Margin %)
```

**Example:**
```
CAC = $500
ARPU = $100/month
Gross Margin = 80%

Payback = $500 / ($100 x 0.80) = 6.25 months
```

### Payback Period Benchmarks

| Segment | Good | Great | Exceptional |
|---------|------|-------|------------|
| Consumer SaaS | < 12 months | < 6 months | < 3 months |
| SMB SaaS | < 12 months | < 9 months | < 6 months |
| Mid-Market SaaS | < 18 months | < 12 months | < 9 months |
| Enterprise SaaS | < 24 months | < 18 months | < 12 months |

### Blended vs. Channel-Level Payback

**Blended Payback**
Total CAC / Average ARPU. Useful for high-level reporting but hides
channel-level performance differences.

**Channel-Level Payback**
Calculate payback for each acquisition channel independently:
```
Channel Payback = Channel CAC / (Channel Customer ARPU x Margin)
```

Channel-level payback reveals:
- Which channels are capital-efficient (invest more)
- Which channels are capital-intensive (optimize or reduce)
- Whether "blended CAC" is masking unprofitable channels

### LTV:CAC Ratio

```
LTV = ARPU x Gross Margin x Customer Lifetime (months)
LTV:CAC Ratio = LTV / CAC
```

**Benchmarks:**
- LTV:CAC > 3: Healthy unit economics (industry consensus minimum)
- LTV:CAC > 5: Strong unit economics, room to invest in growth
- LTV:CAC > 8: Potentially under-investing in growth (leaving market
  share on the table)
- LTV:CAC < 3: Unit economics under pressure; improve retention,
  increase ARPU, or reduce CAC

## Section 5: Scenario Planning

### Why Scenario Planning

Growth models are predictions, and predictions are wrong. Scenario
planning builds multiple models reflecting different assumptions to
bound the range of possible outcomes and stress-test strategic
decisions.

### The Three-Scenario Framework

**Bear Case (Pessimistic)**
- Assumptions: Conversion rates decline 10%, churn increases 20%,
  CAC increases 15%, market growth slows
- Purpose: What is the worst realistic outcome? Can we survive it?

**Base Case (Expected)**
- Assumptions: Current trends continue, planned improvements deliver
  expected results
- Purpose: The planning target for resource allocation

**Bull Case (Optimistic)**
- Assumptions: Planned improvements over-deliver, market tailwinds
  accelerate growth, viral coefficient improves
- Purpose: What does success look like? What would we need to
  capitalize on it?

### Sensitivity Analysis

For each key assumption, model the impact of +/- 20% variation:

```
| Assumption          | Bear (-20%) | Base | Bull (+20%) | Revenue Impact |
|---------------------|-------------|------|-------------|---------------|
| Conversion rate     | 2.4%        | 3.0% | 3.6%        | +/- $150K MRR |
| Monthly churn       | 6.0%        | 5.0% | 4.0%        | +/- $200K MRR |
| ARPU                | $80         | $100 | $120        | +/- $100K MRR |
| New leads/month     | 800         | 1000 | 1200        | +/- $120K MRR |
```

Sensitivity analysis reveals which assumptions have the greatest impact
on outcomes. Focus optimization efforts on the highest-sensitivity
variables.

### Monte Carlo Simulation

For advanced scenario planning, use Monte Carlo simulation:
1. Define probability distributions for each input variable
2. Run 10,000+ simulations with random draws from each distribution
3. Analyze the distribution of outcomes
4. Report the median, 25th percentile, and 75th percentile outcomes
5. Identify the probability of achieving target outcomes

Monte Carlo provides probability-weighted forecasts rather than single-
point estimates, enabling better risk management.

## Summary

Growth modeling transforms growth strategy from qualitative intuition
into quantitative prediction. Growth accounting decomposes net growth
into its component drivers—new, expansion, reactivation, churn, and
contraction—enabling precise diagnosis of growth dynamics. Growth loop
modeling quantifies the efficiency of self-reinforcing systems and
identifies the highest-leverage optimization points. K-factor and viral
cycle time determine whether organic amplification can reduce
dependence on paid acquisition. CAC payback and LTV:CAC ratios ensure
that customer acquisition is economically sustainable. Scenario planning
with sensitivity analysis and Monte Carlo simulation bounds the range
of outcomes and stress-tests strategic decisions. The Growth Brain
models before it acts, ensuring that every experiment and investment
is grounded in quantitative rigor.
