# Price Changes — Increase Execution, Communication, Grandfathering, and Mitigation

## Overview

Price changes are among the highest-stakes decisions in business. A well-executed price
increase can significantly improve revenue and margins while maintaining customer trust.
A poorly executed one can trigger churn waves, public backlash, and lasting brand
damage. This module provides the complete playbook for price increase execution
including timing strategy, customer communication (with 60-90 day notice requirements),
grandfathering approaches, phased rollout, churn mitigation tactics, and competitive
response planning.

## 2. Timing Strategy

### When to Announce

```
OPTIMAL TIMING:

  BEST MONTHS: January, September
    January: New budget year; customers expect cost changes
    September: Q4 budget planning; time to adjust before year-end

  AVOID:
    December: Holiday season; attention is elsewhere
    Q4 (for enterprise): Budget already allocated; no room for increase
    During incidents: Never raise prices when trust is damaged
    During competitor launch: Competitive pressure makes timing bad
    During economic downturn: Sensitivity is heightened

  WEEK:
    Announce Tuesday-Thursday (avoid Monday backlog, Friday neglect)
    Time: Morning (customer's timezone) for maximum engagement

  NOTICE PERIOD:
    Minimum: 30 days (many contracts require this)
    Standard: 60 days (professional courtesy)
    Best practice: 90 days (maximum preparation time for customer)
    Enterprise: 90+ days (procurement cycles require lead time)
```

### Rollout Strategy

```
PHASED ROLLOUT OPTIONS:

  OPTION 1: NEW CUSTOMERS FIRST (Lowest Risk)
    Month 1: New customers see new prices
    Month 3-6: Existing customers notified; take effect at renewal
    Risk: None for existing base; tests market acceptance first
    Drawback: Revenue impact delayed

  OPTION 2: SEGMENT-BASED ROLLOUT (Moderate Risk)
    Month 1: New customers + smallest accounts
    Month 3: Mid-market accounts
    Month 6: Enterprise accounts (at renewal)
    Risk: Moderate; smaller accounts test reaction first

  OPTION 3: ALL AT ONCE (Highest Risk, Fastest Impact)
    Month 1: All customers notified (90-day notice)
    Month 4: New prices take effect for everyone
    Risk: High; no learning period; full base impacted
    Use only when: Strong evidence and business urgency
```

## 3. Communication Strategy

### Communication Principles

```
PRICE INCREASE COMMUNICATION PRINCIPLES:

  1. LEAD WITH VALUE, NOT PRICE
     Start with what the customer has gained, not what they'll pay
     "Over the past year, we've added [features], improved [performance],
     and expanded [capabilities]..."

  2. BE DIRECT AND HONEST
     State the increase clearly with specific numbers
     Never bury the increase in vague language
     "Your plan will increase from $49/month to $59/month"

  3. JUSTIFY WITH SPECIFICS
     Generic "to continue providing excellent service" is weak
     Specific "we've added AI-powered search, 3x faster exports,
     and 24/5 chat support" is strong

  4. GIVE AMPLE NOTICE
     60-90 days minimum; earlier for enterprise
     Show respect for the customer's planning process

  5. PROVIDE OPTIONS
     Offer ways to mitigate: annual commitment, downgrade, lock in
     Never make it feel like "accept or leave"

  6. PERSONAL TOUCH FOR HIGH-VALUE
     Top 20% of accounts by revenue get personal outreach
     Email from account manager or executive, not marketing blast
```

### Communication Sequence

```
COMMUNICATION CADENCE:

  T-90 DAYS: INITIAL NOTIFICATION
    Channel: Email (personal from account lead for top accounts)
    Content: Full announcement with rationale, specifics, and options
    Tone: Respectful, value-focused, transparent

  T-60 DAYS: FAQ AND OPTIONS REMINDER
    Channel: Email + in-app notification
    Content: FAQ addressing common questions; reminder of options
    Include: Link to FAQ page, support contact

  T-30 DAYS: FINAL REMINDER
    Channel: Email + in-app banner
    Content: "Your plan changes in 30 days. Here are your options."
    Include: One-click annual conversion, downgrade option

  T-7 DAYS: LAST CHANCE
    Channel: Email + in-app
    Content: "Last week to lock in annual pricing / make changes"

  T-0: PRICE TAKES EFFECT
    Channel: Email confirmation
    Content: "Your plan has been updated to [$New/month]"
    Include: Summary of what's included, support contact

  T+30 DAYS: CHECK-IN
    Channel: Email (top accounts: personal call)
    Content: "How are things going? Any questions about the change?"
    Purpose: Catch lingering issues before they become churn
```

## 4. Grandfathering Approaches

### Grandfathering Options

| Approach | Description | Revenue Impact | Customer Sentiment |
|----------|------------|---------------|-------------------|
| **No grandfather** | All customers move to new price | Highest revenue | Highest churn risk |
| **Time-limited** | Old price for 6-12 months, then new price | Moderate; delayed | Good; gives adjustment time |
| **Partial increase** | Existing customers get smaller increase | Moderate | Good; feels fair |
| **Permanent grandfather** | Existing customers keep old price forever | Lowest | Best; but revenue limited |
| **Feature-based** | Old price but new features only at new price | Moderate | Good; value-clear |

### Grandfathering Decision Framework

```
DECISION MATRIX:

  If churn risk is HIGH (competitive market, low switching costs):
    → Permanent or long-term grandfather + lock-in incentives

  If churn risk is MODERATE:
    → Time-limited grandfather (6-12 months) or partial increase

  If churn risk is LOW (high switching costs, strong lock-in):
    → No grandfather or short-term grandfather (3-6 months)

  If customer segment is ENTERPRISE:
    → Honor contract terms; increase at renewal
    → Offer multi-year lock-in at current rate

  If customer segment is SMB/CONSUMER:
    → Partial increase or time-limited grandfather
    → Annual commitment at current rate as option

ANTI-PATTERN:
  Permanent grandfather for all creates "legacy pricing" debt
  that compounds over years. Eventually, new customers pay 2-3x
  what old customers pay for the same product. This is unsustainable
  and feels unfair to new customers.
```

## 5. Churn Mitigation

### Pre-Increase Churn Prevention

```
BEFORE ANNOUNCING:

  1. IDENTIFY AT-RISK ACCOUNTS
     - Low engagement / usage
     - Recent complaints or low CSAT
     - Competitive evaluation signals
     - Contract renewal within 90 days of price change

  2. PROACTIVE OUTREACH TO AT-RISK
     - Personal call from account manager
     - Value reinforcement: "Here's what you've accomplished with us"
     - Address any outstanding issues BEFORE price increase
     - Offer incentives: lock-in, extended trial of premium features

  3. PREPARE RETENTION OFFERS
     Tier 1 (standard): Annual lock-in at current rate
     Tier 2 (at-risk): 6-month extension at current rate
     Tier 3 (high-value at-risk): Custom negotiation
```

### Post-Increase Churn Management

```
MONITORING (first 90 days after increase):

  DAILY:
    - Cancellation rate vs. baseline
    - Downgrade rate vs. baseline
    - Support ticket volume about pricing
    - Social media sentiment

  WEEKLY:
    - Churn by customer segment
    - Churn by price increase magnitude
    - Retention offer acceptance rates
    - Competitive mention analysis

  RESPONSE THRESHOLDS:
    Churn increase <5%:  Normal; continue monitoring
    Churn increase 5-10%: Concerning; increase retention outreach
    Churn increase 10-20%: Critical; activate retention team
    Churn increase >20%: Emergency; consider rollback or adjustment

SAVE PLAYS:

  Customer says "I'm going to cancel":

    STEP 1: Understand why
      "I want to understand your situation. Is this about the
       price itself, or is there something else going on?"

    STEP 2: Demonstrate value
      "Let me share what you've accomplished with us:
       [specific metrics, outcomes, time savings]"

    STEP 3: Offer alternatives
      "Let me see what options we have:
       - Annual commitment at [old price or reduced increase]
       - Downgrade to [lower plan] to reduce cost
       - [Custom offer based on authority]"

    STEP 4: If customer insists
      "I understand. We'll process your request. Your account will
       remain active until [date]. If anything changes, we'd love
       to have you back."
      Note: Do NOT beg. Maintain dignity. Some churn is acceptable.
```

## 6. Competitive Response Planning

### Anticipating Competitive Reaction

```
COMPETITIVE SCENARIOS:

  SCENARIO 1: COMPETITOR DOES NOTHING
    Most likely if your increase is <15% and you're already competitive
    Action: No change to plan

  SCENARIO 2: COMPETITOR LOWERS PRICES
    Possible if competitor sees opportunity to poach
    Action: Do NOT match. Compete on value, not price.
    Response: "We've invested in [improvements] that justify our pricing.
    Here's the ROI you're getting: [data]"

  SCENARIO 3: COMPETITOR RUNS TARGETED WIN-BACK CAMPAIGNS
    Common if competitor is aggressive
    Action: Prepare competitive battle cards for sales/support
    Response: Proactive outreach to at-risk accounts with value data

  SCENARIO 4: COMPETITOR ALSO RAISES PRICES
    Possible in mature markets with multiple price increases
    Action: This validates your decision. Communicate accordingly.

COMPETITIVE MONITORING:
  - Check competitor pricing pages weekly for 90 days post-increase
  - Monitor G2/Capterra reviews for competitor mentions
  - Track lost deals attributed to pricing in CRM
  - Alert sales team to any competitive pricing changes immediately
```

## 7. Price Decrease Strategy

### When to Lower Prices

```
PRICE DECREASE INDICATORS:

  - Conversion rate significantly below benchmarks
  - Win/loss analysis shows price as top loss reason (>30%)
  - WTP research shows current price above acceptable range
  - New competitor offering comparable product at much lower price
  - Market is commoditizing and differentiation is decreasing
  - Penetration strategy requires lower price for market share
  - Free tier is insufficient for PLG motion

PRICE DECREASE RISKS:
  - Signals weakness to market
  - Reduces perceived quality (price-quality inference)
  - Revenue per customer drops immediately
  - Difficult to raise prices again later
  - Existing customers may feel they overpaid

MITIGATION:
  - Frame as "new plan" rather than "price cut"
  - Add the lower price as a new tier (below existing)
  - Bundle with reduced features to protect higher tier
  - Position as strategic (penetration) not reactive (desperation)
```
