# Email Copywriting — Subject Lines, Body Copy, and Persuasion

## 1. Subject Line Formulas (50+ Proven Frameworks)

The subject line determines whether your email lives or dies. It is the most scrutinized, most tested, and most impactful single line of copy in all of marketing. Below are proven frameworks organized by psychological mechanism.

### Curiosity-Based Subject Lines

1. **The Information Gap**: "The one thing most [audience] get wrong about [topic]"
2. **The Reveal**: "We analyzed [large number] [things]. Here's what we found"
3. **The Secret**: "The hidden [feature/strategy] that [benefit]"
4. **The Question**: "Are you making this [topic] mistake?"
5. **The Counter-Intuitive**: "Why [common belief] is actually wrong"
6. **The Incomplete**: "This changed everything about how we [action]..."
7. **The List Tease**: "7 [things] that [outcome] (number 4 is surprising)"
8. **The Behind-the-Scenes**: "How we actually [achieved result]"

### Urgency-Based Subject Lines

9. **The Deadline**: "[Offer] ends at midnight"
10. **The Countdown**: "Only [X] hours left"
11. **The Scarcity**: "Only [X] spots remaining"
12. **The Last Chance**: "Final call: [offer description]"
13. **The Now-or-Never**: "This won't be available again until [future date]"
14. **The Early Access**: "You get first access (24 hours before everyone else)"

### Personalization-Based Subject Lines

15. **The Name Drop**: "[First name], this is for you"
16. **The Location**: "Best [things] in [city/region]"
17. **The Behavior Reference**: "Based on your recent [action]"
18. **The Milestone**: "Congrats on [achievement], [name]"
19. **The Recommendation**: "Because you liked [previous product/content]"
20. **The History**: "Your [timeframe] in review"

### Value-Based Subject Lines

21. **The How-To**: "How to [achieve desired outcome] in [timeframe]"
22. **The Template**: "The [thing] template that saves [time/money]"
23. **The Guide**: "The complete guide to [topic]"
24. **The Shortcut**: "The fastest way to [achieve goal]"
25. **The Resource**: "[Number] [resources] for [specific audience]"
26. **The Tool**: "A free tool to [solve problem]"

### Social Proof Subject Lines

27. **The Testimonial**: "How [customer] achieved [result]"
28. **The Number**: "Join [X,000] [people] who [action]"
29. **The Celebrity/Expert**: "[Respected name] recommends [thing]"
30. **The Trend**: "Why everyone is switching to [thing]"
31. **The Bestseller**: "Our most popular [product/content] ever"

### Pattern Interrupt Subject Lines

32. **The Single Word**: "Wow" / "Finally" / "Oops"
33. **The Emoji Break**: Using a single, unexpected emoji in a B2B context
34. **The Lowercase**: "a quick note about your account"
35. **The Confession**: "We messed up (and here's what we're doing about it)"
36. **The Personal**: "Can I ask you something?"
37. **The Unexpected Sender**: Email from CEO or founder (not the usual sender)
38. **The Negative**: "Don't open this email"

### Benefit-Driven Subject Lines

39. **The Result**: "[Outcome] in [timeframe] — here's how"
40. **The Savings**: "Save [X]% on [thing] this week"
41. **The Problem-Solution**: "Tired of [problem]? Try this"
42. **The Before-After**: "From [bad state] to [good state]"
43. **The Effortless**: "[Achieve goal] without [common pain point]"

### Emotional Subject Lines

44. **The Nostalgia**: "Remember when [shared experience]?"
45. **The Aspiration**: "Imagine [ideal future state]"
46. **The Empathy**: "We know [frustration] is hard"
47. **The Celebration**: "You earned this, [name]"
48. **The Gratitude**: "Thank you (we mean it)"
49. **The Humor**: "We put a [unexpected thing] in your [thing]"
50. **The Story**: "[Character] had a problem. Here's what happened next"

### Advanced Formulas

51. **The Re-engagement**: "It's been [timeframe]. We [action] for you"
52. **The Comparison**: "[Product A] vs [Product B]: Which is right for you?"
53. **The Update**: "What's new in [month/quarter] at [brand]"
54. **The Invitation**: "You're invited: [event/opportunity]"
55. **The Warning**: "Heads up: [important change]"

## 2. Preview Text Optimization

Preview text (preheader) is the second line of copy visible in the inbox. It complements the subject line and can increase open rates by 5-15% when optimized.

### Preview Text Strategies

**Complementary**: Extends the subject line with additional context
- Subject: "Your monthly report is ready"
- Preview: "Revenue up 23%, plus 3 insights you shouldn't miss"

**Contrasting**: Creates tension with the subject line
- Subject: "Bad news about your account"
- Preview: "Just kidding. Your account has never looked better."

**CTA Preview**: Reveals the action available inside
- Subject: "New features just launched"
- Preview: "Try them free for 14 days, no credit card required"

**Social Proof**: Adds credibility to the subject line promise
- Subject: "The email template that converts"
- Preview: "Used by 10,000+ marketers. Average 3x conversion lift."

### Preview Text Technical Notes

- **Length**: 40-130 characters visible (varies by client and device)
- **Mobile**: Shows more preview text than desktop (larger % of screen)
- **Default behavior**: If no preview text is set, the first line of body copy is used
- **Hiding hack**: Use `&zwnj;&nbsp;` repeated after your preview text to prevent body copy from appearing
- **Testing**: Always check preview text rendering in Gmail, Apple Mail, and Outlook

## 3. Body Copy Structure

### The Hook-Value-CTA Framework

Every email body should follow this three-part structure:

**The Hook (First 1-3 lines)**
The hook must accomplish one thing: keep the reader reading. It should NOT summarize the email — it should create enough intrigue or relevance to earn the next paragraph.

Effective hook patterns:
- **The Bold Claim**: "Most email marketing advice is wrong. Here's why."
- **The Personal Story**: "Last Tuesday, I made a mistake that cost us 10,000 subscribers."
- **The Data Point**: "Only 21% of marketing emails are ever opened."
- **The Direct Address**: "If you've ever stared at a blank email draft for 30 minutes, this is for you."
- **The Question**: "What would you do with 2 extra hours every week?"

**The Value Section (Middle)**
This is the substance of the email. Structure depends on content type:

For educational emails:
- Problem statement → Solution framework → Specific steps → Example

For promotional emails:
- Offer statement → Benefits (not features) → Social proof → Risk reversal

For storytelling emails:
- Setup → Conflict → Resolution → Lesson → Application

**The CTA (Final section)**
Clear, specific, and motivated. The CTA should feel like the natural next step, not an interruption.

### The Hemingway Principle for Email

Ernest Hemingway's prose style — short sentences, simple words, powerful imagery — is the gold standard for email copy.

**Hemingway rules for email**:
1. **Short sentences**: Average 10-15 words. Maximum 25 words.
2. **Short paragraphs**: 1-3 sentences maximum. Single-sentence paragraphs are powerful.
3. **Common words**: Write at an 8th-grade reading level (Flesch-Kincaid score 60-70)
4. **Active voice**: "We launched the feature" not "The feature was launched by us"
5. **Concrete nouns**: "47 customers" not "many customers"
6. **Strong verbs**: "Cut your costs" not "Help facilitate a reduction in your expenditures"
7. **Remove adverbs**: "Sales doubled" not "Sales increased dramatically"

### Reading Level Optimization

Research from Boomerang (analysis of 40M+ emails):
- Emails written at a 3rd-grade reading level received the highest response rates
- Response rates declined steadily as reading level increased
- This applies even to sophisticated B2B audiences

The reason: ease of processing (cognitive fluency) correlates with positive evaluation and action. Complex writing does not signal expertise — it signals poor communication.

## 4. Storytelling in Email

### The Story Structure for Email

Adapted from classic narrative structure for the email format:

1. **Character** (the reader or someone relatable): "Sarah was drowning in spreadsheets"
2. **Situation** (relatable context): "Her team had grown from 3 to 30 in a year"
3. **Conflict** (the problem): "But their processes hadn't scaled. Mistakes were everywhere."
4. **Turning point** (discovery): "Then she found a better way to manage it all"
5. **Resolution** (the outcome): "Within 2 weeks, errors dropped 90%"
6. **Moral** (the lesson): "The right tool doesn't just save time — it saves sanity"
7. **Bridge to CTA**: "Want to see if it works for you?"

### Why Stories Work in Email

- **Neural coupling**: Stories activate the same brain regions in the reader as the teller experienced
- **Oxytocin release**: Character-driven narratives trigger empathy hormones
- **Transportation**: Engaged readers lower their critical defenses
- **Memory**: Stories are 22x more memorable than facts alone (Stanford research)

### Story Types for Different Email Goals

| Email Goal | Story Type | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Build trust | Origin story | How the company/product was born |
| Drive conversion | Customer success | How a customer achieved a result |
| Educate | Teaching story | Lesson learned from experience |
| Re-engage | Nostalgia/progress | How far the subscriber has come |
| Build community | Shared experience | "We all face this challenge" |

## 5. Tone Modulation by Email Type

### The Tone Spectrum

```
Casual ←──────────────────────────────────────────→ Formal
Fun    ←──────────────────────────────────────────→ Serious
Warm   ←──────────────────────────────────────────→ Professional
```

### Tone Guidelines by Email Type

| Email Type | Tone | Why |
|-----------|------|-----|
| Welcome | Warm, enthusiastic, personal | First impression; build emotional connection |
| Newsletter | Conversational, knowledgeable | Ongoing relationship; establish expertise |
| Promotional | Exciting, direct, confident | Drive action; convey value and urgency |
| Transactional | Clear, reassuring, professional | Reduce anxiety; convey reliability |
| Re-engagement | Warm, slightly vulnerable, generous | Rebuild connection; show you care |
| Win-back | Direct, honest, incentive-forward | Last chance; acknowledge the gap |
| Crisis/Apology | Humble, transparent, solution-focused | Rebuild trust; show accountability |
| Celebration | Joyful, generous, congratulatory | Share positivity; deepen bond |

## 6. Personalization Tokens

### Beyond First Name

| Token | Source | Example Usage |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| `{first_name}` | Profile | "Hi {first_name}, here's your weekly digest" |
| `{company}` | Profile/CRM | "How {company} can benefit from..." |
| `{city}` | Profile/IP | "Events near {city} this month" |
| `{last_product_viewed}` | Behavior | "Still thinking about {last_product_viewed}?" |
| `{days_since_signup}` | Calculated | "It's been {days_since_signup} days — here's your progress" |
| `{loyalty_points}` | Account | "You have {loyalty_points} points. Here's what you can get" |
| `{last_purchase}` | Transaction | "How's your {last_purchase} working out?" |
| `{recommended_product}` | Algorithm | "Based on your history, you'll love {recommended_product}" |

### Fallback Values

Always set fallback values for personalization tokens:
- `{first_name|"there"}` → "Hi there" if first name is missing
- `{company|"your company"}` → "How your company can benefit..."
- Never display raw merge tags to subscribers

## 7. Dynamic Content Blocks

Dynamic content displays different content to different subscribers within the same email send. It is more efficient than creating multiple email versions.

### Dynamic Content Applications

**Product recommendations**: Show products based on browsing history, purchase history, or predicted interest.

**Content recommendations**: Display articles, guides, or resources matching subscriber interests.

**Location-based content**: Show local events, store locations, or region-specific offers.

**Lifecycle-stage content**: New subscribers see onboarding tips while veterans see advanced features.

**Weather-based content**: Outdoor and retail brands adjust messaging based on local weather conditions.

### Implementation Approaches

1. **ESP-native dynamic content**: Most modern ESPs support if/then content blocks
2. **API-driven content**: Pull recommendations from external engine at send time
3. **AMP for Email**: Interactive, real-time content within Gmail (limited adoption)
4. **Live content**: Images and content that update at open time (Movable Ink, Liveclicker)

## 9. Writing for Mobile

Over 60% of emails are first opened on mobile devices. Mobile-first copywriting is not optional.

### Mobile Copy Rules

1. **Subject lines**: Keep under 40 characters for full mobile visibility
2. **Preview text**: First 50 characters are most visible on mobile
3. **First line**: Must be compelling — mobile shows less above the fold
4. **Paragraph length**: 1-2 sentences maximum on mobile
5. **CTA size**: Buttons minimum 44x44 pixels (Apple's tap target guideline)
6. **CTA placement**: Within first scroll on mobile (above ~300px)
7. **Link spacing**: At least 10px between tappable elements
8. **Font size**: Minimum 14px for body copy, 22px for headlines on mobile

## 10. Copy Testing and Optimization

### What to Test in Email Copy

| Element | Test Approach | Impact Potential |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|
| Subject line | A/B test two variants per send | High (10-30% lift) |
| Preview text | A/B test alongside subject line | Medium (5-15% lift) |
| Sender name | Test company vs person name | Medium (5-20% lift) |
| CTA copy | Test action verbs and value propositions | Medium (5-25% lift) |
| Email length | Short vs long for same message | Variable |
| Tone | Formal vs casual for same audience | Variable |
| Personalization depth | Name only vs behavioral vs predictive | Medium-High |

### Copy Testing Process

1. **Hypothesis**: "Curiosity-based subject lines will outperform benefit-based for our newsletter"
2. **Variants**: Create two subject lines, one per approach
3. **Sample**: Send to 20% of list (10% per variant)
4. **Duration**: Wait 2-4 hours for subject line tests
5. **Winner**: Send winning variant to remaining 80%
6. **Document**: Log hypothesis, variants, results, and learning
7. **Iterate**: Build on winning patterns, abandon losing ones
