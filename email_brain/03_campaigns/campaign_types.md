# Campaign Types — Email Campaign Taxonomy and Planning

## 1. Campaign Type Taxonomy

Email campaigns fall into distinct categories, each serving different objectives, following different design patterns, and measured by different KPIs. Understanding the taxonomy enables strategic campaign mix planning.

### Overview Matrix

| Campaign Type | Primary Goal | Frequency | Key Metric | Personalization Level |
|---------------|-------------|-----------|------------|----------------------|
| Promotional | Revenue | 2-4x/month | Revenue per email | High |
| Content Newsletter | Engagement | 1-4x/month | Click rate, read time | Medium |
| Event-Driven | Attendance/Action | As needed | Registration rate | Medium |
| Seasonal | Revenue + Brand | Calendar-driven | Revenue, engagement | Medium-High |
| Announcement | Awareness | As needed | Open rate, sentiment | Low-Medium |
| Win-Back | Re-engagement | Triggered | Reactivation rate | High |
| Milestone | Retention | Triggered | Engagement, sentiment | Very High |

## 2. Promotional Campaigns

Promotional campaigns drive immediate revenue through offers, launches, and incentives. They are the most common and most scrutinized campaign type.

### Promotional Campaign Subtypes

**Flash Sales**: Time-limited offers (24-72 hours) leveraging urgency and scarcity.
- Structure: Announcement email, reminder at midpoint, final hours email
- Psychology: Loss aversion (Kahneman), temporal scarcity
- Typical lift: 2-5x normal conversion rate
- Risk: Overuse trains subscribers to wait for sales

**Product Launch**: Introducing new products or features to the subscriber base.
- Structure: Teaser (7 days before), launch announcement, follow-up with reviews/social proof
- Psychology: Novelty, exclusivity, anticipation building
- Key element: Segmentation — different messages for prospects vs existing customers

**Clearance/End-of-Season**: Moving inventory with deeper discounts.
- Structure: Initial announcement, progressively deeper discounts, final call
- Psychology: Increasing urgency with genuine scarcity
- Segmentation: Target previous buyers of similar categories

**Bundle/Upsell Promotions**: Increasing average order value.
- Structure: Recommendation email based on purchase history
- Psychology: Anchoring (bundle price vs individual prices), complementary framing
- Personalization: Product recommendation engine integration

### Promotional Campaign Best Practices

1. **Exclusivity framing**: "For subscribers only" outperforms generic promotions
2. **Genuine scarcity**: Real deadlines, real inventory limits (never fake urgency)
3. **Clear savings math**: Show original price, discount, and final price explicitly
4. **Single product focus**: Featuring 1-3 products outperforms catalog-style emails
5. **Segmented offers**: Different discounts for different customer tiers
6. **Post-promotion follow-up**: Thank you + related product recommendations

## 3. Content Newsletters

Content newsletters build relationships, establish authority, and maintain mindshare between promotional pushes. They are the trust-building engine of the email program.

### Newsletter Formats

**Curated Newsletter**: Aggregating the best content from your blog, industry, or both.
- Example: Morning Brew, The Hustle, TLDR Newsletter
- Value proposition: Save subscribers time by curating what matters
- Structure: 5-10 items with brief summaries and links

**Original Content Newsletter**: Publishing unique insights, analysis, or stories.
- Example: Stratechery, Lenny's Newsletter, The Generalist
- Value proposition: Exclusive expertise not available elsewhere
- Structure: Long-form essay or multiple original short sections

**Hybrid Newsletter**: Combination of original commentary and curated links.
- Example: Most corporate newsletters
- Structure: 1-2 original pieces + 3-5 curated items + 1 CTA

**Product Update Newsletter**: Regular updates on product changes and improvements.
- Example: Changelog emails, release notes
- Structure: Feature highlights with screenshots/GIFs, tips, upcoming previews
- Audience: Existing customers, not prospects

### Newsletter Design Principles

1. **Consistent structure**: Readers should know exactly what to expect in every issue
2. **Scannable format**: Bold headlines, short paragraphs, clear section breaks
3. **Personality**: Newsletters succeed when they have a distinct editorial voice
4. **Value-first**: Every newsletter should deliver value independent of any commercial intent
5. **Social sharing**: Make individual sections shareable
6. **Feedback loop**: Include reply prompt or survey to understand audience preferences

### Newsletter Metrics Beyond Open Rate

- **Click-to-open rate (CTOR)**: Clicks / unique opens — measures content relevance
- **Read rate**: Time spent reading (measured by scroll tracking or pixel timing)
- **Forward/share rate**: Indicates content resonance and growth potential
- **Reply rate**: Measures connection and engagement depth
- **Subscriber growth from newsletter**: Viral coefficient of content

## 4. Event-Driven Campaigns

Event-driven emails promote and support specific events — webinars, conferences, product launches, community events.

### Event Email Sequence

| Timing | Email | Goal | Content |
|--------|-------|------|---------|
| 4 weeks before | Save the date | Awareness | Date, topic, speakers, early-bird |
| 2 weeks before | Registration push | Registration | Full details, social proof, scarcity |
| 1 week before | Last chance | Urgency | Countdown, testimonials from past events |
| 1 day before | Reminder | Attendance | Logistics, how to join, what to prepare |
| Day of | Go-time | Attendance | Link to join, quick summary, excitement |
| 1 day after | Follow-up | Engagement | Recording, slides, key takeaways |
| 1 week after | Action | Conversion | Related offer, next event, feedback survey |

### Event Campaign Optimization

- **Segmentation**: Invite only relevant segments (topic-interest match)
- **Social proof**: "500 people already registered" or "Featuring [notable speaker]"
- **FOMO elements**: Limited seats, early-bird pricing, exclusive access
- **Calendar integration**: Include .ics file or "Add to Calendar" link
- **Reminder cadence**: 3 reminders (1 week, 1 day, 1 hour) reduces no-show by 30-40%

## 5. Seasonal Campaigns

Seasonal campaigns align with calendar events, holidays, and cultural moments. They capitalize on existing consumer behavior patterns and emotional readiness.

### Seasonal Campaign Calendar (E-Commerce Focus)

| Season | Key Dates | Campaign Theme | Lead Time |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| Q1 | New Year, Valentine's Day, Presidents' Day | Resolution, gifting, clearance | 4-6 weeks |
| Q2 | Easter, Mother's Day, Memorial Day | Spring refresh, gifting, outdoor | 4-6 weeks |
| Q3 | Back-to-School, Labor Day | Preparation, transitions | 6-8 weeks |
| Q4 | Halloween, Black Friday/Cyber Monday, Holiday | Major revenue season | 8-12 weeks |

### Black Friday/Cyber Monday Email Strategy

The highest-volume email period of the year requires special planning:

**Pre-BFCM (October)**:
- Warm up sending volume gradually (avoid sudden spikes that damage reputation)
- Build anticipation with "coming soon" teasers
- Segment VIP early-access lists
- Test email templates and deliverability

**BFCM Week**:
- Monday: Week preview and early access for VIPs
- Wednesday: Full sale announcement
- Thursday (Thanksgiving): Gratitude email + midnight launch
- Friday: Black Friday main event (2-3 emails)
- Saturday: Extended deals / surprise additions
- Sunday: Transition to Cyber Monday preview
- Monday: Cyber Monday (2-3 emails)
- Tuesday: Final hours / extended by popular demand

**Post-BFCM**:
- Order confirmation and shipping communication excellence
- Gift guide for remaining holiday shopping
- Year-end clearance

### Seasonal Campaign Pitfalls

1. **Inbox fatigue**: Everyone increases volume during holidays — standout through quality, not quantity
2. **Early start, early burnout**: Starting holiday campaigns in September trains subscribers to ignore them
3. **One-size-fits-all**: Not all subscribers celebrate the same holidays — segment appropriately
4. **Deliverability risk**: Volume spikes without proper IP warming can trigger ISP throttling

## 6. Announcement Campaigns

Announcement campaigns communicate important news: new features, policy changes, brand evolution, company milestones.

### Announcement Types and Approaches

**Product/Feature Announcements**:
- Segment by relevance (don't announce enterprise features to free users)
- Show, don't tell (screenshots, GIFs, videos)
- Include clear "try it now" CTA
- Follow up with usage tips email

**Company News/Milestones**:
- Humanize with founder/CEO voice
- Share the "why" behind the milestone
- Connect milestone to subscriber value
- Keep it concise — subscribers care about impact on them

**Policy/Terms Changes**:
- Lead with "what's changing for you"
- Plain language (no legal jargon in the email — link to full terms)
- Explain the benefit or reason for the change
- Provide timeline and action required
- Compliance note: Some policy changes require explicit acknowledgment

**Crisis/Service Disruption Communications**:
- Send immediately when issue is confirmed
- Be transparent about what happened and what you are doing
- Provide status update timeline
- Follow up when resolved with post-mortem summary

## 7. Win-Back Campaigns

Win-back campaigns target subscribers or customers who have become inactive. They are trigger-based but often treated as campaigns due to their multi-email structure.

### Win-Back Campaign Framework

**Email 1 — The Gentle Reminder** (Day 0 of inactivity trigger)
- Tone: Friendly, non-aggressive
- Content: "We noticed you haven't visited in a while. Here's what's new."
- No offer yet — pure value delivery

**Email 2 — The Value Proposition** (Day 7)
- Tone: Helpful
- Content: "Here's why [X] subscribers love [product/content]"
- Social proof: Testimonials, usage stats, ratings
- Soft offer: "Come back and get [free content/feature/trial extension]"

**Email 3 — The Incentive** (Day 14)
- Tone: Direct
- Content: "We want you back. Here's an exclusive offer."
- Hard offer: Discount, free month, bonus content
- Urgency: Time-limited offer (7 days)

**Email 4 — The Breakup** (Day 28)
- Tone: Respectful, final
- Content: "We'll stop emailing if we don't hear from you"
- Final CTA: "Click here to stay subscribed"
- Clear statement: No action = unsubscription

### Win-Back Metrics

- **Reactivation rate**: % of win-back recipients who take any action (benchmark: 5-15%)
- **Revenue recovery**: Revenue generated from reactivated subscribers (within 30 days)
- **Cost efficiency**: Cost of win-back offer vs lifetime value of recovered subscriber
- **List impact**: Net list reduction from those who do not respond (this is positive for health)

## 8. Milestone Campaigns

Milestone campaigns celebrate subscriber or customer achievements and anniversaries. They are among the highest-performing email types because they are inherently personal.

### Milestone Types

- **Anniversary**: Subscriber signup anniversary, customer anniversary
- **Usage milestone**: 100th login, 1000th action, feature achievement
- **Purchase milestone**: 10th purchase, loyalty tier achievement
- **Birthday**: Personal celebration (requires date collection)
- **Shared milestone**: Company anniversary shared with subscribers

### Milestone Email Best Practices

1. **Make it about them**: "You've accomplished X" not "We've been around X years"
2. **Include data**: "You've read 47 newsletters this year" — personal data delights
3. **Offer a reward**: Discount, exclusive content, loyalty points
4. **Enable sharing**: Social sharing buttons for achievements
5. **Personalize deeply**: Include their name, specific achievements, preferences

## 9. Campaign Planning Process

### Step 1: Define Campaign Objective
- What business goal does this campaign serve?
- What action do we want subscribers to take?
- How will we measure success?

### Step 2: Identify Target Audience
- Which segments should receive this campaign?
- Are there segments that should be excluded?
- What do we know about this audience's preferences?

### Step 3: Design Campaign Structure
- Single send or multi-email series?
- What is the narrative arc?
- What content, creative, and copy are needed?

### Step 4: Create Content
- Write copy aligned with campaign objective and audience
- Design email template (mobile-first)
- Create supporting assets (images, GIFs, landing pages)
- Write A/B test variants

### Step 5: Configure Send
- Set up segmentation and exclusions
- Configure A/B test parameters
- Set send time (or send-time optimization)
- Configure tracking (UTM parameters, conversion tracking)

### Step 6: Quality Assurance
- Proof copy for errors
- Test rendering across email clients
- Verify all links
- Check personalization tokens
- Send test emails to team
- Review on mobile devices

### Step 7: Approve and Send
- Get required approvals (marketing lead, legal if needed)
- Schedule or send
- Monitor delivery in real-time for the first 30 minutes

### Step 8: Analyze and Report
- 24-hour preliminary results
- 72-hour full results
- Campaign report with insights and learnings
- Apply learnings to next campaign

## 10. Multi-Touch Campaign Design

Complex campaigns span multiple emails, multiple days, and sometimes multiple channels. Designing multi-touch campaigns requires careful sequencing.

### Multi-Touch Principles

1. **Narrative progression**: Each email builds on the previous one
2. **Non-repetition**: Don't say the same thing twice — add new information
3. **Escalating urgency**: Each touch increases urgency or sweetens the offer
4. **Behavioral branching**: Adjust subsequent emails based on previous engagement
5. **Respectful frequency**: Multi-touch does not mean daily bombardment

### Behavioral Branching Example

```
Email 1: Product Announcement
├── Opened + Clicked → Email 2A: Deep Dive + Demo CTA
│   ├── Clicked Demo → Email 3A: Post-Demo Follow-Up
│   └── No Click → Email 3B: Case Study + Social Proof
├── Opened, No Click → Email 2B: Different Angle + Testimonial
│   └── Any Click → Email 3A: Post-Demo Follow-Up
└── No Open → Email 2C: New Subject Line + FOMO Element
    └── Any Open → Email 3B: Case Study + Social Proof
    └── No Open → End (Do Not Send More)
```

## 11. A/B Testing in Campaigns

### What to Test (Priority Order)

1. **Subject line**: Highest impact, easiest to test
2. **Send time/day**: Significant impact, easy to test
3. **Sender name**: Moderate impact, easy to test
4. **CTA copy and design**: Moderate impact, requires variant creation
5. **Content structure**: Lower individual impact, requires more effort
6. **Personalization level**: Variable impact, requires data infrastructure

### Testing Methodology

- **Sample size**: Minimum 1,000 per variant for statistical significance
- **Test duration**: 2-4 hours for subject line tests, 24 hours for engagement tests
- **Confidence level**: Target 95% statistical significance
- **One variable**: Test one element at a time (unless multivariate testing)
- **Document everything**: Maintain a test log with hypothesis, results, and learnings

## 12. Campaign Calendar Management

### Calendar Structure

- **Annual planning**: Seasonal campaigns, major launches, tentpole events
- **Quarterly planning**: Campaign themes, content pillars, testing roadmap
- **Monthly planning**: Specific campaign briefs, content creation, design
- **Weekly planning**: Send scheduling, A/B test setup, QA

### Calendar Coordination Rules

1. **Maximum send frequency**: No more than [X] emails per subscriber per week
2. **Campaign stacking**: No two campaigns to the same segment within 48 hours
3. **Automation priority**: Triggered automations take priority over scheduled campaigns
4. **Suppression management**: Subscribers in active automation sequences may be suppressed from campaigns
5. **Channel coordination**: Align email sends with SMS, push, and paid media calendars

### Calendar Tools

- Spreadsheet-based: Google Sheets/Excel for simple programs (< 10 sends/month)
- Project management: Asana, Monday, Notion for medium programs
- Dedicated tools: CoSchedule, Litmus calendar for complex programs
- ESP-native: Most ESPs include basic calendar functionality
