# Community Brain — Glossary

---

## Purpose

This glossary defines the precise meaning of terms used throughout the Community Brain. When a term appears in any module, pattern, or template, its meaning is defined here. Ambiguity in community terminology leads to misaligned strategy; precision in language enables precision in execution.

---

## Community Types

### Product Community
A community organized around a specific product or service. Members share the common experience of using the product. Examples: Figma community, Salesforce Trailblazers, Notion community. Per Spinks (2021), product communities drive support deflection, product feedback, and customer retention.

### Community of Practice (CoP)
A community organized around a shared professional discipline or craft. Members learn from each other through shared practice. Coined by Etienne Wenger and Jean Lave (1991). Examples: DevOps communities, UX research communities, data science communities. Members share domain knowledge, not product usage.

### Community of Interest
A community organized around a shared interest, identity, or cause that is not tied to a specific product or profession. Examples: running communities, book clubs, sustainability communities. Motivation is intrinsic interest rather than professional development or product support.

### Hybrid Community
A community that blends elements of product, practice, and interest community types. Most real-world communities are hybrids. The classification helps identify primary and secondary motivations for community participation.

## Community Lifecycle (Millington Model)

### Inception Stage
The earliest phase of community development. Fewer than 50 active members. The community manager does most of the work: creating content, initiating discussions, personally welcoming every member. Focus is on establishing initial activity patterns and recruiting founding members. Per Millington (2012), this stage requires 80% direct outreach and 20% content creation.

### Establishment Stage
The community has achieved critical mass (typically 50-300 active members). Regular activity occurs without community manager initiation. Norms and culture are solidifying. Sub-groups may begin forming. Focus shifts from creating activity to facilitating and guiding emergent activity.

### Maturity Stage
The community is self-sustaining with strong norms, active member leaders, and consistent activity patterns. The community manager's role shifts to strategic oversight, program management, and handling edge cases. Typically 300+ active members with organic growth momentum.

### Mitosis Stage
The community has grown large enough that it naturally fragments into sub-communities. Per Millington, this is healthy and should be facilitated rather than resisted. Sub-communities develop their own leaders, norms, and identity while maintaining connection to the parent community.

## SPACES Model (Spinks)

The SPACES model, defined by David Spinks in *The Business of Belonging* (2021), categorizes the six types of business value that communities generate:

### Support (S)
Community members helping each other solve problems, reducing the load on official support channels. Measured through support deflection rate and peer-to-peer resolution time.

### Product (P)
Community providing product feedback, feature requests, bug reports, and beta testing. Measured through feedback volume, feature adoption from community suggestions, and product improvement velocity.

### Acquisition (A)
Community driving new customer or member acquisition through word-of-mouth, referrals, and content creation. Measured through referral rate, community-attributed signups, and organic content reach.

### Contribution (C)
Community members contributing content, code, translations, documentation, or other assets. Measured through contribution volume, contributor diversity, and contribution quality.

### Engagement (E)
Community increasing overall engagement, satisfaction, and emotional connection with the brand or organization. Measured through NPS lift, retention rate improvement, and engagement depth.

### Success (S)
Community helping members achieve their goals and become more successful with the product or in their domain. Measured through member outcome achievement, certification completion, and skill development.

## Engagement Terminology

### DAU (Daily Active Users)
The number of unique members who perform at least one meaningful action in the community within a 24-hour period. "Meaningful action" must be defined per community (e.g., posting, reacting, reading for >30 seconds).

### MAU (Monthly Active Users)
The number of unique members who perform at least one meaningful action in the community within a 30-day period.

### DAU/MAU Ratio (Stickiness)
The ratio of daily active users to monthly active users, expressed as a percentage. Indicates how frequently members return. A 30% DAU/MAU ratio means the average monthly active member visits roughly 9 days per month. Industry benchmark for healthy communities: 15-30%.

### Engagement Rate
The percentage of total community members who are active within a defined period. Formula: (Active Members / Total Members) x 100. Must specify the time period and definition of "active."

### Lurker
A community member who reads or observes community content but does not actively contribute through posts, comments, or reactions. Per the 90-9-1 rule (Nielsen, 2006), approximately 90% of community members are lurkers. Lurking is a valid form of participation and often precedes active contribution.

### Contributor
A community member who actively creates content, answers questions, or participates in discussions. Per the 90-9-1 rule, approximately 9% of community members are contributors.

### Super User / Power User
A community member who contributes disproportionately to community activity and value. Typically the top 1% of contributors. Per the 90-9-1 rule, they create approximately 90% of community content.

### Activation
The process of converting a new member from passive observer to active participant. Measured by the percentage of new members who complete a defined activation action within a specified time window (e.g., "posted within first 7 days").

### First Value Moment (FVM)
The point at which a new member first receives tangible value from the community. This could be getting a question answered, finding a useful resource, or making a meaningful connection. Optimizing time-to-FVM is critical for activation and retention.

### Engagement Ladder
A progression framework mapping the stages of member participation from lurker to leader. Typically: Lurker -> Reactor -> Contributor -> Regular -> Leader -> Ambassador. Each rung represents increased investment and community value.

### Engagement Loop
A self-reinforcing cycle where community activity generates triggers that prompt further activity. Per Eyal's Hook Model: Trigger -> Action -> Variable Reward -> Investment. Healthy engagement loops create habitual community participation.

## Moderation Terminology

### Community Guidelines
The explicit behavioral expectations and rules governing community participation. Distinct from Terms of Service (legal) and Code of Conduct (values-based). Guidelines should be specific, enforceable, and written in plain language.

### Moderation
The practice of reviewing and managing community content and behavior to ensure compliance with community guidelines and maintain a healthy environment. Can be proactive (preventing issues) or reactive (responding to violations).

### Content Moderation
Review and management of user-generated content (posts, images, links, files) for guideline compliance. Can be manual (human review), automated (AI/rules-based), or hybrid.

### Behavioral Moderation
Management of member behavior patterns (harassment, spam, trolling, bad faith participation) that may not violate specific content rules but harm community health.

### Escalation
The process of routing a moderation decision to a higher authority when it exceeds the current moderator's scope or authority. Escalation paths should be clearly defined with response time expectations.

### De-escalation
Techniques for reducing the emotional intensity of a conflict before it escalates further. Includes acknowledging feelings, moving conversations to private channels, and finding common ground.

### Trust Level
A progressive permission system where members earn increased capabilities based on participation history and demonstrated trustworthiness. Discourse pioneered this with Trust Levels 0-4. Reduces moderation load by giving established members more privileges.

## Growth Terminology

### Community-Led Growth (CLG)
A go-to-market strategy where community is the primary driver of acquisition, activation, and retention. Distinct from product-led growth (PLG) or sales-led growth. Per Spinks (2021), CLG works through the "Acquisition" component of the SPACES model.

### Viral Coefficient (K-Factor)
The average number of new members each existing member brings to the community. K > 1 indicates viral growth. Formula: K = (invitations per member) x (conversion rate of invitations). Most communities have K < 1 and supplement with other growth channels.

### Network Effect
The phenomenon where each additional community member increases the value of the community for all existing members. Direct network effects (more members = more answers) and indirect network effects (more members = more content = more search traffic = more members).

### Critical Mass
The minimum number of active members required for a community to sustain activity without constant community manager intervention. Varies by community type but generally falls between 50-150 active members (aligning with Dunbar's inner circles).

### Dunbar's Number
Robin Dunbar's cognitive limit on the number of stable social relationships an individual can maintain, approximately 150. Relevant to community design because sub-groups and channels should respect cognitive limits. Dunbar's layers: 5 (intimate), 15 (close), 50 (friends), 150 (meaningful contacts), 500 (acquaintances), 1500 (recognizable faces).

## Program Terminology

### Ambassador Program
A structured program that recognizes, trains, and empowers community members to represent the community or brand. Ambassadors typically receive benefits (access, swag, compensation) in exchange for defined contributions (content creation, event hosting, mentoring).

### Champion
An informal community advocate who promotes and supports the community without formal program membership. Champions are often identified for ambassador program recruitment.

### Moderator (Volunteer)
A community member granted moderation privileges to help maintain community health. Volunteer moderators require training, clear guidelines, and ongoing support. They are not employees and must not be treated as unpaid labor.

### Office Hours
Regularly scheduled live sessions where community members can ask questions of experts, leaders, or staff. Format borrowed from academia. Effective for deepening relationships and providing high-value synchronous engagement.

### AMA (Ask Me Anything)
A structured event format where a notable individual answers community questions in real-time or asynchronously. Originated on Reddit. Effective for attraction, engagement, and content generation.

## Metrics Terminology

### Community Health Score
A composite metric combining multiple community indicators into a single health assessment. Typically includes engagement rate, growth rate, response time, sentiment, and contributor diversity. Should be weighted based on community goals.

### Support Deflection
The reduction in official support tickets attributable to community peer-to-peer support. Formula: (Community-resolved questions / Total questions that would have been tickets) x Average ticket cost. Key ROI metric for product communities.

### Pipeline Influence
Revenue influenced by community touchpoints in the customer journey. Measured by tracking community members through the sales pipeline and comparing conversion rates and deal sizes to non-community members.

### Net Promoter Score (NPS)
A standardized measure of member willingness to recommend the community. Survey question: "How likely are you to recommend this community to a friend or colleague?" (0-10 scale). Promoters (9-10) minus Detractors (0-6) = NPS. Community NPS benchmark: 30-50 is good, 50+ is excellent.

### Sentiment Score
A quantitative measure of the emotional tone of community conversations. Can be measured through NLP analysis of community content, regular surveys, or reaction analysis. Tracked over time to identify trends and the impact of community interventions.

### Time to First Response (TTFR)
The elapsed time between a member posting a question and receiving the first reply. Critical metric for support communities. Industry benchmark: <4 hours for healthy communities. Includes responses from both community members and staff.

## Operational Terminology

### Community Manager
The professional responsible for day-to-day community operations, member engagement, content creation, and moderation oversight. Per CMX research, the community manager role typically covers strategy (30%), engagement (30%), content (20%), and operations (20%).

### Community Architect
A senior community professional responsible for community strategy, program design, and organizational community vision. Focused on systems and structures rather than daily operations.

### Community Tooling Stack
The integrated set of platforms and tools used to operate a community. Typically includes: primary platform (Discord/Slack/Circle), analytics tools, moderation tools, automation tools, CRM integration, and communication tools.

### Knowledge Base
A structured, searchable repository of community knowledge including FAQs, how-to guides, best practices, and curated discussion highlights. Reduces repetitive questions and preserves institutional knowledge.

### Community Wiki
A collaboratively maintained knowledge repository where community members can contribute and edit content. Distinguished from a knowledge base by its collaborative authorship model.
