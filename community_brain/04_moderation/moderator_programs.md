# Moderator Programs — Authoritative Module

Volunteer moderators are the operational backbone of scaled communities.
They extend the community team's reach, embed cultural norms, and provide
the human judgment that automated systems cannot replicate. This document
codifies the recruitment, training, management, and support of volunteer
moderator programs.

---

## 1. THE CASE FOR VOLUNTEER MODERATORS

### Why Volunteer Moderators Are Essential

| Scale | Staff-Only Moderation | With Volunteers |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| <500 members | Manageable | Not yet needed |
| 500–2,000 | Stretched | 3–5 volunteers needed |
| 2,000–10,000 | Unsustainable | 10–20 volunteers needed |
| 10,000–50,000 | Impossible | 20–50 volunteers needed |
| 50,000+ | Dangerous without | 50+ volunteers + automation |

**The math is simple:** A community manager can meaningfully moderate
approximately 500 active members. Beyond that threshold, either volunteer
moderators scale the operation or quality degrades.

### What Volunteer Moderators Provide

- **Coverage:** Timezone coverage that no staff team can match
- **Cultural knowledge:** Deep understanding of community norms
- **Speed:** Faster response to issues (they are already present)
- **Trust:** Members trust peers more than staff for many interactions
- **Scalability:** Linear scaling without proportional cost
- **Succession:** Pipeline for future community team hires

## 2. RECRUITMENT

### Identifying Potential Moderators

**Behavioral Signals (Observable):**
- Consistently helpful to other members
- Respectful and constructive in disagreements
- Active for 3+ months with consistent presence
- Self-moderating (redirects off-topic, de-escalates)
- Diverse perspective (not a clone of existing moderators)

**Quantitative Indicators:**
- Response rate: Answers other members' questions regularly
- Activity consistency: Active most days/weeks, not just bursts
- Tenure: Minimum 3 months of active participation
- Report accuracy: If reporting system exists, accurate reports
- Content quality: Thoughtful, well-written contributions

### Recruitment Process

**Step 1: Nomination (Internal)**
```
Community team identifies candidates based on:
├── Behavioral observation (3+ months)
├── Activity data review
├── Existing moderator recommendations
└── Diversity assessment (timezone, background, perspective)
```

**Step 2: Invitation (Private)**
```
Direct message to candidate:
├── Express appreciation for their contributions
├── Explain the moderator role and its importance
├── Describe time commitment and expectations
├── Ask if they are interested (no pressure)
└── Give them time to consider (1 week)
```

**Step 3: Interview (Conversation)**
```
30-minute conversation covering:
├── Motivation for moderating
├── Availability and timezone
├── Scenario-based questions (see below)
├── Alignment with community values
└── Questions from the candidate
```

**Step 4: Trial Period (2–4 weeks)**
```
Limited moderator access:
├── Shadow an existing moderator
├── Handle low-severity issues with oversight
├── Participate in moderator discussions
└── Receive feedback on decisions
```

**Step 5: Full Appointment**
```
After successful trial:
├── Full moderator access granted
├── Added to moderator channels
├── Announced in community (with consent)
└── Ongoing support begins
```

### Interview Scenarios

Present these scenarios and evaluate judgment:

| Scenario | What You Are Evaluating |
|----------|----------------------|
| "A member posts something borderline offensive. Some members are upset, others think it is fine." | Nuance, de-escalation, policy application |
| "A long-time respected member starts violating guidelines repeatedly." | Fairness, courage, consistency |
| "You disagree with a moderation decision made by another moderator." | Teamwork, process respect, communication |
| "A member reports harassment via DM that happened outside the community." | Scope understanding, empathy, escalation |
| "A large group of members is unhappy with a community policy change." | Listening, advocacy, boundary management |

### Diversity in Moderator Teams

Active recruitment must ensure diversity across:
- **Timezone:** Coverage requires geographic distribution
- **Background:** Different perspectives prevent blind spots
- **Tenure:** Mix of experienced and newer moderators
- **Role type:** Some focused on content, others on relationships
- **Communication style:** Balance of formal and informal

## 3. TRAINING

### Moderator Training Program

**Phase 1: Foundation Training (Week 1)**
| Module | Duration | Content |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Community values and culture | 30 min | Mission, values, cultural norms |
| Guidelines deep dive | 45 min | Every guideline with examples |
| Enforcement framework | 45 min | Severity levels, actions, process |
| Tools training | 60 min | Platform moderation tools, bots, logs |
| Scenario practice | 60 min | 10 real scenarios with discussion |

**Phase 2: Shadow Period (Week 2–3)**
- Paired with experienced moderator
- Observe real moderation decisions
- Discuss reasoning behind each decision
- Handle supervised cases (experienced mod reviews before action)

**Phase 3: Supported Independence (Week 3–4)**
- Handle cases independently
- All decisions reviewed within 24 hours
- Weekly debrief with mentor moderator
- Access to immediate escalation channel

**Phase 4: Full Operation (Month 2+)**
- Independent moderation authority
- Monthly team training sessions
- Quarterly skill refreshers
- Annual comprehensive review

### Training Materials

**Moderator Handbook (Required Reading):**
1. Community guidelines (full text)
2. Enforcement framework and severity guide
3. Escalation procedures
4. Communication templates (warnings, suspensions)
5. Tool documentation
6. Emergency procedures
7. Moderator code of conduct (elevated standard)
8. Self-care resources

### Ongoing Training Topics

| Quarter | Topic | Format |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Q1 | De-escalation techniques | Workshop |
| Q2 | Bias awareness in moderation | Discussion |
| Q3 | Platform tool updates | Tutorial |
| Q4 | Crisis response review | Simulation |
| Rotating | Case study review | Group discussion |
| As needed | Policy change briefing | Documentation |

## 4. MODERATOR GUIDELINES

### Operating Principles

1. **Assume good intent** until evidence proves otherwise
2. **Private first** — Address issues privately before publicly
3. **Consistent application** — Same rules for everyone, regardless of status
4. **Document everything** — Every action logged with reasoning
5. **Escalate when uncertain** — Better to ask than to make a wrong call
6. **Team decisions for serious matters** — No lone-wolf bans
7. **Self-awareness** — Recuse yourself from situations where you are biased
8. **Represent the community** — You serve members, not the company alone

### Communication Standards

**When issuing a warning:**
```
Template:
"Hi [name], I noticed your recent [post/comment] in [channel].
This appears to [describe specific issue] which is [not aligned with /
a violation of] our community guideline on [specific guideline].

I wanted to flag this so we can keep our community [positive/productive/safe].
Would you mind [specific corrective action]?

If you have questions about our guidelines, I am happy to discuss.
Thank you for being part of our community."
```

**When muting:**
```
Template:
"Hi [name], your account has been temporarily muted in [channel/community]
for [duration]. This is due to [specific violation with evidence].

This is a [first/second/third] action on your account. Our community
guidelines on [topic] require [expected behavior].

Your mute will expire automatically on [date/time]. If you would like to
discuss this, you can reach the moderation team at [contact].

We value your participation and look forward to your continued engagement
within our guidelines."
```

**When suspending:**
```
Template:
"Hi [name], your account has been suspended from [community name]
for [duration]. This action was taken due to [specific violation(s)]
that [severity description].

Evidence: [reference specific content/behavior]

Suspension period: [start date] to [end date]
Conditions for return: [acknowledgment of guidelines / specific requirements]

You may appeal this decision within 14 days by [appeal process].

[Signed by moderation team, not individual moderator]"
```

### Decision Documentation

Every enforcement action must be logged:
```
Date: [YYYY-MM-DD]
Moderator: [name]
Member: [name/ID]
Violation: [specific guideline violated]
Evidence: [link/screenshot]
Prior history: [previous actions, if any]
Action taken: [warning/mute/suspension/ban]
Duration: [if applicable]
Reasoning: [why this severity level]
Follow-up needed: [yes/no, what]
```

### Platform-Specific Tools

|------|----------|

| Timeout | Temporary mute (built-in) |

| AutoMod | Automated keyword and pattern filtering |

**Discourse:**
| Tool | Function |
|------|----------|
| Trust levels | Automatic permission scaling |
| Flags | Member reporting system |
| Silence | Prevent posting temporarily |
| Suspend | Temporary account suspension |
| Staff actions | Review queue for flagged content |
| Watched words | Automated content filtering |

**Slack:**
| Tool | Function |
|------|----------|
| Channel management | Archive, restrict, set purpose |
| User management | Deactivate, guest access |
| Workflow builder | Automated reporting |
| Enterprise features | DLP, audit logs (paid) |

### Custom Tooling

For scaled communities, invest in:
- **Moderation dashboard:** Centralized view of all actions and queues
- **Report aggregation:** Unified reporting across platforms
- **Pattern detection:** Identify repeat offenders and coordinated behavior
- **Macro/template system:** Consistent messaging at speed
- **Analytics:** Moderator workload, response time, action distribution

## 6. BURNOUT PREVENTION

### Understanding Moderator Burnout

Moderators face unique psychological stressors:
- Exposure to toxic, hateful, and disturbing content
- Emotional labor of managing conflicts
- Pressure to be available (always-on expectation)
- Thankless work (noticed only when it fails)
- Identity conflict (moderator role vs. community member role)
- Decision fatigue from constant judgment calls

### Burnout Indicators

| Signal | What It Looks Like |
|--------|-------------------|
| Withdrawal | Less active, slower response, shorter messages |
| Irritability | Harsher enforcement, less patience |
| Cynicism | Negative comments about community or members |
| Over-work | Moderating during off-hours, refusing breaks |
| Physical symptoms | Fatigue, sleep disruption, anxiety |
| Role blurring | Cannot separate moderation from personal time |

### Prevention Strategies

**Structural:**
1. **Maximum hours:** Cap at 5–10 hours/week per moderator
2. **Rotation schedule:** Shift-based coverage, not always-on
3. **Mandatory breaks:** Minimum 1 week off per quarter
4. **Severe content routing:** Designate specific moderators for worst content
5. **Team size:** Enough moderators that no one is indispensable

**Social:**
1. **Moderator community:** Private space for moderators to support each other
2. **Regular check-ins:** Monthly 1:1 with community manager about well-being
3. **Team bonding:** Social events for the moderation team
4. **Peer support:** Buddy system for processing difficult situations
5. **Exit interviews:** When moderators step down, understand why

**Psychological:**
1. **Debriefing:** After severe incidents, team debrief and processing
2. **External resources:** Access to mental health resources
3. **Normalization:** Acknowledge that moderation is emotionally demanding
4. **Permission to step back:** Explicit permission to take breaks
5. **Celebrate success:** Recognize the positive impact of moderation

## 7. RECOGNITION

### Moderator Recognition Program

| Level | Mechanism | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Ongoing | Verbal thanks in moderator channel | Weekly |
| Monthly | Public shoutout in community (with consent) | Monthly |
| Quarterly | Gift/swag package | Quarterly |
| Annual | Moderator of the year award | Annually |
| Special | Bonus recognition for exceptional response | As earned |

### Recognition Principles

- **Specific:** Recognize specific actions, not generic "thanks for modding"
- **Timely:** Recognize soon after the action, not months later
- **Public (with consent):** Community should see moderators valued
- **Varied:** Mix verbal, written, material, and experiential recognition
- **Equitable:** Ensure all moderators receive recognition, not just the loudest

## 8. MODERATOR LIFECYCLE

### Tenure and Transition

| Stage | Duration | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Trial | 2–4 weeks | Learning, supervised practice |
| Active | 6–24 months | Full moderation duties |
| Senior | 12+ months | Mentoring, policy input, training |
| Emeritus | Post-active | Advisory, occasional input |
| Alumni | Post-departure | Community member, no mod duties |

### Graceful Exit

When moderators step down:
1. Thank them privately and publicly (with consent)
2. Remove moderation access promptly
3. Transition any ongoing cases
4. Alumni recognition (badge, title)
5. Exit conversation to gather feedback
6. Maintain relationship (they may return or refer others)

## 9. SCALING THE MODERATOR TEAM

### Team Structure at Scale

**5–10 Moderators:**
- Flat structure
- All moderators equal authority
- One community manager coordinates

**10–25 Moderators:**
- Team leads (regional or topical)
- Tiered authority (some decisions require lead approval)
- Regular team meetings (weekly)

**25+ Moderators:**
- Hierarchical structure (leads, seniors, moderators)
- Specialized roles (content mod, event mod, trust & safety)
- Formal governance and policy committee
- Training program with certification

### Moderator-to-Member Ratio

| Community Type | Recommended Ratio |
|---------------|------------------|
| Low-conflict (professional, niche) | 1:500–1:1,000 |
| Medium-conflict (general interest) | 1:200–1:500 |
| High-conflict (debate, opinion, public) | 1:50–1:200 |
| Real-time chat (Discord, Slack) | 1:100–1:300 |
| Forum (Discourse, Reddit) | 1:500–1:1,000 |

## 10. ANTI-PATTERNS

| Anti-Pattern | Description | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Unpaid labor exploitation | Treating volunteers as free employees | Clear boundaries, genuine appreciation |
| No training | Expecting moderators to figure it out | Structured training program |
| All stick, no carrot | Only consequences, no recognition | Active recognition program |
| One moderator army | One person does everything | Recruit and distribute |
| No escalation path | Moderators handle everything alone | Clear escalation + support |
| Ghost moderators | Inactive moderators retaining access | Regular activity review |

---

**Moderator programs are a trust-based partnership between the community
team and dedicated volunteers. When this partnership is built on clear
expectations, genuine support, and meaningful recognition, it creates a
moderation capacity that scales with the community while preserving its
culture and safety. Treat your moderators as your most valuable community
asset, because they are.**
