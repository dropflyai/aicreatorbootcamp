# Engagement Strategy — Authoritative Module

Engagement is the measure of a community's vitality. Not activity volume, not
member count — engagement measures whether members derive value, form habits,
and deepen participation over time. This document codifies the engagement
framework, from the engagement ladder model to activation tactics,
gamification, and recognition programs.

---

## 1. THE ENGAGEMENT LADDER

### From Lurker to Leader

The engagement ladder (originally from Kim, 2000; refined by Bacon, 2012)
describes the progressive levels of community participation:

```
Level 5: LEADER      (1–2%)   — Govern, mentor, create programs
Level 4: CREATOR     (3–5%)   — Produce original content, facilitate
Level 3: CONTRIBUTOR (10–15%) — Answer questions, share experiences
Level 2: CONSUMER    (20–30%) — Read, attend events, follow discussions
Level 1: LURKER      (50–70%) — Browse occasionally, passive presence
Level 0: STRANGER    (—)      — Unaware of community
```

### The 90-9-1 Rule (Nielsen, 2006)

In most online communities:
- **90%** of members lurk (consume but never contribute)
- **9%** contribute occasionally
- **1%** create the majority of content

**This is not a problem to solve — it is a reality to design for.**

The goal is not to convert all lurkers to creators. The goal is:
1. Make lurking valuable (so 90% stay)
2. Lower the barrier for the 9% to contribute more
3. Support and retain the 1% who create

### Engagement Ladder Design

For each rung, define:

| Level | Behaviors | Value Received | Triggers to Next Level |
|-------|-----------|---------------|----------------------|
| Lurker | Reading, browsing | Knowledge, awareness | Find personally relevant content |
| Consumer | Following topics, saving content | Curated information | Find a question they can answer |
| Contributor | Replying, sharing, reacting | Recognition, connections | See impact of their contributions |
| Creator | Writing posts, producing content | Influence, reputation | Invitation to lead a program |
| Leader | Moderating, mentoring, organizing | Authority, insider access | Formal leadership role |

## 2. ACTIVATION TACTICS

### Defining Activation

Activation is the moment a new member transitions from passive to active.
It is the community equivalent of "aha moment" in product (Chamath
Palihapitiya's growth framework).

### Activation Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Time to first action | Days from join to first post/reaction | <7 days |
| Activation rate | % of new members who take first action within 30 days | >30% |
| Action to habit | Days from first action to regular activity | <30 days |
| Habit formation | % of activated members who become regular (weekly) | >25% |

### Activation Tactics by Channel

**Onboarding Activation:**
| Tactic | Description | Expected Impact |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Welcome prompt | Ask new members to introduce themselves | 20–40% post rate |
| Interest survey | Ask about interests to personalize | Better content matching |
| First win | Direct to a resource that solves their need | Early value delivery |
| Buddy assignment | Pair with existing member | 2x activation rate |
| Guided tour | Walkthrough of key community areas | Reduced confusion |

**Content Activation:**
| Tactic | Description | Expected Impact |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Question of the day | Low-barrier prompt anyone can answer | 5–10% response rate |
| Poll | Binary or multiple choice question | 15–30% participation |
| Challenge | Time-bound task with recognition | 5–15% participation |
| Tag-a-friend | Invite to involve someone specific | Network expansion |
| Controversial take | Opinionated post that invites debate | High engagement, requires moderation |

**Event Activation:**
| Tactic | Description | Expected Impact |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| New member welcome event | Dedicated event for newcomers | 3x activation rate |
| Office hours | Low-barrier drop-in format | New member confidence |
| Show and tell | Showcase personal work | Creator pipeline |
| Pair programming/review | Collaborative activity | Deep relationship formation |

### Reducing Friction

Every point of friction reduces activation. Audit and eliminate:
- Account creation complexity (SSO, social login)
- Platform learning curve (simplified onboarding)
- Content intimidation (welcoming first-post channels)
- Social anxiety (structured prompts, templates)
- Value uncertainty (clear benefit messaging)

## 3. GAMIFICATION

### Gamification Theory

Gamification applies game mechanics to non-game contexts. In community,
it makes contribution visible, progress measurable, and achievement
celebrated.

**Foundational Framework: Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985)**

Intrinsic motivation requires three elements:
- **Autonomy:** Choice in how to participate
- **Competence:** Sense of growth and mastery
- **Relatedness:** Connection to others

Gamification that supports these three needs enhances engagement.
Gamification that substitutes for them (points without meaning) fails.

### Gamification Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Description | Best For | Risk |
|-----------|-------------|---------|------|
| Points | Numeric score for activities | Activity tracking | Meaningless accumulation |
| Badges | Visual recognition for achievements | Milestone marking | Badge inflation |
| Levels/ranks | Progressive status indicators | Long-term progression | Elitism |
| Leaderboards | Public ranking by metric | Competition | Discourages new members |
| Streaks | Consecutive activity tracking | Habit formation | Guilt/anxiety |
| Challenges | Time-bound goals | Engagement spikes | Fatigue if overused |
| Unlocks | Access to features/content at levels | Incentivize progression | Gatekeeping |

### Gamification Design Rules

1. **Reward contribution, not consumption.** Points for helping > points for logging in.
2. **Multiple pathways.** Different activities earn different recognition.
3. **Visible progress.** Members must see how they are advancing.
4. **Meaningful milestones.** Achievements should feel earned, not automatic.
5. **Social recognition > private scores.** Public acknowledgment motivates more.
6. **Decay is optional.** Consider whether activity should expire (streaks vs. lifetime).
7. **Inclusive design.** Ensure gamification does not disadvantage part-time members.

### Points System Design (Example)

| Activity | Points | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Post a question | 5 | Encourages asking |
| Answer a question | 10 | Rewards helping |
| Accepted answer | 25 | Quality signal |
| Create a resource/guide | 50 | High-value content |
| Attend an event | 10 | Participation |
| Refer a new member | 20 | Growth |
| Receive a thanks | 5 | Peer recognition |
| Complete a challenge | 30 | Engagement |
| Mentor a member (monthly) | 100 | Leadership |

### Level Progression (Example)

| Level | Name | Points Required | Perks |
|-------|------|----------------|-------|
| 1 | Newcomer | 0 | Basic access |
| 2 | Member | 50 | Custom avatar, reactions |
| 3 | Contributor | 200 | Access to special channels |
| 4 | Expert | 500 | Expert badge, event priority |
| 5 | Champion | 1,000 | Direct team access, beta features |
| 6 | Legend | 5,000 | Advisory role, annual summit invite |

## 4. RECOGNITION PROGRAMS

### Recognition Framework

Recognition is the most powerful engagement driver. It satisfies the
fundamental human need to be seen and valued.

### Recognition Types

| Type | Description | Example | Effort |
|------|-------------|---------|--------|
| Micro-recognition | Small, frequent acknowledgment | Reactions, thanks | Minimal |
| Peer recognition | Member-to-member appreciation | Shoutout channel, nomination | Low |
| Staff recognition | Official acknowledgment from team | Spotlight post, certificate | Medium |
| Program recognition | Formal award or title | Member of the month, MVP | High |
| Material recognition | Physical or monetary reward | Swag, credits, gifts | High |
| Experiential recognition | Access or opportunity | Conference ticket, meeting CEO | Very High |

### Member Spotlight Program

**Frequency:** Weekly or bi-weekly

**Selection Criteria:**
- Exceptional contribution (quality, not just quantity)
- Helpfulness to other members
- Embodiment of community values
- Diversity of spotlight recipients

**Spotlight Template:**
```
🌟 Member Spotlight: [Name]

[Name] has been an incredible part of our community. This [week/month],
they [specific contribution].

What makes [Name] special:
- [Specific achievement or quality]
- [Another specific example]
- [How they have helped others]

[Quote from the member about the community]

Thank you, [Name], for making our community better every day.
```

### Annual Awards Program

| Award | Criteria | Selection Method |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Most Helpful | Highest quality answers/support | Community vote + data |
| Most Creative | Best original content produced | Staff selection |
| Rising Star | Greatest growth in a newcomer | Data + nomination |
| Community Builder | Most events/programs led | Staff selection |
| Mentor of the Year | Best mentorship outcomes | Mentee nominations |
| Culture Champion | Best embodiment of values | Peer nomination |

## 5. ENGAGEMENT MEASUREMENT

### Core Engagement Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Benchmark |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| DAU/MAU ratio | Daily active / Monthly active | 15–30% (healthy) |
| Engagement rate | Members who posted / total members | 5–15% |
| Response rate | Questions answered / questions asked | >80% |
| Time to response | Median time from question to first answer | <4 hours |
| Contribution depth | Avg words per post | Trending stable/up |
| Return visit rate | Members visiting 2+ times per week | >30% |
| Active contributor % | Members who contributed this month | >10% |
| Thread depth | Avg replies per thread | >3 |

### Engagement Health Score

Composite score from weighted metrics:

```
Engagement Health Score =
  (DAU/MAU Ratio x 25%) +
  (Response Rate x 20%) +
  (Contribution Depth x 15%) +
  (Return Visit Rate x 20%) +
  (Active Contributor % x 20%)

Scale: 0–100
  90–100: Exceptional (rare, typically small/focused communities)
  70–89:  Healthy (target for mature communities)
  50–69:  Adequate (typical for growing communities)
  30–49:  Concerning (intervention needed)
  0–29:   Critical (significant redesign required)
```

### Engagement Dashboards

**Daily Dashboard:**
- Active members today
- New posts and replies
- Unresolved questions
- Flagged content

**Weekly Dashboard:**
- WAU trend
- Top contributors
- Popular content
- New member activation

**Monthly Dashboard:**
- MAU trend
- Engagement health score
- Member journey progression
- Program participation rates
- Content production metrics

## 6. ENGAGEMENT LIFECYCLE

### Seasonal Patterns

Most communities experience engagement seasonality:
- **January:** High (new year energy, resolutions)
- **Summer:** Low (vacations, outdoor activities)
- **September:** Recovery (back to work/school)
- **November–December:** Declining (holidays)

### Engagement Revival

When engagement drops below healthy thresholds:

**Immediate Actions (Week 1):**
1. Audit recent content quality and relevance
2. Personal outreach to top 10 contributors
3. Launch a time-bound challenge or event
4. Address any unresolved negative experiences

**Short-Term Actions (Month 1):**
1. Refresh content strategy
2. Introduce new programming (event series, program)
3. Invite an external expert for an AMA
4. Survey members about what they want

**Medium-Term Actions (Quarter 1):**
1. Revisit community strategy alignment
2. Platform audit and potential changes
3. Moderator and contributor recognition push
4. New member acquisition campaign

## 7. ADVANCED ENGAGEMENT TACTICS

### Cross-Pollination

Move members between contexts to deepen engagement:
- Online → in-person (or vice versa)
- Consumer → contributor (first contribution prompt)
- Text → voice/video (live events)
- Community → product (beta access)
- Product → community (in-app community links)

### Engagement Loops

Design self-reinforcing engagement cycles:

```
Member asks question
    → Another member answers
        → Asker marks as helpful
            → Helper earns recognition
                → Helper answers more questions
                    → More members get help
                        → More members return to ask
```

### Content Series

Recurring content that creates anticipation and habit:
- **Monday Motivation:** Share wins from last week
- **Tutorial Tuesday:** Weekly how-to from a member
- **Wednesday Workshop:** Live learning session
- **Thoughtful Thursday:** Discussion prompt on industry topic
- **Friday Showcase:** Member project highlights

## 8. ANTI-PATTERNS

| Anti-Pattern | Description | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Engagement theater | Forced activity that creates noise, not value | Value-first design |
| Notification spam | Over-communicating to drive returns | Bounded notification strategy |
| Gamification addiction | Points replace genuine value | Intrinsic motivation focus |
| Leader dependency | Community dies when leaders go silent | Distributed engagement |
| Metric fixation | Optimizing numbers over experience | Qualitative + quantitative balance |
| One-size-fits-all | Same engagement approach for all members | Segment-appropriate design |

---

**Engagement is not a metric to be optimized — it is a signal of genuine
value delivery. When members engage deeply and consistently, it means the
community is fulfilling its purpose. When engagement drops, it means the
community is failing to deliver. Always diagnose the root cause of engagement
changes; never treat the symptom with superficial tactics.**
