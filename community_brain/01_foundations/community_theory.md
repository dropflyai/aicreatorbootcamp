# Community Theory — Authoritative Foundation

Community is not audience. Community is not a Slack channel. Community is not
a growth hack. Community is a structured social system where members derive
identity, belonging, and mutual value from shared participation.

This document codifies the theoretical foundations that govern all community
work within this brain.

---

## 1. DEFINITIONAL FRAMEWORK

### Community vs. Audience

| Dimension | Audience | Community |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Direction | One-to-many | Many-to-many |
| Value flow | Creator → consumer | Member ↔ member |
| Identity | Passive recipient | Active participant |
| Ownership | Brand-owned | Co-owned |
| Departure cost | Low (unsubscribe) | High (social ties) |
| Content source | Central authority | Distributed members |

A community exists when members form relationships with each other,
not merely with the brand. If members would not notice each other's
absence, you have an audience, not a community.

### Community vs. Network

Networks connect nodes. Communities bind identities. A LinkedIn connection
graph is a network. A group of open-source maintainers who know each other's
work, review each other's code, and defend each other's reputations is a
community. The distinction matters because networks optimize for reach while
communities optimize for depth.

## 2. WENGER'S COMMUNITIES OF PRACTICE (CoP)

Etienne Wenger's (1998) framework defines communities of practice through
three structural elements:

### 2.1 Domain

The shared area of interest that gives the community its identity. The domain
is not merely a topic — it is a commitment to a shared competence. Members
value their collective expertise and learn from each other.

- Domain must be specific enough to create shared identity
- Domain must be broad enough to sustain long-term engagement
- Newcomers must be able to identify whether they belong

### 2.2 Community

The social fabric — relationships, trust, mutual engagement. Members interact,
help each other, share information, and build rapport. A website or repository
alone is not community; community requires sustained human interaction.

- Shared history and stories
- Mutual respect and trust
- Willingness to share both successes and failures

### 2.3 Practice

The shared repertoire of resources — tools, frameworks, vocabulary, stories,
documents, methods. Practice is what the community produces and refines over
time. It is both the output and the medium of learning.

- Shared frameworks, templates, playbooks
- Common vocabulary and jargon
- Stories and case studies that encode institutional knowledge
- Tools and artifacts refined through collective use

### 2.4 Legitimate Peripheral Participation (Lave & Wenger, 1991)

Newcomers enter a community at the periphery and gradually move toward full
participation. This trajectory is not passive observation — it is legitimate
participation at a level appropriate to the newcomer's current capability.

```
Peripheral ──→ Active ──→ Core ──→ Leadership

Lurking         Asking      Answering    Mentoring
Observing       Trying      Teaching     Governing
Reading         Sharing     Creating     Strategizing
```

Designing for legitimate peripheral participation means:
- Making observation valuable and accessible
- Providing low-risk entry points for contribution
- Creating visible pathways from periphery to core
- Rewarding progression without gatekeeping

## 3. McMILLAN & CHAVIS: SENSE OF COMMUNITY (1986)

The McMillan-Chavis model identifies four elements that create psychological
sense of community:

### 3.1 Membership

The feeling of belonging, of being part of something. Membership requires
boundaries — not everyone can be a member. Boundaries create safety,
investment, and identity.

**Components:**
- **Boundaries** — Clear criteria for who belongs
- **Emotional safety** — Trust that vulnerability is protected
- **Sense of belonging** — Personal identification with the group
- **Personal investment** — Members invest time, effort, reputation
- **Common symbol system** — Shared language, rituals, inside references

### 3.2 Influence

Members must feel they matter to the group, and the group must matter to
its members. Influence is bidirectional:

- Member → Community: "My voice is heard; my contributions matter"
- Community → Member: "The community shapes my thinking and behavior"

Communities that fail to provide influence in both directions lose
engagement. If members cannot shape the community, they leave.

### 3.3 Integration and Fulfillment of Needs

Members must receive value — social, intellectual, professional, emotional.
The community must provide reinforcement that meets individual needs.

**Value categories:**
- Status and recognition
- Knowledge and skill development
- Professional opportunity and advancement
- Emotional support and friendship
- Shared values and purpose

### 3.4 Shared Emotional Connection

The definitive element. Shared history, shared experience, shared investment
create emotional bonds that transcend individual transactions.

**Drivers:**
- Contact hypothesis — frequency of interaction
- Quality of interaction — depth, not just breadth
- Closure to events — shared milestones, celebrations, crises
- Personal investment — what members have risked or sacrificed
- Shared valent events — experiences with emotional weight

## 4. DUNBAR'S NUMBER AND SOCIAL SCALING

Robin Dunbar's (1992) research on primate neocortex size and social group
size establishes cognitive limits on meaningful relationships:

### Dunbar Layers

| Layer | Size | Relationship Type | Community Implication |
|-------|------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Intimate | ~5 | Deep trust, daily contact | Core team, co-founders |
| Close | ~15 | Strong bonds, weekly contact | Moderators, champions |
| Sympathy | ~50 | Good friends, regular contact | Active contributors |
| Active | ~150 | Meaningful acquaintances | Engaged community |
| Acquaintance | ~500 | Recognized faces/names | Extended community |
| Known | ~1,500 | Can attach name to face | Total membership ceiling (per sub-community) |

### Implications for Community Design

- **Sub-community strategy is mandatory above 150 members.** A single
  flat community cannot sustain meaningful relationships beyond Dunbar's
  number. You must create sub-groups (cohorts, chapters, interest groups).

- **Intimacy does not scale.** Design for concentric circles, not uniform
  density. The core must remain small enough for real relationships.

- **Recognition matters at every layer.** Members at the 500-layer still
  need to feel seen, even if they cannot know everyone personally.

- **Scaling strategy:**
  - 0–50: One space, one conversation
  - 50–150: Start differentiating roles and sub-topics
  - 150–500: Sub-communities become necessary
  - 500–1,500: Formal governance and structure required
  - 1,500+: Federation model — communities of communities

## 5. SOCIAL IDENTITY THEORY (Tajfel & Turner, 1979)

People derive part of their self-concept from group membership. This has
profound implications for community design:

### In-Group Identification

Members must see the community as part of their identity. "I am a
[community member]" must feel meaningful. This requires:

- **Distinctiveness** — The community must be different from alternatives
- **Prestige** — Membership must confer status
- **Salience** — Community identity must be frequently activated

### Identity Badges

Communities that succeed in creating social identity provide visible markers:
- Titles and roles (contributor, maintainer, champion)
- Exclusive access or capabilities
- Shared vocabulary and inside references
- Visual markers (badges, avatars, swag)

### Warning: Identity and Toxicity

Strong in-group identity creates the risk of out-group hostility.
Community design must actively prevent:
- "Us vs. them" mentality toward competing communities
- Gatekeeping that excludes qualified newcomers
- Elitism that devalues peripheral participation

## 6. COMMUNITY LIFECYCLE MODEL

Communities are living systems with predictable lifecycle stages:

### Stage 1: Inception (0–3 months)

- Founding vision articulated
- Initial members hand-recruited
- Culture established through founding behavior
- Everything is personal and high-touch

### Stage 2: Establishment (3–12 months)

- First rituals emerge organically
- Early leaders self-identify
- Norms codified (code of conduct, guidelines)
- Value proposition becomes clear to new members

### Stage 3: Maturity (1–3 years)

- Self-sustaining engagement patterns
- Multiple sub-communities or tracks
- Formal governance structures
- Institutional memory and culture
- New member onboarding is smooth

### Stage 4: Mitosis or Decline (3+ years)

Two paths diverge:
- **Mitosis:** Community spawns sub-communities, chapters, or new
  communities. The organism reproduces. This is healthy.
- **Decline:** Activity decreases, core members depart, new members
  find less value. Without intervention, the community dies.

### Lifecycle Management Principles

- Each stage requires different leadership behavior
- Premature scaling (adding features/members too fast) is the #1 killer
- Culture is set in the first 100 members and is nearly immutable after
- Transitions between stages must be intentionally managed

## 7. COMMUNITY TYPES TAXONOMY

### By Primary Purpose

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Practice | Shared craft, skill development | Stack Overflow, Figma Community |
| Product | Users of a shared product | Salesforce Trailblazers, Notion |
| Interest | Shared passion or topic | Reddit subreddits, Hacker News |
| Place | Geographic proximity | Meetup groups, local Slack communities |
| Circumstance | Shared life situation | Patient communities, new parent groups |
| Action | Shared goal or cause | Open-source projects, advocacy |

### By Ownership Model

| Model | Control | Economics | Example |
|-------|---------|-----------|---------|
| Brand-owned | High | Company-funded | Salesforce Trailblazers |
| Community-owned | Distributed | Member-funded | Wikipedia |
| Platform-dependent | Low | Platform-subsidized | Facebook Groups |
| Hybrid | Shared | Mixed | Many open-source projects |

### By Platform Architecture

| Architecture | Pros | Cons |
|-------------|------|------|
| Synchronous (Discord, Slack) | Real-time energy, low friction | High noise, FOMO, hard to search |
| Asynchronous (Discourse, Forum) | Thoughtful discourse, searchable | Slower engagement, higher effort |
| Hybrid (Circle, Mighty Networks) | Flexible, balanced | Complexity, feature bloat |
| Distributed (GitHub + Discord + Blog) | Platform-optimized | Fragmented identity |

## 8. THEORETICAL INTEGRATION

Community work within this brain synthesizes these frameworks:

1. **Wenger** provides the structural model (domain, community, practice)
2. **McMillan-Chavis** provides the psychological model (belonging, influence,
   value, connection)
3. **Dunbar** provides the scaling model (group size limits, sub-community
   triggers)
4. **Social Identity** provides the motivation model (why people identify
   with and defend communities)
5. **Lifecycle** provides the temporal model (how communities evolve)

No community decision should be made without considering all five dimensions.

## 9. ANTI-PATTERNS IN COMMUNITY THEORY

| Anti-Pattern | What It Looks Like | Root Cause |
|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| Audience masquerade | One-way content with "community" label | No member-to-member value |
| Premature scaling | Adding 1,000 members before culture is set | Growth metrics over health |
| Feature fetish | Adding channels, bots, integrations before need | Platform confusion with community |
| Democracy theater | Polls and votes that do not influence decisions | Influence imbalance |
| Engagement theater | Manufactured activity, forced participation | Metrics over meaning |
| Homogeneity trap | Only core-similar members welcomed | Boundary design failure |
