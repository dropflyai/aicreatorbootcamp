# Brand Score — Quality Enforcement (Authoritative)

This document defines how brand quality is evaluated.
Every branding deliverable must be scored before it is considered complete.

If brand quality is not measurable, it is not enforced.

---

## SCORING RULES (MANDATORY)

Each branding deliverable must be scored across the following 8 dimensions.
Score each category from **1 (poor)** to **5 (excellent)**.

### Hard Fail Dimensions
These dimensions are critical. Score <3 = immediate remediation required:
- **Brand Clarity**
- **Consistency**
- **Voice Authenticity**

### Passing Criteria
- Average score across all dimensions >= 4.0
- No hard-fail dimension < 3
- Brand Equity is NOT a hard fail — evaluated as a long-term trajectory

### Long-Term Trajectory Note
Brand Equity is evaluated as a trajectory, not a snapshot. Brand equity takes time to build. What matters is the direction and rate of change, not the current absolute level. A new brand scoring 2 on equity but trending up rapidly is healthier than an established brand scoring 3 but stagnant.

## 1. BRAND CLARITY

**Question:**
Can anyone in the company articulate the brand in 10 seconds or less?

### What to Measure
- **Elevator Test:** Ask 10 random employees to describe the brand. Consistency and clarity of answers.
- **Customer Echo:** Ask 10 customers what the brand stands for. Alignment with intended positioning.
- **One-Line Test:** Can the brand be captured in a single, non-generic sentence?
- **Competitive Distinction:** Does the clarity statement apply ONLY to this brand, or could it describe competitors?
- **Simplicity:** Can a non-marketing person explain it?

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — Every employee can articulate the brand consistently in <10 seconds. Customers echo the same message. One-line statement is unique and memorable. Passes the "put your hand over the logo" test (statement cannot apply to competitors).
- **4** — Most employees can articulate the brand. Minor variations but same core message. Customers mostly aligned. One-line statement is clear but could be sharper.
- **3** — Employees give different descriptions. Core theme exists but execution varies. Customers have a vague sense. One-line statement exists but is generic.
- **2** — Employees struggle to describe the brand. Customers are confused or indifferent. No clear one-line statement. Brand could be mistaken for competitors.
- **1** — No one can describe the brand consistently. Customers have no association. Brand is invisible or meaningless.

### Evidence Required
- Internal brand articulation survey (minimum 10 employees)
- Customer brand perception survey (minimum 10 customers)
- One-line brand statement with competitive distinction test
- Brand clarity audit report

Score <3 → Stop all brand activities. Conduct brand clarity workshop. Define the one-line brand statement before any other brand work proceeds.

## 2. CONSISTENCY

**Question:**
Does the brand present a unified identity across every touchpoint?

### What to Measure
- **Cross-Channel Audit Score:** Visual and verbal consistency across website, social media, email, product, sales materials, customer support, physical materials
- **Guideline Compliance:** Percentage of materials that comply with brand guidelines
- **Deviation Tracking:** Number and severity of brand guideline violations
- **Template Adoption:** Percentage of team using brand templates vs. creating ad hoc materials
- **Partner/Vendor Compliance:** External parties representing the brand correctly

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — Cross-channel audit score >95%. Guideline compliance >98%. Deviations are rare and minor. Templates are universally adopted. Partners/vendors are consistently on-brand.
- **4** — Cross-channel audit score 85-95%. Guideline compliance >90%. Deviations are tracked and corrected. Templates widely adopted. Partner compliance is monitored.
- **3** — Cross-channel audit score 70-85%. Guideline compliance 75-90%. Noticeable inconsistencies across channels. Templates exist but not universally used. Partner compliance is uneven.
- **2** — Cross-channel audit score 50-70%. Guideline compliance <75%. Significant inconsistencies. Templates ignored or outdated. Partners freelancing the brand.
- **1** — No cross-channel consistency. Guidelines do not exist or are ignored. Every touchpoint looks like a different brand. No template system. Partners misrepresenting the brand.

### Evidence Required
- Cross-channel brand audit report with screenshots/examples
- Guideline compliance tracking data
- Brand deviation log with severity and resolution
- Template usage analytics
- Partner/vendor brand compliance review

Score <3 → Conduct full cross-channel brand audit. Update or create brand guidelines. Implement brand governance system.

## 3. DIFFERENTIATION

**Question:**
Would a customer recognize this brand with the logo removed?

### What to Measure
- **Competitive Distinction:** Can you describe this brand without it sounding like any competitor?
- **Category Ownership:** Does the brand own a specific concept, position, or attribute in its category?
- **Visual Distinctiveness:** Are brand visuals recognizable without the logo?
- **Verbal Distinctiveness:** Does the brand voice sound different from competitors?
- **Experience Distinctiveness:** Is the customer experience memorably different?
- **White Space Exploitation:** Has the brand claimed unoccupied territory in the market?

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — Brand is unmistakable. Owns a category concept. Visual identity is instantly recognizable without logo. Voice is distinctive and imitable. Customer experience has signature moments. Clear white space ownership.
- **4** — Brand is clearly differentiated. Strong positioning in the category. Visual identity is distinctive. Voice has personality. Customer experience has unique elements. White space identified and partially claimed.
- **3** — Brand has some differentiation. Positioning exists but overlaps with competitors. Visual identity is professional but not distinctive. Voice is competent but not memorable. Experience is good but not unique.
- **2** — Brand blends into the category. Positioning is generic. Visual identity could be any competitor. Voice is indistinguishable. Experience is interchangeable with competitors.
- **1** — Brand is invisible in the category. No positioning. Visual identity is generic template. No discernible voice. Experience is commoditized.

### Evidence Required
- Competitive brand audit (minimum 5 competitors)
- Perceptual mapping showing brand position vs. competitors
- "Remove the logo" test results (can people identify the brand?)
- Category ownership analysis
- Distinctive assets inventory

Score <4 → Conduct competitive differentiation analysis. Identify white space opportunities. Define the brand's distinctive assets.

## 4. EMOTIONAL RESONANCE

**Question:**
Does the brand make people feel something specific and intentional?

### What to Measure
- **Brand Sentiment:** Net positive vs. negative sentiment in customer feedback and social mentions
- **Emotional Association Strength:** Specific emotions consistently linked to the brand
- **Story Power:** Does the brand origin story or mission generate emotional response?
- **Community Formation:** Are customers forming identity around the brand?
- **Advocacy Behavior:** Do customers voluntarily recommend or defend the brand?
- **Cultural Relevance:** Does the brand connect to cultural moments or movements authentically?

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — Strong, consistent positive sentiment. Specific emotions are universally associated with the brand. Brand story generates emotional response. Active community forming. Customers are vocal advocates. Brand is culturally relevant and authentic.
- **4** — Positive sentiment dominant. Clear emotional associations exist. Brand story resonates with most audiences. Community is growing. Advocacy is organic. Cultural connections are developing.
- **3** — Sentiment is mixed or neutral. Emotional associations are vague or inconsistent. Brand story exists but does not resonate strongly. Community is passive. Advocacy requires prompting. Cultural relevance is weak.
- **2** — Sentiment trends negative or apathetic. No consistent emotional association. Brand story is ignored or unknown. No community formation. No organic advocacy. Brand feels disconnected from culture.
- **1** — Sentiment is negative or nonexistent. Brand evokes no emotion. No story. No community. No advocacy. Culturally irrelevant.

### Evidence Required
- Sentiment analysis report (social media, reviews, support interactions)
- Emotional association survey (what 3 words do you associate with the brand?)
- Brand story resonance testing results
- Community metrics (engagement, growth, activity)
- NPS or advocacy measurement
- Cultural relevance audit

Score <4 → Define target emotional response. Audit all touchpoints for emotional alignment. Develop emotional resonance strategy.

## 5. RECOGNITION

**Question:**
Can people identify and recall the brand without prompting?

### What to Measure
- **Aided Awareness:** Percentage of target audience who recognize the brand when shown the name or logo
- **Unaided Awareness:** Percentage of target audience who name the brand when asked about the category
- **Visual Distinctiveness:** Recognition of brand visual elements (colors, typography, imagery style) without the logo
- **Sonic Recognition:** If applicable, recognition of brand sounds, jingles, or audio signatures
- **Mental Availability:** How quickly the brand comes to mind in relevant buying situations
- **Distinctive Asset Strength:** Individual strength of each brand asset (logo, color, shape, mascot, tagline, etc.)

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — Unaided awareness >50% in target audience. Aided awareness >90%. Visual assets are strongly distinctive. Brand is top-of-mind in buying situations. Multiple distinctive assets are well-established.
- **4** — Unaided awareness 25-50%. Aided awareness >75%. Visual assets are recognizable. Brand is in the consideration set. Key distinctive assets are established.
- **3** — Unaided awareness 10-25%. Aided awareness 50-75%. Visual assets have some recognition. Brand requires prompting in buying situations. Distinctive assets are developing.
- **2** — Unaided awareness <10%. Aided awareness 25-50%. Visual assets not recognized. Brand is not in the consideration set. Distinctive assets are weak.
- **1** — Unaided awareness near 0%. Aided awareness <25%. Brand is unknown. No distinctive assets recognized.

### Evidence Required
- Brand awareness survey (aided and unaided) with sample size and methodology
- Visual distinctiveness test results
- Distinctive asset audit with strength ratings
- Mental availability research
- Year-over-year awareness trend data

Score <4 → Audit distinctive assets. Invest in consistent exposure of strongest assets. Develop asset-building strategy.

## 6. BRAND ARCHITECTURE

**Question:**
Is the relationship between the parent brand and sub-brands/products logically structured and intuitively understood?

### What to Measure
- **Portfolio Logic:** Is there a clear rationale for how brands/products are organized?
- **Naming Hierarchy:** Do names communicate the relationship between brands/products?
- **Sub-Brand Clarity:** Does each sub-brand have a clear role and distinct audience?
- **Architecture Model:** Is the chosen model (branded house, house of brands, endorsed, hybrid) the right one?
- **Migration Paths:** Are there clear rules for when products graduate, merge, or retire?
- **Customer Navigation:** Can customers intuitively find the right product/brand for their need?

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — Architecture model is deliberately chosen and defensible. Portfolio logic is intuitive. Naming hierarchy communicates relationships clearly. Each sub-brand has a distinct purpose. Migration rules are documented. Customers navigate effortlessly.
- **4** — Architecture model is clear. Portfolio is mostly logical. Naming is generally consistent. Sub-brand roles are defined. Migration paths exist for key brands. Customer navigation requires minimal effort.
- **3** — Architecture model exists but has exceptions. Some portfolio logic gaps. Naming is inconsistent in places. Sub-brand overlap exists. Migration rules are informal. Customer navigation requires some effort.
- **2** — No deliberate architecture model. Portfolio feels random. Naming is confusing. Sub-brands compete with each other. No migration rules. Customers struggle to navigate.
- **1** — Complete chaos. No discernible logic to brand organization. Naming creates confusion. Sub-brands cannibalize each other. Customers are lost.

### Evidence Required
- Brand architecture diagram with rationale
- Naming hierarchy document
- Sub-brand role definitions with audience mapping
- Architecture model analysis (why this model vs. alternatives)
- Customer navigation testing results
- Migration and lifecycle rules documentation

Score <4 → Map the current brand architecture. Identify overlaps and gaps. Choose and document the architecture model with rationale.

## 7. VOICE AUTHENTICITY

**Question:**
Does the brand sound like one consistent person who is not pretending to be someone else?

### What to Measure
- **Tone Consistency:** Does the brand sound the same across all channels and contexts?
- **Personality Distinctiveness:** Would you recognize this brand's writing with the name removed?
- **Audience Appropriateness:** Does the voice match the audience's expectations and communication style?
- **Authenticity:** Does the voice reflect the company's actual culture and values (not an aspirational fiction)?
- **Adaptability:** Can the voice flex for different contexts (serious, playful, urgent) while remaining recognizably the same brand?
- **Internal Alignment:** Do employees naturally write in brand voice, or does it require heavy editing?

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — Voice is instantly recognizable. Consistent across all channels. Perfectly matched to audience. Authentically reflects company culture. Flexes naturally for context. Employees embody the voice naturally.
- **4** — Voice is distinctive. Mostly consistent, minor deviations. Well-suited to audience. Authentically grounded. Adapts for context with some effort. Most employees can write in brand voice.
- **3** — Voice exists but is not distinctive. Inconsistent across channels or authors. Adequate for audience. Some gap between voice and reality. Struggles with contextual adaptation. Brand voice requires heavy editing.
- **2** — Voice is generic. Highly inconsistent. Mismatched with audience in places. Voice feels performative. Cannot adapt to different contexts. Most writing does not sound like the brand.
- **1** — No discernible voice. Every piece of communication sounds different. Audience is not considered. Voice is either absent or inauthentic. No adaptability. Writing is personality-free.

### Evidence Required
- Voice consistency audit across 5+ channels
- "Remove the name" test (blind identification of brand copy)
- Audience communication style research
- Voice-to-culture alignment assessment
- Voice guide with examples of contextual adaptation
- Employee brand voice proficiency assessment

Score <3 → Define brand voice with personality traits, do's and don'ts. Create voice guide with examples. Train all content creators.

## 8. BRAND EQUITY

**Question:**
Does the brand create measurable business value beyond the product itself?

### What to Measure
- **NPS (Net Promoter Score):** Customer willingness to recommend
- **Loyalty Metrics:** Repeat purchase rate, retention rate, lifetime value
- **Brand Premium:** Can the brand charge more than generic alternatives? How much more?
- **Acquisition Cost Impact:** Does brand awareness reduce customer acquisition cost?
- **Talent Attraction:** Does the brand attract talent (employer brand equity)?
- **Partnership Value:** Do partners seek association with the brand?
- **Crisis Resilience:** Does the brand have enough goodwill to survive mistakes?

### Scoring Guide
- **5** — NPS >50. High loyalty and retention. Brand commands significant premium (>20% above generic). Brand reduces CAC measurably. Top talent seeks out the company. Partners compete for association. Brand has survived and recovered from past challenges.
- **4** — NPS 30-50. Good loyalty metrics. Brand commands moderate premium (10-20%). Brand awareness supports acquisition. Brand aids talent recruitment. Partners value the association. Brand has goodwill reserves.
- **3** — NPS 10-30. Average loyalty. Brand commands small premium (<10%). Brand awareness has limited acquisition impact. Brand is neutral in talent attraction. Partners are indifferent. Brand has limited goodwill reserves.
- **2** — NPS 0-10. Below-average loyalty. No brand premium (competing on price). Brand does not aid acquisition. Brand is not a talent attractor. Partners are uninterested. Brand has no crisis buffer.
- **1** — NPS <0. Poor loyalty. Brand is a liability (must discount to compete). Brand increases acquisition cost. Brand repels talent. Partners avoid association. Brand would not survive a public mistake.

### Evidence Required
- NPS survey results with trend data
- Retention and loyalty metrics with benchmarks
- Brand premium analysis (willingness-to-pay study or price comparison)
- CAC analysis with brand attribution
- Employer brand metrics (application rates, offer acceptance rates)
- Partnership pipeline and brand attribution
- Crisis simulation or historical resilience analysis

Score <4 → Conduct brand equity audit. Identify the largest equity gaps. Create brand equity building plan with measurable targets.

## FINAL BRAND SCORE DECISION

**Hard Fail Dimensions (Brand Clarity, Consistency, Voice Authenticity):**
- Score <3 → **IMMEDIATE REMEDIATION REQUIRED**

**All Dimensions:**
- Average score >= 4.0 → **BRAND WORK MAY PROCEED**
- Average score < 4.0 → **BRAND STRATEGY REVIEW REQUIRED**

**Brand Equity:**
- NOT a hard fail
- Evaluated as long-term trajectory (direction matters more than current level)
- Score <3 triggers strategic review, not automatic stop

Scores must be stated explicitly before any branding deliverable is approved.

## SCORING FREQUENCY

| Trigger | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| New brand launch | Full Brand Score before launch |
| Brand refresh or rebrand | Full Brand Score before and after |
| Quarterly brand review | Full Brand Score for primary brand |
| New sub-brand introduction | Score Brand Architecture + Brand Clarity |
| Campaign launch | Score Consistency + Voice Authenticity |
| Competitive threat | Score Differentiation + Recognition |
| Customer perception shift | Score Emotional Resonance + Brand Equity |

---

## ESCALATION PROTOCOL

| Condition | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Any hard-fail dimension <3 | Escalate to brand leadership within 24 hours |
| Average score <3.0 | Emergency brand review |
| Average score declining 2 consecutive quarters | Brand strategy overhaul |
| Consistency score <3 | Brand governance intervention |
| Differentiation score declining | Competitive analysis sprint |
| Three dimensions declining simultaneously | Full brand audit with external perspective |

---

## ENFORCEMENT RULE

Brand quality is enforced, not assumed.
Do not justify low scores.
Remediate until standards are met.
A brand that cannot be measured cannot be managed.
