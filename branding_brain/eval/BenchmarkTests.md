# Branding Benchmark Tests — Competency Verification (Authoritative)

This document contains scenario-based tests to verify branding competency.
Every scenario must be answered with strategic depth, not surface-level advice.

If the response does not include clear rationale, specific recommendations,
and measurable outcomes, it has not passed the benchmark.

---

## HOW TO USE THESE BENCHMARKS

1. Present the scenario exactly as written
2. Evaluate the response against the scoring criteria provided
3. Score each response from 1 (inadequate) to 5 (exceptional)
4. Minimum passing score: 4 on every scenario
5. Score <4 on any scenario = competency gap requiring remediation
6. Document the score with specific evidence from the response

---

## SCENARIO 1: BRAND ARCHITECTURE DECISION

**Situation:**
A company has 3 products: a project management tool (PMFlow), a time tracking app (TimeSnap), and a design collaboration tool (DesignPulse). Each was launched independently over 3 years with its own visual identity, voice, and customer base. The company (Apex Software) has no brand presence itself — customers know the product names, not the company. The CEO wants to "unify everything" before raising a Series B.

**Task:**
Design the brand architecture. Defend your choice: branded house, house of brands, endorsed brands, or hybrid.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Analyzes the current state: 3 independent brands, company brand invisible
- [ ] Evaluates all four architecture options with pros/cons for this specific situation
- [ ] Considers brand equity transfer: how much equity exists in each product name?
- [ ] Assesses audience overlap (same customers using multiple products vs. different segments)
- [ ] Evaluates naming implications for each architecture model
- [ ] Considers the Series B context (what do investors want to see?)
- [ ] Addresses migration risk (customer confusion during transition)
- [ ] Provides a phased implementation timeline
- [ ] Includes a measurement plan to validate the architecture is working
- [ ] Makes a clear recommendation with confidence level and fallback strategy

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 2: INTERNAL-EXTERNAL BRAND ALIGNMENT

**Situation:**
A brand audit reveals a critical disconnect. When employees are asked to describe the brand, they say "fast, scrappy, move-fast-break-things." When customers are asked, they say "reliable, trustworthy, dependable." Marketing materials emphasize "reliability" but internal culture rewards speed over quality. Customer satisfaction is 4.1/5 but trending down. Employee NPS is high.

**Task:**
Design a strategy to align the internal and external brand.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Correctly identifies the root problem: internal culture contradicts external brand promise
- [ ] Recognizes the customer satisfaction decline as a symptom of this misalignment
- [ ] Does NOT recommend simply changing the marketing message
- [ ] Evaluates two paths: evolve culture toward reliability OR evolve brand toward speed
- [ ] Considers which path preserves the most value (existing customer equity vs. employee engagement)
- [ ] Addresses the paradox: employee NPS is high BECAUSE of the scrappy culture
- [ ] Designs a bridge strategy that does not destroy either side
- [ ] Includes internal communication plan (explaining the change without demoralizing the team)
- [ ] Includes customer communication plan (reinforcing the right expectations)
- [ ] Defines metrics to track alignment over time
- [ ] Provides a realistic timeline (culture change is slow)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 3: POST-ACQUISITION REBRAND

**Situation:**
Company A (established 15 years, B2B, strong brand recognition in enterprise market, brand personality: authoritative and institutional) acquires Company B (4 years old, B2B, strong brand recognition in mid-market, brand personality: approachable and modern). 60% of Company B's customers cite "brand and culture" as a top reason for choosing them. Company A's leadership wants to rebrand everything under Company A's name within 6 months.

**Task:**
Design the brand integration strategy. Preserve equity from both brands.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Pushes back on the 6-month "rebrand everything" mandate with data-driven reasoning
- [ ] Quantifies the risk: 60% of Company B customers chose the brand — forced rebrand = churn risk
- [ ] Evaluates architecture options (absorption, endorsement, co-brand, maintain separate)
- [ ] Considers the market positioning advantage (enterprise + mid-market = full market coverage)
- [ ] Proposes a phased approach rather than big-bang rebrand
- [ ] Addresses the personality clash (institutional vs. approachable)
- [ ] Designs research plan to test customer reaction before committing
- [ ] Includes employee integration considerations (Company B team identity)
- [ ] Defines brand equity preservation metrics (awareness, preference, sentiment)
- [ ] Provides decision criteria for each phase (go/no-go gates)
- [ ] Models the financial impact of brand equity loss vs. integration efficiency

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 4: BRAND CRISIS RESPONSE

**Situation:**
A viral social media post (2.5M views, 50K shares) accuses the company of "performative diversity" — using diverse imagery in marketing while having an all-white, all-male leadership team. The post includes screenshots from the company's website side-by-side with the leadership page. The accusations are factually accurate. Media outlets are picking up the story.

**Task:**
Design the brand crisis response strategy.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Does NOT recommend a defensive or dismissive response
- [ ] Acknowledges the factual accuracy of the critique immediately
- [ ] Separates the immediate response (24-48 hours) from the long-term response
- [ ] Designs the immediate response: statement, spokesperson, channel selection
- [ ] Avoids corporate-speak and performative language in the response
- [ ] Proposes concrete, measurable commitments (not vague promises)
- [ ] Addresses the root cause: leadership diversity pipeline
- [ ] Designs accountability mechanism (public reporting on commitments)
- [ ] Addresses internal impact (employee morale, recruitment)
- [ ] Plans for ongoing narrative management (this story will not die in one news cycle)
- [ ] Includes stakeholder-specific communication (employees, customers, investors, partners)
- [ ] Addresses the marketing imagery question directly (keep it, change it, or explain it)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 5: DIFFERENTIATION IN A COMMODITIZED MARKET

**Situation:**
A B2B SaaS company sells a CRM. The market has Salesforce, HubSpot, Pipedrive, and 200+ other CRMs. Feature parity is high — no significant functional differentiation. The company's product is good but not dramatically better. Current positioning: "The easy CRM for growing teams." Three competitors use nearly identical positioning. Brand awareness: 2% unaided in target market.

**Task:**
Design a differentiation strategy that creates a distinctive brand in this crowded market.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Acknowledges that feature-based differentiation is nearly impossible in this market
- [ ] Does NOT recommend "just build better features"
- [ ] Explores non-product differentiation levers: brand personality, customer experience, community, values, audience niche, use case focus
- [ ] Proposes a specific and narrow positioning (not trying to appeal to everyone)
- [ ] Identifies a white space that no major competitor occupies
- [ ] Designs distinctive brand assets that create recognition at low awareness
- [ ] Includes a brand voice strategy that sounds fundamentally different from competitors
- [ ] Plans for category creation or sub-category ownership (rather than competing in the existing frame)
- [ ] Provides examples of brands in other commoditized markets that successfully differentiated
- [ ] Includes measurement plan for differentiation effectiveness
- [ ] Addresses the resource reality (cannot outspend Salesforce on awareness)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 6: EMPLOYER BRAND DISCONNECT

**Situation:**
A tech company has a strong consumer brand (people love the product) but a terrible employer brand. Glassdoor rating: 2.8/5. "Toxic culture" appears in 40% of reviews. Engineering candidate acceptance rate: 35% (industry average: 65%). The company is growing and needs to hire 100 engineers in the next year. The product brand is excellent — NPS 65, strong loyalty.

**Task:**
Design an employer brand strategy that leverages the strong product brand without being undermined by the culture reality.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Recognizes that employer branding alone cannot fix a culture problem
- [ ] Addresses the culture issue as a prerequisite (not just a branding exercise)
- [ ] Designs a two-track approach: culture improvement + employer brand building
- [ ] Does NOT recommend hiding or suppressing the negative reviews
- [ ] Proposes authentic employer brand narrative that acknowledges the past
- [ ] Leverages the strong product brand as an asset (mission, impact, product pride)
- [ ] Designs Glassdoor response strategy (genuine engagement, not boilerplate)
- [ ] Includes employee advocacy program (turning culture improvements into stories)
- [ ] Addresses the 35% acceptance rate with candidate experience redesign
- [ ] Provides timeline with realistic culture change milestones
- [ ] Includes measurement: target Glassdoor rating, acceptance rate, time-to-hire
- [ ] Addresses the risk of performative employer branding (promising what you have not yet delivered)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 7: BRAND REFRESH VS. REBRAND

**Situation:**
A 20-year-old B2B company has strong brand equity in its market (65% aided awareness, NPS 42). However, the visual identity looks dated (designed in 2008, minor updates since). The company is expanding from its core market (financial services) into healthcare and government. Existing customers love the brand. New target markets find it "too corporate" and "cold."

**Task:**
Determine whether this requires a brand refresh or a full rebrand. Design the approach.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Clearly defines the difference between refresh and rebrand
- [ ] Analyzes what to keep (strong equity, customer loyalty, awareness) vs. what must change
- [ ] Evaluates the risk of alienating existing customers with too much change
- [ ] Evaluates the cost of not changing enough to appeal to new markets
- [ ] Recommends refresh or rebrand with clear rationale (refresh is almost certainly the right answer here)
- [ ] Identifies specific elements to evolve (visual updates) vs. preserve (brand values, positioning core)
- [ ] Designs market-specific adaptations within a unified brand (sector-specific messaging, imagery)
- [ ] Includes customer research plan (test changes with existing customers before committing)
- [ ] Plans for gradual rollout vs. big reveal
- [ ] Addresses internal change management (employees attached to current brand)
- [ ] Provides before/after brand perception targets for each market

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 8: MULTI-AUDIENCE BRAND MESSAGING

**Situation:**
A healthcare SaaS platform serves three distinct audiences: hospital administrators (care about cost reduction and compliance), clinical staff (care about ease of use and patient outcomes), and patients (care about accessibility and privacy). Each audience has fundamentally different priorities. The brand must resonate with all three, but current messaging tries to speak to everyone and resonates with no one.

**Task:**
Design the multi-audience brand messaging architecture.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Identifies the core brand truth that connects all three audiences
- [ ] Does NOT create three separate brands (maintains unified brand)
- [ ] Designs a messaging hierarchy: umbrella message + audience-specific layers
- [ ] Creates specific value propositions for each audience segment
- [ ] Maps messaging to touchpoints (which audience sees what, where)
- [ ] Addresses potential message conflicts (cost reduction vs. patient outcomes)
- [ ] Provides specific copy examples for each audience
- [ ] Designs a tone adaptation framework (same brand, different register)
- [ ] Includes channel strategy (different audiences, different channels)
- [ ] Plans for situations where audiences see each other's messaging
- [ ] Tests the messaging framework for clarity and resonance

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 9: BRAND NAMING PROJECT

**Situation:**
A startup is launching a new product in the personal finance space — an AI-powered savings tool that automatically moves money to the highest-yield accounts. Target audience: millennials and Gen Z. The founders want a name that feels "modern, trustworthy, and a little playful." The name must have: available .com domain, no trademark conflicts in the financial services category, and work internationally (no negative connotations in Spanish, Mandarin, or Arabic).

**Task:**
Design the naming process and deliver 5 name candidates with rationale.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Outlines a structured naming methodology (not just brainstorming)
- [ ] Defines naming criteria based on strategy (not just the founders' adjectives)
- [ ] Explores multiple naming conventions (descriptive, suggestive, abstract, invented, experiential)
- [ ] Delivers 5 candidates with detailed rationale for each
- [ ] Each name is evaluated against: memorability, pronounceability, domain availability, trademark risk, international viability
- [ ] Provides a shortlisting framework (how to narrow from 5 to 1)
- [ ] Includes brand story potential for each name (can the name support a narrative?)
- [ ] Addresses the tension between "trustworthy" and "playful" in finance
- [ ] Includes testing recommendation (how to validate with the target audience)
- [ ] Considers the name's longevity (will it age well if the product evolves beyond savings?)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 10: BRAND MEASUREMENT DESIGN

**Situation:**
The CMO wants a "brand dashboard" but the company has never measured brand health systematically. Budget for brand research: $25,000/year. The brand is B2B SaaS, 3 years old, 500 customers, 5,000 monthly website visitors. Category: HR technology. No previous brand tracking data exists.

**Task:**
Design a brand measurement program within the budget constraint.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Designs a measurement framework appropriate for the company's size and stage
- [ ] Does NOT recommend enterprise-level research methodology for a $25K budget
- [ ] Identifies the 5-7 most critical brand metrics for this stage
- [ ] Proposes measurement methods that fit the budget (surveys, social listening, analytics, customer interviews)
- [ ] Provides cost breakdown for each measurement activity
- [ ] Establishes baselines as the first priority (cannot track trends without starting point)
- [ ] Designs a cadence that balances thoroughness with budget reality
- [ ] Includes both quantitative and qualitative methods
- [ ] Connects brand metrics to business outcomes (why these metrics matter)
- [ ] Plans for scaling the program as the company and budget grow
- [ ] Includes a simple dashboard design (what the CMO actually sees)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 11: BRAND EXTENSION EVALUATION

**Situation:**
A popular direct-to-consumer coffee brand (known for premium single-origin beans, minimalist design, and sustainability) is considering three brand extensions: (A) a coffee shop chain, (B) a line of coffee-flavored alcoholic beverages, (C) branded coffee brewing equipment. Brand is 5 years old with strong loyalty (NPS 72) and a cult following. Revenue: $15M/year.

**Task:**
Evaluate each extension against brand fit. Recommend a go/no-go for each with rationale.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Defines brand extension evaluation criteria (brand fit, market opportunity, execution capability, equity risk)
- [ ] Evaluates each extension individually against the criteria
- [ ] Considers brand stretch: how far can the brand extend without dilution?
- [ ] Assesses cannibalization risk for each extension
- [ ] Evaluates operational capability for each extension (coffee shops vs. brewing equipment are very different businesses)
- [ ] Considers brand permission: would customers believe and accept each extension?
- [ ] Analyzes competitive landscape for each extension category
- [ ] Provides clear go/no-go for each with supporting rationale
- [ ] Recommends sequencing if multiple extensions are approved
- [ ] Addresses the brand equity protection plan (what guardrails prevent dilution)
- [ ] Includes failure criteria (when to pull back if an extension is not working)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 12: BRAND VOICE OVERHAUL

**Situation:**
A fintech company's brand voice is described internally as "professional and trustworthy." In practice, every piece of content sounds like it was written by a corporate lawyer. Customer feedback: "Your product is great but your emails are boring" and "I can never tell if your blog posts are satire because they are so dry." The CEO wants the voice to become "more human" without losing credibility.

**Task:**
Design the brand voice transformation strategy.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Diagnoses the root cause: "professional" was interpreted as "eliminate personality"
- [ ] Does NOT recommend swinging to the opposite extreme (too casual for fintech)
- [ ] Defines the new voice with specific personality traits (not just "more human")
- [ ] Creates a voice spectrum showing the range: where are we now, where do we want to be
- [ ] Provides before/after examples for different content types (email, blog, social, support, legal)
- [ ] Addresses the credibility concern directly (how to be human AND trustworthy in finance)
- [ ] Studies examples of fintech brands with effective human voices
- [ ] Creates a voice training program for content creators
- [ ] Designs a phased rollout (not overnight change)
- [ ] Includes a feedback mechanism to calibrate the new voice
- [ ] Plans for compliance review process (ensuring "human" voice still meets regulatory requirements)

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 13: BRAND LOCALIZATION

**Situation:**
A US-based SaaS brand is expanding to Japan, Germany, and Brazil. The brand name works in English but has no meaning in other languages. The brand personality is "bold, direct, and a little irreverent." Japanese business culture values subtlety and formality. German market expects precision and thoroughness. Brazilian market is warm and relationship-driven.

**Task:**
Design the brand localization strategy.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Distinguishes between translation (words) and localization (cultural adaptation)
- [ ] Identifies which brand elements are universal vs. which must adapt
- [ ] Addresses the personality adaptation challenge for each market specifically
- [ ] Proposes a localization framework: what stays (brand values, visual identity) vs. what flexes (voice, imagery, messaging)
- [ ] Does NOT recommend losing the core brand personality entirely
- [ ] Finds the culturally appropriate expression of each personality trait per market
- [ ] Includes local market research recommendation
- [ ] Plans for local team empowerment with global brand guardrails
- [ ] Addresses legal and regulatory naming requirements per market
- [ ] Includes measurement to validate localization effectiveness
- [ ] Provides examples of brands that successfully localized across these markets

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 14: BRAND POSITIONING PIVOT

**Situation:**
A brand positioned as "the affordable alternative" has grown and improved its product significantly. It now has feature parity with premium competitors and better customer support. However, the "affordable" positioning is attracting price-sensitive customers with high churn and low lifetime value. The CEO wants to move upmarket. Current price: $29/month. Premium competitors: $99-149/month.

**Task:**
Design the positioning pivot strategy from "affordable" to "premium value."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Analyzes the risk: existing customers chose "affordable" — price increase will cause churn
- [ ] Quantifies the trade-off: losing low-LTV customers vs. gaining high-LTV customers
- [ ] Designs a gradual positioning migration (not overnight rebrand)
- [ ] Proposes pricing strategy (grandfather existing, tiered, value-based)
- [ ] Addresses the product narrative: what justifies premium positioning?
- [ ] Designs the new positioning statement with competitive differentiation
- [ ] Plans for the "in-between" period (neither cheap nor premium)
- [ ] Includes customer communication strategy (explain the evolution)
- [ ] Addresses sales enablement (selling premium requires different approach)
- [ ] Defines measurement: LTV, churn by segment, brand perception shift
- [ ] Provides timeline with milestones and go/no-go gates

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 15: BRAND AUDIT

**Situation:**
You are hired to conduct a comprehensive brand audit for a 10-year-old B2B company. They have never had a formal brand audit. They have "brand guidelines" from 2018 that nobody follows. They have 3 product lines, 200 employees, and operations in 5 countries. The CEO says "our brand is fine" but the VP of Marketing says "our brand is a mess."

**Task:**
Design the brand audit methodology and deliverable structure.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Defines audit scope (internal, external, competitive)
- [ ] Designs stakeholder interview guide (who to talk to, what to ask)
- [ ] Includes internal brand perception research (employees at all levels)
- [ ] Includes external brand perception research (customers, prospects, partners)
- [ ] Plans competitive brand analysis methodology
- [ ] Designs cross-channel consistency audit (web, social, email, sales materials, product)
- [ ] Includes brand guideline compliance assessment
- [ ] Addresses the multi-country dimension (brand consistency across geographies)
- [ ] Designs the deliverable structure (executive summary, detailed findings, recommendations)
- [ ] Prioritizes findings by business impact (not just brand purity)
- [ ] Includes quick wins vs. strategic initiatives
- [ ] Plans for presenting findings to a skeptical CEO

**Score:** /5

## SCENARIO 16: BRAND COMMUNITY BUILDING

**Situation:**
A developer tools company wants to build a brand community. They have 10,000 users, a public Slack with 500 members (mostly asking support questions), a blog with 2,000 monthly readers, and a Twitter/X account with 3,000 followers. Current community engagement is transactional — users come when they need help and leave. The brand wants to become a "movement" in its category.

**Task:**
Design the brand community strategy that transforms transactional users into brand advocates.

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- [ ] Distinguishes between a support community and a brand community
- [ ] Identifies the shared identity or belief that can unite community members
- [ ] Designs community value proposition (why would someone participate beyond getting help?)
- [ ] Proposes community architecture (platforms, rituals, roles, progression)
- [ ] Includes content strategy that feeds community engagement
- [ ] Designs community rituals (recurring events, challenges, celebrations)
- [ ] Plans for community leadership development (turning users into champions)
- [ ] Addresses the transition from transactional to relational engagement
- [ ] Includes measurement framework (engagement depth, advocacy behavior, community health)
- [ ] Provides a phased rollout with realistic milestones
- [ ] Addresses sustainability (how does the community self-sustain without constant company effort)

**Score:** /5

## BENCHMARK ADMINISTRATION RULES

- Scenarios must be presented without leading hints
- Responses must be evaluated against ALL criteria, not just a subset
- Partial credit is reflected in the 1-5 score (not pass/fail per criterion)
- Evaluator must document specific evidence for score justification
- Re-testing after remediation uses different scenarios of equivalent difficulty
- Benchmark results are recorded in the branding competency log

---

## ENFORCEMENT RULE

These benchmarks test real branding competency, not theoretical knowledge.
Vague answers score 1. Strategic but incomplete answers score 2-3.
Only comprehensive, actionable, measurable responses score 4-5.
If it would not build brand equity in practice, it does not pass the benchmark.
