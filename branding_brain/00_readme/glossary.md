# Glossary — Branding Brain

## Canonical Definitions

This glossary establishes the authoritative definitions for all brand-related terminology used within the Branding Brain. When terms are used in any brain output, these definitions govern. Definitions are sourced from academic literature and established practitioner frameworks.

---

## A

### Aided Awareness
The percentage of a target audience that recognizes a brand when prompted with the brand name or logo. Contrast with **Unaided Awareness**. Measured via survey: "Which of the following brands do you recognize?" (Keller, 2013).

### Archetype (Brand)
A universally recognized character pattern (e.g., Hero, Creator, Caregiver) used as a strategic framework for brand personality. Derived from Jungian psychology. Popularized by Margaret Mark and Carol Pearson in *The Hero and the Outlaw* (2001). The twelve archetypes provide a shortcut to emotional resonance.

### Asset (Brand)
A recognizable element uniquely associated with a brand — including but not limited to the name, logo, color, shape, sound, character, tagline, and typeface. Jenni Romaniuk defines distinctive brand assets as elements that trigger the brand in memory without the brand name being present (Romaniuk, 2018).

### Association (Brand)
Any mental link connected to a brand in consumer memory. Keller's CBBE model categorizes associations by type (attributes, benefits, attitudes), favorability, strength, and uniqueness (Keller, 1993). Strong brands have favorable, strong, and unique associations.

---

## B

### Brand
A brand is a **name, term, sign, symbol, design, or combination** that identifies the goods or services of one seller and differentiates them from competitors (American Marketing Association). More broadly, Neumeier defines a brand as "a person's gut feeling about a product, service, or organization" — it exists in the mind, not on the page (Neumeier, 2006).

### Brand Architecture
The organizing structure of a brand portfolio that defines the roles and relationships between brands. Primary models: **Branded House** (single master brand), **House of Brands** (independent brands), **Endorsed Brands** (parent endorses sub-brands), and **Hybrid** (mixed approach). See Aaker & Joachimsthaler (2000).

### Brand Audit
A systematic examination of a brand's current position, performance, and health relative to competitors and its own strategy. Includes internal analysis (brand identity, consistency), external analysis (awareness, perception, competitive position), and gap analysis.

### Brand Book
A comprehensive document (physical or digital) that codifies a brand's identity system — including strategy, visual identity, verbal identity, and usage guidelines. Also called brand guidelines, brand standards, or brand manual.

### Brand Equity
The incremental value a brand adds to a product or service beyond its functional benefits. Keller defines **Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE)** as "the differential effect that brand knowledge has on consumer response to the marketing of that brand" (Keller, 1993). Aaker defines brand equity as "a set of assets and liabilities linked to a brand that add to or subtract from the value provided by a product or service" (Aaker, 1991).

### Brand Essence
The single most compelling thing a brand can say about itself. A distillation of the brand's identity into a short phrase (typically 2-5 words) that captures its fundamental nature. Not a tagline — an internal strategic tool.

### Brand Extension
The use of an established brand name to enter a new product category. Success depends on perceived fit between the parent brand and the extension category (Aaker & Keller, 1990). Contrast with **Line Extension** (same category, new variant).

### Brand Gap
The distance between business strategy and customer experience, as defined by Marty Neumeier. The Brand Gap exists when what a company says about itself does not match what customers actually experience (Neumeier, 2006).

### Brand Health
A composite measure of a brand's performance across key metrics: awareness, consideration, preference, usage, loyalty, and advocacy. Tracked over time via **Brand Health Dashboards**.

### Brand Identity
The aspirational set of associations a brand strategist seeks to create or maintain. Aaker's Brand Identity Planning Model distinguishes between **Core Identity** (timeless essence) and **Extended Identity** (elements providing texture and completeness). Kapferer's **Brand Identity Prism** maps six facets: Physique, Personality, Culture, Relationship, Reflection, Self-image.

### Brand Image
The actual set of associations consumers hold about a brand. Brand Image is the **receiver's** perception; Brand Identity is the **sender's** intention. The gap between identity and image represents a branding failure.

### Brand Loyalty
The degree to which consumers consistently choose a brand over alternatives. Aaker identifies five levels: Switchers, Habitual Buyers, Satisfied Buyers with Switching Costs, Brand Likers, and Committed Buyers (Aaker, 1991).

### Brand Mantra
A short articulation (3-5 words) of a brand's core promise and values. Used internally to guide decisions. Example: Nike's brand mantra is "Authentic Athletic Performance" (Keller, 2013).

### Brand Personality
The set of human characteristics associated with a brand. Aaker's Brand Personality Scale identifies five dimensions: Sincerity, Excitement, Competence, Sophistication, and Ruggedness (Aaker, 1997).

### Brand Positioning
The act of designing a brand's offering and image to occupy a distinct place in the target market's mind. Ries and Trout (1981) define positioning not as what you do to a product, but what you do to the mind of the prospect.

### Brand Premium
The price increment a branded product commands over an unbranded or generic equivalent. A direct financial manifestation of brand equity.

### Brand Promise
The explicit or implicit commitment a brand makes to its customers about the experience they can expect. Must be deliverable, differentiating, and relevant.

### Brand Resonance
The pinnacle of Keller's CBBE pyramid. Achieved when customers feel a deep psychological bond with the brand, characterized by behavioral loyalty, attitudinal attachment, sense of community, and active engagement (Keller, 2001).

### Brand Salience
The degree to which a brand is thought about or noticed when a customer is in a buying situation. Byron Sharp argues salience (mental availability) is the primary driver of growth (Sharp, 2010).

### Brand Valuation
The financial estimation of a brand's worth. Methodologies include **Interbrand** (role of brand x brand strength x financial forecast), **Royalty Relief** (hypothetical licensing cost), and **Conjoint Analysis** (consumer-derived value attribution).

### Branded House
A brand architecture model where a single master brand spans all products/services. Example: Google (Google Maps, Google Drive, Google Cloud). Maximizes brand investment efficiency but concentrates risk.

## C

### Category Entry Point (CEP)
The internal cues (needs, motivations, occasions) that consumers use to access their memory when faced with a buying situation. Romaniuk and Sharp argue that brands must link to as many CEPs as possible to maximize mental availability (Romaniuk, 2018).

### Co-Branding
A strategic alliance where two brands combine to create a product or experience. Success requires brand fit, complementary strengths, and clear value for the consumer.

### Color Psychology (Brand)
The study of how colors influence brand perception and consumer behavior. Colors carry cultural, contextual, and category-specific meanings. Brand color selection must consider distinctiveness within category, cultural associations, accessibility (contrast ratios), and emotional resonance.

### Consideration Set
The subset of brands a consumer actively evaluates when making a purchase decision. Brand strategy aims to ensure the brand enters the consideration set for as many category entry points as possible.

---

## D

### Design System (Brand)
A systematized collection of brand tokens (colors, typography, spacing, radii), components, patterns, and guidelines that enable consistent brand expression across platforms and touchpoints. The brand layer of a design system sits atop the functional design system.

### Distinctive Assets
Brand elements that are uniquely associated with a specific brand and trigger brand recognition without the brand name. Romaniuk's Distinctive Asset Grid maps assets on two dimensions: Fame (% who associate asset with brand) and Uniqueness (% who associate asset only with that brand).

### Double Jeopardy Law
An empirical regularity in brand metrics: smaller brands have both fewer buyers AND lower loyalty among those buyers. Discovered by Andrew Ehrenberg and extensively documented by the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute. Implication: growth comes primarily from acquiring new buyers, not increasing loyalty.

---

## E

### Employer Brand
The perception of an organization as an employer — among current employees, candidates, and the broader talent market. Distinct from consumer brand but must align with it.

### Employer Value Proposition (EVP)
The unique set of benefits an employee receives in return for their skills, capabilities, and experience. The EVP is to talent what brand positioning is to customers.

### Endorsed Brand
A brand architecture model where sub-brands carry a visible endorsement from a parent brand. Example: Courtyard by Marriott. The parent provides credibility; the sub-brand provides category-specific positioning.

---

## H

### House of Brands
A brand architecture model where a parent company operates multiple independent brands with minimal visible connection to the corporate brand. Example: Procter & Gamble (Tide, Pampers, Gillette). Maximizes brand targeting but increases investment requirements.

---

## I

### Identity Prism
Jean-Noel Kapferer's model mapping six facets of brand identity: **Physique** (physical features), **Personality** (character), **Culture** (values), **Relationship** (mode of conduct), **Reflection** (consumer mirror), and **Self-Image** (internal consumer mirror). The prism distinguishes between externalization (physique, relationship, reflection) and internalization (personality, culture, self-image).

---

## K

### Keller's CBBE Pyramid
A four-level model of Customer-Based Brand Equity: (1) **Identity** — Who are you? (salience), (2) **Meaning** — What are you? (performance + imagery), (3) **Response** — What about you? (judgments + feelings), (4) **Relationships** — What about you and me? (resonance). Each level builds on the one below.

---

## M

### Mental Availability
The probability that a brand will be thought of in a buying situation. Driven by the quantity and quality of memory structures (brand-linked associations) and the breadth of category entry points linked to the brand. Central concept in Sharp's *How Brands Grow* (2010).

### Messaging Framework
A hierarchical document that organizes brand messages by audience, context, and priority. Typically includes: brand narrative, positioning statement, value propositions (by segment), proof points, and key messages (by channel/audience).

---

## N

### Net Promoter Score (NPS)
A metric developed by Fred Reichheld measuring customer loyalty by asking: "On a scale of 0-10, how likely are you to recommend [brand] to a friend?" Promoters (9-10) minus Detractors (0-6) = NPS. While widely used, NPS has limitations as a standalone brand metric.

---

## P

### Perceived Quality
The consumer's judgment about a product's overall excellence or superiority. One of Aaker's four pillars of brand equity. Not objective quality — the consumer's subjective assessment based on brand cues, experience, and word-of-mouth.

### Physical Availability
The ease with which a consumer can find and buy a brand. Alongside mental availability, one of Sharp's two growth levers. Includes distribution, shelf presence, and digital findability.

### Positioning Statement
A formal articulation of brand positioning following the structure: "For [target audience], [brand] is the [frame of reference] that [point of difference] because [reason to believe]."

---

## S

### Semiotics (Brand)
The study of signs and symbols in brand communication. Brand semiotics analyzes how visual, verbal, and experiential signs create meaning. Includes icon (resemblance), index (causal connection), and symbol (arbitrary convention) per C.S. Peirce.

### Sensory Branding
The strategic use of multi-sensory cues (sight, sound, touch, smell, taste) to build brand associations and trigger memory structures. Martin Lindstrom's research demonstrates that multi-sensory branding significantly strengthens brand recall and emotional attachment.

---

## T

### Tagline
A memorable phrase that captures the brand's positioning or promise for external communication. Distinct from **Brand Mantra** (internal) and **Brand Essence** (strategic). Must be distinctive, memorable, and aligned with positioning.

### Tone of Voice
The emotional inflection applied to brand voice in specific contexts. While brand voice remains constant (e.g., "confident, warm, precise"), tone shifts based on situation (e.g., celebratory in wins, empathetic in crises). Voice is personality; tone is mood.

### Touchpoint
Any point of interaction between a brand and a stakeholder. Includes pre-purchase (advertising, content, word-of-mouth), purchase (store, website, sales), post-purchase (support, packaging, loyalty program), and passive (media coverage, reviews, social mentions).

---

## U

### Unaided Awareness
The percentage of a target audience that can recall a brand without prompting when asked about a product category. "Which brands come to mind when you think of [category]?" The strongest measure of brand salience.

---

## V

### Visual Identity System
The collection of visual elements that together express a brand's identity: logo (and variations), color palette, typography, iconography, photography style, illustration style, layout principles, and motion/animation guidelines.

### Voice (Brand)
The consistent expression of a brand's personality through language. Defined by a set of voice attributes (3-5 adjectives) that govern all written and spoken communication. Voice remains constant across contexts; **Tone** adapts.

---

## W

### White Space (Brand)
Unoccupied positions in a competitive landscape where no brand has established strong associations. Identifying brand white space is a key strategic exercise for positioning and extension.
