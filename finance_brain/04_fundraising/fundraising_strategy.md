# Fundraising Strategy — Stages, Timing, Sizing, and Process Design

## Overview

Fundraising is the process of securing external capital to accelerate business
growth. The strategy must balance dilution against growth velocity, match the
right investor type to the company's stage, and execute a process that creates
competitive tension. This module covers the venture fundraising lifecycle from
pre-seed through pre-IPO, round sizing mathematics, dilution mechanics, investor
segmentation, and process orchestration.

References: Kupor (Secrets of Sand Hill Road), Feld & Mendelson (Venture Deals),
Wilson (AVC blog), YC Startup Library, Bessemer Venture Partners Cloud Index.

## Fundraising Stages

### Stage Overview

| Stage | Typical Size | Valuation | Dilution | Purpose |
|-------|-------------|-----------|----------|---------|
| Pre-Seed | $250K-$1M | $2-6M post | 15-20% | Validate idea, build MVP |
| Seed | $1-4M | $6-15M post | 15-25% | Product-market fit |
| Series A | $5-15M | $20-60M pre | 15-25% | Repeatable growth |
| Series B | $15-50M | $60-200M pre | 15-20% | Scale operations |
| Series C | $50-150M | $200-500M pre | 10-15% | Market dominance |
| Series D+ | $100M+ | $500M+ pre | 5-15% | Pre-IPO, expansion |
| Growth/Pre-IPO | $200M+ | $1B+ pre | 5-10% | IPO preparation |

### Stage-Specific Milestones

**Pre-Seed**
- Founding team assembled
- Problem validated through user research
- MVP or prototype built
- Initial users or design partners

**Seed**
- Product live with early customers
- Initial traction metrics (users, revenue, engagement)
- Early signs of product-market fit
- Clear go-to-market hypothesis

**Series A**
- Product-market fit demonstrated quantitatively
- Repeatable customer acquisition channel(s)
- Unit economics trending positive (or clear path)
- $1-3M ARR (SaaS) or strong engagement metrics (consumer)
- Clear path to $100M+ revenue

**Series B**
- $5-15M ARR
- Strong growth rate (2-3x YoY)
- Proven unit economics (LTV:CAC > 3x)
- Expanding into new segments or geographies
- Management team buildout

**Series C+**
- $20-50M+ ARR
- Category leadership or clear path to it
- International expansion
- M&A capability
- IPO trajectory visible

## Timing Signals

### When to Raise

Raise when you have **maximum leverage**, not when you need money:

```
Ideal timing indicators:
  - Revenue growth is accelerating (not just growing)
  - Key milestone just achieved (product launch, major customer)
  - Market timing is favorable (sector is hot)
  - You have 9-12 months of runway remaining
  - You can articulate clear use of funds

Warning signs to delay:
  - Metrics are declining or flat
  - You are raising out of desperation (< 3 months runway)
  - The market is in a downturn (compressed valuations)
  - You cannot clearly articulate what changes with funding
```

### Runway Calculation

```
Monthly Burn Rate = Monthly Cash Outflow - Monthly Cash Inflow
Runway (months) = Cash Balance / Monthly Burn Rate

Example:
  Cash balance: $2,000,000
  Monthly revenue: $150,000
  Monthly expenses: $350,000
  Net burn: $200,000/month
  Runway: 10 months

Start fundraising at 9-12 months runway:
  Process takes 3-6 months
  You want to close with 6+ months remaining
```

## Round Sizing

### How Much to Raise

```
Target Raise = Monthly Burn Rate (post-raise) * Months to Next Milestone + Buffer

Formula:
  Raise = (Current_Burn + Planned_Hiring_Burn) * 18-24 months * 1.2 (buffer)

Example:
  Current burn: $200K/month
  Planned hiring: +$150K/month by month 6
  Average burn over 18 months: ~$310K/month
  Raise needed: $310K * 18 * 1.2 = $6.7M → raise $7M
```

### Raising Too Little vs Too Much

| Too Little | Just Right | Too Much |
|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Constant fundraising mode | 18-24 months runway | Excessive dilution |
| Distracted from building | Clear milestones for next round | Pressure to deploy capital |
| Weak negotiating position | Competitive tension achievable | Valuation expectations too high |
| Risk of bridge/down round | Focus on execution | Governance complexity |

## Dilution Mathematics

### Basic Dilution

```
New Investor Ownership = Investment / Post-Money Valuation
Founder Dilution = 1 - (Pre-money / Post-money)

Example:
  Pre-money valuation: $20M
  Investment: $5M
  Post-money valuation: $25M
  New investor ownership: $5M / $25M = 20%
  Existing shareholders diluted: 100% -> 80% (20% dilution)
```

### Cumulative Dilution Across Rounds

```
Round    Pre-Money  Raise   Post-Money  New %   Founder Ownership
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Seed     $5M        $1.5M   $6.5M       23.1%   76.9%
Series A $25M       $7M     $32M        21.9%   60.1%
Series B $100M      $25M    $125M       20.0%   48.1%
Series C $300M      $50M    $350M       14.3%   41.2%
IPO      $800M      $100M   $900M       11.1%   36.6%

Note: does not include option pool dilution (additional 10-20%)
```

### Option Pool Shuffle

Investors often require an option pool to be created or expanded BEFORE
the investment, diluting existing shareholders (not the new investor):

```
Without option pool shuffle:
  Pre-money: $20M, Raise: $5M, Post-money: $25M
  Investor gets: 20%, Founders retain: 80%

With 15% option pool created pre-money:
  Effective pre-money for founders: $20M - ($25M * 15%) = $16.25M
  Investor gets: 20%, Option pool: 15%, Founders retain: 65%

Impact: 15 percentage points of additional dilution
```

## Investor Types

### By Stage

| Investor Type | Typical Check Size | Stage | Value-Add |
|--------------|-------------------|-------|-----------|
| Friends & Family | $25K-$250K | Pre-Seed | Trust, patience |
| Angel investors | $25K-$500K | Pre-Seed/Seed | Network, mentorship |
| Angel syndicates | $250K-$2M | Seed | Pooled angels, deal flow |
| Micro VCs | $500K-$5M | Seed/A | Hands-on, specialized |
| Institutional VCs | $5M-$50M+ | Series A-C | Board, network, brand |
| Growth equity | $25M-$200M+ | Series C+ | Operational, IPO prep |
| Corporate VCs (CVC) | Variable | Any | Strategic, distribution |
| Crossover funds | $50M-$500M+ | Late/Pre-IPO | Public market expertise |

### What Investors Look For By Stage

**Seed**: Team, market size, insight, early traction
**Series A**: Product-market fit proof, repeatable GTM, unit economics path
**Series B**: Growth rate, scalable GTM, strong retention, team depth
**Series C+**: Market leadership, path to profitability, IPO readiness

## Process Design

### Timeline

```
Weeks -4 to -2: Preparation
  - Update financials, metrics deck, data room
  - Build target investor list (50-80 firms)
  - Research partner-level preferences
  - Warm intro campaign through existing investors

Weeks -2 to 0: Soft Launch
  - Share deck with 5-10 closest relationships
  - Gather feedback, iterate on narrative
  - Identify objections and prepare answers

Weeks 1-3: Main Process
  - Launch formal process (all meetings in compressed window)
  - First meetings with 20-30 firms in Week 1
  - Partner meetings / deep dives in Week 2-3
  - Reference checks by investors

Weeks 3-5: Term Sheets and Close
  - Receive term sheets
  - Negotiate terms (see term_sheets.md)
  - Select lead investor
  - Close round (legal, wire funds)
```

### Fundraising Materials

| Material | Purpose | Length |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Teaser email | Get the first meeting | 5-7 sentences |
| Pitch deck | Tell the story in a meeting | 15-20 slides |
| Metrics appendix | Detailed performance data | 10-15 slides |
| Financial model | Demonstrate planning rigor | Spreadsheet |
| Data room | Due diligence materials | 20-50 documents |
| Memo | Investor's internal write-up | You draft first |

### Pitch Deck Structure (15-20 Slides)

```
1.  Cover: Company name, tagline, stage
2.  Problem: Pain point, who feels it, how they solve it today
3.  Solution: Your product, key differentiator
4.  Demo / Product: Screenshots, workflow
5.  Market: TAM/SAM/SOM with bottom-up analysis
6.  Traction: Revenue, users, growth rate, retention
7.  Business model: Revenue streams, pricing, unit economics
8.  Go-to-market: Channels, CAC, sales motion
9.  Competition: Differentiation matrix (NOT a feature grid)
10. Team: Founders and key hires, relevant experience
11. Financials: P&L summary, key assumptions
12. Use of funds: What this raise enables
13. Ask: Amount raising, use of proceeds, target milestones
```

### Creating Competitive Tension

```
Principles:
  - Run a tight process (3-5 weeks, not 3-5 months)
  - Create a compressed meeting schedule (signal demand)
  - Do not share who else you are talking to
  - Set a clear timeline for term sheets
  - Be transparent about process stage

FOMO triggers:
  - "We have a term sheet and are finalizing next week"
  - "We are meeting with N firms this week"
  - "Our metrics just hit [milestone]"
  - References from respected angels/operators
```

## Investor Selection Criteria

### Evaluation Framework

| Criterion | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| Brand/Signal value | 0.20 | Does the firm's brand help with recruiting, customers, next round? |
| Partner quality | 0.25 | Does this specific partner add value? Board experience? |
| Terms | 0.20 | Valuation, governance, pro-rata, information rights |
| Domain expertise | 0.15 | Does the firm know your market? |
| Portfolio support | 0.10 | Platform services (recruiting, marketing, finance) |
| Fund lifecycle | 0.10 | New fund (active) vs end of fund (less capital for follow-on) |

### Reference Check Questions

Ask founders in the investor's portfolio:
1. How did [partner] behave when things went badly?
2. Did they lead or follow in difficult board decisions?
3. How helpful were they with recruiting and business development?
4. Would you take their money again?
5. How responsive are they when you need help?

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Prevention |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Raising too early | Poor terms, excessive dilution | Wait for leverage |
| No competitive tension | Single term sheet, weak terms | Run parallel process |
| Optimizing only for valuation | Bad partner, bad terms | Evaluate holistically |
| Too long a process | Signal weakness, distraction | 3-5 week compressed process |
| Sharing too much too early | Lose negotiating leverage | Gate information |
| Not checking references | Bad board member | Always reference check |
