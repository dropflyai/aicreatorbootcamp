# Exit Planning — IPO, Acquisition, and Alternative Exit Paths

## Overview

Exit planning is the strategic preparation for a liquidity event that allows
founders, employees, and investors to realize returns on their investment. The
three primary exit paths -- IPO, acquisition, and direct listing -- each have
distinct requirements, timelines, and trade-offs. This module covers exit path
selection, IPO readiness requirements, S-1 preparation, lock-up considerations,
SPAC mechanics, and management buyout structures.

References: Draho (The IPO Decision), Espinasse (IPO: A Global Guide),
SEC Form S-1 requirements, NYSE/NASDAQ listing standards, Latham & Watkins
(IPO Guide), Goldman Sachs Equity Capital Markets.

## Exit Path Comparison

| Factor | IPO | Acquisition | Direct Listing | SPAC |
|--------|-----|-------------|----------------|------|
| Capital raised | Yes (primary) | No (cash to shareholders) | No (secondary only) | Yes |
| Valuation control | Market-driven | Negotiated | Market-driven | Negotiated |
| Timeline | 12-24 months | 3-12 months | 6-12 months | 4-8 months |
| Cost | $5-15M | $1-5M (legal, banking) | $3-8M | $5-10M |
| Ongoing obligations | Full public reporting | None (if fully acquired) | Full public reporting | Full public reporting |
| Shareholder liquidity | Partial (lock-up) | Full (at close) | Full (at listing) | Partial |
| Control retained | Partial | None | Full | Varies |
| Best for | Large, growing companies | Strategic fit, fatigue | Well-known brands | Faster path to public |

### When to Pursue Each Path

```
IPO:
  - ARR > $100M or strong growth trajectory to $100M+
  - Predictable revenue (subscription model, high retention)
  - Public market comparable exists
  - Company wants long-term independence
  - Need primary capital for growth

Acquisition:
  - Strategic synergies with buyer > standalone value
  - Founders seeking full liquidity
  - Market conditions unfavorable for IPO
  - Company has plateaued or is in competitive pressure
  - Acqui-hire (talent value exceeds business value)

Direct Listing:
  - Strong brand recognition (no need for IPO "roadshow marketing")
  - No need for primary capital
  - Existing shareholders want full liquidity (no lock-up)
  - Example: Spotify, Coinbase, Roblox
```

## IPO Readiness

### IPO Readiness Assessment

```
Financial readiness:
  [ ] 3 years of audited financials (or 2 for emerging growth company)
  [ ] GAAP-compliant revenue recognition (ASC 606)
  [ ] Clean audit opinions (no material weaknesses)
  [ ] Predictable revenue forecasting (< 5% miss rate)
  [ ] Gross margins > 60% (SaaS)
  [ ] Path to profitability articulated

Operational readiness:
  [ ] SOX 404 controls implemented (or timeline for EGC)
  [ ] Board composition: majority independent directors
  [ ] Audit committee: financially literate, independent
  [ ] Compensation committee: independent
  [ ] Investor relations function staffed
  [ ] Public company accounting team hired (controller, SEC reporting)

Legal readiness:
  [ ] IP portfolio documented and protected
  [ ] No material litigation pending
  [ ] All equity grants properly documented (409A, 83(b))
  [ ] Cap table clean and reconciled
  [ ] Related party transactions documented and disclosed
  [ ] D&O insurance increased to public company levels
```

### IPO Timeline

```
T-24 months: Begin IPO readiness workstream
  - Engage external auditor (Big 4)
  - Implement SOX controls
  - Hire VP Finance / Controller with public company experience
  - Begin building IR function

T-12 months: Select underwriters
  - "Bake-off": invite 5-7 banks to pitch
  - Select 1-2 lead underwriters (bookrunners)
  - Engage IPO counsel (company and underwriter counsel)

T-9 months: Organizational meeting
  - All parties meet to kick off process
  - Timeline set, responsibilities assigned
  - Begin drafting S-1

T-6 months: S-1 drafting
  - Business description, risk factors, financials
  - MD&A (Management Discussion and Analysis)
  - Multiple rounds of SEC comments

T-3 months: Confidential S-1 filing
  - Filed with SEC (non-public for up to 15 days before roadshow)
  - SEC review and comment letters
  - Respond to comments, amend S-1

T-6 weeks: Public S-1 filing
  - S-1 becomes public
  - Testing-the-waters meetings (JOBS Act for EGC)

T-2 weeks: Roadshow
  - 1-2 weeks, 7-10 cities
  - CEO and CFO present to institutional investors
  - Book-building: investors indicate interest and price sensitivity

T-0: Pricing and trading
  - Price set night before trading begins
  - Shares begin trading on NYSE or NASDAQ
  - First-day "pop" target: 10-20%
```

## S-1 Preparation

### Key S-1 Sections

| Section | Content | Critical Elements |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| Prospectus summary | Business overview, offering terms | Compelling narrative |
| Risk factors | Material risks to the business | Comprehensive but specific |
| Use of proceeds | How IPO capital will be used | Specific allocation |
| Business | Detailed company description | Market, product, strategy |
| MD&A | Management analysis of financials | Key metrics, trends, drivers |
| Financial statements | 3 years audited + interim | GAAP-compliant, audit opinion |
| Compensation | Executive and director pay | Stock comp, performance |
| Related party | Transactions with insiders | Complete disclosure |
| Shares eligible for sale | Lock-up, insider holdings | Lock-up schedule |

### Key Metrics for SaaS S-1

```
Must-include metrics:
  - ARR and growth rate
  - Net Revenue Retention (NRR / NDR)
  - Number of customers (and >$100K ACV customers)
  - Dollar-Based Net Retention (DBNR)
  - Remaining Performance Obligation (RPO)
  - Non-GAAP operating income and FCF
  - Rule of 40 (growth + margin)
  - Customer acquisition efficiency (magic number)

Nice-to-have:
  - Gross retention rate
  - LTV:CAC ratio
  - Payback period
  - Platform usage metrics (API calls, data processed)
```

## Lock-Up Agreements

### Standard Terms

```
Duration:   180 days (6 months) from IPO date
Applies to: Officers, directors, > 1% shareholders, VC investors
Restriction: Cannot sell, transfer, or hedge shares during lock-up
Early release: Underwriter may release 25-50% at 90 days

Lock-up expiration effect:
  - Sudden increase in tradeable shares (supply shock)
  - Stock price often drops 5-15% around lock-up expiry
  - Managed through staggered release and IR communication
```

### Planning for Lock-Up

```
Pre-IPO liquidity options:
  - Secondary sale: sell shares in tender offer before IPO
  - Rule 10b5-1 plans: set up automatic selling plans before lock-up
  - Insider trading windows: typically 2 weeks after earnings

Post-lock-up selling plan:
  Week 0: Lock-up expires
  Week 1: Sell 10% of holdings (test market absorption)
  Month 1-3: Sell additional 15-20% on a schedule
  10b5-1 plan: pre-programmed selling removes decision-making
```

## SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company)

### How SPACs Work

```
1. SPAC IPO: blank-check company raises capital in IPO ($200M-$1B+)
2. De-SPAC: SPAC identifies and merges with a private company
3. Result: private company becomes public through the merger

Timeline:
  SPAC has 18-24 months to complete a merger, or returns cash to investors

For the target company:
  - Faster path to public (4-8 months vs 12-24 for traditional IPO)
  - Negotiated valuation (not market-determined)
  - Can include forward projections in proxy (not allowed in S-1)
  - PIPE (Private Investment in Public Equity) often included
```

### SPAC Economics

```
SPAC sponsor economics:
  - Sponsor gets 20% of post-IPO shares ("promote") for minimal investment
  - Founders' shares dilute other shareholders significantly
  - Warrants add further dilution

Example:
  SPAC raises $300M in IPO
  Sponsor owns: 20% (75M shares = $75M value for $25K investment)
  Total shares: 375M
  Target valued at: $1B

  Post-merger:
    SPAC cash: $300M (minus redemptions)
    Sponsor shares: ~7% dilution to target shareholders
    Warrant dilution: additional 3-5%

Effective cost: SPACs are expensive compared to traditional IPO
```

### SPAC Considerations

| Pro | Con |
|-----|-----|
| Speed to market | Sponsor dilution (15-25%) |
| Certainty of value | SPAC share redemptions reduce capital |
| Forward projections allowed | Regulatory scrutiny increasing |
| Less market risk | Negative market perception (2022-2024) |
| Public currency for M&A | Higher long-term cost than IPO |

## Management Buyout (MBO)

### When to Consider

```
Appropriate situations:
  - Founders want to buy out investor shareholders
  - Company is profitable but not on VC-return trajectory
  - Private equity recapitalization opportunity
  - Division or product spin-out from larger company

Not appropriate:
  - Company requires significant additional capital
  - Management cannot secure financing
  - Investor relationships are contentious
```

### MBO Structure

```
Sources of financing:
  Management equity:     10-20% (rollover + new investment)
  Senior debt:           40-50% (bank term loan)
  Mezzanine/sub debt:    10-20% (higher rate, flexible)
  Sponsor equity:        20-30% (if PE-backed)

Example:
  Company EV: $50M
  Senior debt (4x EBITDA of $5M): $20M (40%)
  Mezzanine debt: $10M (20%)
  PE sponsor equity: $15M (30%)
  Management rollover + new: $5M (10%)

Management owns 10-20% initially, with option to increase
through performance incentives (ratchets).
```

## Exit Value Maximization

### Pre-Exit Optimization

```
12-24 months before exit:
  - Accelerate revenue growth (highest value driver)
  - Improve retention and NRR (stability premium)
  - Reduce customer concentration (risk discount)
  - Clean up financials (consistent GAAP, clear metrics)
  - Resolve legal issues (IP, litigation, compliance)
  - Build management depth (reduce key-person risk)
  - Optimize pricing (demonstrate pricing power)
  - Document everything (reduce diligence friction)
```

### Valuation Drivers

```
Premium factors (increase multiple):
  - High growth rate (>40% YoY): +2-5x revenue multiple
  - NRR > 120%: +1-3x revenue multiple
  - Gross margin > 80%: premium to SaaS comps
  - Rule of 40 > 40%: significantly higher multiples
  - Market leadership position
  - Large TAM with low penetration
  - Predictable, recurring revenue

Discount factors (decrease multiple):
  - Customer concentration (top 3 > 40%): -1-3x
  - Declining growth: -2-5x
  - Below-market retention: -1-3x
  - Pending litigation: case-by-case
  - Key person dependency: -0.5-2x
  - Technical debt: -0.5-1x
```
