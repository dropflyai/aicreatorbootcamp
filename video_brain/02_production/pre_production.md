# Pre-Production -- Concept Development, Scripting, Storyboarding, and Planning

## The Pre-Production Imperative

Pre-production is the highest-leverage phase of video production. Every hour invested in pre-production saves 3-5 hours in production and 5-10 hours in post-production. The inverse is also true: every shortcut in pre-production compounds into costly problems downstream. Films, commercials, and professional video productions allocate 30-40% of total project time to pre-production. Creator and marketing video should allocate no less than 25%.

The objective of pre-production is to eliminate uncertainty before expensive resources (time, people, equipment, locations) are committed.

---

## 1. Concept Development

### The Creative Brief

Every video begins with a creative brief. No exceptions. A video without a creative brief is a production without a destination -- it may arrive somewhere, but rarely where it needed to go.

**Mandatory Creative Brief Elements:**

| Element | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Objective | What business or content goal does this video serve? | "Drive 500 email sign-ups from YouTube" |
| Target Audience | Who specifically is this for? Demographics, psychographics, viewing context. | "SaaS founders, 25-40, watching during lunch" |
| Key Message | The single most important takeaway. If the viewer remembers one thing. | "Automated testing saves 20 hours/week" |
| Platform(s) | Where will this video live? Determines format, length, and style. | "YouTube primary, Shorts + Reels repurpose" |
| Format | What type of video? (Tutorial, testimonial, narrative, etc.) | "Screen-share tutorial with talking head intro" |
| Length Target | Approximate duration based on platform and content depth. | "8-12 minutes" |
| Tone | Emotional register and communication style. | "Professional but approachable, slight humor" |
| CTA | What specific action should the viewer take? | "Click link in description for free template" |
| Success Metrics | How will success be measured? | "10,000 views, 8% CTR, 500 link clicks" |
| Constraints | Budget, timeline, equipment, location, talent limitations. | "$0 budget, home studio, solo presenter" |
| References | Example videos that represent the desired style or quality. | "[links to 2-3 reference videos]" |

### Concept Validation Framework

Before committing to production, validate the concept:

**Audience Demand Test:**
- Does search volume exist for this topic? (YouTube keyword research via TubeBuddy, vidIQ, or Google Trends)
- Are competitors creating similar content? If yes, what is the differentiation angle?
- Has the target audience expressed interest in this topic? (Comments, social media, surveys)

**Uniqueness Test:**
- What does this video offer that existing content does not?
- Can the unique angle be communicated in the title/thumbnail?
- Is the differentiation obvious within the first 10 seconds?

**Feasibility Test:**
- Can this be produced within the stated constraints (budget, time, equipment)?
- Does the team have the skills required?
- Are there legal or licensing requirements (music, footage, talent)?

**Strategic Alignment Test:**
- Does this video serve the channel's content pillars?
- Does it align with the current marketing funnel priority?
- Does it contribute to the long-term content strategy?

## 2. Scriptwriting Process

### The Scriptwriting Pipeline

```
Research --> Outline --> First Draft --> Hook Optimization -->
Retention Review --> Read-Aloud Test --> Final Draft --> Approval
```

### Step 1: Research

Before writing a single word, assemble the information architecture:

**Content Research:**
- Identify the 3-5 key points the video must cover
- Research each point to ensure accuracy and depth
- Identify counterarguments or common misconceptions to address
- Find data, statistics, or evidence to support claims

**Audience Research:**
- Review comments on related videos for questions and pain points
- Analyze search queries related to the topic (YouTube autocomplete, Google)
- Identify the audience's current knowledge level (beginner, intermediate, advanced)
- Determine what emotional state the audience arrives with

**Competitive Research:**
- Watch the top 3-5 existing videos on this topic
- Identify what they do well and what they miss
- Find the gap -- the angle, depth, or approach no one has covered
- Note retention patterns if you have access to competitive analytics

### Step 2: Outline Structure

The outline is the skeleton of the video. It defines the information architecture before committing to specific language.

**Standard Outline Template:**

```
HOOK (0:00-0:30)
  - Opening statement/question/visual
  - Promise of value (what the viewer will learn/gain)
  - Credibility indicator (why should they listen?)

CONTEXT (0:30-1:30)
  - Why this topic matters now
  - The problem or opportunity being addressed
  - What most people get wrong (creates contrast)

MAIN CONTENT
  Section 1: [Topic]
    - Key point
    - Evidence/example
    - Transition to next section

  Section 2: [Topic]
    - Key point
    - Evidence/example
    - Re-engagement hook ("Here's where it gets interesting...")
    - Transition

  Section 3: [Topic]
    - Key point
    - Evidence/example
    - Transition

  [Additional sections as needed]

CLIMAX / KEY INSIGHT
  - The most valuable or surprising point
  - The "aha moment" the video builds toward

CTA + CLOSE
  - Summarize key takeaways (3 bullets max)
  - Clear call to action
  - Teaser for next video (if series)
```

### Step 3: Hook Optimization

The hook receives disproportionate attention because it determines whether the rest of the video is seen. Write 3-5 hook variations and select the strongest.

**Hook Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it create a curiosity gap within 5 seconds?
- Does it promise specific, valuable information?
- Does it align with what the thumbnail and title promise?
- Does it address the viewer directly ("you")?
- Would this stop your own scroll?

### Step 4: Retention Review

Walk through the script and identify every point where a viewer might stop watching:

- Any section longer than 90 seconds without a visual change, new subtopic, or re-engagement hook
- Any tangent that does not directly serve the video's objective
- Any section where the pacing drops (long explanations without examples)
- Any section where the viewer might feel they have "gotten the point" and leave

For each risk point, add a retention device: open loop, new visual, question, energy shift, or teaser.

### Step 5: Read-Aloud Test

Read the entire script aloud at presentation pace. Time it. Evaluate:

- Does it sound conversational, not written? (Spoken language differs from written language)
- Are sentences short enough to deliver in a single breath?
- Is the pacing varied? (Mix of fast delivery and deliberate pauses)
- Does the length match the target? (Average speaking pace: 130-160 words per minute)
- Are there tongue-twisters or awkward phrasing?

## 3. Storyboarding

### When to Storyboard

Storyboarding is essential for:
- Narrative or cinematic content
- Product videos with specific visual sequences
- Content with multiple locations or setups
- Any video involving a production team larger than one person

Storyboarding is optional for:
- Solo talking-head content
- Screen capture tutorials
- Simple interview setups

### Storyboard Methodology

Each storyboard panel represents a key visual moment and includes:

**Visual:** Sketch or description of the frame composition
**Shot Type:** Wide, medium, close-up, etc.
**Camera Movement:** Static, pan, tilt, dolly, etc.
**Duration:** Approximate time on screen
**Audio:** Dialogue, music cue, sound effect
**Notes:** Special instructions for lighting, props, or talent

**Storyboard Depth Levels:**

| Level | Detail | Use Case |
|-------|--------|----------|
| Thumbnail storyboard | Rough sketches, key moments only | Quick planning, solo production |
| Standard storyboard | Detailed panels for each scene | Team production, client approval |
| Animatic | Storyboard panels edited into a timed sequence with scratch audio | Complex productions, pre-visualization |

## 4. Shot Lists

### Shot List Architecture

The shot list translates the storyboard into a production-ready checklist organized for shooting efficiency, not narrative sequence.

**Shot List Organization Principles:**

**Group by location:** Shoot all shots at one location before moving. Location changes are the most time-consuming production activity.

**Group by setup:** Within a location, shoot all shots that share the same camera setup (position, lens, lighting) before changing the setup.

**Prioritize critical shots:** Mark shots as essential (must-have) or supplementary (nice-to-have). If time runs short, essential shots are protected.

**Shot List Template:**

| Shot # | Scene | Shot Type | Description | Camera Move | Audio | Duration | Setup | Priority |
|--------|-------|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------|----------|-------|----------|
| 1 | Intro | MCU | Presenter delivers hook | Static | Sync | 15s | A | Essential |
| 2 | Intro | CU | Product on desk | Dolly-in | VO | 5s | B | Essential |
| 3 | Demo | Wide | Workspace establishing | Pan R | Ambient | 4s | C | Nice-to-have |

## 5. Production Scheduling

### The Production Schedule

A production schedule maps every activity from pre-production through delivery against a timeline.

**Schedule Components:**

**Pre-Production Phase:**
- Creative brief approval: Day 1
- Research and outline: Days 2-3
- Script draft: Days 3-4
- Script review and revision: Days 5-6
- Storyboard / shot list: Days 6-7
- Location/equipment/talent confirmation: Day 7
- Final approval: Day 8

**Production Phase:**
- Setup and lighting: [time allocation based on complexity]
- A-roll capture: [time allocation]
- B-roll capture: [time allocation]
- Review footage (dailies): [end of shoot day]

**Post-Production Phase:**
- Ingest and organize footage: Day 1
- Rough cut assembly: Days 1-3
- Fine cut with pacing adjustments: Days 3-5
- Color grading: Days 5-6
- Sound design and mixing: Days 5-6
- Graphics and titles: Days 5-6
- Review and revision: Days 7-8
- Final export: Day 8
- Thumbnail and metadata: Day 8
- Upload and scheduling: Day 9

### Shooting Day Structure

**Solo Production Day (8 hours):**
```
Hour 1:    Setup (lighting, camera, audio, set dressing)
Hour 2:    Test and adjust (white balance, levels, framing)
Hour 2-3:  A-roll capture (talking head / main content)
Hour 3:    Review footage, identify pickups
Hour 4:    B-roll capture (cutaways, product shots, demonstrations)
Hour 4-5:  Additional A-roll if needed (pickups, alternate takes)
Hour 5:    Breakdown and equipment storage
Hour 5-8:  Begin post-production (optional same-day start)
```

**Team Production Day (10 hours):**
```
Hour 1-2:   Setup (all departments: camera, lighting, audio, art)
Hour 2:     Tech check (levels, focus, audio, monitors)
Hour 2-3:   Blocking and rehearsal
Hour 3-6:   Principal photography (A-roll)
Hour 6-7:   Lunch and setup change
Hour 7-9:   B-roll, inserts, supplementary footage
Hour 9-10:  Review dailies, identify pickups, breakdown
```

## 6. Equipment Planning

### Equipment Selection by Production Tier

**Tier 1: Phone Production**
- Camera: Modern smartphone (iPhone 14+, Pixel 7+, Samsung S23+)
- Audio: Wireless lavalier (Rode Wireless Go II, DJI Mic)
- Lighting: Window light + $30 ring light or LED panel
- Stabilization: $20 phone tripod
- Total investment: $100-300

**Tier 2: Creator Production**
- Camera: Mirrorless (Sony ZV-E10, Canon R50, Fuji X-S20)
- Lens: Kit zoom + 35mm or 50mm prime
- Audio: Shotgun (Rode VideoMic NTG) + wireless lav
- Lighting: 2x LED panels (Aputure Amaran, Elgato Key Light)
- Stabilization: Tripod + desktop mount
- Total investment: $1,500-4,000

**Tier 3: Professional Production**
- Camera: Full-frame mirrorless (Sony A7IV, Canon R6 III, Panasonic S5 II)
- Lenses: 24-70mm zoom, 50mm f/1.4, 85mm f/1.8
- Audio: Shotgun on boom + dual wireless lav + audio recorder
- Lighting: 3-point LED setup (Aputure 300d, 120d, or equivalent)
- Grip: C-stands, flags, diffusion frames
- Stabilization: Tripod, slider, gimbal
- Total investment: $8,000-20,000

### The Minimum Viable Setup

The most common production failure is not starting because of perceived equipment inadequacy. The minimum viable setup:

1. Any camera that shoots 1080p (including smartphones)
2. Any external microphone (even a $30 lavalier)
3. Any light source you can control (window + a lamp)
4. Any stable mount (tripod, stack of books, shelf)

Content quality, scripting, and consistency matter exponentially more than equipment quality. A well-scripted video on a phone outperforms a poorly scripted video on a cinema camera.

## 7. Talent and Performance Preparation

### On-Camera Performance Coaching

Most non-actors require coaching to perform well on camera. Key areas:

**Energy Calibration:** On-camera energy should be 20-30% higher than conversational energy. What feels "normal" reads as low-energy on screen. What feels "slightly over the top" reads as engaged and enthusiastic.

**Eye Contact with Lens:** The lens is the viewer. Looking at the lens is making eye contact. Looking at the monitor, the script, or around the room breaks the connection. For interview formats, eye contact with the interviewer (not the lens) is appropriate.

**Pacing and Pauses:** Nervous speakers rush. Coach deliberate pauses: 1-2 seconds between key points, 2-3 seconds before important statements. Pauses create emphasis and give the viewer processing time.

**Teleprompter vs. Outline vs. Memorization:**
- Teleprompter: Best for precise messaging (corporate, advertising). Requires practice to avoid "reading" appearance.
- Outline with bullet points: Best for natural delivery. Speaker covers key points but uses their own words.
- Full memorization: Best for short scripts (under 60 seconds). Risk: sounds rehearsed rather than authentic.
- No script (free talk): Only appropriate for highly experienced on-camera talent or intentionally casual formats.

**Body Language:**
- Open posture (shoulders back, chin level, slight forward lean)
- Hand gestures add emphasis but should not be constant
- Minimize swaying, fidgeting, and self-touching
- Smile naturally when appropriate -- forced smiles read as insincere

## 8. Legal and Compliance Pre-Production

### Music Licensing

Using copyrighted music without a license creates legal liability and platform penalties.

**Licensing Options:**
| Source | Cost | Quality | Risk |
|--------|------|---------|------|
| Royalty-free libraries (Epidemic Sound, Artlist) | $10-20/month | High | None |
| Creative Commons (YouTube Audio Library) | Free | Variable | Low (verify license) |
| Direct licensing from artist | Variable | Original | Low (with proper contract) |
| Original composition | Production cost | Unique | None |
| No license (copyrighted music) | N/A | N/A | High (DMCA, demonetization) |

### Talent Releases

Any recognizable person appearing in a video intended for commercial use requires a signed talent release form. This applies to employees, customers (testimonials), hired talent, and bystanders who are identifiable.

### Location Permits

Public spaces may require filming permits depending on jurisdiction. Private properties always require owner permission. Commercial use of footage filmed on private property without permission creates legal liability.

### Disclosure Requirements

FTC guidelines require clear disclosure when:
- Content is sponsored or paid for by a brand
- Products were received for free in exchange for review
- Affiliate links are used
- The creator has a material relationship with the featured product/service

Disclosure must be clear, conspicuous, and within the video itself (not only in the description).
