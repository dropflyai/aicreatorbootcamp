# Production -- Camera, Lighting, Audio, and Directing

## The Production Phase

Production is the execution of the pre-production plan. When pre-production is thorough, production is efficient. When pre-production is inadequate, production becomes expensive problem-solving. The objective of the production phase is to capture all required footage at the highest achievable quality within the allocated time and budget.

This module covers the technical and creative craft of production: camera setup, lighting execution, audio capture, and directing talent.

---

## 1. Camera Setup and Configuration

### Exposure Triangle

Every image is controlled by three interdependent variables. Mastering their interaction is fundamental to consistent, intentional footage.

**ISO (Sensor Sensitivity):**
- Lower ISO = cleaner image, less noise (100-400 ideal)
- Higher ISO = brighter image, more noise/grain
- Native ISO varies by camera; stay at or below native ISO when possible
- Modern cameras maintain usable quality up to ISO 3200-6400
- Rule: Set ISO last, after aperture and shutter speed are determined

**Aperture (f-stop):**
- Lower f-number = wider aperture = more light + shallower depth of field
- Higher f-number = narrower aperture = less light + deeper depth of field
- f/1.4-2.8: Shallow DOF, subject isolation, background blur (talking head, interview)
- f/4-5.6: Moderate DOF, balanced (general purpose)
- f/8-11: Deep DOF, most of frame in focus (landscapes, wide shots, group)
- Sweet spot for sharpness: typically 2-3 stops above maximum aperture

**Shutter Speed:**
- The 180-degree shutter rule: shutter speed = 2x frame rate for natural motion blur
- 24fps = 1/48s (or 1/50s), 30fps = 1/60s, 60fps = 1/120s
- Faster shutter speeds create staccato, sharp motion (Saving Private Ryan effect)
- Slower shutter speeds create dreamy, blurred motion (generally avoided)
- Use ND filters to maintain correct shutter speed in bright conditions rather than increasing shutter speed

### White Balance

White balance ensures accurate color rendition by telling the camera what "white" looks like under the current lighting conditions.

**Setting options:**
- Auto White Balance (AWB): Acceptable for run-and-gun, but may shift between shots
- Preset (Daylight/Tungsten/Fluorescent): Quick and consistent within lighting type
- Custom/Manual (Kelvin value): Most precise. Set to match the primary light source
- Grey card calibration: The most accurate method for critical color work

**Critical rule:** If shooting with mixed light sources (e.g., window daylight + tungsten lamp), either match all lights to one color temperature using gels or choose which source to balance for and accept the color cast from the other.

### Focus Strategy

**Autofocus (AF):**
- Modern mirrorless cameras have reliable eye-detection AF for talking head and interview
- Set AF to continuous (AF-C) for subjects in motion
- Set AF to face/eye priority for interview and presentation
- AF may hunt in low light or low contrast -- switch to manual in those conditions

**Manual Focus:**
- Required for precise product shots, cinematic work, and rack focus effects
- Use focus peaking (camera overlay highlighting in-focus areas) for accuracy
- Mark focus positions on the lens barrel for repeatable pulls

**Depth of Field Management:**
- Shallow DOF (f/1.4-2.8) isolates subjects but makes focus critical -- minor movement takes subject out of focus
- For solo creators without a focus puller, f/2.8-4 provides adequate separation with more forgiving focus tolerance
- For group shots or movement, f/5.6-8 ensures multiple subjects remain in focus

### Recording Settings

**Resolution:**
- 4K (3840x2160): Current standard for future-proofing and crop flexibility. Records 4x the pixels of 1080p, allowing reframing in post.
- 1080p (1920x1080): Acceptable for web delivery if 4K is not available or storage is limited.
- Recording in 4K for 1080p delivery provides crop flexibility for reframing, punch-ins, and multi-platform export.

**Frame Rate:**
- 24fps: Cinematic feel, standard for narrative and most YouTube content
- 30fps: Broadcast standard, slightly smoother motion, common for corporate and web
- 60fps: Smooth motion, used for slow-motion source (played back at 24 or 30fps = 2-2.5x slow motion)
- 120fps+: Dramatic slow motion source

**Color Profile:**
- Standard/Natural: Baked-in look, less flexibility in post, suitable for quick turnaround
- Log/Flat profile (S-Log, C-Log, V-Log): Maximum dynamic range, requires color grading in post
- Rule: If you color grade, shoot log. If you deliver straight from camera, shoot standard.

**Bit Rate:**
- Higher bit rate = more data = better quality = larger files
- For YouTube delivery: 50-100 Mbps in 4K is excellent
- For archival or heavy post-production: 200+ Mbps or RAW

## 2. Lighting Execution

### Three-Point Lighting Setup (Step-by-Step)

**Step 1: Key Light Placement**
1. Position the key light 30-45 degrees to one side of the camera-subject axis
2. Elevate the light 15-30 degrees above the subject's eye line
3. Adjust intensity to properly expose the lit side of the face
4. Choose hard or soft quality based on desired mood:
   - Soft: Use a large softbox, diffusion panel, or bounce
   - Hard: Use a bare bulb, Fresnel, or small source

**Step 2: Fill Light Placement**
1. Position opposite the key light, at or slightly below eye level
2. Set intensity based on desired key-to-fill ratio:
   - 2:1 (subtle shadow): Fill at 50% of key intensity
   - 4:1 (dramatic): Fill at 25% of key intensity
   - Alternative: Use a bounce reflector instead of a powered fill for natural-looking fill

**Step 3: Back Light Placement**
1. Position behind the subject, elevated 30-45 degrees
2. Aim at the back of the head and shoulders
3. Adjust intensity to create a visible rim of light separating subject from background
4. Flag or barn-door the back light to prevent lens flare

**Step 4: Background Lighting (Optional)**
1. Illuminate the background separately to create depth
2. Use colored gels for mood (common in tech/creator content)
3. Practical lights (lamps, monitors, LED strips) add visual interest
4. Ensure background is not brighter than the subject (draws attention away)

### Natural Light Execution

**Window Light Interview Setup:**
1. Position subject facing a large window (the key light)
2. Place a reflector or white surface opposite the window (the fill)
3. Subject should be 3-5 feet from the window for soft light
4. Avoid direct sunlight -- overcast or north-facing windows are ideal
5. Use curtains or diffusion to soften harsh direct light
6. Background should be away from the window to avoid overexposure

**Outdoor Shooting:**
1. Prefer golden hour (1 hour after sunrise / 1 hour before sunset) for warm, directional light
2. Open shade (under a building overhang, tree canopy) provides even, flattering light midday
3. Avoid direct overhead sun (creates harsh shadows under eyes and nose)
4. Use a reflector to fill shadows when shooting in directional sunlight
5. Overcast conditions are excellent for even, soft lighting with no harsh shadows

### Common Lighting Mistakes

| Mistake | Problem | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Overhead only | Dark eye sockets, unflattering | Add frontal key light at 30-45 degrees |
| Back-lit subject | Subject silhouetted, face dark | Add key light to front, or expose for face and let background blow out |
| Mixed color temperature | Unnatural color casts | Gel lights to match or use single source type |
| No back light | Subject blends into background | Add rim/hair light from behind |
| Over-lit background | Eye drawn to background, not subject | Dim background lights, increase key light |
| Single harsh source | Unflattering shadows | Diffuse the source (softbox, bounce, diffusion panel) |

## 3. Audio Capture

### Audio Setup Checklist

Before recording, complete this checklist:

- [ ] Primary microphone positioned and tested
- [ ] Backup/redundant microphone positioned and tested
- [ ] Audio levels checked: peaks at -12dB to -6dB (leaving headroom)
- [ ] Room noise assessed: HVAC, refrigerator, traffic, electronics
- [ ] Reduce room noise: turn off HVAC, close windows, mute phones
- [ ] Monitor audio with headphones during recording (not just levels)
- [ ] Record 10 seconds of room tone (silence) for post-production noise floor matching
- [ ] Clap sync (or slate) at the start of each take for multi-source sync

### Microphone Technique by Type

**Lavalier (clip-on):**
- Clip to clothing at chest level, approximately 6-8 inches below the chin
- Route cable under clothing to reduce rustle noise
- Test for clothing friction by having talent move naturally
- If using wireless, check for RF interference at the recording location
- Windscreens are essential for outdoor use

**Shotgun (directional):**
- Point directly at the speaker's mouth from above or below frame
- Optimal distance: 12-24 inches from the sound source
- Mount on boom pole for off-camera positioning, or on-camera hot shoe for mobile work
- Shotgun mics are directional but not laser-focused -- they reject off-axis sound, not eliminate it
- Indoor use: Be aware that shotgun mics can emphasize room reflections in reverberant spaces

**Desktop/Broadcast (dynamic or condenser):**
- Position 6-12 inches from the speaker's mouth
- Slightly off-axis (30 degrees) to reduce plosives
- Use a pop filter or windscreen to minimize plosive bursts (P, B, T sounds)
- Shock mount isolates from desk vibration and handling noise
- Consistent distance is critical -- small changes create significant volume variations

### Audio Levels and Monitoring

**Target levels:**
- Dialogue peaks: -12dB to -6dB
- Average dialogue: -18dB to -12dB
- Leaving headroom prevents clipping (digital distortion), which is unrecoverable
- It is always better to record slightly quiet (fixable in post) than slightly loud (clipping is permanent)

**Monitoring protocol:**
- Always monitor with closed-back headphones during recording
- Headphones should be plugged into the recording device, not the camera (if recording externally)
- Listen for: noise floor hiss, clothing rustle, plosives, room echo, external noise intrusion
- If anything sounds wrong, stop and fix it immediately -- post-production cannot fix fundamentally flawed audio

### Dual-System Audio

For professional production, recording audio on a dedicated recorder (Zoom H6, Tascam DR-40X, Sound Devices MixPre) separate from the camera provides:
- Higher quality preamps and converters
- Independent level control per channel
- Redundancy (if one system fails, the other captures)
- Better format options (WAV 24-bit vs. compressed camera audio)

**Sync method:** Use a clap (hands or slate) at the start of each take to create a visual and audio spike for alignment in post. Alternatively, use timecode sync for automated alignment.

## 4. Directing

### Directing Non-Actors

Most video production involves non-actors: founders, employees, customers, subject matter experts. Directing non-actors requires different techniques than directing trained performers.

**Create Comfort:**
- Spend 5-10 minutes in casual conversation before camera rolls
- Explain the process: "We'll do several takes, there's no wrong answer"
- Remove audience: minimize crew and bystanders in the room
- Let the subject watch a test recording of themselves to normalize the experience

**Elicit Natural Performance:**
- Ask questions rather than request monologues (edit questions out in post)
- Use the "tell me about..." prompt to get conversational responses
- If a line sounds rehearsed, ask the talent to explain it "like you're telling a friend"
- Allow imperfection -- verbal fillers, restarts, and natural phrasing often sound more authentic than polished delivery

**Manage Energy:**
- If energy is too low: increase your own energy, ask the talent to stand up, take a break, change topic
- If energy is too high: ask for a slower version, suggest pausing between points, use calming body language
- Energy typically peaks in the first 30 minutes and declines after 60-90 minutes of recording
- Schedule the most important content in the first hour

### Directing for Interview

**Interview Setup Principles:**
- Interviewer sits next to the camera, slightly to one side (subject's eyeline passes just off-lens)
- Subject looks at the interviewer, not the camera (creates more natural, conversational feel)
- Camera at subject's eye level or slightly above (flattering angle)
- If two-camera setup: Camera A on subject (primary), Camera B on wider or alternate angle

**Interview Technique:**
- Prepare questions in advance but be willing to deviate based on responses
- Ask open-ended questions ("Tell me about..." not "Did you...?")
- Allow silence after answers -- the subject often fills silence with deeper, more authentic content
- Re-ask questions if the answer was good but the phrasing was unclear
- Ask the subject to incorporate the question in their answer ("The biggest challenge was..." not just "Staffing")

### Directing for Product Video

- Start with the hero shot: the single most compelling image of the product
- Show the product in use before showing it in isolation (context before detail)
- Hands in frame demonstrate scale and create human connection
- Slow, deliberate movements read as premium; fast movements read as energetic or casual
- Light the product to emphasize texture, form, and key features
- Avoid fingerprints, dust, and reflections on reflective surfaces (use gloves, air blower)

## 5. B-Roll Capture Strategy

### B-Roll Categories

**Contextual B-Roll:** Footage that establishes where the story takes place (office, factory, city, home).
**Illustrative B-Roll:** Footage that visually represents what the narrator is discussing (product in use, process demonstration).
**Atmospheric B-Roll:** Footage that establishes mood without directly illustrating content (time-lapses, nature, abstract).
**Detail B-Roll:** Close-up shots of specific elements mentioned in narration (typing on keyboard, product feature, facial expression).

### The 5-Shot Rule

A foundational approach for capturing comprehensive B-roll of any activity or subject. Capture five shots to tell a complete visual story:

1. **Close-up of hands** (or primary action)
2. **Close-up of face** (reaction, concentration, emotion)
3. **Wide shot** (establishes environment and context)
4. **Over-the-shoulder** (viewer becomes a participant)
5. **Creative angle** (unusual perspective that adds visual interest)

### B-Roll Quantity Rule

Capture 3-5x more B-roll than you think you need. In post-production, having too much B-roll is a luxury; having too little is a crisis. Every 10 minutes of finished video typically requires 5-10 unique B-roll shots.

### B-Roll Technical Considerations

- Shoot B-roll at the same frame rate and color profile as A-roll for consistency
- If possible, shoot some B-roll at 60fps for optional slow-motion in post
- Use a gimbal or stabilizer for moving B-roll shots
- Hold each shot for at least 10 seconds to provide editing flexibility
- Vary shot types (wide, medium, close-up) and angles (eye level, low, high)

## 6. Multi-Camera Production

### When to Use Multi-Camera

- Interviews (A-angle + B-angle eliminates jump cuts)
- Live events (coverage cannot be re-shot)
- Dialogue between multiple subjects
- Product demonstrations requiring simultaneous wide and detail views
- Any production where continuity must be maintained across long takes

### Multi-Camera Setup Protocol

1. **Match cameras** when possible (same model, same settings) for consistent color
2. **Match white balance** precisely across all cameras (use grey card)
3. **Match exposure** using a waveform monitor or histogram
4. **Assign distinct angles:** Each camera must provide a meaningfully different perspective
5. **Sync method:** Timecode (professional) or audio clap (accessible)
6. **Monitor all feeds** during recording if possible (multiview monitor or switcher)

### Two-Camera Interview Setup

```
Camera A: Primary angle
  - MCU or CU of subject
  - Positioned next to interviewer
  - Eye-level or slightly above

Camera B: Secondary angle
  - Wider framing or alternate angle (15-30 degrees from Camera A)
  - Provides cutting option to remove jump cuts
  - Can also serve as a wide/establishing shot
```

## 7. Production Day Protocols

### Morning Setup Checklist

- [ ] All batteries charged
- [ ] All memory cards formatted (after confirming previous footage is backed up)
- [ ] Camera settings verified (resolution, frame rate, color profile, white balance)
- [ ] Audio tested (levels, monitoring, backup system)
- [ ] Lighting positioned and tested
- [ ] Set dressed and checked on monitor (remove distracting elements)
- [ ] Teleprompter loaded and tested (if applicable)
- [ ] Shot list printed and accessible

### During Production Checklist

- [ ] Monitor audio continuously via headphones
- [ ] Check focus between takes
- [ ] Review footage periodically (every 30 minutes or between major setups)
- [ ] Note take numbers and quality observations on the shot list
- [ ] Ensure continuity between setups (clothing, hair, set position, lighting)
- [ ] Back up footage at lunch break if possible (dual card recording or data dump)

### End-of-Day Checklist

- [ ] Review all footage to confirm nothing was missed from the shot list
- [ ] Back up all footage to at least two separate locations
- [ ] Label and organize footage by scene/setup
- [ ] Note any pickups needed for the next shooting day
- [ ] Charge all batteries
- [ ] Clean and store equipment properly
