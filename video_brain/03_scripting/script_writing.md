# Script Writing -- Hook Structures, Narrative Arc, and CTA Placement

## The Script as Blueprint

The script is the single most important determinant of video performance. YouTube Creator Academy data consistently shows that scripting quality -- specifically hook effectiveness and retention optimization -- correlates more strongly with video success than any production variable. A brilliant script with mediocre production outperforms a mediocre script with brilliant production.

This module covers the universal principles of video scriptwriting applicable across all formats. Format-specific scripting (short-form and long-form) is covered in dedicated modules.

---

## 1. The Anatomy of a Video Script

### Script Components

Every video script contains these structural elements, regardless of format:

**Hook (0-30 seconds):** The opening sequence designed to capture attention and commit the viewer to watching. The hook is the most important element of any video script.

**Setup/Context (30-90 seconds):** Establishes why the topic matters, who it's for, and what the viewer will gain by watching. Creates the contract between creator and viewer.

**Body/Content (Variable):** The substantive content delivery -- the information, story, or demonstration that fulfills the hook's promise. Structured in discrete sections with re-engagement points.

**Climax/Key Insight (80-90% mark):** The most valuable, surprising, or impactful moment in the video. The payoff for the viewer's time investment.

**CTA/Close (Final 10-15%):** The call to action and conclusion. Directs the viewer toward the desired next step and provides closure.

### Script Format

Video scripts differ from screenplays. The standard video script format includes:

```
VISUAL                          AUDIO
[What the viewer sees]          [What the viewer hears]

MCU - Presenter at desk         "Have you ever wondered why some
                                videos get millions of views while
                                others get stuck at 200?"

CUT TO: Screen recording of     "The answer isn't production quality.
YouTube analytics dashboard      It's not even the topic. It's this
                                one thing that most creators
                                completely ignore..."
HIGHLIGHT: Retention graph

CUT TO: Presenter, lean in      "...and I'm going to show you
                                exactly how to fix it."
```

**Alternative: Outline Script**

For conversational formats where word-for-word scripting sounds unnatural:

```
SECTION 1: Hook [0:00-0:30]
- Open with the statistic about viewer attention
- Ask the rhetorical question about why some videos fail
- Promise: "By the end, you'll know the exact framework"

SECTION 2: The Problem [0:30-2:00]
- Most creators focus on production quality
- Show example of high-production video that flopped
- Reveal the real issue: scripting and retention structure
- Key stat: 80% of performance determined by script
```

## 2. Hook Engineering

### The Science of Hooks

The hook exists to overcome the viewer's default behavior: leaving. YouTube data shows that the first 30 seconds determine the video's trajectory. If a video retains 70%+ of viewers at the 30-second mark, it typically performs well algorithmically. If it drops below 50% by 30 seconds, recovery is nearly impossible.

### Hook Architecture

An effective hook contains three components delivered in rapid succession:

**1. Attention Arrest (0-3 seconds):**
The first visual and/or verbal element that breaks the viewer's inertia. This is the pattern interrupt -- the element that stops the scroll or prevents the click-away.

Methods:
- Bold, direct statement: "This is the biggest mistake in video marketing."
- Startling data: "87% of marketing videos fail for the same reason."
- Provocative question: "What if everything you've been told about YouTube is wrong?"
- Visual disruption: Unexpected image, rapid motion, dramatic lighting
- Mid-action start: Begin in the middle of a demonstration or story, not with a greeting

**2. Value Promise (3-10 seconds):**
Explicitly or implicitly communicates what the viewer will gain by watching.

Methods:
- Outcome promise: "By the end of this video, you'll know exactly how to..."
- Transformation promise: "This technique took my channel from 100 to 100,000 subscribers."
- Information promise: "I'm going to show you the 3 frameworks that..."
- Curiosity extension: "And the third one is something nobody talks about."

**3. Credibility Signal (10-20 seconds):**
Establishes why the presenter or source is authoritative on this topic.

Methods:
- Results-based: "After producing 500+ videos for Fortune 500 companies..."
- Evidence-based: "Research from Stanford shows that..."
- Social proof: "This framework is used by [notable names/companies]"
- Vulnerability-based: "I spent three years making this mistake before..."

### Hook Templates

**Template 1: The Problem-Agitate-Promise Hook**
```
[PROBLEM] Most [audience] struggle with [pain point].
[AGITATE] They try [common approach], but it doesn't work because [reason].
[PROMISE] In this video, I'm going to show you [solution] that [result].
```

**Template 2: The Contrarian Hook**
```
[CONVENTIONAL WISDOM] Everyone says you should [common advice].
[CONTRARIAN CLAIM] That's actually wrong. Here's why.
[CREDIBILITY] After [evidence of authority], I discovered that [truth].
[PROMISE] Let me show you what actually works.
```

**Template 3: The Story Hook**
```
[IN MEDIAS RES] [Start in the middle of a dramatic moment].
[CONTEXT] Let me back up. [Brief setup of how you got there].
[PROMISE] What happened next changed everything. Here's the full story.
```

**Template 4: The Data Hook**
```
[SURPRISING STATISTIC] [Stat] of [audience] do [thing] wrong.
[IMPLICATION] This means [consequence].
[PROMISE] But there's a simple fix. I'll show you exactly what to do.
```

**Template 5: The Demonstration Hook**
```
[SHOW THE RESULT] [Visual of the end result/transformation].
[CREATE GAP] Most people think this takes [perceived effort/time/cost].
[PROMISE] I'm going to show you how to do it in [faster/cheaper/easier].
```

## 3. Narrative Arc for Video

### The Three-Act Structure (Adapted for Video)

**Act 1 -- Setup (First 15-20% of video):**
- Hook: Capture attention
- Context: Establish why this matters
- Promise: Tell the viewer what they'll learn/experience
- Stakes: What happens if they don't act on this information

**Act 2 -- Development (Middle 60-70% of video):**
- Deliver on the promise made in the hook
- Organize content in escalating value (save the best for later)
- Include re-engagement hooks every 60-90 seconds
- Build toward the key insight/climax
- Maintain tension through open loops

**Act 3 -- Resolution (Final 15-20% of video):**
- Deliver the climax (key insight, transformation, biggest value point)
- Summarize key takeaways
- Call to action
- Tease future content (optional)

### The Inverted Pyramid (For Informational Content)

For purely informational content where the audience may not watch to the end, the inverted pyramid structure front-loads value:

```
Most important information (hook + key insight)
     ↓
Supporting details and evidence
     ↓
Additional context and examples
     ↓
Advanced nuances and edge cases
     ↓
CTA and related content
```

This structure ensures that viewers who leave early still receive the most valuable information, while viewers who stay are rewarded with increasing depth.

### The Listicle/Framework Structure

For numbered-list content (e.g., "5 Strategies for..."), this structure maximizes retention:

1. **Hook with the list and tease the best item:** "I'm going to share 5 strategies, and number 4 is the one most people have never heard of."
2. **Start with the second-best item:** Frontloading value confirms the viewer's decision to watch.
3. **Place the weakest item in the middle:** The middle has the highest natural drop-off; weaker content here has the least impact.
4. **Escalate toward the strongest item:** Building momentum and anticipation.
5. **End with a synthesis or bonus:** "Now, here's how all five work together..."

## 4. Retention Devices in Scripts

### Open Loops

An open loop is a question raised or promise made that is not immediately fulfilled. The viewer's cognitive need for closure (Zeigarnik Effect) keeps them watching to find the resolution.

**Types of open loops:**

**Explicit Preview:** "In a minute, I'll show you the exact template I use."
**Implicit Curiosity:** "But there was one problem nobody anticipated."
**Sequential Tease:** "Step three is where it gets really interesting, but first..."
**End Tease:** "And at the end, I'll share the bonus strategy that changed everything."

**Rule:** Open at least one loop before closing the previous one. The viewer should always have at least one unresolved question.

### Re-Engagement Hooks

Every 60-90 seconds, the script should include a re-engagement element that renews the viewer's commitment to watching.

**Re-engagement types:**
- **Topic shift:** "Now let's talk about [new subtopic]..."
- **Energy shift:** Change in vocal energy, pacing, or emotional tone
- **Visual shift direction:** "[Cut to screen recording]" or "[Cut to B-roll]"
- **Direct address:** "Now, this next part is crucial for anyone who..."
- **Surprise/Contrast:** "Here's where most people get it completely wrong."
- **Story insertion:** A brief anecdote that illustrates the point
- **Data/Evidence:** A compelling statistic or research finding

### Bucket Brigade Phrases

Transitional phrases that carry the viewer from one section to the next without creating a natural exit point:

| Phrase | Function |
|--------|----------|
| "But here's the thing..." | Creates anticipation of a revelation |
| "Now, it gets interesting..." | Promises escalating value |
| "Here's what most people miss..." | Creates an information gap |
| "And that's not even the best part..." | Promises more value ahead |
| "Let me show you exactly what I mean..." | Promises concrete demonstration |
| "But wait -- there's a catch..." | Creates tension |
| "The real question is..." | Reframes and deepens the topic |
| "What happened next surprised everyone..." | Story-based forward momentum |

## 5. CTA (Call to Action) Strategy

### CTA Placement Principles

**Early CTA (first 20%):** Appropriate for channel-building CTAs (subscribe, notifications). Light-touch, not hard-sell. "If this type of content interests you, consider subscribing."

**Mid-Roll CTA (40-60%):** Appropriate for engagement CTAs (comment, share) or soft conversion CTAs (download a free resource). The viewer has received enough value to feel the CTA is earned.

**End CTA (final 10-15%):** The primary conversion CTA. By this point, the viewer has consumed the content and is most receptive to a next-step recommendation.

**Contextual CTA (any point):** Triggered by relevant content. "I just mentioned [tool] -- I'll put a link in the description."

### CTA Hierarchy

Structure CTAs from lowest to highest commitment:

1. **Engagement CTA:** "Drop a comment below with your biggest takeaway." (Lowest friction)
2. **Subscription CTA:** "Subscribe if you want more content like this." (Low friction)
3. **Content CTA:** "Watch this next video to go deeper on [topic]." (Medium friction)
4. **Lead CTA:** "Download the free template -- link in the description." (Medium friction)
5. **Conversion CTA:** "Enroll in the course / Buy the product." (Highest friction)

**Rule:** Never ask for more than 2 CTAs per video. More than 2 creates decision paralysis and reduces action on all CTAs.

### CTA Scripting Framework

Effective CTAs follow a structure:

**1. Transition from content:** "Now that you know the framework..."
**2. Value reminder:** "...and you've seen how it doubled our engagement..."
**3. Action instruction:** "...click the link in the description to get the free template..."
**4. Effort minimization:** "...it takes 30 seconds to download..."
**5. Outcome reinforcement:** "...and you'll have the exact same system I used."

## 6. Writing for Spoken Delivery

### Spoken vs. Written Language

Video scripts are written to be spoken, not read. Spoken language differs fundamentally from written language:

| Written Language | Spoken Language |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Long, complex sentences | Short, punchy sentences |
| Formal vocabulary | Conversational vocabulary |
| Passive voice acceptable | Active voice preferred |
| Abstract concepts | Concrete examples |
| Dense paragraphs | One idea per breath |
| Precision | Clarity |

### Script Readability Rules

**Sentence length:** Average 10-15 words. No sentence longer than 25 words.
**Paragraph length:** 2-3 sentences maximum before a visual break, topic shift, or pause.
**Vocabulary:** Use the simplest word that accurately conveys the meaning. "Use" not "utilize." "Start" not "commence."
**Contractions:** Always use contractions. "Don't" not "do not." "It's" not "it is." Uncontracted speech sounds robotic.
**Active voice:** "The algorithm rewards retention" not "Retention is rewarded by the algorithm."
**Second person:** "You" is the most powerful word in a video script. Address the viewer directly.

### The "Bar Test"

Read the script aloud and ask: "Would I say this to someone at a bar?" If not, rewrite it in the way you would actually say it. This eliminates academic language, awkward phrasing, and unnatural constructions.

## 7. Educational Video Script Structure

### The ADEPT Method

A framework for explaining complex concepts clearly:

**A - Analogy:** Start with something the viewer already understands that is structurally similar to the new concept.
**D - Diagram:** Provide a visual representation of the concept.
**E - Example:** Give a concrete, specific instance of the concept in action.
**P - Plain Language:** Define the concept in the simplest possible terms.
**T - Technical Definition:** Provide the precise, technical definition for those who want it.

### The Progressive Disclosure Structure

For tutorial and educational content, reveal information in layers:

1. **Overview:** What are we building/learning? What does the end result look like?
2. **Big Picture:** The 3-5 major steps or concepts at a high level
3. **Deep Dive:** Each major step broken into detailed sub-steps
4. **Edge Cases:** Common mistakes, troubleshooting, advanced variations
5. **Synthesis:** How all the pieces connect. The "aha moment."

### Cognitive Load Management in Scripts

- Introduce one new concept at a time
- Provide a concrete example immediately after introducing an abstract concept
- Use transition phrases that explicitly connect sections ("Now that you understand X, let's look at how Y builds on it")
- Repeat key terms consistently (do not vary terminology for the same concept)
- Pause (script a beat) after delivering critical information

## 8. Script Review Checklist

Before approving any script for production:

### Hook Review
- [ ] Does the hook create a curiosity gap in the first 5 seconds?
- [ ] Is there a clear value promise within the first 15 seconds?
- [ ] Does the hook align with the planned thumbnail and title?
- [ ] Would this hook stop you from clicking away?

### Structure Review
- [ ] Is the narrative arc clear (setup, development, climax, resolution)?
- [ ] Does the content escalate in value (best point not at the beginning)?
- [ ] Are there re-engagement hooks every 60-90 seconds?
- [ ] Are open loops used to maintain forward momentum?
- [ ] Is there a clear climax/key insight moment?

### Retention Review
- [ ] Are there any sections where a viewer would feel "done" and leave?
- [ ] Is every section necessary for the video's objective?
- [ ] Is the pacing varied (not monotonous)?
- [ ] Are bucket brigades used at section transitions?

### CTA Review
- [ ] Is there a clear primary CTA?
- [ ] Is the CTA earned (sufficient value delivered before asking)?
- [ ] Is the CTA specific and actionable?
- [ ] Are there no more than 2 CTAs?

### Delivery Review
- [ ] Does it sound natural when read aloud?
- [ ] Are sentences short enough for spoken delivery?
- [ ] Is the vocabulary conversational?
- [ ] Is the target length achievable at speaking pace (130-160 WPM)?
