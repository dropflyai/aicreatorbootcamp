# Strategic Planning -- From Vision to OKRs

## Overview

Strategic planning is the discipline of translating long-term vision into
measurable, time-bound objectives that drive organizational behavior. It
answers the question: "Given where we want to go and where we are now,
what specifically must we accomplish in the next year, quarter, and sprint
to close the gap?"

The strategic planning cascade -- Vision, Mission, Strategy, OKRs, Tactics
-- ensures alignment from the boardroom to the individual contributor. Without
this cascade, organizations suffer from strategic ambiguity: everyone is busy,
but no one knows if the work matters.

---

## The Strategic Planning Cascade

```
VISION (10+ years)
  "The world we want to create"
        |
        v
MISSION (Enduring)
  "How we create that world"
        |
        v
STRATEGY (3-5 years)
  "The approach we take"
        |
        v
OBJECTIVES AND KEY RESULTS (Annual/Quarterly)
  "The measurable outcomes we commit to"
        |
        v
TACTICS AND INITIATIVES (Weekly/Sprint)
  "The specific work we do"
```

### Vision

The vision is the long-term aspiration -- the world the company wants to
create. It is deliberately ambitious, even audacious.

Characteristics of great vision:
- **Inspirational** -- people want to be part of this future
- **Clear** -- a 10-year-old could understand it
- **Ambitious** -- it stretches the organization beyond current capability
- **Enduring** -- it does not change with quarterly results
- **Actionable** -- it implies a direction, not just a feeling

Examples:
- Microsoft (1975): "A computer on every desk and in every home"
- Tesla: "To accelerate the world's transition to sustainable energy"
- Stripe: "Increase the GDP of the internet"

### Mission

The mission is HOW the company pursues its vision. It defines the company's
purpose, its customers, and its value proposition.

Components of a mission statement:
1. **Who we serve** (target customer)
2. **What we do** (core offering)
3. **How we are different** (unique value)
4. **Why it matters** (impact)

Template: "We [what we do] for [who we serve] by [how we are different]
so that [why it matters]."

### Strategy

Strategy is the set of choices that position the company to achieve its
mission. It answers: "What approach will we take, and -- equally important
-- what approaches will we NOT take?"

Michael Porter's essential insight: strategy is about making tradeoffs.
A strategy that tries to be all things to all people is not a strategy.

## Porter's Generic Strategies

### The Three Strategies

Michael Porter (Competitive Strategy, 1980) identified three fundamental
strategic positions:

```
                    STRATEGIC ADVANTAGE
                    Uniqueness    Low Cost
                 +------------+----------+
    Industry-    |            |          |
    wide         |DIFFERENTIA-| COST     |
STRATEGIC        |TION        | LEADERSHIP|
TARGET           |            |          |
                 +------------+----------+
    Specific     |            |          |
    segment      | FOCUSED    | FOCUSED  |
                 | DIFFER.    | COST     |
                 +------------+----------+
```

#### Cost Leadership
Be the lowest-cost producer in the industry.
- Requires: Scale, efficiency, operational excellence
- Risk: Cost leaders can be disrupted by technology shifts
- Brain focus: Operations Brain, Finance Brain, Engineering Brain
- Example: Walmart, Ryanair, Amazon (infrastructure)

#### Differentiation
Offer something uniquely valuable that customers will pay a premium for.
- Requires: Innovation, brand, customer intimacy, design
- Risk: Differentiators can be imitated; premium may not justify cost
- Brain focus: Design Brain, Product Brain, Branding Brain, Innovation Brain
- Example: Apple, Tesla, Salesforce

#### Focus (Cost or Differentiation)
Serve a narrow market segment better than broad competitors.
- Requires: Deep customer knowledge, specialized capabilities
- Risk: Niche may shrink; broad competitors may enter
- Brain focus: Research Brain, Customer Success Brain, Product Brain
- Example: Shopify (e-commerce SMB), Veeva (pharma CRM)

### The Stuck-in-the-Middle Trap

Companies that fail to commit to a clear strategic position are "stuck in
the middle" -- not cheap enough to win on cost, not different enough to
win on value. This is the most common strategic failure.

CEO application: The CEO Brain must enforce strategic clarity. When brains
propose initiatives that are inconsistent with the chosen strategy, the
CEO Brain rejects them with reference to the strategic position.

## Annual and Quarterly Planning

### The Annual Planning Process

```
Timeline: Starts 2-3 months before fiscal year end

Month 1: CONTEXT SETTING
  - CEO presents strategic context (market, competition, financials)
  - Each function/brain presents its state of the world
  - CEO facilitates discussion of strategic themes for next year

Month 2: OBJECTIVE SETTING
  - CEO proposes 3-5 annual objectives
  - Functions/brains propose supporting objectives
  - Negotiation and alignment
  - Resource allocation (budget, headcount)

Month 3: KEY RESULT DEFINITION
  - Each objective gets 3-5 measurable key results
  - Key results are committed (not aspirational)
  - Dependencies between functions/brains are mapped
  - Q1 plans are finalized

Output: Annual strategic plan with OKRs, budget, headcount plan
```

### Quarterly Planning Process

```
Timeline: Last 2 weeks of each quarter

Week 1: REVIEW
  - Review previous quarter OKR attainment
  - Identify what worked, what did not, why
  - Update context (market changes, new information)
  - CEO grades each OKR (0.0-1.0)

Week 2: PLANNING
  - Confirm or adjust annual objectives
  - Set quarterly key results
  - Assign owners for each key result
  - Define the 2-3 "big rocks" for the quarter
  - Identify dependencies and risks

Output: Quarterly OKR document, big rocks, dependency map
```

## The OKR System

### Objectives

Objectives are WHAT you want to achieve. They are:
- **Qualitative** -- they describe a desired outcome, not a metric
- **Inspirational** -- they motivate action
- **Time-bound** -- they have a deadline (quarterly or annual)
- **Achievable but ambitious** -- stretch, do not fantasize

Good objectives: "Establish market leadership in SMB invoicing"
Bad objectives: "Increase revenue" (too vague), "Launch 47 features" (output, not outcome)

### Key Results

Key results are HOW you measure progress. They are:
- **Quantitative** -- they are numbers, not narratives
- **Measurable** -- you can track them objectively
- **Outcome-based** -- they measure results, not activities
- **Ambitious** -- 70% achievement is considered success (Google model)

Good key results: "Achieve $2M ARR" / "NPS score of 50+" / "30% MoM growth"
Bad key results: "Launch new feature" (activity) / "Have 5 meetings" (output)

### OKR Scoring

```
0.0 - 0.3: Failed to make meaningful progress
0.4 - 0.6: Made progress but fell short
0.7 - 1.0: Achieved the key result

Target: Average 0.6-0.7 across all OKRs
(Consistently scoring 1.0 means goals are not ambitious enough)
```

### OKR Alignment Cascade

```
COMPANY OKRs (CEO Brain)
  |
  +-- Business Strategy OKRs (MBA Brain)
  +-- Product OKRs (Product Brain)
  +-- Engineering OKRs (Engineering Brain)
  +-- Growth OKRs (Marketing + Growth Brains)
  +-- Operations OKRs (Operations Brain)
  +-- People OKRs (HR Brain)

Each brain's OKRs support at least one company OKR.
No brain OKR should be orphaned (disconnected from company goals).
```

## Resource Allocation

### The Resource Allocation Framework

Resource allocation is strategy made real. Where you spend money and
people IS your strategy, regardless of what your strategy document says.

```
ALLOCATION PRINCIPLE: 70/20/10

70% of resources: Core business
  - Existing products, existing markets
  - Revenue-generating activities
  - Maintenance and improvement

20% of resources: Adjacent opportunities
  - New products for existing customers
  - Existing products for new markets
  - Expanding the core

10% of resources: Transformational bets
  - New products for new markets
  - Emerging technology exploration
  - Long-shot innovation
```

### Headcount Allocation

The CEO's most consequential resource allocation: where to add headcount.

```
Question: Where does the next hire go?

Framework:
1. What is the biggest constraint on our current strategy?
2. Will a hire in [function] relax that constraint?
3. What is the expected ROI of this hire vs. alternatives?
4. Can we fund this hire for 18+ months? (hiring is a commitment)
```

### Budget Allocation

```
Budget Allocation Decision Tree:
      |
      v
Does this support a company OKR?
  NO  --> Do not fund (or deprioritize)
  YES --> Continue
      |
      v
What is the expected ROI?
  HIGH --> Fund fully
  MEDIUM --> Fund partially, review at midpoint
  LOW --> Fund only if strategically critical
  UNKNOWN --> Fund as an experiment with kill criteria
```

## Strategy Communication

### Why Strategy Communication Fails

Most strategy communication fails because:
1. **Too abstract** -- "We will be the leading platform" means nothing actionable
2. **Too detailed** -- 50-page strategy documents are not read
3. **One-time** -- strategy communicated once and forgotten
4. **One-direction** -- leadership talks, nobody listens

### The CEO Communication Cascade

```
STRATEGY MESSAGE
      |
      +-- Board (detailed, financial, quarterly)
      |
      +-- Executive team (detailed, operational, weekly)
      |
      +-- All-hands (inspirational, directional, monthly)
      |
      +-- Individual teams (translated to their context, sprint)
      |
      +-- External (customers, partners, press, as appropriate)
```

### The Strategy One-Pager

Every company should have a one-page strategy document that anyone can
understand in 5 minutes:

```
STRATEGY ONE-PAGER
+--------------------------------------------------+
| VISION: [One sentence]                           |
|                                                  |
| MISSION: [One sentence]                          |
|                                                  |
| STRATEGIC POSITION: [Cost/Differentiation/Focus] |
|                                                  |
| THIS YEAR'S THEME: [One phrase]                  |
|                                                  |
| TOP 3 OBJECTIVES:                                |
| 1. [Objective] -- Key Result: [Metric]          |
| 2. [Objective] -- Key Result: [Metric]          |
| 3. [Objective] -- Key Result: [Metric]          |
|                                                  |
| WE WILL NOT:                                     |
| - [Explicit strategic tradeoff 1]               |
| - [Explicit strategic tradeoff 2]               |
|                                                  |
| BIGGEST RISK: [One sentence]                     |
| MITIGATION: [One sentence]                       |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

### Repetition is the Strategy

Andy Grove: "I believe the answer is so obvious it escapes most managers:
you have to communicate the strategy over and over and over again. And
just when you think you have communicated it enough, you have to
communicate it again."

The CEO should be able to articulate the strategy in:
- 30 seconds (elevator pitch)
- 5 minutes (one-pager walkthrough)
- 30 minutes (detailed presentation)
- 3 hours (deep working session)

All four versions must be consistent.

## Strategic Planning Anti-Patterns

### Anti-Pattern 1: Planning as Theater

The plan is created, presented, filed, and ignored. No one refers to it.
Quarterly reviews are skipped. OKRs are not scored.

Fix: OKRs are reviewed weekly in leadership meeting. Quarterly grading is
mandatory. Annual plan is a living document.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Strategy Without Tradeoffs

The strategy includes everything: "We will be the best product at the
lowest price with the best customer service in every market."

Fix: Every strategy must include an explicit "We will NOT" section. If
you are not saying no to something, you do not have a strategy.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Annual Planning Only

Strategy is revisited once a year. The quarterly cadence is ignored. The
world changes; the plan does not.

Fix: Annual objectives are stable. Quarterly key results are adjusted
based on learning. Sprint tactics are fully flexible.

### Anti-Pattern 4: OKRs as Performance Reviews

OKRs are used to evaluate individual performance, which incentivizes
sandbagging (setting easy goals to ensure you "hit your OKRs").

Fix: OKRs are organizational alignment tools, not performance evaluation
tools. Ambitious OKRs with 70% achievement are healthy.

## CEO Brain Strategic Planning Protocol

When the CEO Brain engages in strategic planning:

1. Assess current company stage (consult company_building.md)
2. Review competitive position (consult competitive_strategy.md)
3. Validate or update Vision and Mission
4. Confirm strategic position (Porter)
5. Set annual objectives (3-5 maximum)
6. Define quarterly key results (3-5 per objective)
7. Allocate resources (70/20/10)
8. Communicate strategy (one-pager + cascade)
9. Establish review cadence (weekly check, quarterly grade)
10. Define kill criteria for major strategic bets

---

**Strategic planning connects vision to daily work. The CEO Brain ensures
that every brain's output contributes to organizational strategy, that
resources flow to strategic priorities, and that the plan evolves as
the organization learns.**
