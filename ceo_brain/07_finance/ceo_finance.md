# CEO Finance -- Reading Financials and Capital Allocation

## Overview

The CEO does not need to be a CFO. But the CEO must be financially
literate enough to read financial statements, understand unit economics,
make capital allocation decisions, manage cash runway, and partner
effectively with the finance function. A CEO who cannot read a P&L is
flying blind. A CEO who cannot manage burn rate will run out of runway.

This module covers the financial knowledge every CEO must have -- not
at CFO depth, but at CEO decision-making depth.

---

## Reading Financial Statements

### The Three Financial Statements

Every CEO must read and understand three statements:

```
1. INCOME STATEMENT (P&L) -- "Are we profitable?"
   Revenue
   - Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
   = Gross Profit
   - Operating Expenses (OpEx)
     - Sales & Marketing
     - Research & Development
     - General & Administrative
   = Operating Income (EBIT)
   - Interest & Taxes
   = Net Income

2. BALANCE SHEET -- "What do we own and owe?"
   Assets = Liabilities + Equity
   Assets:
     - Cash and equivalents
     - Accounts receivable
     - Property and equipment
     - Intangible assets
   Liabilities:
     - Accounts payable
     - Debt
     - Deferred revenue
   Equity:
     - Invested capital
     - Retained earnings

3. CASH FLOW STATEMENT -- "Where is the cash going?"
   Cash from Operations
   + Cash from Investing
   + Cash from Financing
   = Net Change in Cash
```

### CEO Financial Literacy Test

The CEO should be able to answer these questions at any time:

```
1. What is our monthly revenue? (Know the number)
2. What is our monthly burn rate? (Revenue - Expenses)
3. How many months of runway do we have? (Cash / Monthly Burn)
4. What is our gross margin? (Gross Profit / Revenue)
5. What is our CAC? (Sales+Marketing cost / New customers)
6. What is our LTV? (Average revenue per customer x Average lifespan)
7. What is our LTV/CAC ratio? (Should be >3x)
8. What is our monthly/annual growth rate?
9. What is our churn rate?
10. Are we default alive or default dead?
    (Paul Graham: If expenses stay flat and revenue grows at current
     rate, do we reach profitability before cash runs out?)
```

## Key Financial Metrics for CEOs

### SaaS Metrics (for Software Companies)

```
GROWTH METRICS:
+---------------------------+------------------+
| Metric                    | Healthy Target   |
+---------------------------+------------------+
| MRR (Monthly Recurring)   | Growing 10%+ MoM |
| ARR (Annual Recurring)    | Growing 100%+ YoY|
| Net Revenue Retention     | >120%            |
| Gross Revenue Retention   | >90%             |
| New MRR                   | Growing MoM      |
+---------------------------+------------------+

EFFICIENCY METRICS:
+---------------------------+------------------+
| Metric                    | Healthy Target   |
+---------------------------+------------------+
| Gross Margin              | >70%             |
| CAC Payback Period        | <18 months       |
| LTV/CAC                   | >3x              |
| Burn Multiple             | <2x              |
|  (Net burn / Net new ARR) |                  |
| Magic Number              | >1.0             |
|  (New ARR / S&M spend)    |                  |
+---------------------------+------------------+

UNIT ECONOMICS:
+---------------------------+------------------+
| Metric                    | Healthy Target   |
+---------------------------+------------------+
| ARPU (Avg Rev Per User)   | Increasing       |
| Expansion Revenue         | >30% of new MRR  |
| Logo Churn                | <5% annually     |
| Revenue Churn             | Negative (NRR>100)|
+---------------------------+------------------+
```

### The Rule of 40

For growth-stage SaaS companies, the Rule of 40 states:

```
Revenue Growth Rate + Profit Margin >= 40%

Examples:
- 80% growth + -40% margin = 40% (Acceptable: high growth)
- 40% growth + 0% margin = 40% (Acceptable: balanced)
- 20% growth + 20% margin = 40% (Acceptable: profitable)
- 10% growth + 10% margin = 20% (Below target: underperforming)
```

The Rule of 40 helps the CEO balance growth and profitability. A company
can trade growth for profit (or vice versa) as long as the sum stays
above 40%.

## Capital Allocation

### The CEO as Capital Allocator

Warren Buffett identifies capital allocation as the CEO's most important
financial function. Every dollar the company has can be allocated to:

```
CAPITAL ALLOCATION OPTIONS:
+--------------------------------------------------+
| 1. INVEST IN EXISTING BUSINESS                   |
|    - R&D, engineering, product improvement       |
|    - Sales and marketing expansion               |
|    - Operational improvement                     |
|                                                  |
| 2. INVEST IN NEW BUSINESS                        |
|    - New products or markets                     |
|    - R&D for future capabilities                 |
|    - Exploratory initiatives                     |
|                                                  |
| 3. ACQUIRE CAPABILITIES                          |
|    - M&A for technology, team, or market access  |
|    - Acqui-hires                                 |
|                                                  |
| 4. RETURN TO INVESTORS                           |
|    - Dividends (rare for startups)               |
|    - Share buybacks (rare for startups)           |
|                                                  |
| 5. HOLD AS CASH                                  |
|    - Strategic reserve                           |
|    - Optionality for future opportunities        |
|    - Insurance against downside scenarios        |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

### Capital Allocation Framework

```
ALLOCATION DECISION TREE:
      |
      v
Is the ROI of investing in existing business > cost of capital?
  YES --> Invest in existing (highest conviction)
  NO  --> Continue
      |
      v
Is there a new business opportunity with compelling ROI?
  YES --> Invest in new business (with kill criteria)
  NO  --> Continue
      |
      v
Is there an acquisition that accelerates our strategy?
  YES --> Evaluate M&A (rigorous due diligence)
  NO  --> Continue
      |
      v
Is there a compelling reason to return capital?
  YES --> Return to investors (dividends, buybacks)
  NO  --> Hold as cash (strategic reserve)
```

### The 70/20/10 Rule (Applied to Capital)

```
70% --> Core business (existing products, existing markets)
20% --> Adjacent opportunities (new products or new markets)
10% --> Transformational bets (new products AND new markets)
```

This ensures the company funds its current business while investing in
future growth.

## Fundraising Timing

### When to Raise

```
RAISE WHEN:
- You have 12+ months of runway AND strong metrics to show
- You need capital to capture a time-sensitive opportunity
- Your metrics justify a valuation step-up
- Market conditions are favorable (investor sentiment positive)
- You have competing interest from investors (leverage)

DO NOT RAISE WHEN:
- You have <6 months runway (desperation = bad terms)
- Metrics are trending negative (low valuation)
- You do not know what you would do with the money
- Market conditions are poor (tight credit, recession)
- You can reach profitability without external capital
```

### Fundraising Math

```
FUNDRAISING CALCULATION:
1. Burn rate: $X/month
2. Months to next milestone: Y months
3. Buffer: 6 months (always)
4. Amount needed: $X * (Y + 6)
5. Target raise: Amount needed * 1.2 (negotiation buffer)
6. Dilution: Raise / Post-money valuation
7. Post-money valuation: Raise / Target dilution (15-25%)
```

### Fundraising Milestones by Stage

| Stage | Milestone to Hit | Typical Raise | Typical Dilution |
|-------|-----------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Pre-seed | Problem validated, team formed | $250K-$1M | 10-15% |
| Seed | MVP, early traction | $1M-$5M | 15-25% |
| Series A | PMF, repeatable growth | $5M-$20M | 15-25% |
| Series B | Scaling, unit economics | $15M-$50M | 15-20% |
| Series C+ | Market leadership | $50M+ | 10-15% |

## Burn Rate Management

### The Burn Rate Dashboard

```
BURN RATE ANALYSIS
+--------------------------------------------------+
| GROSS BURN: Total monthly expenses       $XXX,000|
| NET BURN: Expenses - Revenue             $XXX,000|
| CASH ON HAND:                          $X,XXX,000|
| RUNWAY (Gross): Cash / Gross Burn        XX months|
| RUNWAY (Net): Cash / Net Burn            XX months|
|                                                  |
| BURN BY CATEGORY:                                |
| Payroll:                        $XXX,000 (XX%)   |
| Infrastructure:                 $XX,000  (XX%)   |
| Marketing:                      $XX,000  (XX%)   |
| Office/Admin:                   $XX,000  (XX%)   |
| Other:                          $XX,000  (XX%)   |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

### The Default Alive/Dead Framework (Paul Graham)

```
DEFAULT ALIVE:
Revenue is growing, and if expenses stay constant,
the company reaches profitability before cash runs out.
--> You have optionality. You can choose to raise or not.

DEFAULT DEAD:
Revenue is not growing fast enough to reach profitability
before cash runs out.
--> You MUST raise capital, cut costs, or increase revenue.
    There is no fourth option.
```

### Cash Conservation Levers

When runway gets short (priority order):

```
1. INCREASE REVENUE (highest priority, hardest)
   - Raise prices
   - Upsell existing customers
   - Accelerate sales pipeline

2. REDUCE VARIABLE COSTS
   - Cut marketing spend (carefully -- not customer acq channels)
   - Renegotiate vendor contracts
   - Reduce infrastructure costs

3. REDUCE HEADCOUNT (last resort)
   - Hiring freeze first
   - Contractor reduction second
   - Layoffs third (if necessary, do it once and deep)
```

## CFO Partnership

### What the CEO Needs from a CFO

| Function | CEO Expectation |
|----------|----------------|
| Financial reporting | Accurate, timely, decision-ready |
| Financial planning | Forward-looking models, scenario analysis |
| Cash management | Optimize cash position, manage runway |
| Fundraising | Financial story, investor materials, diligence |
| Compliance | Legal compliance, audit readiness |
| Strategic finance | Investment analysis, M&A support |
| Board reporting | Board-ready financial packages |

### CEO-CFO Operating Rhythm

```
WEEKLY: 30-minute check-in
- Cash position update
- Revenue vs. plan
- Any financial red flags

MONTHLY: 60-minute review
- Full P&L review
- Variance analysis (actual vs. plan)
- Reforecast if needed
- Cash flow projection update

QUARTERLY: Half-day planning
- Board prep
- OKR financial alignment
- Budget reallocation
- Fundraising timing assessment
```

### When to Hire a CFO vs. VP Finance

```
VP FINANCE / HEAD OF FINANCE (earlier hire):
- Manage day-to-day accounting
- Financial reporting and compliance
- Budget management
- Tax preparation
- Typically: $1M-$10M ARR

CFO (later hire):
- Strategic financial leadership
- Fundraising and investor relations
- M&A evaluation and execution
- Board-level financial partnership
- Complex financial modeling
- Typically: $10M+ ARR or pre-IPO
```

## Financial Decision Framework

Before any significant financial decision:

```
FINANCIAL DECISION CHECKLIST
- [ ] What is the cost? (one-time and ongoing)
- [ ] What is the expected return? (quantified)
- [ ] What is the payback period?
- [ ] What is the impact on runway?
- [ ] What is the opportunity cost? (what else could we do?)
- [ ] Is this reversible if it does not work?
- [ ] What are the kill criteria?
- [ ] Does the CFO agree with the analysis?
```

---

**CEO finance is not about mastering accounting -- it is about making
informed financial decisions that ensure the company has the resources to
execute its strategy. The CEO Brain maintains financial literacy
sufficient to partner with the Finance Brain and make sound capital
allocation decisions.**
