# CEO Brain -- Glossary

## Core Terminology

This glossary defines every term used across the CEO Brain modules. Terms are drawn from organizational theory, strategic management, executive leadership, and the specific vocabulary of the DropFly brain system.

---

## A

### Agency Cost
The cost incurred when an agent (specialist brain) does not perfectly align with the principal's (CEO Brain's) objectives. Includes monitoring costs, bonding costs, and residual loss. Source: Jensen & Meckling (1976).

### All-Hands
A communication format where the CEO addresses the entire organization. In the brain system, this refers to a broadcast communication to all active brains establishing context, priorities, and strategic direction. See `06_communication/executive_communication.md`.

### Arbitration
The CEO Brain's function of resolving disagreements between specialist brains. When Design Brain and Engineering Brain disagree on feasibility, the CEO Brain arbitrates by applying the Decision Quality Framework.

---

## B

### BHAG (Big Hairy Audacious Goal)
A long-term, audacious strategic goal that galvanizes organizational effort. Coined by Collins and Porras in *Built to Last* (1994). The CEO Brain sets BHAGs; specialist brains execute toward them.

### Board Meeting
A structured strategic review cadence. In the brain system, this refers to the highest-level review of organizational performance, strategic direction, and resource allocation. See `04_execution/operating_cadence.md`.

### Bottleneck
A constraint that limits the throughput of the entire system. Per Goldratt's *Theory of Constraints* (1984), the CEO Brain identifies and addresses the binding constraint. A brain becomes a bottleneck when it has more work than capacity.

### Brain
A self-governing specialist AI system with deep domain expertise, its own governance (CLAUDE.md), quality standards, and memory. Each brain operates autonomously within its domain and coordinates with other brains through the CEO Brain.

### Brain Catalog
The master index of all 37 brains in the system, including their specialties, locations, interfaces, and when to call each. See `08_brain_catalog/brain_index.md`.

### Brain Routing
The CEO Brain's function of directing incoming requests to the appropriate specialist brain(s). See `02_orchestration/brain_routing.md`.

---

## C

### Cadence
A recurring rhythm of activities at defined intervals (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, annually). Per Grove, cadence creates predictability and reduces coordination costs. See `04_execution/operating_cadence.md`.

### Chandlerian Insight
"Structure follows strategy." Alfred Chandler (*Strategy and Structure*, 1962) demonstrated that organizational design must serve strategic objectives. The brain system's architecture reflects this: specialist brains exist because the strategy requires specialized expertise.

### Conflict Resolution
The process by which the CEO Brain resolves disagreements between specialist brains. Uses the Decision Quality Framework with specific arbitration criteria. See `02_orchestration/coordination.md`.

### Coordination Cost
The overhead required to align multiple specialist brains toward a common goal. Includes task decomposition, handoff definition, quality gates, and integration review. The CEO Brain's primary cost center.

### Critical Path
The longest sequence of dependent tasks that determines the minimum project duration. The CEO Brain identifies the critical path during task decomposition to ensure it receives priority. See `02_orchestration/task_decomposition.md`.

---

## D

### Decision Quality Framework
The CEO Brain's 7-step framework for making high-quality decisions: Classify, Identify Decider, Audit Incentives, Second-Order Effects, Tradeoff Transparency, Kill Criteria, Speed Calibration. See `01_foundations/decision_making.md`.

### Delegation
The CEO Brain's primary execution mechanism: assigning work to specialist brains with clear objectives, constraints, context, authority, and quality criteria. The CEO Brain delegates; it does not do.

### Dependency Graph
A directed acyclic graph (DAG) of tasks where edges represent dependencies between tasks. Used in task decomposition to sequence multi-brain work. See `02_orchestration/task_decomposition.md`.

### Drucker's Effective Executive
Peter Drucker's framework (*The Effective Executive*, 1967) for executive effectiveness: manage time, focus on contribution, build on strengths, concentrate on the few major areas, make effective decisions.

---

## E

### Execution Excellence
The discipline of converting decisions into outcomes reliably and at high quality. The CEO Brain's execution function draws from Grove's OKR system, Drucker's MBO, and Horowitz's "take care of the people, the products, and the profits -- in that order." See `04_execution/execution_excellence.md`.

### Executive Decision
A decision that requires cross-functional context, involves irreversible commitments, or cannot be delegated to a single specialist brain. The CEO Brain's exclusive domain.

---

## F

### First-Order Effect
The immediate, obvious consequence of a decision. CEO Brain thinking requires going beyond first-order effects to second-order and third-order consequences.

### Flywheel
A self-reinforcing cycle where each component accelerates the next. Coined by Jim Collins (*Good to Great*, 2001). The CEO Brain identifies and builds organizational flywheels. See `01_foundations/systems_leadership.md`.

---

## G

### Grove's Law of Management
"A manager's output equals the output of the organization under their control plus the output of the neighboring organizations under their influence." Applied to the CEO Brain: output = sum of all brain outputs, modulated by coordination quality.

---

## H

### Handoff Contract
A formal agreement between two brains defining what one brain delivers to the next: format, content, quality standards, and acceptance criteria. The CEO Brain defines handoff contracts for multi-brain coordination.

### High Output Management
Andy Grove's management framework: focus on the leverage of your activities, conduct one-on-ones, monitor leading indicators, and make the organization's output your output.

---

## I

### Integration Point
The moment when outputs from multiple specialist brains must be combined into a coherent whole. The CEO Brain reviews at every integration point.

---

## K

### Kill Criteria
Pre-defined conditions under which an initiative should be abandoned or reversed. Every major CEO Brain decision must include kill criteria. This prevents sunk cost fallacy and escalation of commitment.

---

## L

### Leverage
Per Grove, the amount of output generated per unit of input. The CEO Brain optimizes for high-leverage activities: decisions that affect many brains, coordination improvements that reduce friction system-wide, quality gates that prevent costly rework.

### Local Optimization
When a specialist brain optimizes for its own domain at the expense of the whole system. Engineering builds the most elegant architecture (slow to ship). Design creates the most beautiful interface (impossible to implement). The CEO Brain prevents local optimization by maintaining the global perspective.

---

## M

### MBO (Management by Objectives)
Drucker's framework for aligning organizational activity with objectives. The predecessor to OKRs. The CEO Brain sets objectives; specialist brains determine how to achieve them.

### Mode
An operating context that determines which protocols, frameworks, and cadences the CEO Brain applies. Five modes: BUILD, OPERATE, CRISIS, STRATEGY, DELEGATE. See CLAUDE.md.

### Moat
A sustainable competitive advantage that protects a business from competition. Source: Buffett. Types: network effects, switching costs, economies of scale, brand, intellectual property, regulatory. See `03_strategy/competitive_strategy.md`.

---

## N

### Negative Space Decision
A decision about what NOT to do. Often more important than positive decisions. The CEO Brain explicitly identifies and communicates what the organization will not pursue.

---

## O

### OKR (Objectives and Key Results)
A goal-setting framework where Objectives are qualitative and aspirational, and Key Results are quantitative and measurable. Originated by Grove at Intel, popularized by Doerr at Google. The CEO Brain's primary execution framework. See `04_execution/execution_excellence.md`.

### One-Way Door
An irreversible or nearly irreversible decision. Requires deep analysis, broad input, and CEO-level judgment. Source: Bezos. Contrast with Two-Way Door.

### Operating Cadence
The recurring rhythm of reviews, decisions, and communications that maintain organizational alignment. See `04_execution/operating_cadence.md`.

### Orchestration
The CEO Brain's core function: coordinating multiple specialist brains toward coherent organizational outcomes. Includes routing, decomposition, sequencing, conflict resolution, and quality oversight.

---

## P

### Peacetime CEO
Per Horowitz, the CEO mode when the company has a clear market advantage and can focus on growth, culture, and strategic expansion. Contrast with Wartime CEO.

### Post-Mortem
A structured review after a project completion or incident. The CEO Brain conducts post-mortems to extract learnings and improve future coordination.

### Principal-Agent Problem
The misalignment between the goals of the principal (CEO Brain/organization) and the agent (specialist brain). Mitigated through clear objectives, quality gates, and accountability.

---

## Q

### Quality Gate
A checkpoint where output is evaluated against predefined criteria before proceeding. The CEO Brain establishes quality gates at every inter-brain handoff and integration point.

---

## R

### Resource Allocation
The CEO Brain's function of directing organizational resources (time, attention, energy, capital) to their highest-leverage use. Per Bower and Gilbert, resource allocation IS strategy.

### Retrospective
A structured review of how work was done, aimed at process improvement. Distinct from post-mortem (which reviews outcomes) in that retrospectives focus on methodology.

### Routing Table
The CEO Brain's master map of which brain handles which type of request. See `02_orchestration/brain_routing.md` and `08_brain_catalog/brain_index.md`.

---

## S

### Second-Order Effect
The consequence of a consequence. First-order: "We cut prices." Second-order: "Competitors respond with their own cuts." Third-order: "Industry margins compress, weaker players exit." The CEO Brain always reasons to second-order and beyond.

### SPINE Framework
Strategy, People, Incentives, Narrative, Execution -- the five systems the CEO Brain must maintain in alignment. See `01_foundations/ceo_theory.md`.

### Systems Thinking
The discipline of seeing wholes, understanding interrelationships rather than isolated events, and recognizing patterns of change. Source: Senge (*The Fifth Discipline*, 1990). See `01_foundations/systems_leadership.md`.

---

## T

### Task Decomposition
The CEO Brain's function of breaking complex, multi-domain goals into a graph of brain-specific tasks with defined dependencies, handoffs, and quality criteria.

### Task-Relevant Maturity (TRM)
Grove's concept that the level of management involvement should depend on the subordinate's experience with the specific task. Applied to brains: new or untested brains get more oversight; mature brains get more autonomy.

### Tradeoff
An exchange where gaining something requires giving up something else. The CEO Brain makes tradeoffs explicit rather than implicit. Every strategic decision involves tradeoffs.

### Two-Way Door
A reversible decision that can be easily undone. Should be made quickly, with minimal deliberation, at the lowest competent level. Source: Bezos.

---

## V

### Vision
The long-term destination that guides all organizational activity. The CEO Brain owns the vision and ensures all brain activity aligns with it.

---

## W

### Wartime CEO
Per Horowitz, the CEO mode when the company faces existential threats: competition attacking core market, imminent bankruptcy, need to pivot. Wartime CEO is directive, focused, and willing to violate social norms. Contrast with Peacetime CEO.
