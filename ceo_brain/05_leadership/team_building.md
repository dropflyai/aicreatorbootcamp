# Team Building -- Executive Team Design and Development

## Overview

The executive team is the CEO's primary lever for organizational
performance. A great CEO with a mediocre executive team produces mediocre
results. A good CEO with an exceptional executive team produces exceptional
results. Executive team building -- hiring, onboarding, developing, and
occasionally firing executives -- is the most consequential work a CEO does.

Patrick Lencioni (*The Five Dysfunctions of a Team*, 2002) demonstrates
that even brilliant individuals fail when team dynamics are broken. This
module codifies the frameworks for building, developing, and maintaining
a high-performing executive team.

## When to Hire Each Executive Function

### The Hiring Sequence

The order in which you hire executives depends on company stage and
strategy, but a common sequence for technology companies:

```
STAGE: PRE-PMF (0-10 employees)
+--------------------------------------------------+
| Founders do everything.                          |
| MAYBE: VP Engineering (if founders are not       |
|        technical) or Head of Design              |
+--------------------------------------------------+

STAGE: PMF TO $1M ARR (10-25 employees)
+--------------------------------------------------+
| VP Engineering (if not already hired)            |
| Head of Product                                  |
| Head of Design (if not already hired)            |
+--------------------------------------------------+

STAGE: $1M-$5M ARR (25-75 employees)
+--------------------------------------------------+
| VP Sales (or Head of Growth for PLG)            |
| VP Marketing                                     |
| Head of Customer Success                         |
| Head of Finance / FP&A                          |
+--------------------------------------------------+

STAGE: $5M-$20M ARR (75-200 employees)
+--------------------------------------------------+
| VP People / HR                                   |
| VP Operations (if relevant)                      |
| General Counsel (or outside counsel)             |
| CFO (upgrade from Head of Finance)              |
+--------------------------------------------------+

STAGE: $20M+ ARR (200+ employees)
+--------------------------------------------------+
| CTO (if VP Engineering needs to separate)        |
| CMO (if VP Marketing needs to separate)          |
| COO (if operational complexity warrants)         |
| Chief Revenue Officer                             |
| CISO (if security is business-critical)          |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

### VP vs. C-Level Timing

```
HIRE VP WHEN:
- The function exists and needs professional leadership
- The company has 25-100 employees
- The function needs to scale from ad hoc to systematic
- You need execution excellence in the function

UPGRADE TO C-LEVEL WHEN:
- The function is strategically critical to the business
- The person needs to represent the company externally
- The person needs board-level authority and accountability
- The company has 100+ employees
- Multiple VPs need a senior coordinator
```

### The Premature Hire Trap

Hiring an executive too early is as dangerous as hiring too late:

| Hire Too Early | Consequence |
|---------------|------------|
| VP Sales before PMF | Builds sales process for wrong product |
| CMO before product | Spends budget with nothing to sell |
| VP Ops at 10 people | Over-processes kill startup speed |
| CFO at $500K ARR | Expensive for the financial complexity |
| CHRO at 15 people | Culture building at this stage is the CEO's job |

## Executive Search Process

### Defining the Role

Before searching, define with precision:

```
EXECUTIVE ROLE DEFINITION
+--------------------------------------------------+
| TITLE: [Title]                                   |
| REPORTS TO: [CEO / Other]                        |
|                                                  |
| WHY NOW:                                         |
| [Why this hire, why at this stage]              |
|                                                  |
| OUTCOMES (First 12 months):                      |
| 1. [Specific, measurable outcome]               |
| 2. [Specific, measurable outcome]               |
| 3. [Specific, measurable outcome]               |
|                                                  |
| NON-NEGOTIABLE REQUIREMENTS:                     |
| 1. [Has done X at scale Y]                     |
| 2. [Specific domain expertise]                   |
| 3. [Cultural value alignment]                    |
|                                                  |
| NICE-TO-HAVE:                                    |
| 1. [Additional experience]                      |
| 2. [Network or relationships]                    |
|                                                  |
| ANTI-PATTERNS (What we do NOT want):             |
| 1. [Specific red flag]                          |
| 2. [Specific red flag]                          |
|                                                  |
| COMPENSATION RANGE: [Range]                      |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

### Sourcing Executives

| Channel | When to Use | Pros | Cons |
|---------|-----------|------|------|
| Executive search firm | Critical hires, C-level | Access to passive candidates | Expensive (25-33% of comp) |
| Network referrals | All levels | Pre-vetted, cultural context | Limited pool |
| LinkedIn outreach | VP level | Broad access, direct | Time-intensive, low response |
| Board connections | C-level | High quality, board-endorsed | Limited diversity |
| Investor portfolio | All levels | Industry-specific | Potential conflicts |

### The Executive Interview Process

```
STAGE 1: SCREENING (30 min, CEO or designee)
- Career narrative
- Why this role, why now
- Compensation expectations
- Red flag check

STAGE 2: DEEP DIVE (60 min, CEO)
- Specific experience relevant to the role
- How they have handled challenges similar to ours
- Their leadership philosophy and style
- What they would do in the first 90 days

STAGE 3: CROSS-FUNCTIONAL (45 min each, 2-3 peers)
- How they collaborate with other functions
- Communication style
- Cultural fit/add assessment

STAGE 4: CASE STUDY (60 min, CEO + team)
- Present a real challenge the company faces
- Candidate presents their approach
- Evaluates strategic thinking, communication, pragmatism

STAGE 5: REFERENCES (CEO personally)
- 4-6 references, including at least 2 back-channel
- Ask: "On a scale of 1-10, how strongly do you recommend?"
  (Anything below 8 is a red flag)
- Ask: "What would this person need to improve?"

STAGE 6: OFFER AND CLOSE (CEO)
- CEO makes the offer personally
- Sell the vision, the role, the opportunity
- Negotiate transparently
```

## Executive Onboarding

### The First 90 Days

Michael Watkins (*The First 90 Days*, 2003) provides the framework:

```
DAYS 1-30: LEARN
+--------------------------------------------------+
| - Meet every direct report (1:1, 60 min)        |
| - Meet every peer executive (1:1, 45 min)       |
| - Meet 10+ customers                            |
| - Review all key metrics and dashboards          |
| - Understand current strategy and OKRs           |
| - Identify the top 3 issues in the function      |
| - DO NOT make major changes yet                  |
+--------------------------------------------------+

DAYS 30-60: DIAGNOSE
+--------------------------------------------------+
| - Present assessment to CEO (strengths, gaps,    |
|   opportunities, threats)                        |
| - Propose 90-day plan with specific objectives   |
| - Identify quick wins (high impact, low effort)  |
| - Begin building key relationships               |
| - Start making small, visible improvements       |
+--------------------------------------------------+

DAYS 60-90: ACT
+--------------------------------------------------+
| - Execute quick wins                             |
| - Begin addressing the top 3 issues              |
| - Establish new meeting cadence for the function |
| - Hire or begin hiring for critical gaps         |
| - Present 12-month plan to CEO                   |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

### CEO's Onboarding Responsibilities

The CEO must:
1. Provide strategic context and expectations in writing
2. Introduce the new executive to key stakeholders personally
3. Schedule weekly 1:1s for the first 90 days (reduce to biweekly after)
4. Provide explicit feedback at 30, 60, and 90 days
5. Protect the new executive from being overwhelmed by legacy issues
6. Make the cultural expectations explicit

## Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions of a Team

### The Pyramid

Patrick Lencioni identifies five dysfunctions that destroy team
effectiveness, each building on the one below:

```
                    /\
                   /  \
                  /    \
                 / IN-  \
                /ATTENTION\
               / TO RESULTS\
              /--------------\
             /  AVOIDANCE OF  \
            / ACCOUNTABILITY   \
           /--------------------\
          /   LACK OF COMMITMENT \
         /------------------------\
        /    FEAR OF CONFLICT      \
       /----------------------------\
      /   ABSENCE OF TRUST           \
     /--------------------------------\
```

### Dysfunction 1: Absence of Trust

Trust is vulnerability-based: team members are willing to be open about
their mistakes, weaknesses, and concerns.

Without trust:
- Executives hide mistakes and weaknesses
- They do not ask for help
- They do not offer genuine feedback to each other
- They spend energy on defensive behavior

CEO remedy:
- Model vulnerability ("I was wrong about X. Here is what I learned.")
- Create psychological safety in executive meetings
- Use personal histories exercise (non-work sharing)
- Conduct 360 feedback with follow-through

### Dysfunction 2: Fear of Conflict

Healthy conflict is ideological -- passionate debate about ideas, not
personal attacks.

Without conflict:
- Meetings are boring and unproductive
- Decisions are made in hallways, not in meetings
- Elephants in the room go unaddressed
- Passive-aggressive behavior replaces honest debate

CEO remedy:
- Model healthy conflict ("I disagree with that approach. Here is why.")
- Designate a devil's advocate for major decisions
- Call out the elephant: "What are we not saying?"
- Distinguish between conflict about ideas (good) and conflict about
  people (bad)

### Dysfunction 3: Lack of Commitment

Commitment requires clarity and buy-in, not consensus.

Without commitment:
- Ambiguity about direction
- Second-guessing decisions after they are made
- Revisiting the same decisions repeatedly
- Lack of alignment in execution

CEO remedy:
- End every meeting with explicit decisions and commitments
- Use "disagree and commit" (Bezos)
- Set deadlines for decisions; decide with available information
- Document decisions and rationale

### Dysfunction 4: Avoidance of Accountability

Accountability means holding peers to high standards.

Without accountability:
- Mediocrity is tolerated
- Deadlines are missed without consequence
- High performers are demoralized
- The CEO becomes the only source of accountability (bottleneck)

CEO remedy:
- Publish OKRs and progress publicly
- Create peer accountability (team reviews, not just CEO reviews)
- Address underperformance directly and promptly
- Celebrate accountability (not just results)

### Dysfunction 5: Inattention to Results

The team must prioritize collective results over individual or
departmental success.

Without results focus:
- Executives optimize for their function, not the company
- Status and ego drive behavior
- The team celebrates activity instead of outcomes
- Collective goals are subordinated to individual goals

CEO remedy:
- Tie compensation to company results (not just functional metrics)
- Report team results, not just individual results
- Celebrate collective wins publicly
- Address departmental silos that undermine company results

## Firing Executives

### When to Fire

The decision to fire an executive is one of the most consequential and
difficult Type 1 decisions a CEO makes.

```
FIRE WHEN:
- Performance does not improve after clear feedback and support
- Values misalignment is fundamental (not fixable)
- The role has outgrown the person and they cannot grow into it
- Trust has been broken irreparably
- The team under them is failing and they cannot diagnose why

DO NOT FIRE WHEN:
- You have not given clear feedback and a chance to improve
- The problem is the role definition, not the person
- You are reacting to a single incident (unless egregious)
- You do not have a succession plan
- You are angry (never fire when emotional)
```

### How to Fire with Dignity

1. **Decide with certainty** -- once you decide, do not second-guess
2. **Prepare the logistics** -- severance, transition, communication
3. **Have the conversation** -- in person, direct, compassionate
4. **Be clear** -- "This is not working, and we need to make a change"
5. **Take responsibility** -- "I did not set you up for success" (if true)
6. **Offer support** -- generous severance, reference, transition help
7. **Communicate to the team** -- promptly, honestly, respectfully
8. **Do not trash the person** -- ever, to anyone

