# Partner Operations — Day-to-Day Management, Communication Cadences, Joint Planning, and QBRs

## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**
- How to structure daily and weekly partnership operations to maximize productivity while minimizing coordination overhead
- What communication cadences are appropriate for different partnership types and maturity levels
- How to design joint business plans that create genuine alignment rather than performative planning documents
- How to run Quarterly Business Reviews that drive accountability and strategic progress

**Mistakes it prevents:**
- Allowing partnerships to drift between milestones due to lack of structured operational cadences
- Over-communicating with low-value partnerships while under-communicating with strategic ones
- Creating joint business plans that are never referenced after the planning session ends
- Running QBRs that devolve into status updates rather than strategic discussions with accountability

**Outputs it enables:**
- Partner operational playbooks with cadence templates and agenda frameworks
- Communication plans matched to partnership type and tier
- Joint business plan templates with shared KPIs, milestones, and accountability mechanisms
- QBR frameworks that balance retrospective analysis with forward-looking strategic planning

---

## The Core Insight

Partner operations is the **metabolism of partnerships** — the ongoing biochemistry that keeps partnerships alive and productive between strategic milestones. Strategy sets direction, agreements define terms, and programs create structure, but operations determine whether a partnership actually functions day to day. The most common mode of partnership death is not dramatic failure — it is **slow starvation through operational neglect.** Partnerships that lack structured cadences, clear accountability, and regular reviews decay invisibly until someone notices that the joint pipeline is empty, the integration is broken, or the partner's champion has left. Operational excellence in partnership management is not glamorous, but it is the single best predictor of partnership longevity and return on investment.

## 1. Partnership Operating Model

### The Operational Cadence Stack

Every active partnership requires a layered cadence of operational activities:

**Daily Operations** (working-level)
- Shared communication channel (Slack, Teams) for real-time coordination
- Deal support requests and technical escalation handling
- Integration monitoring and incident response
- Lead passing and deal registration processing

**Weekly Operations** (tactical)
- Pipeline review: New opportunities, deal progression, blockers
- Activity check: Marketing deliverables, enablement progress, integration milestones
- Issue triage: Open issues, resolution progress, escalation decisions
- Capacity planning: Upcoming demands on both teams' resources

**Monthly Operations** (operational strategy)
- KPI review: Performance against monthly targets
- Campaign review: Co-marketing performance and optimization
- Integration health: Technical performance metrics and improvement priorities
- Resource alignment: Staffing changes, priority shifts, upcoming initiatives

**Quarterly Operations** (strategic)
- QBR: Comprehensive review of partnership health and strategic direction
- Business plan review: Progress against joint business plan objectives
- Executive alignment: Sponsor-to-sponsor strategic discussion
- Roadmap sync: Product and integration roadmap alignment for next quarter

**Annual Operations** (strategic planning)
- Annual business plan: Set objectives, targets, and investment levels for the year
- Program review: Evaluate partnership tier, benefits, and structural adjustments
- Strategic reassessment: Confirm continued strategic alignment and partnership thesis
- Executive summit: Senior leadership alignment on partnership direction

### Operating Model by Partnership Type

| Partnership Type | Daily | Weekly | Monthly | Quarterly | Annual |
|-----------------|-------|--------|---------|-----------|--------|
| Referral | Shared channel | Lead review (15 min) | KPI check (30 min) | Performance review (1 hr) | Renewal review |
| Co-marketing | Shared channel | Campaign check (30 min) | Performance review (1 hr) | QBR (2 hr) | Annual plan |
| Technology | Shared channel | Pipeline + tech review (45 min) | Full review (1.5 hr) | QBR (3 hr) | Annual plan + roadmap |
| Channel | Shared channel | Pipeline + activity (1 hr) | Full review (2 hr) | QBR (3 hr) | Annual plan + tier review |
| Strategic | Shared channel | Pipeline + strategy (1 hr) | Executive briefing (1.5 hr) | QBR (4 hr) | Summit + annual plan |

## 2. Communication Cadence Design

### The Communication Hierarchy

**Level 1: Working-Level Communication**
- Who: Partner managers, sales reps, technical contacts
- Channel: Slack/Teams, email, partner portal
- Cadence: Continuous (async) + weekly sync (30-60 min)
- Purpose: Operational coordination, deal support, issue resolution
- Norms: Response within 4 business hours for standard requests, 1 hour for escalations

**Level 2: Management Communication**
- Who: Directors of partnerships, product managers, sales directors
- Channel: Video calls, formal email
- Cadence: Monthly sync (60-90 min)
- Purpose: KPI review, resource alignment, strategic issue discussion
- Norms: Structured agenda distributed 48 hours in advance, action items documented

**Level 3: Executive Communication**
- Who: VP/C-level sponsors
- Channel: Video calls, in-person meetings
- Cadence: Quarterly minimum (QBR), ad-hoc for escalations
- Purpose: Strategic alignment, investment decisions, relationship health
- Norms: Pre-read materials distributed 1 week in advance, decisions documented within 24 hours

### Communication Anti-Patterns

- **Ghost partnerships**: No communication for 30+ days signals a dying partnership. If no one is talking, no one is working.
- **Meeting inflation**: More meetings does not mean better communication. Every recurring meeting must justify its cadence with clear purpose and accountability.
- **Asymmetric communication**: One party driving all communication while the other passively receives. Healthy partnerships have balanced communication initiation.
- **Status-only communication**: Meetings that cover only what happened, never what should happen next. Operational meetings must be forward-looking.

## 3. Joint Business Planning

### The Joint Business Plan (JBP) Framework

A joint business plan is the strategic alignment document that both parties commit to executing. It is not a wish list — it is a contract of mutual intent with measurable milestones.

**Section 1: Partnership Vision** (1 page)
- Shared view of the market opportunity
- How the partnership creates value that neither party could create alone
- 12-month aspirational outcome statement

**Section 2: Shared Objectives** (1 page)
- 3-5 measurable objectives for the planning period
- Each objective has an owner from each organization
- Each objective has a quantified target and timeline
- Example: "Generate $2M in partner-sourced pipeline by Q3, with 25% conversion to closed revenue"

**Section 3: Go-to-Market Plan** (2-3 pages)
- Target customer profile for joint selling
- Co-marketing activities with calendar and budget
- Co-selling motions with rules of engagement
- Channel activities (if applicable) with recruitment and activation targets
- Launch activities for new integrations or offerings

**Section 4: Technical Roadmap** (1-2 pages)
- Integration milestones for the planning period
- Engineering resource commitments from each party
- API or product dependencies that must be resolved
- Testing and quality milestones

**Section 5: Resource Commitment** (1 page)
- Named individuals assigned to partnership activities from each organization
- Budget commitments (MDF, engineering time, marketing spend)
- Escalation points for resource conflicts

**Section 6: Measurement Framework** (1 page)
- KPIs for each objective with data sources and reporting cadence
- Monthly milestone checkpoints
- Quarterly review schedule
- Success criteria and underperformance triggers

### JBP Best Practices

**Co-create the plan**: The JBP must be developed jointly, not created by one party and presented to the other. Joint creation builds ownership.

**Keep it concise**: The JBP should be 6-8 pages maximum. Longer documents are not read or referenced.

**Make it visible**: The JBP should be accessible in the partner portal and referenced in every QBR. It is a living document, not a filing exercise.

**Review monthly**: At least one section of the JBP should be reviewed monthly against actual progress. Deviations should be discussed and adjustments made.

## 4. Quarterly Business Reviews (QBRs)

### The QBR Framework

QBRs are the most important recurring partnership ritual. A well-run QBR drives accountability, surfaces issues, and maintains strategic alignment. A poorly run QBR is a waste of everyone's time.

### QBR Structure (3-4 hours for strategic partnerships)

**Block 1: Retrospective** (60 min)
- Performance against KPIs (quantitative dashboard review)
- Wins and highlights (celebrate joint successes)
- Misses and root causes (honest assessment of underperformance)
- Customer feedback (joint NPS, customer health data, testimonials)

**Block 2: Current State** (45 min)
- Pipeline review: Current joint pipeline by stage, expected close dates, blockers
- Integration health: Technical performance, open issues, upcoming changes
- Program health: Partner tier status, certification progress, enablement utilization
- Competitive landscape: New market entrants, competitor partnership moves, market shifts

**Block 3: Forward Planning** (60 min)
- Next quarter objectives: Updated targets based on current trajectory
- Go-to-market activities: Planned campaigns, events, and co-selling initiatives
- Technical roadmap: Integration milestones and engineering commitments
- Resource planning: Any staffing or budget changes needed

**Block 4: Strategic Discussion** (45 min)
- Executive-level strategic alignment discussion
- Market opportunity assessment for the partnership
- Long-term partnership evolution and investment decisions
- Relationship health check (are both parties satisfied with the partnership?)

**Block 5: Action Items and Close** (15-30 min)
- Document all action items with owners and deadlines
- Confirm next QBR date
- Executive sponsor closing remarks

### QBR Preparation

**2 weeks before**: Send QBR preparation checklist to partner, including data requests
**1 week before**: Distribute pre-read materials (performance dashboard, pipeline summary, proposed agenda)
**3 days before**: Finalize agenda based on partner feedback and current issues
**Day of**: Ensure all presenters are prepared, technology is working, and key stakeholders are confirmed

### QBR Anti-Patterns

- **Status report theater**: QBR degenerates into a slide deck walkthrough with no discussion or decisions
- **Blame sessions**: QBR focuses on who failed rather than why outcomes missed and what to change
- **Executive no-shows**: Executive sponsors skip QBRs, signaling that the partnership is not a priority
- **No action items**: QBR produces interesting discussion but no documented commitments with owners and deadlines
- **One-sided preparation**: Only one party prepares for the QBR; the other party arrives unprepared

## 5. Partner Manager Operating Rhythm

### The Partner Manager's Week

A partner manager's time allocation should approximately follow:

| Activity | Time Allocation | Purpose |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| Partner communication | 25% | Maintaining relationships, answering questions, providing support |
| Pipeline management | 20% | Reviewing partner pipeline, supporting deals, removing blockers |
| Internal coordination | 20% | Working with sales, marketing, product, and engineering on partner needs |
| Strategic planning | 15% | JBP development, QBR preparation, program analysis |
| Reporting and administration | 10% | CRM updates, performance reporting, program administration |
| Professional development | 10% | Market knowledge, partnership skills, product training |

### Portfolio Sizing

The optimal portfolio size for a partner manager depends on partnership type:
- **Strategic partnerships**: 3-5 per manager (high-touch, complex coordination)
- **Technology partnerships**: 5-10 per manager (moderate touch, technical coordination)
- **Channel partnerships**: 15-25 per manager (structured cadence, scaled processes)
- **Referral partnerships**: 25-50 per manager (light touch, mostly automated)

Overloading partner managers beyond these ratios produces superficial management across the portfolio.

## Failure Modes

1. **Cadence without content**: Holding recurring meetings without prepared agendas, meaningful data, or actionable outcomes — meetings become obligations rather than value-creating activities
2. **Planning without execution**: Creating detailed joint business plans that are never referenced or tracked — the plan becomes a shelf document rather than an operating agreement
3. **QBR as performance review**: Running QBRs that focus exclusively on backward-looking metrics without forward-looking strategy — missing the opportunity to adapt and improve
4. **Communication decay**: Allowing communication frequency and quality to decline over time — the partnership slowly dies of neglect while metrics lag the reality
5. **Partner manager overload**: Assigning too many partnerships to each partner manager — producing surface-level management that catches problems too late to fix them
6. **Operational rigidity**: Applying the same operational cadence to every partnership regardless of type, maturity, and complexity — over-managing simple partnerships while under-managing complex ones

## The Operator's Framework

**Step 1: Match the cadence to the partnership.** Select the appropriate operational cadence from the operating model table based on partnership type and tier.

**Step 2: Establish communication norms.** Define response time expectations, meeting preparation standards, and escalation protocols. Document and share with both organizations.

**Step 3: Co-create the joint business plan.** Facilitate a joint planning session. Produce a concise JBP with shared objectives, measurable targets, and named owners.

**Step 4: Build the QBR rhythm.** Schedule QBRs for the full year. Create QBR preparation checklists. Establish the 5-block QBR structure. Ensure executive sponsors commit to attendance.

**Step 5: Right-size partner manager portfolios.** Assign partnerships to partner managers based on portfolio sizing guidelines. Monitor workload and redistribute as the portfolio evolves.

**Step 6: Monitor operational health.** Track communication frequency, meeting attendance, action item completion, and JBP milestone progress as leading indicators of partnership health.

## Summary

Partner operations is the daily execution discipline that determines whether partnerships deliver on their strategic promise:

1. **Operational cadences create the rhythm of partnership execution** — without structured cadences, partnerships drift between milestones and decay through neglect
2. **Communication must be layered, structured, and balanced** — working-level coordination, management alignment, and executive sponsorship each serve distinct purposes and require distinct cadences
3. **Joint business plans are operating agreements, not planning exercises** — they must be concise, measurable, co-created, and regularly referenced
4. **QBRs are the most important partnership ritual** — they must combine retrospective accountability, current state awareness, forward planning, and strategic alignment in a structured format
5. **Partner manager capacity determines partnership quality** — overloaded managers produce superficial management that catches problems too late
6. **Operational cadence must match partnership complexity** — the operating rhythm for a referral partnership is fundamentally different from that of a strategic alliance
