# Glossary — Partnership Brain

## Core Partnership Terminology

This glossary provides precise definitions for the terminology used throughout the Partnership Brain. Terms are organized by domain to facilitate contextual understanding. Definitions draw from alliance management literature, industry practice (ASAP, PartnerHacker, Crossbeam), and academic research.

---

## Partnership Types and Structures

### Strategic Partnership
A deep, multi-faceted relationship between two organizations that aligns at the executive level around shared strategic objectives. Characterized by joint investment, co-development, and mutual dependency. Distinguished from tactical partnerships by the breadth of engagement, executive sponsorship, and long-term commitment horizon. Per Doz & Hamel (1998), strategic alliances involve "the pooling, trading, or co-development of resources for mutual benefit."

### Technology Partnership
A partnership centered on product or platform integration. Involves connecting systems, sharing APIs, or co-developing joint technical solutions. The integration itself is the primary value driver. Subcategories include API partnerships, embedded partnerships, and co-development partnerships.

### Channel Partnership
A partnership where one party (the channel partner) resells, refers, or distributes the other party's product or service. Includes resellers, value-added resellers (VARs), managed service providers (MSPs), system integrators (SIs), and distributors. Channel economics are governed by margin structures and deal registration.

### Marketing Partnership (Co-Marketing)
A partnership focused on joint demand generation and brand amplification. Partners collaborate on content, events, webinars, or campaigns to reach overlapping or complementary audiences. Value is measured in shared pipeline generation and brand association.

### Referral Partnership
A lightweight partnership where one party refers leads or opportunities to the other in exchange for a referral fee (typically 10-20% of first-year revenue). Lower commitment than channel but lower friction to establish.

### OEM Partnership (Original Equipment Manufacturer)
A partnership where one company's product is embedded within another's product under the latter's brand. The OEM partner white-labels or deeply integrates the technology. Characterized by volume-based pricing, deep technical integration, and brand invisibility.

### White-Label Partnership
Similar to OEM but specifically refers to a product or service that is rebranded entirely by the partner. The end customer typically has no awareness of the original provider. Governed by licensing agreements with brand guidelines.

### Affiliate Partnership
A performance-based partnership where affiliates earn commissions for driving traffic or sales through tracked links. Typically managed at scale through affiliate platforms. Lowest-touch partnership type with the most standardized terms.

## Partnership Economics

### Revenue Share
A compensation model where partners split revenue generated from joint activities. Expressed as a percentage split (e.g., 70/30 or 80/20). The revenue share model must specify: gross vs. net revenue base, payment timing, attribution methodology, and duration of revenue share (one-time vs. recurring).

### Referral Fee
A one-time payment made when a referred lead converts to a paying customer. Typically calculated as a percentage of first-year contract value or Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR). Distinguished from revenue share by its one-time nature.

### Margin Stack
The cumulative set of margins across a channel partnership. If a product is priced at $100, and the reseller gets 20% margin, and the distributor gets 10%, the margin stack is 30%, meaning the vendor nets $70. Understanding margin stacks is critical for channel program economics.

### Deal Registration
A process where channel partners register specific opportunities to receive pricing protection, higher margins, or exclusive rights to pursue the deal. Prevents channel conflict by establishing which partner "owns" an opportunity.

### Minimum Commitment
A contractual obligation for a partner to deliver a minimum level of activity or revenue within a defined period. Used to ensure partner engagement and justify the investment in partnership enablement. Also called "minimum annual commitment" (MAC).

### Partner-Sourced Revenue
Revenue from deals where the partner originated the opportunity. The partner identified the prospect, made the initial introduction, and the deal would not have existed without the partner. This is the strongest form of partnership attribution.

### Partner-Influenced Revenue
Revenue from deals where the partner did not originate the opportunity but materially accelerated or enabled its closure. The partner may have provided a reference, participated in a demo, or validated the solution. Weaker attribution than partner-sourced but still meaningful.

### Total Partnership Value (TPV)
The comprehensive value generated by a partnership including direct revenue (sourced + influenced), cost savings (reduced CAC, shared engineering), strategic value (market access, credibility), and option value (future expansion potential). TPV attempts to capture the full economic impact beyond direct revenue.

## Alliance Management

### Alliance Manager
The dedicated professional responsible for managing one or more partner relationships. Functions as the "account manager" for the partnership, owning relationship health, joint planning, QBRs, and escalation management. Kale & Singh (2009) identify the alliance manager role as a critical component of alliance management capability.

### Quarterly Business Review (QBR)
A structured meeting between partner organizations held quarterly to review performance against joint objectives, discuss pipeline, resolve issues, and plan upcoming initiatives. The QBR is the primary governance mechanism for active partnerships.

### Joint Business Plan (JBP)
A collaborative planning document created by both partners that defines shared objectives, mutual commitments, resource allocation, timelines, and success metrics for a defined period (typically annual). The JBP serves as the operating agreement for day-to-day partnership execution.

### Partner Health Score
A composite metric that assesses the overall vitality of a partnership across dimensions including engagement (activity levels), performance (revenue and pipeline), satisfaction (NPS/CSAT), and strategic alignment. Analogous to customer health scores in customer success.

### Executive Sponsor
A senior leader within each partner organization who serves as the strategic champion for the partnership. Executive sponsors provide air cover, remove blockers, and signal organizational commitment. Research consistently shows that executive sponsorship is the strongest predictor of partnership success.

### Escalation Protocol
A defined process for elevating partnership issues from working-level teams to senior leadership when resolution cannot be achieved through normal channels. Effective escalation protocols specify thresholds, timelines, and decision rights at each level.

## Ecosystem and Platform Terms

### Ecosystem
A network of interdependent organizations (partners, competitors, complementors, customers, and suppliers) that collectively create and deliver value. Per Adner (2017), an ecosystem is "the alignment structure of the multilateral set of partners that need to interact for a focal value proposition to materialize."

### Platform
A technology foundation upon which other products, services, and applications are built. Platforms create value by enabling third-party innovation and reducing transaction costs. Partnerships with platforms differ fundamentally from bilateral partnerships because the platform mediates the relationship.

### Complementor
An organization whose product or service increases the value of another organization's offering. The classic example is software applications (complementors) on an operating system (platform). Identifying and cultivating complementors is a core ecosystem strategy task.

### Network Effects
The phenomenon where the value of a product or service increases as more users or partners adopt it. In partnership contexts, network effects manifest when a larger partner ecosystem makes the platform more attractive to both customers and additional partners, creating a virtuous cycle.

### Marketplace
A platform that connects buyers and sellers (or users and solution providers). In the partnership context, typically refers to cloud marketplaces (AWS Marketplace, Azure Marketplace, Salesforce AppExchange) where partners list and sell their solutions.

### ISV (Independent Software Vendor)
A company that develops and sells software products. In partnership contexts, ISVs are often the partners being recruited into platform ecosystems or channel programs.

### SI (System Integrator)
A company that specializes in implementing, customizing, and integrating technology solutions for end customers. SIs are critical channel partners because they influence technology selection decisions and provide implementation capacity.

### VAR (Value-Added Reseller)
A company that resells technology products with additional services, customization, or bundling that adds value beyond the original product. VARs combine products from multiple vendors into integrated solutions.

### MSP (Managed Service Provider)
A company that provides ongoing management and support for technology infrastructure or applications. MSPs are high-value channel partners because they have deep, recurring relationships with their clients.

## Negotiation and Legal Terms

### Term Sheet
A non-binding document that outlines the key commercial terms of a proposed partnership. Serves as the basis for negotiation before formal agreement drafting. Includes scope, economics, exclusivity, duration, and termination provisions.

### Exclusivity
A contractual provision granting one partner exclusive rights within a defined scope (geography, vertical, customer segment, or technology domain). Exclusivity increases partner commitment but reduces flexibility. Always requires sunset clauses and performance thresholds.

### Most Favored Nation (MFN)
A contractual provision guaranteeing that one partner receives terms at least as favorable as those offered to any other partner. Common in channel agreements to prevent price undercutting and ensure competitive positioning.

### Sunset Clause
A provision that causes a contractual term (often exclusivity or minimum commitments) to expire automatically after a defined period or upon failure to meet specified conditions. Essential for managing partnership risk and maintaining flexibility.

### Non-Compete / Non-Solicitation
Provisions restricting partners from competing directly or recruiting each other's employees during and after the partnership. Must be carefully scoped to be enforceable and avoid antitrust concerns.

### Termination for Convenience
A contractual right allowing either party to end the partnership without cause, typically with a specified notice period (30-90 days). Provides an exit mechanism when strategic alignment deteriorates. Distinguished from termination for cause, which requires a material breach.

### Intellectual Property (IP) Ownership
Provisions defining who owns what was created during the partnership. Pre-existing IP remains with the originating party. Jointly developed IP must have clear ownership and licensing provisions. This is frequently the most contentious area of partnership negotiation.

## Metrics and Measurement

### Partner Attach Rate
The percentage of deals that include a partner (either sourced or influenced). High attach rates indicate strong ecosystem integration. Calculated as: (Deals with partner involvement / Total deals) x 100.

### Partner Activation Rate
The percentage of recruited partners who become actively engaged (as defined by specific activity thresholds). Low activation rates signal onboarding or program design issues. Calculated as: (Active partners / Total recruited partners) x 100.

### Time-to-First-Deal (TTFD)
The elapsed time from partner agreement signing to the partner's first closed deal. TTFD is a leading indicator of program effectiveness and partner quality. Industry benchmarks vary by partnership type: referral (30-60 days), reseller (60-120 days), technology (90-180 days).

### Cost of Partner Acquisition (COPA)
The total investment required to recruit, onboard, and activate a new partner. Includes BD team costs, marketing, training, and enablement. Analogous to Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC). Calculated as: Total partner recruitment investment / Number of activated partners.

### Ecosystem Qualified Lead (EQL)
A lead generated through the partner ecosystem that meets qualification criteria. Distinct from Marketing Qualified Leads (MQLs) and Sales Qualified Leads (SQLs) because the lead arrives with partner context and often higher intent.

### Influence Multiplier
The ratio of total revenue influenced by partnerships to the direct investment in the partnership function. Represents the leverage effect of partnerships. Calculated as: Total partner-influenced revenue / Partnership program investment.
