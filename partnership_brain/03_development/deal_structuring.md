# Deal Structuring — Agreements, Revenue Sharing, IP Frameworks, Governance, and Term Sheets

## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**
- How to structure partnership agreements that align incentives, protect interests, and enable execution
- What revenue sharing models to use based on partnership type, value creation mechanism, and power dynamics
- How to allocate intellectual property rights for co-created assets without destroying the incentive to innovate
- What governance structures ensure effective joint decision-making without bureaucratic paralysis

**Mistakes it prevents:**
- Signing agreements with ambiguous terms that create disputes when real money is at stake
- Implementing revenue share models that incentivize one party to under-invest or over-extract
- Leaving IP ownership undefined until a co-created asset becomes valuable enough to fight over
- Establishing governance structures that are either too heavy (stifling execution) or too light (allowing drift)

**Outputs it enables:**
- Partnership term sheets that define commercial, operational, and legal terms before full agreement drafting
- Revenue sharing models with scenario analysis across bear/base/bull projections
- IP allocation frameworks that preserve innovation incentives while protecting both parties
- Governance charters that define decision rights, escalation paths, and review cadences

---

## The Core Insight

Deal structuring is **incentive architecture applied to inter-organizational relationships.** The structure of a partnership agreement does not merely record what the parties have agreed — it **shapes the behaviors the partnership will produce.** Revenue sharing models that reward volume over quality produce volume at the expense of quality. Governance structures that require consensus on every decision produce paralysis. IP frameworks that assign all rights to one party eliminate the other's incentive to innovate. The art of deal structuring lies in designing terms that make it economically rational for both parties to do what the partnership needs them to do — and irrational for either party to defect, shirk, or exploit. Williamson's (1985) transaction cost economics provides the theoretical foundation: the deal structure is a governance mechanism that must manage the inherent risks of bounded rationality and opportunism in inter-firm relationships.

## 1. Agreement Architecture

### The Layered Agreement Model

Partnership agreements are most effective when structured in layers, each serving a distinct purpose:

**Layer 1: Master Partnership Agreement (MPA)**
The foundational document that governs the overall relationship:
- Parties and recitals (who is entering the agreement and why)
- Term and renewal (duration, auto-renewal conditions, termination rights)
- Confidentiality obligations (scope, duration, permitted disclosures)
- Data protection and processing (GDPR, CCPA, data sharing protocols)
- Liability and indemnification (risk allocation between parties)
- Dispute resolution (mediation, arbitration, jurisdiction)
- General provisions (assignment, force majeure, notice requirements)

**Layer 2: Commercial Schedule**
The specific commercial terms that govern value exchange:
- Revenue sharing model and calculation methodology
- Payment terms and reconciliation process
- Pricing commitments and discount authorities
- Minimum commitments (if applicable)
- Commission structures and bonus triggers

**Layer 3: Operational Schedule**
The working-level terms that govern day-to-day execution:
- Integration specifications and SLAs
- Support responsibilities and escalation paths
- Co-marketing rights and brand usage guidelines
- Training and enablement obligations
- Reporting requirements and data sharing

**Layer 4: Governance Charter**
The decision-making framework for the partnership:
- Joint steering committee composition and cadence
- Decision rights matrix (who decides what)
- Escalation procedures and timelines
- Review cadences (QBRs, annual strategic reviews)
- Change management process for agreement modifications

### Agreement Negotiation Principles

**Principle 1: Start from mutual value, not from positions**
Frame every term as a mechanism to enable mutual value creation. When parties anchor on positions (e.g., "we need 70% revenue share"), negotiations become adversarial. When parties focus on interests (e.g., "we need enough revenue to justify the sales investment"), creative solutions emerge.

**Principle 2: Make the agreement operable**
Every clause must have a clear operational implication. If a term cannot be operationalized — measured, enforced, and managed — it creates ambiguity that will surface as conflict.

**Principle 3: Design for evolution**
Markets change, strategies evolve, and partnerships must adapt. Build in mechanisms for periodic review and amendment rather than attempting to anticipate every future contingency.

**Principle 4: Address failure upfront**
The easiest time to agree on how to handle underperformance, disputes, and termination is before they occur. Addressing these topics at signing prevents them from becoming existential crises later.

## 2. Revenue Sharing Models

### Model Taxonomy

**Model 1: Referral Fee**
- Structure: Fixed fee or percentage of first-year revenue for each referred customer
- Best for: Low-integration partnerships, referral relationships
- Typical range: 10-25% of first-year contract value
- Pros: Simple, low operational overhead
- Cons: No ongoing alignment after referral, no incentive for partner to ensure customer success

**Model 2: Revenue Share**
- Structure: Ongoing percentage of revenue generated through the partnership
- Best for: Co-selling partnerships, technology integrations with revenue attribution
- Typical range: 15-30% of ongoing revenue for resale; 5-15% for influence
- Pros: Long-term alignment, partner incentivized for customer success
- Cons: Attribution complexity, revenue recognition implications

**Model 3: Margin-Based Share**
- Structure: Partner receives a percentage of gross margin rather than revenue
- Best for: Partnerships where cost of delivery varies significantly
- Typical range: 20-40% of gross margin
- Pros: Aligns incentives with profitability, not just volume
- Cons: Requires margin transparency, more complex calculation

**Model 4: Tiered Revenue Share**
- Structure: Revenue share percentage increases as the partner hits volume thresholds
- Best for: Channel partnerships where you want to incentivize growth
- Typical range: 15% base scaling to 30% at top tier
- Pros: Rewards partner investment and growth
- Cons: Administrative complexity, potential for gaming threshold boundaries

**Model 5: Joint Revenue Pool**
- Structure: Revenue from co-created offering is pooled and split based on agreed allocation
- Best for: Co-developed products, deeply integrated offerings
- Typical range: Negotiated based on relative contribution (40/60 to 50/50)
- Pros: Maximum alignment on joint success
- Cons: Requires trust, transparent accounting, and agreed attribution methodology

### Revenue Share Scenario Modeling

Every revenue share agreement MUST be modeled across three scenarios:

| Scenario | Assumptions | Partner Revenue | Your Revenue | Net Economics |
|----------|-------------|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| Bear | 50% of projected pipeline converts, 20% lower ACV | Calculate | Calculate | Must be break-even or better |
| Base | Projected pipeline at expected conversion rates | Calculate | Calculate | Must meet minimum ROI threshold |
| Bull | 150% of projected pipeline, 10% higher ACV | Calculate | Calculate | Demonstrates upside alignment |

The critical test: **Is the partnership economically viable in the bear case?** If not, the deal structure needs revision.

### Attribution Methodology

Attribution is the most contentious operational element of revenue sharing. Define methodology before launch:

- **Partner-sourced**: The partner originated the lead. The customer had no prior relationship with you. Clear attribution.
- **Partner-influenced**: The customer was already in your pipeline, but the partner materially influenced the deal (joint demo, technical validation, executive introduction). Requires influence criteria.
- **Partner-attached**: The customer uses both products but neither party influenced the other's sale. Weakest attribution claim.

Best practice: Establish a joint attribution committee that reviews disputed deals monthly, with a pre-agreed escalation path for unresolved cases.

## 3. Intellectual Property Frameworks

### IP Allocation Principles

**Principle 1: Background IP Stays**
Each party retains full ownership and control of all IP that existed before the partnership (background IP). The partnership agreement should include a clear definition and inventory of background IP.

**Principle 2: Foreground IP Requires Explicit Allocation**
IP created during and because of the partnership (foreground IP) must be explicitly allocated. Do not rely on default legal rules, which vary by jurisdiction and rarely reflect commercial intent.

**Principle 3: Usage Rights Can Be Separated from Ownership**
IP ownership and IP usage rights are distinct. One party can own the IP while granting the other a license to use it. This allows for clean ownership while ensuring both parties can benefit.

### IP Allocation Models

| Model | Ownership | Usage Rights | Best For |
|-------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Creator owns** | The party that creates the IP owns it | Other party gets license | Clearly separable work |
| **Contribution-based** | Ownership proportional to contribution | Cross-licenses for both parties | Roughly equal contribution |
| **Joint ownership** | Both parties co-own | Both parties can use freely | Deeply intertwined co-creation |
| **Field-of-use split** | Each party owns within their field | Exclusive in own field, none in other | Non-competing partners |
| **Assignee with license-back** | One party owns, other gets perpetual license | Licensee can use but not sublicense | Unequal contribution |

### IP Risk Mitigation

- **Contamination protection**: Ensure that partnership work does not inadvertently create claims on either party's background IP
- **Open source compliance**: If the partnership involves open source components, ensure license compliance obligations are clearly allocated
- **Third-party IP clearance**: Verify that neither party is infringing third-party IP through the partnership activities
- **Post-termination rights**: Define exactly what happens to foreground IP if the partnership terminates — this is the most frequently litigated IP issue in partnerships
- **Improvement rights**: If one party improves the other's technology during the partnership, define who owns the improvement

## 4. Governance Design

### The Governance Spectrum

| Partnership Type | Governance Weight | Decision Cadence | Typical Structure |
|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|-------------------|
| Referral | Minimal | Quarterly review | Single point of contact each side |
| Co-marketing | Light | Monthly sync | Joint marketing committee |
| Technology integration | Medium | Biweekly syncs | Joint product committee + technical leads |
| Channel | Medium-Heavy | Weekly operations + monthly strategy | Joint steering committee + field coordination |
| Strategic alliance | Heavy | Multiple cadences | Executive sponsor + steering committee + working groups |

### The Decision Rights Matrix (RACI for Partnerships)

| Decision Category | Party A Role | Party B Role | Mechanism |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| Product roadmap affecting integration | Inform | Consult | Quarterly roadmap review |
| Pricing of joint offering | Joint decide | Joint decide | Pricing committee |
| Marketing content and messaging | Approve (own brand) | Approve (own brand) | Joint marketing committee |
| Customer escalation | Responsible (own customer) | Support | Shared escalation protocol |
| Partnership scope expansion | Joint propose | Joint decide | Steering committee |
| Resource allocation changes | Decide (own resources) | Inform | Quarterly business review |

### Governance Best Practices

**Executive sponsorship is non-negotiable.** Every strategic partnership must have a named executive sponsor from each organization. The sponsors meet quarterly (minimum) and are the ultimate escalation point.

**Working-level autonomy within boundaries.** Day-to-day partnership operations should be managed by working-level teams with authority to make operational decisions. Strategic decisions escalate to the steering committee.

**Documented decision log.** Every joint decision should be documented, including the rationale. This prevents revisionism and provides institutional memory.

**Annual governance review.** The governance structure itself should be reviewed annually. Is it too heavy? Too light? Are the right people in the right roles?

## 5. Term Sheet Design

### The Partnership Term Sheet

A term sheet captures the essential commercial and operational terms before investing in full agreement drafting. It is non-binding (except for confidentiality and exclusivity provisions) and serves as the blueprint for the legal agreement.

### Term Sheet Components

**Section 1: Partnership Overview**
- Partnership type and scope
- Strategic rationale (2-3 sentences on why this partnership exists)
- Key objectives and success metrics

**Section 2: Commercial Terms**
- Revenue sharing model and rates
- Payment terms and reconciliation frequency
- Minimum commitments (if any)
- Exclusivity provisions (if any), with geographic and temporal scope
- Pricing commitments for joint offerings

**Section 3: Operational Terms**
- Integration scope and timeline
- SLA commitments from each party
- Support responsibilities and escalation paths
- Training and enablement obligations
- Reporting requirements

**Section 4: IP and Data**
- Background IP inventory (or reference to exhibits)
- Foreground IP allocation model
- Data sharing scope and restrictions
- Data processing agreements
- Brand usage guidelines

**Section 5: Governance**
- Executive sponsors (named individuals)
- Steering committee composition and cadence
- Decision rights for key decision categories
- Escalation procedures

**Section 6: Term and Termination**
- Initial term and renewal mechanism
- Termination triggers (for cause and for convenience)
- Notice periods
- Wind-down obligations and transition assistance
- Post-termination rights and restrictions

**Section 7: Conditions and Next Steps**
- Due diligence items still outstanding
- Internal approvals required from each party
- Timeline to definitive agreement
- Exclusivity during negotiation (if applicable)

## Failure Modes

1. **Ambiguous attribution**: Launching a revenue-sharing partnership without agreed attribution methodology — guaranteeing disputes when the first significant deal occurs and both parties claim credit
2. **Handshake IP**: Failing to define IP ownership for co-created assets — creating a legal and commercial time bomb that detonates when the co-created asset becomes valuable
3. **Over-governed partnerships**: Implementing heavy governance structures for lightweight partnerships — drowning simple referral relationships in committee meetings and decision matrices
4. **Revenue share misalignment**: Designing revenue share models that incentivize the wrong behavior — rewarding volume over quality, or short-term wins over long-term customer success
5. **Missing exit architecture**: Neglecting to define termination triggers and wind-down procedures — creating partnerships that cannot be cleanly exited when they underperform
6. **Term sheet drift**: Allowing the definitive agreement to diverge significantly from the term sheet — eroding trust built during negotiations and introducing terms that were not agreed

## The Operator's Framework

**Step 1: Select the agreement architecture.** Based on partnership type and complexity, determine which agreement layers are needed. Simple partnerships may need only the MPA and a commercial schedule. Complex partnerships need all four layers.

**Step 2: Model the revenue share.** Select the appropriate revenue sharing model. Run the three-scenario analysis. Ensure the partnership is economically viable in the bear case. Define attribution methodology explicitly.

**Step 3: Define the IP framework.** Inventory background IP. Select the foreground IP allocation model. Define post-termination rights. Ensure IP protection mechanisms are proportional to the IP value at stake.

**Step 4: Design governance.** Select the appropriate governance weight. Create the decision rights matrix. Define escalation procedures. Name executive sponsors.

**Step 5: Draft the term sheet.** Capture all essential terms in a structured term sheet. Review with both parties' leadership before investing in full legal drafting.

**Step 6: Negotiate and finalize.** Use the term sheet as the negotiation framework. Ensure the definitive agreement faithfully reflects the term sheet. Flag any material divergences for executive review.

## Summary

Deal structuring transforms partnership intent into operational reality through deliberate incentive architecture:

1. **Layered agreements separate concerns** — master terms, commercial specifics, operational details, and governance each serve distinct purposes and should be structured independently
2. **Revenue sharing models must be scenario-tested** — every model must be viable in the bear case, not just attractive in the bull case
3. **IP ownership must be explicit before creation begins** — ambiguity about IP ownership is a partnership-destroying timebomb that becomes more expensive to resolve the longer it persists
4. **Governance must match partnership weight** — over-governance kills lightweight partnerships with bureaucracy; under-governance allows strategic partnerships to drift without accountability
5. **Term sheets prevent negotiation drift** — capturing essential terms in a non-binding term sheet before legal drafting ensures alignment and prevents late-stage surprises
6. **Deal structure shapes behavior** — the terms of the agreement do not just record what the parties agreed; they create the incentive structures that determine how the partnership will actually operate
