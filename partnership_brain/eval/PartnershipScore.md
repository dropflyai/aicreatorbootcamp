# Partnership Score -- Quality Enforcement (Authoritative)

This document defines how partnership program quality is evaluated.
Every partnership initiative must be scored before it is considered successful.

If quality is not measurable, it is not enforced.
If partnerships do not drive mutual value, they are not partnerships -- they are liabilities.

---

## SCORING RULES (MANDATORY)

Each partnership initiative must be scored across the following 8 dimensions.
Score each category from **1 (poor)** to **5 (excellent)**.

### Hard Fail Dimensions

These dimensions are critical. Score <3 = immediate intervention required:
- **Revenue Impact**
- **Strategic Alignment**
- **Partner Activation**

### Passing Criteria

- Average score across all dimensions >= 4.0
- No hard-fail dimension < 3
- Relationship Depth is NOT a hard fail but score <3 triggers relationship review
- All dimensions must be explicitly scored -- no skipping

### Partnership Type Context Rule

Scores must be evaluated relative to partnership type. A technology integration partner,
a channel reseller, and a strategic co-marketing partner have different expectations.
The evaluator MUST state the partnership type, stage, and strategic goal before scoring.

### Partnership Types

| Type | Description | Primary Value |
|------|-------------|---------------|
| Technology | API integration, product bundling | Product enhancement, joint solution |
| Channel | Resellers, distributors, referral partners | Revenue, market access |
| Strategic | Co-development, co-marketing, joint ventures | Market position, innovation |
| Ecosystem | Platform partners, ISVs, developers | Ecosystem growth, stickiness |
| Affiliate | Commission-based referral | Lead generation, awareness |

## 1. PARTNER PIPELINE

**Question:**
Is there a healthy, growing pipeline of qualified partner prospects?

### What to Evaluate

- **Pipeline Volume:** Number of qualified partners in active pipeline
- **Pipeline Quality:** Partners match the ideal partner profile (IPP)
- **Conversion Rate:** % of prospects that become active partners
- **Pipeline Velocity:** Average time from first contact to signed agreement
- **Source Diversity:** Partners sourced from multiple channels (inbound, outbound, events, referrals)
- **Pipeline Coverage:** Pipeline value is 3x or more of the partner acquisition target

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- Pipeline has 3x+ coverage of target; >80% of prospects match IPP; conversion rate >30%; velocity meets or beats benchmark; diverse sourcing channels; pipeline growing quarter over quarter
- **4** -- Pipeline has 2-3x coverage; 60-80% match IPP; conversion rate 20-30%; velocity is acceptable; sourcing from 3+ channels; pipeline stable or growing
- **3** -- Pipeline has 1-2x coverage; 40-60% match IPP; conversion rate 10-20%; velocity is slow; sourcing from 1-2 channels; pipeline flat
- **2** -- Pipeline has <1x coverage; <40% match IPP; conversion rate <10%; velocity is unpredictable; single sourcing channel; pipeline declining
- **1** -- No structured pipeline; prospects are ad hoc; no qualification criteria; no conversion tracking; no systematic sourcing

### Ideal Partner Profile (IPP) Criteria

The IPP should define:
- Market presence and reach (geography, vertical, customer size)
- Technical capability (integration readiness, developer resources)
- Business model alignment (how they make money with us)
- Cultural and values alignment
- Growth trajectory and stability
- Willingness to invest in the partnership

### Red Flags

- Pipeline consists of inbound requests only (no proactive sourcing)
- No disqualification criteria (everyone is a "partner")
- Pipeline metrics not tracked or reported
- Same prospects in pipeline for >6 months without progress
- Partners do not match the ICP (ideal customer profile) of the product

Score <3 --> redesign partner sourcing and qualification process.

## 2. REVENUE IMPACT

**Question:**
Are partnerships driving measurable revenue growth?

### What to Evaluate

- **Partner-Sourced Revenue:** Revenue from deals originated by partners
- **Partner-Influenced Revenue:** Revenue from deals where partners played a role but did not originate
- **Revenue Growth Rate:** Quarter-over-quarter growth in partner revenue
- **Revenue per Partner:** Average revenue contribution per active partner
- **Revenue Predictability:** Partner revenue forecast accuracy
- **Payback Period:** Time to recover partner acquisition and enablement costs

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- Partner-sourced revenue exceeds targets; partner-influenced revenue tracked and growing; QoQ growth >20%; top partners are material revenue contributors; forecast accuracy >85%; payback period <6 months
- **4** -- Partner-sourced revenue meets targets; partner-influenced revenue tracked; QoQ growth 10-20%; healthy revenue per partner; forecast accuracy 70-85%; payback period 6-12 months
- **3** -- Partner-sourced revenue below target; partner-influenced revenue not tracked; QoQ growth 0-10%; uneven revenue distribution (few partners carry most); forecast accuracy 50-70%; payback period 12-18 months
- **2** -- Partner-sourced revenue significantly below target; no attribution model; flat or declining revenue; most partners generate no revenue; forecast unreliable; payback period >18 months
- **1** -- No measurable partner revenue; no attribution; no tracking; partners are a cost center with no revenue return

### Revenue Attribution Model

Partnerships MUST have a clear attribution model:

| Attribution Type | Definition | Weight |
|------------------|------------|--------|
| Partner-Sourced | Partner originated the opportunity | 100% partner credit |
| Partner-Influenced | Partner involved in existing opportunity | 25-50% partner credit |
| Partner-Delivered | Partner manages implementation/success | Based on contract |

### Red Flags

- No partner revenue attribution model exists
- Partner revenue is not tracked separately from direct sales
- A single partner accounts for >30% of all partner revenue (concentration risk)
- Revenue per partner is declining while partner count is growing
- Partner acquisition cost exceeds 2 years of partner revenue contribution
- Revenue targets for partnerships do not exist

Score <3 --> HARD FAIL. Restructure partnership revenue strategy immediately.

## 3. PARTNER ACTIVATION

**Question:**
What percentage of signed partners are actively selling, integrating, or co-marketing?

### What to Evaluate

- **Activation Rate:** % of signed partners that complete onboarding and make first deal/integration
- **Time-to-First-Deal:** Average time from signing to first revenue-generating activity
- **Active Partner Ratio:** % of all partners that generated activity in the last 90 days
- **Enablement Completion:** % of partners that complete training and certification
- **Resource Utilization:** Partners use provided sales tools, marketing materials, and technical resources
- **Self-Service Capability:** Partners can operate independently after onboarding

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- Activation rate >80%; time-to-first-deal <60 days; >70% of partners active in last 90 days; enablement completion >90%; high resource utilization; partners are self-sufficient after onboarding
- **4** -- Activation rate 60-80%; time-to-first-deal 60-90 days; 50-70% active; enablement completion 70-90%; moderate resource utilization; most partners self-sufficient
- **3** -- Activation rate 40-60%; time-to-first-deal 90-120 days; 30-50% active; enablement completion 50-70%; low resource utilization; partners require ongoing hand-holding
- **2** -- Activation rate 20-40%; time-to-first-deal >120 days; <30% active; enablement completion <50%; resources unused; partners are passive
- **1** -- Activation rate <20%; most signed partners never activate; no enablement program; partners signed but dormant; no activation tracking

### Activation Funnel

```
Signed Partners
  |
  v
Onboarded (completed training)
  |
  v
Enabled (has tools, access, materials)
  |
  v
Active (made first deal/integration)
  |
  v
Productive (generating regular value)
  |
  v
Expanded (growing the partnership)
```

Each stage must be measured and optimized.

### Red Flags

- More than 50% of signed partners have never completed onboarding
- Partners complain they do not know how to sell or integrate the product
- Enablement materials are outdated or generic
- No partner onboarding process exists (partners figure it out themselves)
- Time-to-first-deal is increasing over time
- "Paper partnerships" where agreements are signed but no activity follows

Score <3 --> HARD FAIL. Redesign partner onboarding and enablement before signing more partners.

## 4. INTEGRATION QUALITY

**Question:**
For technology partnerships, are integrations reliable, well-supported, and delivering user value?

### What to Evaluate

- **API Integration Uptime:** >99.9% uptime for partner-facing APIs
- **Support Ticket Volume:** Low volume of partner-reported integration issues
- **User Satisfaction:** End users of the integration rate it positively
- **Documentation Quality:** Integration docs are complete, accurate, and current
- **Versioning and Compatibility:** Breaking changes communicated with ample lead time
- **Developer Experience:** Partners can integrate independently using documentation and sandbox

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- API uptime >99.99%; near-zero support tickets; user satisfaction >90%; documentation is comprehensive and current; versioning is semantic with 6+ month deprecation; partners integrate in <1 week using self-service resources
- **4** -- API uptime 99.9-99.99%; low support ticket volume; user satisfaction 80-90%; documentation covers all use cases; versioning with 3-6 month deprecation; partners integrate in 1-4 weeks
- **3** -- API uptime 99-99.9%; moderate support ticket volume; user satisfaction 60-80%; documentation has gaps; versioning inconsistent; partners require 1-2 months and support to integrate
- **2** -- API uptime <99%; high support ticket volume; user satisfaction <60%; documentation is outdated; breaking changes with <1 month notice; partners require dedicated engineering support
- **1** -- Frequent API outages; integration breaks regularly; no user satisfaction measurement; no documentation; no versioning strategy; partners cannot integrate without embedded support

```markdown

| Metric | Target | Last 30 Days | Trend |
|--------|--------|--------------|-------|
| API Uptime | >99.9% | | |
| Avg Response Time | <200ms | | |
| Error Rate | <0.1% | | |
| Support Tickets | <5/month | | |

```

### Red Flags

- Partner integrations break after internal releases
- No staging or sandbox environment for partner testing
- Documentation written by engineers, never reviewed by partners
- No automated monitoring of partner-facing API health
- Partners learn about breaking changes after they break
- Integration quality is not measured or tracked

Score <3 --> fix integration infrastructure before onboarding new technology partners.

## 5. PROGRAM HEALTH

**Question:**
Is the partner program well-structured, well-received, and generating positive partner sentiment?

### What to Evaluate

- **Partner NPS:** Net Promoter Score from partner satisfaction surveys
- **Partner Engagement Score:** Composite score of partner activity, communication, and feedback
- **Certification Rate:** % of partners completing certification programs
- **Tier Distribution:** Healthy distribution across partnership tiers
- **Program Renewal Rate:** % of partners renewing or upgrading their partnership
- **Support Satisfaction:** Partners rate the partner team's responsiveness and helpfulness

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- Partner NPS >60; engagement score high across >70% of partners; certification rate >80%; balanced tier distribution; renewal rate >90%; support satisfaction >90%
- **4** -- Partner NPS 40-60; engagement score moderate to high; certification rate 60-80%; mostly balanced tiers; renewal rate 75-90%; support satisfaction 75-90%
- **3** -- Partner NPS 20-40; engagement score mixed; certification rate 40-60%; tier imbalance; renewal rate 60-75%; support satisfaction 60-75%
- **2** -- Partner NPS 0-20; low engagement across most partners; certification rate <40%; heavily imbalanced tiers; renewal rate <60%; support satisfaction <60%
- **1** -- Partner NPS negative; disengaged partners; no certification; no tier structure; high churn; poor support perception

### Partner Program Tier Structure Example

| Tier | Requirements | Benefits | Expected % of Partners |
|------|-------------|----------|------------------------|
| Registered | Signed agreement | Basic access, directory listing | 50-60% |
| Silver | Certification + first deal | Enhanced support, co-marketing | 25-35% |
| Gold | Revenue target + multiple deals | Dedicated manager, MDF, priority | 10-15% |
| Platinum | Strategic impact + exec alignment | Joint roadmap, co-development, advisory board | 2-5% |

### Red Flags

- No partner satisfaction measurement (NPS or equivalent)
- Partners cannot articulate the benefits of the partnership
- High partner churn with no exit interviews or analysis
- Partner team is purely reactive (no proactive engagement)
- No partner advisory board or feedback mechanism
- Tier benefits do not meaningfully differentiate from each other

Score <3 --> redesign partner program structure and invest in partner satisfaction.

## 6. STRATEGIC ALIGNMENT

**Question:**
Do partnerships align with the company's strategic goals and fill meaningful gaps?

### What to Evaluate

- **ICP Match:** Partners' customers match the company's ideal customer profile
- **Product Gap Coverage:** Partners fill product gaps or extend capabilities
- **Market Reach Extension:** Partners provide access to new markets, verticals, or geographies
- **Competitive Positioning:** Partnerships strengthen competitive position
- **Strategic Roadmap Alignment:** Partner capabilities align with the product roadmap
- **Mutual Value Creation:** Both parties derive strategic value (not one-sided)

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- All active partnerships directly support strategic priorities; partners fill critical product gaps; partnerships open significant new markets; competitive position is materially strengthened; partner roadmaps align with ours; clear mutual value in every partnership
- **4** -- Most partnerships support strategic priorities; partners address important product gaps; some market expansion through partnerships; competitive position improved; reasonable roadmap alignment; mutual value in most partnerships
- **3** -- Strategic alignment is mixed; some partners do not fit strategic priorities; limited product gap coverage; minimal market expansion; competitive impact unclear; roadmap alignment is coincidental; value exchange is uneven
- **2** -- Partnerships are opportunistic, not strategic; partners do not match ICP; no product gap strategy; partnerships do not extend reach; competitive position unchanged; no roadmap coordination; one-sided value
- **1** -- No strategic framework for partnerships; partnerships are random; no ICP alignment; no gap analysis; partnerships may weaken competitive position; no mutual value


### Red Flags

- Partnerships signed because a partner approached, not because they fit strategy
- No strategic partnership criteria exist
- Partnerships that duplicate internal capabilities without adding value
- Partner portfolio is concentrated in one vertical or geography
- Partnerships create dependency without strategic return
- Competitor partnerships are ignored in strategic planning

Score <3 --> HARD FAIL. Audit partner portfolio for strategic fit and prune misaligned partnerships.

## 7. CO-MARKETING ROI

**Question:**
Are joint marketing efforts with partners generating quality leads and measurable return?

### What to Evaluate

- **Joint Campaign Performance:** Leads, pipeline, and revenue from co-marketing campaigns
- **Lead Quality:** Partner-sourced leads convert at comparable rates to other sources
- **Attribution Clarity:** Clear attribution model for co-marketing leads
- **Content Collaboration:** Joint content (webinars, whitepapers, case studies) drives engagement
- **Brand Enhancement:** Co-marketing strengthens both brands
- **Cost Sharing:** Marketing costs are fairly shared between partners

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- Co-marketing campaigns consistently exceed ROI targets; lead quality matches or exceeds direct marketing; clear attribution; rich content collaboration with measurable engagement; both brands enhanced; fair cost sharing
- **4** -- Co-marketing campaigns meet ROI targets; lead quality is good; attribution mostly clear; regular content collaboration; positive brand impact; reasonable cost sharing
- **3** -- Co-marketing campaigns have mixed results; lead quality is inconsistent; attribution is unclear; occasional content collaboration; brand impact neutral; cost sharing uneven
- **2** -- Co-marketing campaigns underperform; lead quality is poor; no attribution; minimal collaboration; no brand impact measurement; one party bears most costs
- **1** -- No co-marketing activity; no joint campaigns; no content collaboration; no brand leverage; no cost sharing framework

### Red Flags

- No feedback loop from campaign results to future planning

Score <3 --> redesign co-marketing strategy or pause co-marketing until ROI framework is established.

## 8. RELATIONSHIP DEPTH

**Question:**
Are partner relationships deep, multi-threaded, and strategically resilient?

### What to Evaluate

- **Executive Relationships:** C-level or VP-level sponsorship on both sides
- **Multi-Threaded:** Relationships exist across multiple roles and departments
- **Strategic vs Transactional:** Partnership discussions include strategy, not just deal mechanics
- **Communication Cadence:** Regular, structured communication at multiple levels
- **Conflict Resolution:** Issues are resolved constructively and quickly
- **Long-Term Planning:** Joint planning extends beyond the current quarter

### Scoring Guide

- **5** -- Executive sponsors actively engaged on both sides; relationships span sales, product, engineering, marketing; strategic QBRs with joint planning; weekly operational cadence; conflicts resolved quickly and strengthen the relationship; joint roadmap extends 12+ months
- **4** -- Executive sponsors identified and engaged periodically; relationships in 3+ departments; regular QBRs; bi-weekly operational cadence; conflicts resolved reasonably; joint planning extends 6-12 months
- **3** -- Executive sponsor on one side only; relationships in 1-2 departments; QBRs are sporadic; monthly or irregular communication; conflicts linger; planning is quarter by quarter
- **2** -- No executive sponsorship; single point of contact; no structured communication; relationship is reactive; conflicts lead to disengagement; no joint planning
- **1** -- No meaningful relationship; interactions are transactional only; communication is ad hoc; single point of failure; conflicts unresolved; at risk of churn

### Red Flags

- Partnership depends on one person on either side (single point of failure)
- Executive sponsors have never met or spoken
- All communication goes through a single channel or person

- Partner learns about product changes from public channels, not the partnership team
- No structured business review cadence

Score <3 --> conduct relationship deepening exercise before the partnership deteriorates.

## FINAL PARTNERSHIP SCORE DECISION

### Hard Fail Dimensions (Revenue Impact, Strategic Alignment, Partner Activation)

- Score <3 --> **IMMEDIATE INTERVENTION REQUIRED**
- These represent fundamental partnership failures that threaten the program

### All Dimensions

- Average score >= 4.0 --> **PARTNERSHIP PROGRAM IS HEALTHY**
- Average score 3.5-3.9 --> **CONDITIONAL** -- program has specific improvement areas
- Average score < 3.5 --> **INTERVENTION REQUIRED** -- program needs restructuring

### Relationship Depth Exception

- Relationship Depth is NOT a hard fail
- Score <3 triggers mandatory relationship review for all top-tier partners
- Score <2 triggers immediate executive engagement

### Partnership Type Context

- All scores MUST be evaluated relative to partnership type
- Technology partners are judged heavily on Integration Quality
- Channel partners are judged heavily on Revenue Impact and Activation
- Strategic partners are judged heavily on Strategic Alignment and Relationship Depth

## SCORING PROCESS

1. State partnership type, stage, and strategic goal
2. Review all partnership data, communications, and metrics
3. Score each dimension independently with written justification
4. Check hard fail dimensions first -- if any fail, stop and intervene
5. Calculate average score
6. Document required actions for any dimension scoring below 4
7. Schedule next review date
8. Record the score card in project documentation

---

## ENFORCEMENT RULE

Quality is enforced, not assumed.
Do not justify low scores.
Do not maintain partnerships that fail hard dimensions.
Mutual value is mandatory, not optional.
Intervene until standards are met.
Measure until outcomes are proven.
