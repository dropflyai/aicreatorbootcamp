# Partnership Brain -- Benchmark Tests

This document contains benchmark scenarios used to evaluate the Partnership Brain's
competence across core partnership management disciplines. Each scenario tests specific
skills and has defined evaluation criteria.

The brain must demonstrate domain expertise, structured thinking, and actionable
recommendations to pass each benchmark.

---

## HOW TO USE BENCHMARK TESTS

1. Present the scenario to the Partnership Brain exactly as written
2. Evaluate the response against the listed criteria
3. Score each criterion as MET or NOT MET
4. A benchmark passes only if ALL criteria are MET
5. Document the response quality and any gaps
6. The brain must pass at least 80% of benchmarks to be considered production-ready

---

## BENCHMARK 1: Revenue Concentration Negotiation

**Scenario:**
"Our top partner generates 30% of our total partner revenue. They are now demanding
exclusive territory rights for the West Coast of the US. If we grant exclusivity,
other partners in that territory will leave. If we refuse, this partner threatens to
switch to a competitor. Our direct sales team also covers the West Coast. Negotiate."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Identifies the core tension: revenue concentration creating leverage asymmetry
- [ ] Does NOT immediately cave to the demand or immediately refuse
- [ ] Proposes a structured negotiation approach with multiple options
- [ ] Addresses the 30% revenue concentration as a strategic risk to reduce regardless
- [ ] Proposes alternatives to exclusive territory (preferred status, first right of refusal, lead priority)
- [ ] Considers the impact on other partners and direct sales
- [ ] Addresses the competitor threat with a realistic assessment
- [ ] Proposes a timeline and milestones for the negotiation
- [ ] Includes a BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement) analysis
- [ ] Recommends long-term strategy to reduce concentration risk

## BENCHMARK 2: Technology Partnership Program Design

**Scenario:**
"Design a technology partnership program for our API product. We have 50+ companies
using our API informally but no formal program. We want to create tiers, define
benefits and requirements, establish economics, and launch within 60 days. Our
API handles 100M+ requests/month across these integrations."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Proposes a tiered program structure (3-4 tiers with clear differentiation)
- [ ] Defines specific requirements per tier (integration quality, volume, co-marketing)
- [ ] Defines specific benefits per tier (support, SLAs, revenue share, co-development)
- [ ] Economics model is sustainable (revenue share or referral fees that scale)
- [ ] Addresses migration of existing 50+ informal partners into the program
- [ ] Proposes a certification or validation process for integrations
- [ ] Includes a partner portal and self-service resources plan
- [ ] Provides a realistic 60-day launch timeline with milestones
- [ ] Addresses API governance (versioning, deprecation, breaking changes)
- [ ] Proposes measurement framework for program success
- [ ] Considers the experience of an individual partner going through the program

## BENCHMARK 3: Integration Incident Management

**Scenario:**
"A partner integration broke after our latest API update. The partner's customers
(2,000 active users) have been without the integration for 18 hours. The partner's
CTO is furious and has posted on social media about our 'broken promises.' Our
engineering team says the fix will take another 6-12 hours. Handle the incident,
communication, and post-incident process."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Immediate response plan within the first hour (not 18 hours)
- [ ] Communication plan for the partner (executive-level, not just technical)
- [ ] Communication plan for the partner's affected customers
- [ ] Addresses the social media situation (do not ignore or escalate publicly)
- [ ] Proposes interim workaround if full fix takes 6-12 hours
- [ ] Takes ownership and does not blame the partner
- [ ] Post-incident review plan covering root cause and prevention
- [ ] Proposes changes to the release process (partner notification, staging, canary)
- [ ] Addresses the relationship damage and recovery plan
- [ ] Proposes a systemic fix (automated integration testing, partner staging environment)
- [ ] Considers a goodwill gesture appropriate to the severity

## BENCHMARK 4: Channel Conflict Resolution

**Scenario:**
"A direct sales rep and a channel partner are both pursuing the same enterprise deal
worth $500K ARR. The partner claims they registered the deal 2 weeks ago. The direct
rep claims they had a relationship with the prospect for 3 months. The prospect is
confused and considering a competitor because of the 'internal chaos.' Resolve this."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Prioritizes the customer experience above internal dynamics
- [ ] Reviews the deal registration system and timeline objectively
- [ ] Proposes a clear resolution framework (not just for this deal, but for future conflicts)
- [ ] Addresses the immediate situation: unify the approach to the prospect
- [ ] Does not simply default to "direct sales wins" or "partner always wins"
- [ ] Considers splitting credit or compensation to satisfy both parties
- [ ] Proposes a prospect communication plan to restore confidence
- [ ] Addresses the systemic problem: how to prevent future conflicts
- [ ] Recommends a deal registration policy with clear rules and timeframes
- [ ] Considers the long-term relationship with both the partner and the direct rep

## BENCHMARK 5: Partner Due Diligence (Red Flags)

**Scenario:**
"A large, well-known company approaches us about a strategic partnership. They have
a strong brand and wide market reach. During due diligence, you discover: (1) They
are in a public dispute with a current partner over IP theft, (2) Their Glassdoor
reviews show significant employee dissatisfaction, (3) Their customer NPS is -15,
and (4) They have a history of acquiring partners and then competing with them.
Should we proceed? What additional diligence is needed?"

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Does not dismiss the red flags because of the brand name
- [ ] Evaluates each red flag independently with severity assessment
- [ ] The IP dispute is treated as a serious legal and reputational risk
- [ ] Employee dissatisfaction is evaluated for operational risk (high turnover in partner team)
- [ ] Negative NPS is evaluated for its impact on joint customer experience
- [ ] Acquisition history is treated as a strategic threat
- [ ] Proposes additional due diligence steps for each red flag
- [ ] Recommends contractual protections if proceeding (IP clauses, non-compete, termination rights)
- [ ] Provides a clear go/no-go recommendation with reasoning
- [ ] Does not automatically say "no" -- evaluates risk-reward comprehensively

## BENCHMARK 6: Partner Reactivation Campaign

**Scenario:**
"We have 200 signed partners. Only 40 are active (made at least one deal or integration
in the last 90 days). The other 160 are dormant. We do not have the budget to assign
a partner manager to each one. Design a reactivation campaign to bring dormant partners
back to active status."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Segments the 160 dormant partners (recently dormant vs long dormant, high potential vs low)
- [ ] Proposes a tiered reactivation approach (not one-size-fits-all)
- [ ] Includes automated/scalable tactics (email sequences, webinars, self-service tools)
- [ ] Includes high-touch tactics for high-potential dormant partners
- [ ] Proposes incentives for reactivation (not just "please come back")
- [ ] Addresses root causes of dormancy (did they fail to onboard? lose their champion? find it too hard?)
- [ ] Defines what "reactivated" means with measurable criteria
- [ ] Sets realistic reactivation rate targets (not 100% -- some partners should be exited)
- [ ] Proposes a timeline with checkpoints
- [ ] Includes a "graceful exit" for partners that should be removed from the program

## BENCHMARK 7: Strategic Partnership Proposal (Co-Development)

**Scenario:**
"A complementary product company proposes co-developing a new product that combines
their AI capabilities with our data infrastructure. The project would take 12 months,
require 3 engineers from each side, and the resulting product would be jointly owned.
They propose a 50/50 revenue split. Evaluate this proposal and negotiate."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Evaluates the strategic value of the joint product (market opportunity, competitive advantage)
- [ ] Assesses the partner's AI capabilities objectively (not just their claims)
- [ ] Addresses IP ownership concerns with co-developed product
- [ ] Challenges the 50/50 split with reasoned analysis (who brings more value?)
- [ ] Proposes a clear governance model for the co-development (decision authority, dispute resolution)
- [ ] Addresses resource commitment risk (3 engineers for 12 months is significant)
- [ ] Proposes milestones and stage gates (not a 12-month commitment upfront)
- [ ] Addresses what happens if one party wants to exit mid-development
- [ ] Considers competitive implications (does this co-development help or hurt our core product?)
- [ ] Proposes a term sheet or LOI structure with key negotiation points

## BENCHMARK 8: Multi-Partner Ecosystem Design

**Scenario:**
"We want to build a marketplace ecosystem around our platform. Currently we have
a closed platform with 5 integrations we built ourselves. Design the ecosystem
strategy including: who to recruit first, how to incentivize development, how to
ensure quality, and how to grow the ecosystem to 50+ partners in 18 months."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Proposes a phased approach (seed partners, growth phase, scale phase)
- [ ] Identifies ideal first partners (strategic, willing to invest, credible in the market)
- [ ] Proposes developer incentives (revenue share, marketing support, early access, MDF)
- [ ] Includes a quality assurance framework (certification, review process, quality gates)
- [ ] Addresses the "chicken and egg" problem (no users without apps, no apps without users)
- [ ] Proposes a marketplace governance model (listing standards, dispute resolution)
- [ ] Includes a developer experience plan (documentation, sandbox, support)
- [ ] Sets realistic milestones for 6, 12, and 18 months
- [ ] Addresses ecosystem economics (how does the platform make money from the ecosystem?)
- [ ] Considers the long-term sustainability of the ecosystem

## BENCHMARK 9: Partner Program ROI Justification

**Scenario:**
"The CFO wants to cut the partnership budget by 50% because 'partnerships have not
proven ROI.' Total partnership program cost is $2M/year including headcount, tools,
events, and MDF. Partner-sourced revenue is $3M and partner-influenced revenue is
estimated at $5M but not formally tracked. Build the ROI case to defend the budget."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Builds a clear ROI model with all costs and revenue streams
- [ ] Addresses partner-sourced revenue with solid attribution
- [ ] Addresses partner-influenced revenue with a defensible attribution methodology
- [ ] Includes non-revenue value: market access, product enhancement, brand credibility
- [ ] Proposes formal attribution tracking to address the CFO's concern
- [ ] Shows what would be lost if partnerships were cut (not just what is gained)
- [ ] Compares partnership CAC to other channels (direct sales, marketing)
- [ ] Proposes improvements to increase measurable ROI
- [ ] Acknowledges weaknesses in current measurement honestly
- [ ] Presents the case with financial rigor, not just relationship arguments

## BENCHMARK 10: International Partner Expansion

**Scenario:**
"We are expanding from the US to Europe. We need local partners in the UK, Germany,
and France. Our product requires local compliance knowledge, local language support,
and local sales presence. We have zero European partnerships today. Design the
expansion strategy."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Proposes a market-by-market approach with prioritization rationale
- [ ] Addresses regulatory differences across UK, Germany, and France
- [ ] Defines the ideal partner profile for each market
- [ ] Proposes sourcing strategies for finding partners in each market
- [ ] Addresses language and cultural considerations
- [ ] Proposes a localization strategy for partner materials
- [ ] Includes a realistic timeline with milestones per market
- [ ] Addresses how to evaluate and select partners in markets where you have no presence
- [ ] Proposes a support model for international partners (timezone, language, cultural)
- [ ] Includes budget and resource requirements for the expansion

## BENCHMARK 11: Partner Acquisition Due Diligence

**Scenario:**
"Our most successful partner (contributing 20% of our total revenue) has received
an acquisition offer from our largest competitor. If the acquisition closes, we
will likely lose the partnership and the associated revenue within 12 months.
We have 60 days before the deal is expected to close. Respond."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Assesses the situation urgently with a clear timeline
- [ ] Proposes immediate actions to protect the relationship (executive engagement)
- [ ] Develops a contingency plan for losing the partnership
- [ ] Identifies alternative partners that could partially replace the revenue
- [ ] Assesses the customer impact and develops a customer retention plan
- [ ] Considers whether a counter-offer or deeper strategic commitment could prevent the acquisition
- [ ] Addresses the 20% revenue concentration as a systemic risk to prevent going forward
- [ ] Proposes an accelerated partner diversification strategy
- [ ] Includes a communication plan (internal, customer, market)
- [ ] Sets up monitoring to know as early as possible if the deal closes

## BENCHMARK 12: Partnership Pricing and Economics

**Scenario:**
"Design the economics for a referral partner program. Our product is $100/month SaaS.
Average customer lifetime is 24 months. Customer acquisition cost through direct
marketing is $400. We want referral partners to be more cost-effective than direct
marketing while still being attractive enough to motivate partners."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Calculates the LTV correctly ($100 x 24 = $2,400)
- [ ] Identifies the maximum allowable CAC through partners (<$400 to be more efficient)
- [ ] Proposes a commission structure with specific percentages and terms
- [ ] Considers one-time vs recurring commissions with tradeoffs
- [ ] Models the economics at different referral volumes
- [ ] Addresses partner motivation (is the commission actually attractive?)
- [ ] Considers tiered commissions to reward higher performance
- [ ] Addresses payment timing and terms
- [ ] Includes clawback provisions for churned customers
- [ ] Compares the total cost of the partner channel to direct marketing at scale

## BENCHMARK 13: Joint Go-to-Market Launch

**Scenario:**
"We are launching a joint solution with a partner in 45 days. The solution combines
our analytics platform with their CRM. Neither company has done a joint launch before.
The target audience is mid-market SaaS companies. Design the joint GTM plan."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Creates a structured launch plan with milestones for the 45-day timeline
- [ ] Defines joint messaging and positioning for the combined solution
- [ ] Proposes a content plan (joint blog, webinar, case study, landing page)
- [ ] Addresses lead routing and follow-up process between the two companies
- [ ] Proposes a joint demand generation campaign with budget allocation
- [ ] Includes a sales enablement plan for both teams
- [ ] Addresses PR and analyst relations for the joint announcement
- [ ] Proposes measurement framework for launch success
- [ ] Addresses post-launch handoff (how ongoing joint selling works after the launch)
- [ ] Identifies the biggest risks to the launch timeline and mitigation plans

## BENCHMARK 14: Partner Program Metrics Dashboard

**Scenario:**
"Build a partnership metrics dashboard specification. The dashboard should give the
VP of Partnerships a single-page view of program health, and allow drill-down into
individual partner performance. We use Salesforce CRM and have basic partner data.
Define the metrics, layout, and data requirements."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Defines 5-8 top-level KPIs for the single-page executive view
- [ ] Includes revenue metrics (partner-sourced, partner-influenced, growth rate)
- [ ] Includes pipeline metrics (partner pipeline, conversion rate)
- [ ] Includes activation metrics (activation rate, time-to-first-deal)
- [ ] Includes program health metrics (partner satisfaction, churn rate)
- [ ] Proposes a drill-down structure for individual partner performance
- [ ] Identifies Salesforce data requirements and gaps
- [ ] Proposes data integration approach for non-Salesforce data
- [ ] Includes comparison views (period over period, partner vs partner)
- [ ] Addresses data quality and reliability concerns

## BENCHMARK 15: Partnership Crisis Management

**Scenario:**
"A partner's product has a major security breach. Customer data from 500 joint customers
may be compromised. The partner has not issued a public statement yet. Media outlets
are asking us for comment. Our legal team wants to distance from the partner. Our
partner team wants to support them. Handle the crisis."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Prioritizes customer protection above all other concerns
- [ ] Proposes immediate customer notification with actionable steps
- [ ] Addresses legal obligations (data breach notification laws per jurisdiction)
- [ ] Proposes a public statement that is honest without throwing the partner under the bus
- [ ] Contacts the partner directly at the executive level before any public action
- [ ] Assesses the scope of impact on joint customers (what data, what risk)
- [ ] Proposes a coordinated response with the partner (preferred) or independent if needed
- [ ] Addresses internal communication (sales team, support team, executive team)
- [ ] Proposes a post-crisis review of partnership security requirements
- [ ] Addresses long-term partnership implications (continue, modify, or exit)

## BENCHMARK 16: Partner Enablement at Scale

**Scenario:**
"We have 100 channel partners. Only 20 can effectively demo and sell our product.
The partner team has 3 people and cannot provide individual training to all 100.
Design a scalable enablement program that gets at least 60 of the 100 partners
to selling proficiency within 90 days."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Proposes a tiered enablement approach (self-service, group, and high-touch)
- [ ] Includes a self-service learning path (videos, documentation, quizzes)
- [ ] Includes group enablement (webinars, office hours, peer learning)
- [ ] Reserves high-touch support for highest-potential partners
- [ ] Defines "selling proficiency" with measurable criteria (certification, demo score)
- [ ] Proposes a certification program with clear milestones
- [ ] Includes a demo environment that partners can use independently
- [ ] Addresses partner motivation to complete training (incentives, requirements)
- [ ] Sets a realistic timeline with weekly/monthly cohort targets
- [ ] Includes measurement to track which partners are progressing and which are stuck

## BENCHMARK 17: Partnership Contract Negotiation

**Scenario:**
"A potential partner sends their standard agreement. Key terms: (1) 3-year exclusive
deal with no performance minimums, (2) They own all joint customer data, (3) Non-compete
prevents us from partnering with any company in their sector, (4) Automatic renewal
unless canceled 12 months before expiration, (5) Their limitation of liability is
$1,000. Redline the agreement."

### Evaluation Criteria

- [ ] Identifies all 5 problematic terms and explains why each is unacceptable
- [ ] Proposes a counter for exclusivity: performance-based exclusivity with minimums
- [ ] Proposes a counter for data ownership: joint ownership or defined data sharing
- [ ] Proposes a counter for non-compete: narrowly scoped and time-limited
- [ ] Proposes a counter for auto-renewal: shorter notice period (90 days) and annual terms
- [ ] Proposes a counter for liability: mutual liability proportional to deal value
- [ ] Provides specific redline language, not just general objections
- [ ] Prioritizes which terms are "must-change" vs "nice-to-change"
- [ ] Recommends involving legal counsel for the formal negotiation
- [ ] Addresses the overall posture: collaborative negotiation, not adversarial
