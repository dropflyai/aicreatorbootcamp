# Go-to-Market Integration — Co-Marketing Playbooks, Joint Campaigns, and Co-Selling Motions

## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**
- How to design co-marketing programs that generate measurable pipeline rather than vanity impressions
- Which joint campaign formats deliver the highest ROI for partnership go-to-market investment
- How to structure co-selling motions that leverage both organizations' strengths without creating confusion
- When to invest in joint GTM activities versus independent marketing that references the partnership

**Mistakes it prevents:**
- Launching co-marketing campaigns without aligned messaging, shared targets, or attribution methodology
- Running joint events that attract tire-kickers rather than qualified prospects because the audience targeting was not jointly designed
- Creating co-selling confusion where customers receive conflicting information from two sales teams
- Spending partnership marketing budget without measuring contribution to pipeline and revenue

**Outputs it enables:**
- Co-marketing playbooks with campaign frameworks, content templates, and distribution strategies
- Joint campaign plans with shared calendars, budget allocation, and performance metrics
- Co-selling playbooks with joint discovery guides, proposal templates, and handoff protocols
- GTM attribution models that fairly measure each party's contribution to joint outcomes

---

## The Core Insight

Go-to-market integration is the **commercial realization of partnership value.** Technical integration creates the product capability; GTM integration creates the customer awareness, demand, and revenue that justify the partnership investment. The critical distinction is between **additive GTM** (each party independently markets the partnership, hoping for aggregate impact) and **integrated GTM** (both parties execute coordinated campaigns with shared targets, unified messaging, and joint accountability). Additive GTM is easy to execute but produces diluted results. Integrated GTM requires significant coordination but produces compound returns — joint campaigns generate qualified leads that neither party could reach alone, co-selling motions close deals faster and at higher value, and shared customer success drives retention and expansion. The investment in GTM integration is justified when the partnership's addressable market is large enough that the coordination cost is small relative to the revenue opportunity.

## 1. Co-Marketing Playbook Design

### The Co-Marketing Spectrum

| Activity | Investment | Complexity | Expected Outcome |
|----------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Logo exchange** | Minimal | Very low | Brand association, credibility signal |
| **Joint blog post** | Low | Low | SEO value, thought leadership, awareness |
| **Co-authored whitepaper** | Medium | Medium | Lead generation, authority building |
| **Joint webinar** | Medium | Medium | Pipeline generation, audience sharing |
| **Co-sponsored event** | High | High | Pipeline generation, relationship building |
| **Joint product launch** | Very high | Very high | Market impact, demand generation |

### Co-Marketing Campaign Framework

**Campaign Planning Phase** (4-6 weeks before launch)
- Define the campaign objective (awareness, lead generation, pipeline acceleration)
- Identify the target audience (joint ICP — the intersection of both partners' ideal customers)
- Develop the joint value proposition (what is the message that is only credible when both brands are together?)
- Agree on content format and distribution channels
- Allocate budget and responsibilities (who creates, who distributes, who follows up)
- Define success metrics and attribution methodology

**Campaign Execution Phase** (campaign duration)
- Joint content creation with both parties' brand guidelines respected
- Coordinated distribution across both parties' channels (email, social, paid, partner portals)
- Shared lead capture with agreed-upon data fields and opt-in language
- Real-time performance monitoring with shared dashboard access

**Campaign Follow-Up Phase** (2-4 weeks after campaign)
- Lead distribution according to pre-agreed rules (geographic, segment, first-to-respond)
- Joint follow-up sequences for highest-value leads
- Campaign performance analysis against success metrics
- ROI calculation with attribution to each party's contribution
- Retrospective to capture learnings for future campaigns

### Content Co-Creation Guidelines

**Brand alignment**: All co-branded content must be approved by both parties' marketing teams. Brand guidelines must be shared and respected. Neither party's brand should dominate.

**Voice and messaging**: Co-authored content should blend both organizations' perspectives while maintaining a unified narrative. Avoid "Company A says... Company B says..." structures; instead, present a unified point of view.

**Review process**: Establish a clear content review workflow with defined review cycles (typically 2 rounds), deadlines, and approval authorities. Content that misses the review deadline holds the campaign.

**Intellectual property**: Co-created content is typically jointly owned with both parties having the right to use and distribute. Clarify this in the partnership agreement.

## 2. Joint Campaign Formats

### Campaign Format 1: Joint Webinar

**Format**: 45-60 minute educational session co-presented by representatives from both organizations
**Audience**: Mid-funnel prospects who are aware of both products but have not evaluated the integration
**Production requirements**: Shared registration page, dual-branded slide deck, moderated Q&A, post-event recording
**Success metrics**: Registrations (target 200-500), attendance rate (target 40-50%), post-event pipeline generated
**Best practice**: Feature a joint customer case study rather than two separate product pitches. Customers selling to other customers is more credible than vendors selling together.

### Campaign Format 2: Co-Sponsored Event (In-Person or Virtual)

**Format**: Half-day or full-day event with keynotes, breakout sessions, and networking
**Audience**: Mix of existing customers and prospects from both organizations
**Production requirements**: Shared event platform, co-branded materials, joint registration and promotion
**Success metrics**: Attendance, pipeline generated, customer satisfaction (NPS), follow-up meeting conversion
**Best practice**: Organize content around customer outcomes, not product features. The event should answer "what can I achieve with these two products together?"

### Campaign Format 3: Co-Authored Thought Leadership

**Format**: Research report, whitepaper, or industry analysis jointly produced and branded
**Audience**: Top-of-funnel awareness and lead generation
**Production requirements**: Joint research, co-writing, dual-branded design, gated distribution
**Success metrics**: Downloads, MQLs generated, citation by industry analysts, social sharing
**Best practice**: Include original research or data that neither party could produce alone. The unique value of co-authored content is the combined perspective.

### Campaign Format 4: Joint Solution Landing Page

**Format**: Persistent web page describing the integrated solution with customer proof points
**Audience**: Prospects evaluating the integration, customers discovering integration capabilities
**Production requirements**: Dedicated URL, co-branded design, clear CTAs (demo request, trial signup, contact)
**Success metrics**: Traffic, conversion rate, demo requests, pipeline influenced
**Best practice**: Host on both parties' domains with canonical URL to avoid SEO duplication. Include a live integration demo or interactive walkthrough.

## 3. Co-Selling Motions

### The Co-Selling Framework

**Motion 1: Joint Discovery**
Both sales teams participate in the initial discovery call. Purpose: demonstrate the breadth of the combined solution and uncover pain points that span both products.
- Format: 45-minute joint call with representatives from both organizations
- Division of labor: Each team covers their domain expertise while building on the other's questions
- Outcome: Shared understanding of customer needs, agreed next steps

**Motion 2: Joint Solution Design**
Both teams collaborate on a solution proposal that integrates both products.
- Format: Internal working session followed by customer-facing presentation
- Division of labor: Each team designs their component; joint session ensures coherence
- Outcome: Integrated solution proposal that demonstrates combined value

**Motion 3: Joint Proof of Concept**
Both products are deployed in a controlled environment to demonstrate integrated value.
- Format: 2-4 week pilot with defined success criteria
- Division of labor: Each team deploys and supports their product; joint team manages the integration
- Outcome: Proven value in the customer's environment with quantified results

**Motion 4: Joint Closing**
Both teams participate in the commercial negotiation and closing process.
- Format: Joint negotiation sessions, bundled or coordinated pricing proposals
- Division of labor: Each team handles their commercial terms; joint coordination on bundled pricing
- Outcome: Signed agreements with both organizations, integrated implementation plan

### Co-Selling Rules of Engagement

**Lead ownership**: The party that originated the lead owns the customer relationship for that deal. The supporting party participates but does not independently pursue the account.

**Communication protocol**: All customer communication related to the joint deal is coordinated. No unilateral customer outreach during an active co-selling engagement.

**Pricing coordination**: If joint pricing is offered, both parties must approve the combined price point. Neither party unilaterally discounts their component.

**Deal intelligence sharing**: Both parties share relevant deal intelligence (budget, timeline, decision-makers, competition) within the boundaries of customer confidentiality.

**Win/loss debrief**: Every joint deal (won or lost) is debriefed jointly. Lessons are documented and shared to improve future co-selling effectiveness.

## 4. GTM Attribution

### The Attribution Challenge

Partnership GTM attribution is inherently complex because multiple parties contribute to the same outcome. Clean attribution requires agreed methodology before the first campaign launches.

### Attribution Models for Partnership GTM

**First-touch attribution**: Credit goes to the channel that first introduced the customer to the partnership value proposition. Simple but ignores the multi-touch reality.

**Last-touch attribution**: Credit goes to the channel that produced the final conversion (demo request, trial signup, purchase). Also ignores multi-touch reality.

**Multi-touch attribution**: Credit is distributed across all touchpoints in the customer journey. More accurate but requires sophisticated tracking and agreed weighting.

**Influence attribution**: Both parties receive credit when they can demonstrate material involvement in a deal, regardless of origination. Allows for double-counting but reduces conflict.

### Recommended Approach

For most partnerships, **dual attribution with primary/secondary designation** is the most practical:
- **Primary**: The party that originated the deal (identified the customer, scheduled the first meeting, drove the initial engagement)
- **Secondary**: The party that materially influenced the deal (participated in demos, provided technical validation, facilitated executive access)
- Primary credit: Full commission/revenue share
- Secondary credit: Influence commission (typically 25-50% of primary rate)

### Attribution Data Requirements

- CRM integration between both parties' systems (or at minimum, shared deal registration data)
- UTM tracking for all co-marketing campaigns with partner-specific parameters
- Lead source tracking that distinguishes partner-sourced from partner-influenced
- Regular (monthly) attribution reconciliation meetings to resolve disputed deals

## 5. Joint GTM Planning

### The Annual GTM Plan

**Q1 Planning** (December-January):
- Review prior year GTM performance
- Set annual joint GTM objectives (pipeline targets, campaign calendar, budget allocation)
- Identify key themes and market moments for the year
- Allocate MDF and co-marketing budget

**Quarterly Execution Cycles**:
- Month 1: Campaign preparation (content creation, audience building, logistics)
- Month 2: Campaign execution (launch, distribution, lead capture)
- Month 3: Follow-up and analysis (lead nurturing, pipeline conversion, performance review)

**Continuous Activities**:
- Joint solution page maintenance and optimization
- Social media cross-promotion
- Customer story development and distribution
- Sales enablement content updates

## Failure Modes

1. **Co-marketing without co-targeting**: Running joint campaigns without aligning on the target audience — attracting leads that match one partner's ICP but not the other's, creating lead quality disputes
2. **Attribution warfare**: Spending more energy fighting about deal credit than generating new deals — a symptom of a partnership where value capture has overtaken value creation
3. **Co-selling without coordination**: Having both sales teams contact the customer independently — creating confusion, undermining professionalism, and often losing the deal
4. **Campaign-without-follow-up**: Generating leads through co-marketing but failing to execute coordinated follow-up — wasting the campaign investment as leads go cold
5. **Vanity GTM**: Producing co-branded content and logos for brand association without tracking pipeline contribution — investing in partnership theater rather than partnership revenue
6. **One-sided investment**: One partner investing significantly more in GTM while the other free-rides — creating resentment and unsustainable economics

## The Operator's Framework

**Step 1: Align on GTM objectives.** Both parties agree on annual pipeline and revenue targets from joint GTM activities. These targets flow into the joint business plan.

**Step 2: Design the campaign calendar.** Plan quarterly campaigns with specific formats, audiences, budgets, and success metrics. Assign ownership for each campaign component.

**Step 3: Build co-selling playbooks.** Define the four co-selling motions (discovery, solution design, POC, closing). Document rules of engagement. Train both sales teams.

**Step 4: Implement attribution.** Select the attribution model. Configure CRM systems for partner tracking. Establish the monthly reconciliation cadence.

**Step 5: Execute and measure.** Launch campaigns according to the calendar. Track performance against success metrics in real time. Follow up on leads within agreed SLAs.

**Step 6: Optimize continuously.** Review campaign performance quarterly. Double down on high-performing formats and audiences. Sunset underperforming activities. Share learnings in QBRs.

## Summary

Go-to-market integration transforms partnership capability into pipeline and revenue through coordinated commercial execution:

1. **Integrated GTM produces compound returns** — coordinated campaigns with shared targets, unified messaging, and joint accountability outperform independent marketing that merely references the partnership
2. **Co-marketing must target the joint ICP** — the audience that is relevant to both partners, not the union of both audiences
3. **Co-selling requires explicit rules of engagement** — without coordinated communication, shared deal intelligence, and pricing alignment, co-selling creates confusion rather than value
4. **Attribution must be agreed before the first campaign** — retroactive attribution discussions are inherently adversarial and damage the collaborative spirit needed for effective GTM
5. **GTM investment must be balanced** — one-sided investment creates resentment and unsustainable partnership economics
6. **Every GTM activity must be measured against pipeline** — brand awareness and impressions are intermediate metrics; pipeline and revenue are the ultimate measures of GTM success
