# Partnership Metrics — Revenue Attribution, Partner-Influenced vs. Partner-Sourced, LTV, and Health Scores

## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**
- How to measure partnership revenue contribution with sufficient precision to justify continued investment
- Whether a partnership is generating genuinely incremental revenue or merely claiming credit for organic business
- How customer lifetime value differs between partner-sourced and direct-sourced customers
- Whether a partnership is healthy and trending positively or deteriorating beneath surface-level revenue numbers

**Mistakes it prevents:**
- Reporting inflated partnership revenue by double-counting deals that would have closed without partner involvement
- Underreporting partnership contribution by failing to capture partner influence on deals attributed to direct sales
- Investing in partnerships that appear profitable on revenue metrics but destroy value on a fully loaded cost basis
- Missing deterioration signals by tracking only lagging revenue metrics without leading health indicators

**Outputs it enables:**
- Revenue attribution reports with clear partner-sourced and partner-influenced breakdowns
- LTV comparison analyses showing the differential value of partner-sourced vs. direct customers
- Partnership health scores that combine quantitative performance with qualitative relationship assessment
- Investment justification models that demonstrate partnership ROI on a fully loaded basis

---

## The Core Insight

Partnership metrics exist to answer one fundamental question: **Is this partnership creating value that would not exist without it?** This question is deceptively simple and operationally complex. Revenue flows through partnerships in ways that defy clean attribution — a customer may discover your product through a partner's recommendation, evaluate it through direct sales, and convert after a joint webinar. Assigning credit to a single touchpoint distorts reality; assigning credit to all touchpoints creates inflation. The solution is not perfect attribution (which is impossible) but **useful attribution** — a measurement framework that is consistent, transparent, jointly agreed upon, and directionally accurate enough to inform investment decisions. The organizations that measure partnerships well invest more wisely, identify underperforming partnerships earlier, and build credibility for the partnership function within the broader organization.

## 1. Revenue Attribution Framework

### The Three Attribution Categories

**Category 1: Partner-Sourced Revenue**
- Definition: Revenue from deals where the partner originated the opportunity. The customer had no prior relationship with your organization, or the opportunity was dormant for 90+ days before the partner engaged.
- Evidence standard: Deal registration showing partner as originator, with customer confirmation during onboarding
- Attribution: Full credit to the partnership (100% of deal value counted as partner-sourced)
- Commission implication: Full partner commission rate applies

**Category 2: Partner-Influenced Revenue**
- Definition: Revenue from deals where the customer was already in your pipeline, but the partner materially influenced the outcome. The partner's involvement increased the deal size, accelerated the timeline, or was essential to winning against competition.
- Evidence standard: Partner participated in at least one customer-facing meeting, provided technical validation, or facilitated an executive introduction. Documented in CRM with partner tagged.
- Attribution: Partial credit (typically counted separately from partner-sourced; not double-counted against direct revenue)
- Commission implication: Influence commission rate applies (typically 25-50% of sourced commission)

**Category 3: Partner-Attached Revenue**
- Definition: Revenue from customers who use both products but where neither party influenced the other's sale. The customer independently purchased both products.
- Evidence standard: Shared customer identified through data matching, but no evidence of partnership-driven influence
- Attribution: No commission credit, but tracked as ecosystem overlap indicator
- Utility: Measures the potential for future co-selling and identifies customers for integration adoption campaigns

### The Attribution Decision Tree

```
Did the partner originate the opportunity?
├── YES → Partner-Sourced (full credit)
├── NO → Was the partner materially involved in the deal?
│   ├── YES → Partner-Influenced (partial credit)
│   └── NO → Does the customer use both products?
│       ├── YES → Partner-Attached (no credit, ecosystem tracked)
│       └── NO → Not partner-related
```

### Attribution Governance

**Joint attribution committee**: A monthly meeting between partnership and sales leadership to review attribution for disputed deals. Committee reviews evidence and makes final determination.

**Dispute resolution timeline**: Maximum 30 days from deal close to attribution determination. After 30 days, the initial classification stands unless new evidence emerges.

**Audit mechanism**: Quarterly audit of 10% of partner-attributed deals to verify classification accuracy. Results inform calibration of attribution criteria.

## 2. Lifetime Value Analysis

### Partner-Sourced Customer LTV

Measuring the LTV of partner-sourced customers compared to direct-sourced customers reveals the true economic value of partnerships:

**LTV Components**:
- **Average contract value (ACV)**: Initial deal size
- **Net retention rate**: Expansion minus churn over time
- **Customer lifetime**: Average duration of the customer relationship
- **Gross margin**: Revenue minus cost of goods sold
- **LTV formula**: ACV x Gross Margin % x (1 / (1 - Net Retention Rate))

### LTV Comparison Framework

| Metric | Partner-Sourced | Direct-Sourced | Difference | Implication |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Average ACV | $X | $Y | +/-% | Partner deals larger or smaller? |
| Net retention | X% | Y% | +/-pp | Partner customers expand/churn differently? |
| Time to close | X days | Y days | +/- days | Partnership accelerates or slows sales? |
| Customer lifetime | X months | Y months | +/- months | Partner customers more or less loyal? |
| Cost to acquire | $X | $Y | +/-% | Partnership more or less efficient? |
| Calculated LTV | $X | $Y | +/-% | Is partnership channel more valuable? |

### Common LTV Patterns

**Pattern: Partner-sourced higher ACV, similar retention**
Meaning: Partners bring larger deals (likely because they have established trust with the customer). The partnership is a premium channel.

**Pattern: Partner-sourced similar ACV, higher retention**
Meaning: Partner involvement during implementation and ongoing support creates stickier customers. The partnership drives customer success.

**Pattern: Partner-sourced lower ACV, lower retention**
Warning: Partners may be over-promising or selling to poor-fit customers. Investigate deal quality and partner qualification criteria.

**Pattern: Partner-sourced higher CAC, similar LTV**
Warning: The partnership channel is less efficient than direct. Evaluate whether the coordination cost is justified by strategic value beyond direct economics.

## 3. Partnership Health Scores

### The Composite Health Score Model

A partnership health score combines quantitative metrics with qualitative assessment to produce a single directional indicator:

**Component 1: Revenue Health** (25% of total score)
- Revenue attainment vs. target (0-10)
- Revenue growth trend (accelerating, stable, decelerating) (0-10)
- Pipeline coverage for next quarter (0-10)

**Component 2: Operational Health** (25% of total score)
- Activity metrics vs. expectations (0-10)
- SLA compliance (lead response, escalation response) (0-10)
- Process adherence (deal registration, reporting) (0-10)

**Component 3: Relationship Health** (25% of total score)
- Communication quality and frequency (0-10)
- Executive sponsor engagement (0-10)
- Multi-thread relationship depth (0-10)

**Component 4: Strategic Health** (25% of total score)
- Strategic alignment durability (0-10)
- Innovation and co-creation activity (0-10)
- Market position reinforcement (0-10)

### Health Score Calculation

Each component produces a score from 0-30 (sum of three sub-components). The total health score ranges from 0-120, normalized to a 0-100 scale.

**Score interpretation**:
- 80-100: **Green** — Thriving. Invest to expand.
- 60-79: **Yellow-Green** — Healthy. Maintain and optimize.
- 40-59: **Yellow** — At risk. Diagnose and intervene.
- 20-39: **Orange** — Distressed. Executive intervention required.
- 0-19: **Red** — Critical. Restructure or exit.

### Health Score Trending

The absolute score matters less than the trend. A partnership scoring 65 and trending upward is healthier than a partnership scoring 75 and trending downward. Track health scores monthly and flag any partnership that declines by 10+ points within a quarter.

## 4. Fully Loaded Partnership Economics

### The True Cost of Partnerships

Revenue attribution captures the benefit side. Fully loaded economics also capture the cost side:

**Direct costs**:
- Partner commissions and revenue share payments
- MDF and co-marketing budget spent
- Partner portal and tooling costs allocated to the partnership
- Travel and entertainment for partner relationship management

**Allocated costs**:
- Partner manager salary and benefits (prorated across portfolio)
- Engineering resources for integration development and maintenance
- Support resources for partner-escalated tickets
- Legal and finance resources for agreement management and commission processing

**Opportunity costs**:
- Direct sales capacity displaced by partner deal support
- Engineering capacity consumed by integration that could have been used for product development
- Marketing budget allocated to co-marketing that could have been used for direct demand generation

### Partnership ROI Calculation

**Partnership ROI = (Partner-Attributed Revenue x Gross Margin - Fully Loaded Partnership Costs) / Fully Loaded Partnership Costs**

**ROI benchmarks**:
- Year 1: Negative to break-even (investment year)
- Year 2: 50-150% (growth year)
- Year 3+: 200%+ (maturity year)

Partnerships that do not reach break-even by Year 2 should be evaluated for restructuring or exit.

## 5. Portfolio-Level Metrics

### The Partnership Portfolio Dashboard

Beyond individual partnership metrics, the portfolio requires its own measurement:

**Portfolio composition**:
- Number of active partnerships by type and tier
- Revenue distribution across partnerships (concentration risk)
- Partnership age distribution (balance of mature, growing, and new)

**Portfolio performance**:
- Total partner-sourced revenue (and as % of total company revenue)
- Total partner-influenced revenue (and as % of total company revenue)
- Average revenue per active partner
- Portfolio ROI (fully loaded)

**Portfolio health**:
- Average health score across the portfolio
- Number of partnerships in each health category (green through red)
- Partner churn rate (partnerships terminated or dormant)
- New partnership activation rate (new partnerships reaching first revenue)

**Portfolio efficiency**:
- Cost per partner-sourced dollar of revenue
- Partner manager capacity utilization
- Time from partnership launch to first revenue (activation velocity)

## Failure Modes

1. **Attribution inflation**: Claiming partner credit for deals that would have closed without partner involvement — inflating partnership metrics to justify the function's existence while eroding credibility with sales leadership
2. **Attribution blindness**: Failing to capture partner influence on direct deals — understating partnership value and underinvesting in a channel that is contributing more than metrics show
3. **Revenue-only measurement**: Tracking only revenue while ignoring fully loaded costs — declaring partnerships profitable that are actually net destroyers when coordination costs are included
4. **Lagging-only metrics**: Measuring only revenue (lagging) without tracking leading indicators (activity, pipeline, health) — discovering underperformance only when it is too late to intervene
5. **Health score gaming**: Manipulating qualitative health score inputs to maintain green ratings — preventing the honest assessment needed to diagnose and address real partnership issues
6. **Portfolio-level blindness**: Managing individual partnerships without portfolio-level visibility — missing concentration risk, portfolio aging, and capacity constraints

## The Operator's Framework

**Step 1: Implement the attribution framework.** Define the three categories (sourced, influenced, attached). Create the attribution decision tree. Establish the joint attribution committee.

**Step 2: Analyze LTV by channel.** Compare partner-sourced and direct-sourced customer LTV across all components. Identify patterns and anomalies. Use findings to inform partnership investment decisions.

**Step 3: Build health scores.** Implement the four-component health score model. Score each partnership monthly. Track trends and flag declines.

**Step 4: Calculate fully loaded economics.** Identify all direct, allocated, and opportunity costs for each partnership. Calculate partnership ROI. Benchmark against maturity-stage expectations.

**Step 5: Create the portfolio dashboard.** Aggregate individual partnership metrics into portfolio-level views. Monitor composition, performance, health, and efficiency.

**Step 6: Report and advocate.** Use the metrics framework to report partnership value to executive leadership. Ground investment requests in data. Build organizational credibility for the partnership function.

## Summary

Partnership metrics transform partnership management from relationship-driven intuition to data-informed decision-making:

1. **Revenue attribution must distinguish sourced, influenced, and attached** — each category has different evidence standards, commission implications, and strategic meaning
2. **LTV analysis reveals the true economic value of partnerships** — partner-sourced customers with higher LTV justify premium partnership investment; lower LTV signals quality problems
3. **Health scores combine quantitative and qualitative signals** — revenue alone cannot capture the full picture; operational, relationship, and strategic health must be measured alongside financial performance
4. **Fully loaded economics prevent profitability illusions** — partnerships that appear profitable on a revenue basis may be net destroyers when coordination costs are included
5. **Portfolio-level metrics reveal systemic patterns** — concentration risk, portfolio aging, and capacity constraints are visible only at the portfolio level
6. **Metrics build organizational credibility** — a partnership function that measures rigorously earns the trust and investment needed to scale
