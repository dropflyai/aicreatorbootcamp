# Customer Marketing — Advocacy, Advisory Board, References, Case Studies, G2 Reviews, NPS

## Overview

Customer marketing transforms satisfied customers into active advocates who generate referrals, provide social proof, influence prospects, and strengthen the brand. Unlike acquisition marketing (which targets strangers), customer marketing leverages existing relationships and proven value to create marketing assets and distribution channels. This module covers the complete customer marketing toolkit: advocacy programs, advisory boards, reference management, case study production, review generation, and Net Promoter Score operationalization.

---

## Section 1: Customer Advocacy Programs

### Building an Advocacy Program

A customer advocacy program formalizes and incentivizes the behaviors that happy customers perform naturally: recommending, reviewing, speaking, and sharing.

**Advocacy Tier Structure:**

| Tier | Activities | Rewards | Target % of Customers |
|------|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| Supporter | G2 review, NPS promoter, social share | Swag, recognition | 20-30% |
| Advocate | Case study, reference call, webinar | Product credits, early access | 5-10% |
| Champion | Speaking engagement, advisory board, co-marketing | Conference tickets, executive access | 1-3% |
| Ambassador | Brand evangelist, content creation, community leadership | Revenue share, co-branding, strategic partnership | <1% |

### Advocacy Request Timing

**When to Ask for Advocacy:**
- After a successful onboarding milestone
- After achieving a quantified business outcome
- After a positive QBR or health score increase
- After resolving a support issue exceptionally
- After the customer has been active for 6+ months
- After a renewal (commitment signal)

**When NOT to Ask:**
- During an open support escalation
- When health score is declining
- During renewal negotiation
- After a product outage or incident
- When the customer has expressed any dissatisfaction

## Section 2: Customer Advisory Board

### Purpose

A Customer Advisory Board (CAB) brings together strategic customers to provide product feedback, validate strategy, and deepen relationships. It is simultaneously a research tool, a relationship tool, and a marketing tool.

### CAB Design

**Size:** 8-15 members (enough for diverse perspectives, small enough for discussion)
**Composition:** Mix of segments, industries, use cases, and company sizes
**Term:** 12-18 months with 50% rotation annually
**Frequency:** Quarterly meetings (2 in-person, 2 virtual per year)
**Commitment:** 4-6 hours per quarter (meetings + async feedback)

### CAB Meeting Structure (Half-Day, In-Person)

```
[0:00-0:30] Welcome and Member Introductions
  - Brief company update (business results, team growth)
  - Member round-table: What's top of mind?

[0:30-1:30] Strategic Discussion
  - Present product vision and roadmap themes
  - Facilitate structured discussion on priorities
  - Capture feedback and reactions

[1:30-2:00] Break / Networking

[2:00-3:00] Deep Dive
  - One specific topic for detailed feedback
  - Live product demo or prototype review
  - Hands-on feedback session

[3:00-3:30] Open Forum
  - Member-raised topics and concerns
  - Cross-member knowledge sharing
  - Peer-to-peer advice and networking

[3:30-4:00] Commitments and Next Steps
  - Summarize what was heard
  - Commit to specific follow-up actions
  - Preview next meeting focus
```

### CAB Member Benefits

- Direct influence on product roadmap
- Early access to new features and beta programs
- Peer networking with other industry leaders
- Executive-level relationship with your leadership
- Recognition as a thought leader and innovator

## Section 3: Reference Management

### Building a Reference Database

Maintain a structured database of customers willing to serve as references for prospects:

| Field | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Company name | Matching to prospect characteristics |
| Contact name and title | Reference call coordination |
| Industry | Industry-specific reference matching |
| Company size | Size-appropriate matching |
| Use case | Use-case-specific matching |
| Products used | Product-specific matching |
| Key outcomes achieved | Quantified results for credibility |
| Reference type | Call, email, site visit, video |
| Last reference date | Prevent over-use |
| Total references given | Track and reward |
| Reference fatigue score | Prevent burnout |

### Reference Request Protocol

**Step 1: Match**
Match the prospect's characteristics (industry, size, use case) to the most relevant reference customer.

**Step 2: Approve**
CSM confirms the reference customer is in good health (health score 70+) and has not been used recently (minimum 60-day gap between reference calls).

**Step 3: Prepare**
Brief the reference customer on the prospect and the specific topics they will likely discuss. Do not script the call—authenticity is the value.

**Step 4: Facilitate**
CSM or AE introduces the parties. The reference call should be customer-to-prospect directly (no vendor on the call unless requested).

**Step 5: Thank**
Send a personalized thank-you to the reference customer within 24 hours. Update the reference database. Recognize reference activity in advocacy program.

### Reference Fatigue Prevention

- Maximum 4 reference calls per customer per year
- Track reference activity and flag overused contacts
- Rotate references to distribute the load
- Offer meaningful rewards for frequent references
- If a customer declines a reference request, respect it immediately

## Section 4: Case Study Production

### Case Study Selection Criteria

Not every customer is a good case study candidate. Select based on:
- Quantified results (measurable outcomes, not just qualitative praise)
- Recognizable brand (brand value adds credibility)
- Compelling narrative (clear before/after transformation)
- Willing to be named (anonymous case studies are 50% less effective)
- Diverse representation (variety of industries, sizes, use cases)

### Case Study Structure

```
TITLE: [Customer Name] + [Key Result] + with [Product]
Example: "Acme Corp Reduces Customer Churn by 40% with [Product]"

QUICK STATS (Sidebar/Header)
- Industry: ___
- Company size: ___
- Products used: ___
- Key metric: [Headline result]

THE CHALLENGE (300-500 words)
- What problem was the customer facing?
- What was the business impact of not solving it?
- What had they tried before?

THE SOLUTION (300-500 words)
- Why did they choose your product?
- How was it implemented?
- What was the adoption process?

THE RESULTS (300-500 words)
- Quantified outcomes (specific numbers, percentages, dollar amounts)
- Qualitative improvements (team efficiency, decision quality)
- Unexpected benefits
- Customer quote (specific, results-oriented)

LOOKING AHEAD (100-200 words)
- Future plans with the product
- Expansion opportunities
- Customer's vision for continued partnership
```

### Case Study Production Process

```
Week 1: Identify candidate, get approval from customer and legal
Week 2: Conduct 30-45 minute interview with customer stakeholders
Week 3: Write first draft, include specific data and quotes
Week 4: Customer review and approval (allow 7-10 business days)
Week 5: Design, format, publish to website
Week 6: Distribute (sales enablement, social, email, paid)
```

## Section 5: G2/Review Site Management

### Why Reviews Matter

92% of B2B buyers are more likely to purchase after reading a trusted review (G2 Research). Review sites (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius) are now a standard part of the B2B buying process.

### Review Generation Strategy

**In-App Review Prompts:**
Trigger a review request at moments of peak satisfaction:
- After completing a successful outcome
- After a positive support interaction
- After a milestone achievement
- After a feature they requested is released

**CSM-Led Review Requests:**
CSMs request reviews from satisfied customers during regular touchpoints:
- During or after positive QBRs
- After successful onboarding
- During annual check-ins
- As part of advocacy program activities

**Campaign-Based Review Drives:**
Periodic campaigns to generate a batch of reviews:
- "Help us reach X reviews on G2" campaign
- Incentivized review drives (gift card, product credit)
- Review as part of advocacy program progression

### Review Response Protocol

**Positive Reviews (4-5 stars):**
- Respond within 48 hours
- Thank the reviewer by name
- Reference a specific point from their review
- Invite them to deeper advocacy activities

**Negative Reviews (1-3 stars):**
- Respond within 24 hours (urgency signals you care)
- Acknowledge the concern without being defensive
- Offer to resolve the issue offline
- Follow up privately with the reviewer
- Update the public response when the issue is resolved

## Section 6: NPS Operationalization

### NPS Mechanics

```
NPS Score = % Promoters (9-10) - % Detractors (0-6)
Range: -100 to +100
```

### NPS Program Design

**Survey Timing:**
- Relationship NPS: Quarterly or semi-annually (overall satisfaction)
- Transactional NPS: After specific interactions (support, onboarding)
- Renewal NPS: 30-60 days before renewal (decision-influencing moment)

### Closed-Loop NPS Process

**For Promoters (9-10):**
1. Thank them within 24 hours
2. Ask for specific advocacy activities (review, reference, case study)
3. Add to advocacy program pipeline
4. Share their feedback with the team (celebrate)

**For Passives (7-8):**
1. Acknowledge within 48 hours
2. Ask what would make them a 9 or 10
3. Create action plan to address gaps
4. Follow up when improvements are made

**For Detractors (0-6):**
1. Acknowledge within 4 hours (urgency)
2. CSM or manager personally reaches out
3. Understand the root cause (listen first)
4. Create and share a remediation plan
5. Follow up until the issue is resolved
6. Re-survey after 90 days to measure recovery

### NPS Benchmarks

| Industry | Average NPS | Good | Excellent |
|----------|-----------|------|-----------|
| B2B SaaS | 30-40 | 50+ | 70+ |
| Consumer Tech | 20-30 | 40+ | 60+ |
| Financial Services | 20-30 | 40+ | 55+ |
