# QBR Design — QBR Structure, Retrospective, Value, Roadmap, Alignment, Executive vs. Operational, Preparation

## Overview

The Quarterly Business Review (QBR) is the highest-leverage touchpoint in the customer success relationship. A well-designed QBR transforms a vendor-customer dynamic into a strategic partnership by demonstrating value delivered, aligning on future objectives, and creating executive-level commitment. Poorly designed QBRs—those that devolve into feature walkthroughs or support ticket reviews—erode the very relationships they are meant to strengthen. This module covers QBR architecture from structural design through preparation, execution, and follow-through, distinguishing between executive-level and operational-level reviews and providing frameworks for each.

---

## Section 1: QBR Purpose and Philosophy

### Why QBRs Exist

The QBR serves four simultaneous purposes, each essential:

**1. Value Demonstration (Retrospective)**
Prove that the investment is delivering measurable returns. Without quantified value, the customer's perception of ROI degrades over time regardless of actual outcomes. The QBR is the forcing function that compiles, presents, and contextualizes value.

**2. Strategic Alignment (Forward-Looking)**
Ensure that the customer's evolving business priorities and your product roadmap remain aligned. Misalignment between customer strategy and vendor capability is the leading cause of strategic churn in enterprise accounts.

**3. Relationship Deepening (Multi-Threading)**
Create face time with executive sponsors who do not participate in daily operations. Executive engagement at QBRs is the single strongest predictor of renewal in accounts above $100K ARR (Gainsight Pulse research, 2023).

**4. Expansion Identification (Commercial)**
Surface expansion opportunities organically by understanding where the customer is going strategically. QBRs that discover expansion needs through dialogue—rather than pitching—convert at 3x the rate of cold expansion outreach (TSIA benchmark).

### QBR Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | What Happens |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| The Feature Demo | Customer already uses the product; they do not need a walkthrough | Perceived as a waste of time; attendance drops |
| The Support Ticket Review | QBR becomes a complaint session with no strategic value | Relationship regresses to vendor-support dynamic |
| The Slide Deck Monologue | Vendor presents 40 slides; customer listens passively | No engagement, no insight, no alignment |
| The Surprise Upsell | QBR pivots to a sales pitch without strategic context | Trust destroyed; customer feels ambushed |
| The No-Prep QBR | CSM shows up without data, outcomes, or an agenda | Signals that the vendor does not value the relationship |

## Section 2: QBR Architecture

### The Four-Block QBR Structure

Every QBR follows a four-block architecture. The proportion of time allocated to each block varies by account maturity, health, and strategic importance.

```
BLOCK 1: RETROSPECTIVE (25% of time)
  "What value have we delivered?"
  - Quantified outcomes vs. success plan goals
  - Usage trends and adoption metrics
  - Key achievements and milestones
  - Honest acknowledgment of gaps

BLOCK 2: CURRENT STATE (20% of time)
  "Where are we today?"
  - Health score and engagement summary
  - Open issues and their resolution status
  - Adoption depth and breadth assessment
  - Stakeholder engagement map

BLOCK 3: STRATEGIC ALIGNMENT (35% of time)
  "Where are you going, and how do we help?"
  - Customer's business priorities for next quarter/year
  - Product roadmap items relevant to their strategy
  - Success plan refresh for the next period
  - Gap identification and remediation approach

BLOCK 4: COMMITMENTS (20% of time)
  "What will each of us do next?"
  - Specific action items with owners and dates
  - Next QBR date and preliminary agenda
  - Escalation items requiring executive action
  - Expansion or growth opportunities identified
```

### Time Allocation by Account Context

| Account Context | Block 1 | Block 2 | Block 3 | Block 4 |
|----------------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| Healthy, mature account | 15% | 10% | 50% | 25% |
| New account (first QBR) | 30% | 25% | 30% | 15% |
| At-risk account | 10% | 30% | 35% | 25% |
| Expansion-ready account | 20% | 10% | 40% | 30% |
| Pre-renewal account | 25% | 15% | 30% | 30% |

## Section 3: Executive vs. Operational QBR

### Executive Business Review (EBR)

**Audience:** C-suite or VP-level executive sponsors, strategic decision-makers
**Frequency:** Semi-annually or annually (for strategic accounts, quarterly)
**Duration:** 60-90 minutes
**Tone:** Strategic, business-outcome focused, forward-looking

**EBR Content Principles:**
- Speak in business outcomes, not product metrics
- Frame everything in the customer's language and KPIs
- Show ROI in terms the CFO would understand (revenue impact, cost reduction, risk mitigation)
- Discuss industry trends and competitive positioning
- Present a strategic vision for the partnership, not a product roadmap

**EBR Agenda Template:**

```
[0:00-0:10] Executive Welcome and Relationship Context
  - Acknowledge the partnership and key milestones
  - Brief personal check-in with executive sponsor

[0:10-0:25] Value and ROI Summary
  - 3-5 headline business outcomes with quantified metrics
  - ROI calculation in customer's terms
  - Peer benchmarking (how they compare to similar organizations)

[0:25-0:45] Strategic Discussion
  - Customer shares upcoming business priorities
  - Vendor maps capabilities to those priorities
  - Joint identification of gaps and opportunities
  - Discussion of industry trends affecting both organizations

[0:45-0:60] Partnership Evolution
  - What does the next phase of the partnership look like?
  - Resource commitments from both sides
  - Expansion or innovation opportunities
  - Executive-to-executive relationship commitments

[0:60-0:75] Open Forum and Customer Feedback
  - Executive sponsor's unfiltered perspective
  - Concerns, suggestions, aspirations for the partnership

[0:75-0:90] Commitments and Next Steps
  - Summarize all commitments with named owners
  - Schedule next executive touchpoint
  - Confirm communication cadence
```

### Operational QBR

**Audience:** Day-to-day stakeholders, power users, team leads, project managers
**Frequency:** Quarterly
**Duration:** 45-60 minutes
**Tone:** Tactical, data-driven, action-oriented

**Operational QBR Content Principles:**
- Focus on usage data, adoption metrics, and feature utilization
- Review support ticket trends and resolution patterns
- Discuss workflow optimization and best practices
- Address training needs and enablement gaps
- Plan specific initiatives for the next quarter

**Operational QBR Agenda Template:**

```
[0:00-0:05] Quick Wins and Highlights
  - Top achievements since last QBR
  - New features adopted and their impact

[0:05-0:20] Usage and Adoption Deep Dive
  - Usage dashboards and trend analysis
  - Feature adoption heatmap
  - User engagement distribution
  - Benchmark against best-in-class customers

[0:20-0:35] Issue Resolution and Optimization
  - Open support tickets and escalation status
  - Workflow bottlenecks and optimization opportunities
  - Training needs assessment
  - Integration health check

[0:35-0:50] Next Quarter Plan
  - Adoption goals and milestones
  - New feature rollout plan
  - Training schedule
  - Process improvement initiatives

[0:50-0:60] Action Items and Scheduling
  - Specific deliverables with owners and dates
  - Next QBR date
  - Interim check-in schedule
```

### When to Combine vs. Separate

| Scenario | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------|
| ARR < $50K | Single combined QBR (operational with brief strategic component) |
| ARR $50K-$200K | Alternating: Q1 operational, Q2 executive, Q3 operational, Q4 executive |
| ARR > $200K | Separate: Quarterly operational + semi-annual executive |
| At-risk account (any size) | Executive review immediately; operational follows within 2 weeks |

## Section 4: QBR Preparation

### The 14-Day Preparation Timeline

**Day -14: Data Collection**
- Pull usage analytics for the quarter (DAU/MAU, feature adoption, engagement trends)
- Compile health score history and component breakdown
- Gather support ticket summary (volume, severity, resolution time, CSAT)
- Collect NPS/CSAT responses from the account
- Review CRM notes from all touchpoints during the quarter

**Day -10: Analysis and Narrative**
- Calculate ROI and value metrics against the success plan
- Identify the 3-5 key stories (achievements, challenges, opportunities)
- Draft the retrospective section with quantified outcomes
- Prepare peer benchmarking data (anonymized comparison to similar customers)
- Identify expansion signals and prepare discussion points

**Day -7: Deck and Materials**
- Build the QBR presentation using the approved template
- Prepare a one-page executive summary (for executives who may not attend full QBR)
- Create data visualizations that tell a clear story
- Draft the proposed success plan for the next period
- Internal review with CS Manager (for strategic or at-risk accounts)

**Day -3: Logistics and Alignment**
- Confirm attendees from both customer and vendor side
- Send agenda and pre-read materials to the customer
- Brief internal participants on account context and strategy
- Prepare for likely questions or objections
- Test technology (video conferencing, screen sharing, presentation tools)

**Day 0: QBR Execution**
- Arrive early (10 minutes for in-person, 5 minutes for virtual)
- Begin with relationship, not slides
- Follow the four-block structure but remain flexible
- Take visible notes on commitments
- End with clear next steps and scheduling

## Section 5: QBR Execution Best Practices

### The 60/40 Rule

The customer should speak for 60% of the QBR; the vendor should speak for 40%. If the vendor is doing most of the talking, the QBR is failing. The most valuable QBR content comes from the customer sharing their priorities, concerns, and aspirations.

### Facilitation Techniques

**Open-Ended Discovery Questions:**
- "What are your top three priorities for the next quarter?"
- "If you could change one thing about how your team uses [Product], what would it be?"
- "What does success look like for you personally in this initiative?"
- "What keeps you up at night regarding [their business area]?"
- "How does your leadership evaluate the ROI of tools like ours?"

**Active Listening Signals:**
- Paraphrase what the customer says before responding
- Reference specific things they mentioned earlier in the meeting
- Ask follow-up questions that demonstrate understanding
- Take notes visibly (signals the information is valued)

**Handling Difficult Moments:**
- If the customer raises an unresolved issue: Acknowledge it directly, provide status, commit to a resolution timeline
- If the executive is disengaged: Pivot to a question about their strategic priorities
- If the meeting goes off-track: Gently redirect with "That is an important topic. Can we schedule a focused session on it and return to [agenda item]?"
- If the customer delivers negative feedback: Thank them for the candor, avoid defensiveness, ask clarifying questions

### Virtual QBR Best Practices

- Camera on for all participants (builds relationship)
- Use screen sharing for data, not for reading slides aloud
- Build in pauses for questions every 10 minutes
- Use chat for real-time Q&A if attendees are reluctant to interrupt
- Record the session (with permission) for stakeholders who cannot attend
- Send the deck and recording within 24 hours

## Section 6: Post-QBR Follow-Through

### The 48-Hour Rule

Within 48 hours of the QBR, the CSM must send a written summary that includes:

```
QBR FOLLOW-UP EMAIL

Subject: [Company] QBR Summary — [Date] — Key Outcomes and Next Steps

Dear [Name],

Thank you for the productive QBR. Here is a summary of what we discussed
and the commitments we made:

KEY OUTCOMES FROM LAST QUARTER:
1. [Outcome with metric]
2. [Outcome with metric]
3. [Outcome with metric]

ACTION ITEMS:

| # | Action | Owner | Due Date |
|---|--------|-------|----------|
| 1 | [Action] | [Name] | [Date] |
| 2 | [Action] | [Name] | [Date] |
| 3 | [Action] | [Name] | [Date] |

SUCCESS PLAN FOR NEXT QUARTER:
[Brief summary of agreed objectives and milestones]

NEXT TOUCHPOINTS:
- [Date]: [Meeting/check-in]
- [Date]: Next QBR

Please let me know if I have missed anything or if any of these
items need adjustment.
```

### QBR Outcome Tracking

Track QBR effectiveness over time:

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| QBR completion rate | 90%+ of eligible accounts | QBRs conducted / QBRs scheduled |
| Executive attendance | 70%+ of EBRs include exec sponsor | EBRs with exec / Total EBRs |
| Action item completion | 85%+ within committed timelines | Items completed on time / Total items |
| Customer satisfaction | 4.5+ out of 5 post-QBR survey | Average rating |
| Expansion pipeline generated | 20%+ of QBRs identify expansion | QBRs with expansion / Total QBRs |

## Summary

The QBR is the most structured and high-impact touchpoint in the customer success relationship. Its four-block architecture—retrospective, current state, strategic alignment, and commitments—ensures that every QBR delivers value demonstration, relationship deepening, and forward-looking alignment. Executive reviews focus on business outcomes and strategic partnership; operational reviews focus on adoption, optimization, and tactical execution. Preparation begins 14 days before the meeting with systematic data collection, analysis, and narrative development. The 60/40 rule ensures that customers speak more than the vendor, transforming the QBR from a presentation into a dialogue. Post-QBR follow-through within 48 hours closes the loop with written commitments and accountability. The Customer Success Brain treats QBRs not as calendar obligations but as the primary instrument for demonstrating, deepening, and expanding customer partnerships.
