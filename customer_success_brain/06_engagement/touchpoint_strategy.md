# Touchpoint Strategy — Cadence by Segment, Multi-Threading, Stakeholder Mapping, Relationship Health

## Overview

Touchpoint strategy defines how, when, and with whom customer success teams engage across the customer lifecycle. The right touchpoint cadence builds trust, surfaces risk early, identifies expansion opportunities, and reinforces value—without overwhelming the customer or consuming disproportionate CSM capacity. Touchpoint strategy is fundamentally a resource allocation problem: how to deliver the maximum relationship value per unit of engagement effort, differentiated by customer segment, lifecycle stage, and strategic importance. This module covers cadence design by segment, multi-threading methodology, stakeholder mapping, and relationship health measurement.

## Section 1: Cadence Design by Segment

### The Engagement Spectrum

Customer engagement exists on a spectrum from fully automated (tech-touch) to fully human (high-touch). The appropriate cadence depends on customer value, complexity, and strategic importance.

| Engagement Tier | ARR Range | CSM Ratio | Primary Channel | Touchpoint Frequency |
|----------------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Tech-Touch | <$5K | 1:1000+ | Automated email, in-app | Triggered, not scheduled |
| Low-Touch | $5K-$25K | 1:200-500 | Email, occasional call | Monthly automated + quarterly human |
| Mid-Touch | $25K-$100K | 1:50-100 | Scheduled calls, email | Bi-weekly to monthly calls |
| High-Touch | $100K-$500K | 1:15-30 | Dedicated CSM, regular calls | Weekly to bi-weekly calls |
| Strategic | $500K+ | 1:3-10 | Named CSM + exec sponsor | Weekly calls + quarterly EBR |

### Tech-Touch Cadence

**Philosophy:** Deliver value at scale through automated, personalized communications triggered by customer behavior and lifecycle milestones.

**Touchpoint Map:**

| Trigger | Touchpoint | Channel | Content |
|---------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Day 1 | Welcome + quickstart | Email | Getting started guide, key resources |
| Day 3 | First value milestone | In-app | "You've completed [action]. Here's what's next." |
| Day 7 | Activation check | Email | Tips based on usage pattern, feature suggestions |
| Day 14 | Engagement assessment | In-app | Survey: "How's it going?" with branching paths |
| Day 30 | First month review | Email | Usage summary, achievements, peer comparison |
| Monthly | Usage digest | Email | Automated usage report with optimization tips |
| Quarterly | Value summary | Email | ROI highlights, new features, community events |
| -60 days | Renewal prep | Email | Auto-renewal reminder, value summary |
| -30 days | Renewal confirmation | Email | Contract details, payment confirmation |

**Tech-Touch Escalation:** If a tech-touch customer shows high engagement or risk signals, automatically escalate to human touchpoint:
- Usage drops >50% over 14 days: Trigger CSM email outreach
- CSAT/NPS detractor score: Trigger CSM phone call
- Usage exceeds plan limits: Trigger expansion conversation
- Support tickets spike: Trigger CSM check-in

### Mid-Touch Cadence

**Quarterly Plan:**

```
MONTH 1:
  Week 1: Automated usage digest (email)
  Week 2: CSM check-in call (30 min) — usage review, open issues
  Week 3: Automated feature tip (in-app or email)
  Week 4: No outreach (breathing room)

MONTH 2:
  Week 1: Automated usage digest (email)
  Week 2: CSM strategic call (45 min) — business alignment, roadmap
  Week 3: Automated training invitation or resource share
  Week 4: No outreach (breathing room)

MONTH 3:
  Week 1: Automated usage digest (email)
  Week 2: QBR (60 min) — full quarterly business review
  Week 3: QBR follow-up (action items, success plan update)
  Week 4: No outreach (breathing room)
```

### High-Touch Cadence

**Monthly Plan:**

```
WEEK 1: Strategic Check-In (45-60 min)
  - Review progress against success plan milestones
  - Discuss business context changes
  - Identify emerging needs or risks
  - Stakeholder updates

WEEK 2: Operational Touchpoint (30 min)
  - Usage data review
  - Support ticket status
  - Feature adoption update
  - Training or enablement needs

WEEK 3: Value-Add Engagement (varies)
  - Industry insight or best practice share
  - Peer connection facilitation
  - Webinar or event invitation
  - Product beta or early access opportunity

WEEK 4: Lightweight Check-In (15-30 min or async)
  - Quick status on open items
  - Any emerging concerns
  - Preview of next week/month
```

### Strategic Account Cadence

Strategic accounts receive the high-touch cadence plus:
- Executive-to-executive touchpoints (quarterly minimum)
- On-site visits (semi-annually for top accounts)
- Advisory board participation
- Custom product roadmap briefings
- Joint success planning with multi-stakeholder workshops
- Priority access to product team for feedback sessions

## Section 2: Multi-Threading

### Why Multi-Threading Matters

**Single-threaded risk:** When only one contact at the customer organization is engaged, the entire relationship depends on that individual. If they leave, get promoted, change roles, or lose enthusiasm, the account is at critical risk. TSIA research shows that accounts with a single point of contact churn at 2-3x the rate of multi-threaded accounts.

**Multi-threading definition:** Building relationships with multiple stakeholders across different levels and functions within the customer organization, ensuring that no single departure can destabilize the partnership.

### Multi-Threading Framework

**Minimum Viable Multi-Threading by Segment:**

| Segment | Minimum Contacts | Levels Required | Functions Required |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| SMB | 2 | 1 (decision maker + user) | 1 |
| Mid-Market | 3-5 | 2 (exec sponsor + operational) | 2 |
| Enterprise | 5-10 | 3 (C-level + VP + operational) | 3+ |
| Strategic | 10+ | 3+ | 4+ (including IT, Finance, Operations) |

### The Three Levels of Multi-Threading

**Level 1: Executive Sponsor**
- Title: C-suite or VP
- Role: Strategic decision-maker, budget authority, renewal approver
- Engagement: Quarterly EBR, annual strategic planning, executive dinner/event
- Owned by: CS Leader or Account Executive + CSM

**Level 2: Champion / Power User**
- Title: Director, Manager, Team Lead
- Role: Day-to-day advocate, adoption driver, internal evangelist
- Engagement: Monthly check-ins, feature training, user community
- Owned by: CSM

**Level 3: End Users**
- Title: Individual contributors, analysts, specialists
- Role: Daily product users, feature feedback source, adoption metrics
- Engagement: In-app guidance, training webinars, support interactions
- Owned by: Support team + automated engagement

### Multi-Threading Tactics

**Executive Access Strategies:**
- Request executive sponsor introduction during onboarding
- Offer exclusive executive briefings on industry trends
- Invite to advisory board or executive peer events
- Have your executives reach out to their executives (peer-to-peer)
- Use QBRs as the recurring executive touchpoint

**Expanding Within a Department:**
- Ask the champion: "Who else on your team would benefit from [feature]?"
- Offer team-wide training sessions
- Share usage data showing who is and is not using the product
- Create team-level challenges or adoption campaigns

**Expanding Across Departments:**
- Ask the champion: "Are there other teams facing [problem we solve]?"
- Present cross-departmental use cases during QBRs
- Offer free pilot or trial for adjacent departments
- Identify organizational initiatives that span departments

## Section 3: Stakeholder Mapping

### The Stakeholder Map

A stakeholder map documents every relevant contact at the customer, their role, influence, sentiment, and engagement level.

```
STAKEHOLDER MAP

Account: _______________
Last Updated: _______________
CSM: _______________

| Name | Title | Role | Influence | Sentiment | Engagement | Last Contact |
|------|-------|------|-----------|-----------|------------|-------------|
|      |       |      |           |           |            |             |

ROLE LEGEND:
  ES = Executive Sponsor    CH = Champion
  DM = Decision Maker       IN = Influencer
  EU = End User             BL = Blocker
  TC = Technical Contact    FI = Finance/Procurement

INFLUENCE LEGEND:
  High / Medium / Low

SENTIMENT LEGEND:
  Advocate / Supporter / Neutral / Skeptic / Detractor

ENGAGEMENT LEGEND:
  Active / Occasional / Disengaged / Unknown
```

### Stakeholder Influence-Sentiment Matrix

Plot stakeholders on a 2x2 matrix to prioritize engagement:

```
                    Positive Sentiment          Negative Sentiment

High Influence      LEVERAGE                    CONVERT
                    (These are your champions.  (These can block renewal.
                     Deepen relationship.        Prioritize engagement.
                     Ask for advocacy.)          Understand concerns.)

Low Influence       NURTURE                     MONITOR
                    (Future champions.           (Low immediate risk.
                     Build relationship.          Track sentiment.
                     Enable their success.)       Address if escalating.)
```

### Stakeholder Map Maintenance

- Update the stakeholder map quarterly (at minimum, before every QBR)
- When a key contact departs: Immediately update map, identify successor, request introduction
- When a new stakeholder is identified: Research their role and priorities, plan introduction
- When sentiment shifts: Document the cause, update engagement strategy
- Review stakeholder coverage gaps: Are there levels or functions with no contacts?

## Section 4: Relationship Health Measurement

### Relationship Health Indicators

Relationship health is distinct from product health (usage metrics) and outcome health (business results). It measures the quality and depth of the human connection between organizations.

| Indicator | Healthy Signal | At-Risk Signal |
|-----------|---------------|----------------|
| Response time | Customer responds to emails within 48 hours | Responses delayed >5 days or no response |
| Meeting attendance | Key stakeholders attend scheduled calls | No-shows, frequent rescheduling |
| Executive access | Executive sponsor participates in QBRs | Executive sponsor unavailable or delegating |
| Proactive communication | Customer reaches out with questions, ideas | Communication is always vendor-initiated |
| Referral willingness | Customer offers references, testimonials | Customer declines or avoids advocacy requests |
| Feedback quality | Customer provides detailed, constructive feedback | Customer gives vague or dismissive responses |
| Multi-thread depth | 5+ engaged contacts across 3+ levels | 1-2 contacts, single level |
| Transparency | Customer shares business context and challenges | Customer is guarded about plans and priorities |

### Relationship Health Score

```
Relationship Health Score (0-100)

Components:
  Executive Engagement (25 points)
    - Executive sponsor participates in QBR: 15 points
    - Executive responds to direct outreach: 10 points

  Champion Strength (25 points)
    - Active champion identified and engaged: 15 points
    - Champion advocates internally for product: 10 points

  Multi-Thread Depth (25 points)
    - Minimum viable contacts met for segment: 15 points
    - Contacts across 2+ organizational levels: 10 points

  Communication Quality (25 points)
    - Customer responds within 48 hours: 10 points
    - Customer initiates communication: 10 points
    - Meeting attendance >80%: 5 points

Scoring:
  80-100: Strong relationship (advocate potential)
  60-79:  Healthy relationship (maintain cadence)
  40-59:  Weakening relationship (increase engagement)
  0-39:   At-risk relationship (escalate immediately)
```

### Relationship Recovery Playbook

When relationship health drops below 60:

**Step 1: Diagnose (Week 1)**
- Review all touchpoint history for the past 90 days
- Identify the inflection point where engagement declined
- Check for champion departure, organizational changes, or negative events
- Assess whether the issue is relationship, product, or business driven

**Step 2: Re-Engage (Week 2)**
- If champion departed: Request introduction to successor from remaining contacts
- If disengagement is gradual: Send a personalized value message (not a generic check-in)
- If caused by a negative event: Acknowledge the issue directly and present remediation
- If executive access lost: Have your executive reach out to their executive

**Step 3: Rebuild (Weeks 3-8)**
- Increase touchpoint frequency temporarily (weekly for 4-6 weeks)
- Deliver a specific, tangible value-add (insight, optimization, training)
- Schedule an in-person or video meeting (higher engagement than email)
- Rebuild the stakeholder map with current contacts and priorities

**Step 4: Stabilize (Weeks 8-12)**
- Return to standard cadence once engagement normalizes
- Confirm multi-thread depth meets minimum requirements
- Conduct a mini-QBR to reset the success plan
- Document learnings in the account history

## Section 5: Touchpoint Effectiveness Measurement

### Touchpoint ROI Framework

Not all touchpoints deliver equal value. Measure effectiveness to optimize the cadence.

| Metric | Definition | Target |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| Touchpoint completion rate | Scheduled touchpoints that actually occur | 85%+ |
| Customer-initiated touchpoints | % of touchpoints initiated by the customer | 20%+ (signals engagement) |
| Touchpoint-to-action rate | % of touchpoints that produce action items | 60%+ |
| Time-to-response | Average time for customer to respond to outreach | <48 hours |
| Touchpoint satisfaction | Post-interaction survey score | 4.5/5+ |
| Engagement trend | Is touchpoint participation increasing or declining? | Stable or increasing |

### Cadence Optimization

Review touchpoint effectiveness quarterly:
- Which touchpoint types generate the most value? (Increase frequency)
- Which touchpoints have low attendance or engagement? (Reduce or redesign)
- Are customers requesting more or fewer touchpoints? (Calibrate to preference)
- What is the correlation between touchpoint completion and renewal rate? (Validate model)

## Summary

Touchpoint strategy is the operational backbone of customer success engagement. Cadence design by segment ensures that every customer receives appropriate attention—from fully automated tech-touch for SMB accounts to weekly strategic engagement for enterprise accounts. Multi-threading protects against single-point-of-failure risk by building relationships across multiple levels and functions. Stakeholder mapping provides the situational awareness needed to navigate complex customer organizations. Relationship health measurement quantifies the quality of the human connection beyond product usage metrics. The Customer Success Brain designs touchpoint strategies that maximize relationship value per unit of engagement effort, ensuring that every interaction advances the customer toward their desired outcomes and deepens the partnership that drives retention and expansion.
