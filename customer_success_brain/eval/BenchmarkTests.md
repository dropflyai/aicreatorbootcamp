# Customer Success Brain — Benchmark Tests (Authoritative)

These benchmark scenarios test the CS Brain's ability to diagnose, design,
execute, and optimize customer success programs. Each scenario must be answered
with rigor, specificity, and empathy for the customer.

Vague answers fail. Template responses fail. "Every customer is different" without follow-up fails.

---

## HOW TO USE THESE BENCHMARKS

1. Present a scenario to the CS Brain
2. Evaluate the response against the provided evaluation criteria
3. Score each response from 1-5 using the scoring rubric
4. A passing score is 4+ on every scenario
5. Any scenario scoring <3 indicates a critical knowledge gap

### Scoring Rubric

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 5 | Expert-level response. Specific, empathetic, actionable, considers stakeholder dynamics |
| 4 | Strong response. Specific and actionable with minor gaps |
| 3 | Adequate response. Directionally correct but lacks specificity or nuance |
| 2 | Weak response. Generic, template-driven, or missing key considerations |
| 1 | Failing response. Incorrect, harmful to customer relationship, or pure theory |

## SCENARIO 1: BROKEN HEALTH SCORE

**Prompt:**
"Your health score shows green for a customer, but they just posted a negative review on G2 saying 'Worst vendor experience of my career.' What happened? Fix the model."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it acknowledge the model failure honestly?
- Does it investigate what the health score was measuring vs what it missed?
- Does it propose immediate customer outreach (not just model fixes)?
- Does it identify specific signal gaps?
- Does it propose both short-term and long-term fixes?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Immediate response: Contact the customer within 24 hours, not to defend but to listen
2. Root cause on the model: What signals were green? (Usage might be high because they are stuck, not because they are happy)
3. Signal gaps: Sentiment not captured, relationship depth not measured, subjective satisfaction not surveyed
4. Specific model fixes:
   - Add NPS/CSAT as a health score component
   - Add support ticket sentiment analysis
   - Add engagement quality metrics (not just quantity)
   - Add relationship breadth (single-threaded = risk)
5. Validation: back-test against other churned accounts
6. Process fix: regular gut-check from CSM against automated score

## SCENARIO 2: ENTERPRISE ONBOARDING

**Prompt:**
"Design an onboarding program for an enterprise customer with 500 users across 3 departments, 3 executive stakeholders, and a 90-day go-live target. The product is a project management platform."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it propose a phased approach with clear milestones?
- Does it address multi-stakeholder complexity?
- Does it account for change management (500 users is an adoption challenge)?
- Does it define success metrics for each phase?
- Does it include a rollback plan if adoption stalls?
- Does it differentiate between technical setup and user adoption?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Pre-onboarding (Days 0-7):
   - Sales-to-CS handoff with complete context
   - Stakeholder mapping: champions, sponsors, detractors
   - Technical requirements assessment
   - Success criteria agreed with each executive stakeholder
   - Internal kickoff (CS team alignment)
2. Phase 1 - Foundation (Days 7-30):
   - Technical setup and integration
   - Admin training for each department
   - Configuration for each department's workflow
   - First department pilot (50-100 users)
   - Weekly check-ins with project sponsor
3. Phase 2 - Expansion (Days 30-60):
   - Roll out to remaining departments
   - Department-specific training sessions
   - Change management support (internal comms, quick-start guides)
   - Usage monitoring by department
   - Bi-weekly executive sponsor updates
4. Phase 3 - Optimization (Days 60-90):
   - Full deployment verified
   - Adoption metrics reviewed per department
   - Advanced training for power users
   - Executive business review at Day 90
   - Transition from onboarding to ongoing CS
5. Success metrics at each phase
6. Risk mitigation plan (what if Department 2 resists?)
7. Escalation criteria and contacts

## SCENARIO 3: NRR IMPROVEMENT PLAN

**Prompt:**
"NRR is 95%. Break down the components and create a plan to get to 115% within 12 months."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it decompose NRR into its components (expansion, contraction, churn)?
- Does it identify which component has the most leverage?
- Does it create a realistic plan (going from 95% to 115% is a major shift)?
- Does it address both reducing churn AND increasing expansion?
- Does it include specific tactics with expected impact?
- Does it consider segment-level differences?

**Expected Elements:**
1. NRR decomposition:
   - Current state: 95% = Starting MRR + X% expansion - Y% contraction - Z% churn
   - Identify the exact numbers for each component
2. Gap analysis: need 20 percentage points of improvement
3. Strategy by component:
   - Churn reduction (e.g., from 8% to 5% = 3pp improvement)
   - Contraction reduction (e.g., from 5% to 3% = 2pp improvement)
   - Expansion increase (e.g., from 8% to 23% = 15pp improvement)
4. Specific tactics:
   - Churn: risk detection, save plays, health score improvement
   - Contraction: value demonstration, usage optimization, proactive outreach
   - Expansion: upsell playbooks, cross-sell programs, seat expansion automation
5. Timeline: quarterly milestones
6. Required investments (headcount, tools, programs)
7. Leading indicators to track monthly
8. Contingency plan if one lever underperforms

## SCENARIO 4: CHAMPION DEPARTURE

**Prompt:**
"Your biggest customer ($500K ARR, 18 months into a 3-year contract) just lost their executive sponsor and internal champion. Both left the company within 2 weeks. What do you do?"

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it treat this as a critical risk requiring immediate action?
- Does it propose a multi-step response plan with specific timelines?
- Does it address both relationship rebuilding and value re-establishment?
- Does it leverage the existing contract and documented value?
- Does it prepare for the worst while working toward the best?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Immediate (24-48 hours):
   - Activate critical risk playbook
   - Identify who the new decision-makers are (check LinkedIn, internal contacts)
   - Reach out to remaining contacts to understand the organizational change
   - Brief executive team internally (this is a $500K risk)
2. Week 1:
   - Secure meeting with new stakeholders
   - Prepare value summary: documented ROI, usage data, outcomes achieved
   - Bring your executive to the meeting (executive-to-executive)
   - Listen first: what are the new stakeholders' priorities?
3. Week 2-4:
   - Present value delivered to date (data-driven, not sales-pitch)
   - Understand new stakeholders' goals and how product aligns
   - Build new relationship map (identify new champion, new sponsor)
   - Offer a "re-onboarding" for new stakeholders
4. Month 2-3:
   - New success plan co-created with new stakeholders
   - Quick win: deliver visible value within 30 days
   - Multi-thread the relationship (never single-threaded again)
   - Regular check-ins at higher frequency until relationship stabilizes
5. Ongoing:
   - Monitor health score closely for 6 months
   - Document this as a risk pattern for proactive prevention

## SCENARIO 5: CUSTOMER DOWNGRADE REQUEST

**Prompt:**
"A $120K ARR customer (Mid-Market) asks to downgrade to the $48K plan at renewal. They say they are 'not using the advanced features.' Their usage data confirms this. How do you handle this?"

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it respect the customer's position (not dismissive)?
- Does it investigate WHY they are not using advanced features?
- Does it distinguish between "don't need" and "never learned how"?
- Does it propose value-driven retention rather than pressure tactics?
- Does it have a realistic outcome (some downgrades are correct)?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Acknowledge the request professionally, do not panic
2. Investigate: Why are advanced features not being used?
   - Were they trained on the features?
   - Did their use case change?
   - Did the champion who used those features leave?
   - Is there a technical blocker?
3. If "never learned": Offer targeted enablement
   - Free training sessions on advanced features
   - Dedicated CSM support for 30-day adoption sprint
   - ROI modeling: "If you use feature X, here is the value"
4. If "don't need": Explore alternatives before downgrade
   - Can a different advanced feature deliver value?
   - Would a custom configuration help?
   - Is there a mid-tier option between $48K and $120K?
5. If downgrade is the right answer: Do it gracefully
   - Process the downgrade without making the customer feel bad
   - Set up success plan on the new plan
   - Create expansion path for the future
6. Internal documentation: Why did we sell $120K if customer only needs $48K? (Sales feedback)

## SCENARIO 6: SCALED CS PROGRAM DESIGN

**Prompt:**
"You have 2,000 SMB customers ($3K-$15K ARR each) and 3 CSMs. Design a scaled CS program that maintains retention and identifies expansion opportunities without 1:1 coverage."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it acknowledge the math (2,000/3 = 667 accounts per CSM is impossible for 1:1)?
- Does it propose a tech-touch and segmented model?
- Does it define which touchpoints are automated vs human?
- Does it include triggers for human intervention?
- Does it measure effectiveness?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Segmentation:
   - Tier 1 ($10K-$15K): Light-touch CSM coverage (portfolio model, 200 accounts per CSM)
   - Tier 2 ($5K-$10K): Tech-touch with human escalation triggers
   - Tier 3 ($3K-$5K): Pure tech-touch with self-serve resources
2. Automated programs:
   - Onboarding email sequence (7-day, personalized by use case)
   - Usage-based triggers (celebration at milestones, intervention at drop-offs)
   - Health-based alerts (CSM intervenes only when health drops)
   - Renewal reminders (automated 90, 60, 30 days before)
   - NPS survey at regular intervals
3. Human touchpoints:
   - Onboarding kickoff call (Tier 1 only, or opt-in for Tier 2)
   - QBR (Tier 1 only, semi-annual)
   - Risk intervention (all tiers, triggered by health score)
   - Expansion conversation (triggered by usage signals)
4. Self-serve resources:
   - Knowledge base / help center
   - Community forum
   - Webinar series (product training, best practices)
   - In-app guides and tooltips
5. Metrics:
   - Retention by tier
   - NPS by tier
   - Time-to-value by tier
   - Expansion rate by tier
   - CSM time allocation analysis

## SCENARIO 7: MULTI-PRODUCT ADOPTION

**Prompt:**
"Your company just launched a second product. 500 existing customers have access to both products. Only 15% have adopted the second product after 3 months. Create an adoption strategy."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it diagnose WHY adoption is low before proposing solutions?
- Does it segment customers by likelihood to adopt?
- Does it propose both proactive and reactive adoption plays?
- Does it define what "adopted" means for the second product?
- Does it set realistic targets and timelines?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Define adoption: What does "adopted" mean? (Logged in once? Regular usage? Value derived?)
2. Diagnose the 15%: Who adopted and why? What do they have in common?
3. Diagnose the 85%: Why have they not adopted?
   - Awareness: Do they know it exists?
   - Relevance: Does it solve a problem they have?
   - Friction: Is the onboarding for product 2 too complex?
   - Capacity: Are they still learning product 1?
4. Segmentation:
   - High propensity: Similar use case to adopters, actively using product 1 features that connect to product 2
   - Medium propensity: Could benefit but need education
   - Low propensity: Product 2 is not relevant to their use case
5. Adoption plays:
   - Awareness campaign (in-app, email, CSM mention in check-ins)
   - Joint QBR: show how both products work together
   - Pilot program: offer guided onboarding for product 2 to high-propensity accounts
   - Integration showcase: demonstrate product 1 + product 2 workflows
6. Targets: 15% to 35% in 6 months (realistic, not 100%)
7. Metrics: adoption funnel (aware -> tried -> active -> value)

## SCENARIO 8: CS OPERATIONS BUILDOUT

**Prompt:**
"You are hired as the first CS Ops hire. The CS team is 12 CSMs, 2 managers, 1 VP. They are using spreadsheets for health scores and Salesforce for CRM. Build the CS Ops function from scratch."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it prioritize ruthlessly (cannot do everything at once)?
- Does it address the most painful gaps first?
- Does it propose a phased implementation plan?
- Does it consider change management for the CS team?
- Does it define success metrics for CS Ops itself?

**Expected Elements:**
1. First 30 days: Assess and quick wins
   - Audit current processes (what works, what is broken)
   - Interview every CSM (what is their biggest pain point?)
   - Document current metrics and reporting
   - Quick win: automate the most painful manual process
2. Days 30-60: Foundation
   - Implement CS platform (Gainsight, Totango, or similar)
   - Migrate health score from spreadsheet to platform
   - Define and document standard CS processes
   - Build core reporting dashboards
3. Days 60-90: Automation
   - Build automated health score with 4+ signal types
   - Create automated onboarding workflow
   - Set up risk alerts and escalation automation
   - Build renewal management workflow
4. Days 90-180: Optimization
   - Validate health score against churn data
   - Build expansion signal detection
   - Implement QBR automation and templates
   - Create reporting package for CS leadership and executives
5. Success metrics for CS Ops:
   - CSM time saved per week
   - Health score accuracy
   - Reporting turnaround time
   - Process adoption rate
   - Data quality improvement

## SCENARIO 9: EXECUTIVE ESCALATION MANAGEMENT

**Prompt:**
"A customer CEO emails your CEO directly: 'Your product has cost us $200K in lost productivity. If this is not resolved in 2 weeks, we are canceling our $300K contract.' The CSM says they had no idea there was a problem. Manage this."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it treat this as a dual crisis (customer relationship AND internal process failure)?
- Does it propose immediate, specific actions for the customer?
- Does it address the internal process failure (CSM had no visibility)?
- Does it define a resolution timeline that matches the 2-week demand?
- Does it prepare for both outcomes (save and lose)?

**Expected Elements:**
1. First 4 hours:
   - CEO-to-CEO acknowledgment (within 2 hours): "We hear you and we are on it"
   - Internal war room: VP CS, CSM, CSM manager, Support lead, Engineering if needed
   - Fact-finding: What happened? What is the $200K claim based on?
2. First 24 hours:
   - Executive-level call with customer (VP or C-level, not just CSM)
   - Listen: Understand the full scope of the problem
   - Do not defend or explain: acknowledge the impact
   - Commit to a resolution plan with daily updates
3. Week 1:
   - Dedicated resource assigned to resolution
   - Daily progress updates to customer
   - Tangible fixes delivered or demonstrable progress
   - Internal root cause analysis running in parallel
4. Week 2:
   - Resolution demonstrated with verification
   - Forward-looking plan to prevent recurrence
   - Executive-level relationship re-established
   - Concession or credit discussion if appropriate
5. Internal fixes:
   - Why did the CSM have no idea? Health score gap? Engagement gap?
   - What process changes prevent this from happening again?
   - Was this a product issue, a support issue, or a CS issue?
6. Post-resolution:
   - Account placed in high-touch monitoring for 6 months
   - Monthly executive check-ins for 1 quarter
   - Success plan rebuilt with customer

## SCENARIO 10: CUSTOMER SEGMENTATION REDESIGN

**Prompt:**
"Your CS team currently segments customers by ARR only (Enterprise >$100K, Mid-Market $25K-$100K, SMB <$25K). Propose a better segmentation model."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it explain why ARR-only segmentation is insufficient?
- Does it propose multiple dimensions for segmentation?
- Does it consider operational feasibility (can the team actually use this)?
- Does it show how the new segmentation changes resource allocation?
- Does it define what each segment gets differently?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Why ARR-only fails: A $90K customer with flat usage and no growth potential needs different treatment than a $90K customer with 10x expansion potential
2. Proposed dimensions:
   - ARR (current revenue)
   - Growth potential (expansion likelihood, whitespace)
   - Health/Risk (current health score)
   - Strategic value (logo value, industry influence, case study potential)
   - Complexity (technical complexity, stakeholder count)
3. Segmentation matrix (not just tiers):
   - High ARR + High potential = Strategic (full-touch, executive alignment)
   - High ARR + Low potential = Protect (retain, maintain, optimize cost-to-serve)
   - Low ARR + High potential = Invest (grow the account, treat as future strategic)
   - Low ARR + Low potential = Efficient (tech-touch, self-serve, scaled)
4. Resource allocation changes per segment
5. Service model per segment (touchpoints, QBR frequency, CSM ratio)
6. Migration plan from old to new segmentation
7. Metrics to track segmentation effectiveness

## SCENARIO 11: RENEWAL RISK MANAGEMENT

**Prompt:**
"You have 50 renewals coming up in Q2 totaling $2.5M ARR. 15 of them are flagged yellow or red. You have 6 weeks. Create a save plan."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it prioritize the 15 at-risk accounts by revenue and save probability?
- Does it create tiered action plans based on risk severity and ARR?
- Does it propose specific save tactics (not generic "improve relationship")?
- Does it set realistic expectations (will not save all 15)?
- Does it track progress weekly?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Prioritization: Stack-rank the 15 by ARR x save probability
2. Tier the response:
   - High ARR + saveable: Dedicated CSM focus, executive involvement, custom save plan
   - High ARR + unlikely to save: Begin wind-down prep, attempt save, prepare for loss
   - Low ARR + saveable: Scaled save play (offer, incentive, value demo)
   - Low ARR + unlikely to save: Accept the loss, focus resources on saveable accounts
3. Specific save tactics:
   - Usage-based: Show underutilized features, offer training
   - Price-based: Offer contract restructure (not discount) if price is the issue
   - Product-based: Escalate feature gaps to Product with urgency
   - Relationship-based: Executive outreach, listen to concerns
4. Weekly tracking: save pipeline with status updates
5. Expected outcomes: realistic prediction (save 8-10 of 15, for example)
6. Revenue at risk analysis: of the $2.5M, how much is truly at risk?
7. Lessons for Q3: how to prevent this many at-risk renewals

## SCENARIO 12: POST-CHURN ANALYSIS

**Prompt:**
"A $200K ARR enterprise customer just churned after 2 years. They moved to a competitor. Conduct the post-mortem."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it follow a structured post-mortem framework?
- Does it gather data from multiple sources (not just the CSM's perspective)?
- Does it identify systemic issues (not just account-specific)?
- Does it produce actionable recommendations?
- Does it ensure the learning propagates to other teams?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Data gathering:
   - Exit interview with customer (what was the real reason?)
   - CSM debrief (what happened from our side?)
   - Health score history (did we see this coming?)
   - Support ticket history (unresolved issues?)
   - QBR notes (were there warning signs?)
   - Usage data trend (when did engagement start declining?)
   - Competitor analysis (what did they switch to and why?)
2. Timeline construction: Map the journey from healthy to churned
3. Root cause analysis:
   - Was it preventable? (If yes, what would have prevented it?)
   - Was it predicted? (If no, why did the health score miss it?)
   - Was it a product gap, relationship failure, or external factor?
4. Systemic findings:
   - Does this pattern exist in other accounts?
   - What process change would prevent this?
   - What product feedback should go to the roadmap?
5. Action items with owners and deadlines
6. Distribution: Share findings with CS, Product, Sales, and Leadership

## SCENARIO 13: CUSTOMER ADVISORY BOARD

**Prompt:**
"Design a Customer Advisory Board program for your SaaS company. You have 200 customers ranging from $10K to $500K ARR."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it define the purpose of the CAB beyond "get feedback"?
- Does it define selection criteria for members?
- Does it structure the meetings for maximum value exchange?
- Does it plan for logistics and sustainability?
- Does it define how CAB input influences decisions?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Purpose: Strategic input on roadmap, product validation, relationship deepening
2. Selection criteria: 10-15 members representing different segments, industries, company sizes, tenure
3. Member value proposition: Why would a customer join? (Influence, networking, early access)
4. Meeting structure: Semi-annual in-person, quarterly virtual
5. Agenda framework: Strategic topics, not product demos
6. Feedback loop: How CAB input gets to Product and back to members
7. Governance: Terms, rotation, expectations
8. Success metrics: Retention of CAB members, NPS of CAB participants, product input adopted

## SCENARIO 14: CS METRICS DASHBOARD

**Prompt:**
"Build the definitive CS metrics dashboard. What metrics, what views, what drill-downs, and who is the audience for each?"

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it organize metrics by audience (CSM, Manager, VP, Board)?
- Does it include both leading and lagging indicators?
- Does it define how each metric is calculated?
- Does it include drill-down capability?
- Does it avoid dashboard bloat (too many metrics)?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Executive dashboard (5-7 metrics): GRR, NRR, NPS, health score distribution, at-risk ARR, expansion pipeline
2. CS Manager dashboard: Team-level retention, onboarding completion, QBR completion, save rate, CSM activity
3. CSM dashboard: My portfolio health, upcoming renewals, at-risk accounts, tasks, engagement history
4. Drill-down examples: Click on NRR to see by segment, by CSM, by cohort
5. Alerting: Which metrics trigger alerts and to whom?
6. Update frequency: Real-time, daily, weekly, monthly by metric

## SCENARIO 15: VALUE REALIZATION FRAMEWORK

**Prompt:**
"A customer says 'We cannot see the ROI of your product.' They are paying $150K/year and their contract renews in 4 months. Design a value realization program."

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it treat this as urgent given the renewal timeline?
- Does it propose measuring ROI in the customer's terms (not vendor's)?
- Does it gather baseline data before measuring impact?
- Does it present value in a compelling, executive-friendly format?
- Does it connect value to renewal and expansion?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Urgency: 4 months to demonstrate value before renewal
2. Discovery: What value did they expect when they bought? What KPIs matter to them?
3. Value framework:
   - Time saved (hours x hourly rate)
   - Revenue enabled (deals closed, pipeline generated)
   - Cost avoided (reduced headcount, fewer errors)
   - Risk reduced (compliance, security incidents prevented)
4. Data gathering: Work WITH the customer to measure
5. Value report: Executive-friendly document showing ROI
6. Timeline: Month 1 (baseline), Month 2 (measure), Month 3 (present), Month 4 (renewal conversation with value data)
7. Even if ROI is unclear: show usage data, benchmark against peers, qualitative testimonials from their team

## SCENARIO 16: HANDLING SIMULTANEOUS CRISES

**Prompt:**
"On Monday morning, you discover: (1) Your #1 customer ($500K ARR) is evaluating a competitor, (2) 3 mid-market customers reported the same critical bug, (3) A CSM just resigned with 40 accounts, and (4) NPS scores dropped 15 points. How do you triage and respond?"

**Evaluation Criteria:**
- Does it prioritize ruthlessly (cannot fix everything simultaneously)?
- Does it assign ownership to each crisis?
- Does it propose parallel workstreams where possible?
- Does it communicate to leadership with appropriate urgency?
- Does it maintain composure and structure under pressure?

**Expected Elements:**
1. Triage by urgency and impact:
   - Priority 1: #1 customer evaluating competitor ($500K at risk, irreversible if lost)
   - Priority 2: Critical bug affecting 3 customers (revenue risk + reputation)
   - Priority 3: CSM resignation (40 accounts need coverage immediately)
   - Priority 4: NPS drop (important but not immediate-action crisis)
2. Parallel workstreams:
   - VP handles #1 customer personally (today)
   - CS Manager escalates bug to Engineering with customer impact data (today)
   - CS Ops begins account redistribution for departed CSM (today)
   - NPS analysis scheduled for later this week
3. Communication:
   - Brief CEO/CRO on #1 customer risk within 2 hours
   - Brief Engineering lead on bug with customer context
   - Brief CS team on CSM departure and interim coverage plan
4. Day 1 goals for each crisis
5. Week 1 action plan for each crisis
6. Systemic question: are these crises connected? (Bug causes NPS drop? Bad experience causes competitor evaluation?)
