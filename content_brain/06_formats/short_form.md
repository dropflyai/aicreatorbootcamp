# Short-Form Content — Social Copy, Ad Copy, Subject Lines, Push Notifications, Hooks

## Overview

Short-form content operates under extreme constraint: limited character
counts, fractional attention windows, and high competition for the
reader's next action. A tweet has 280 characters. A push notification
has approximately 50 visible characters on a lock screen. An email
subject line has 41 characters before mobile truncation. A Facebook ad
headline has 40 characters of optimal length. These constraints demand
a different writing discipline than long-form—every word must earn its
place, every sentence must advance a single objective, and every piece
must be testable. This module covers the craft and science of writing
short-form content that stops the scroll, earns the click, and drives
measurable action.

## Section 1: Social Copy

### Platform-Specific Constraints and Conventions

Each social platform has distinct character limits, audience expectations,
and algorithmic preferences.

**LinkedIn**
- Character limit: 3,000 (but only ~210 visible before "see more")
- Optimal length: 1,200–1,500 characters for engagement
- Tone: Professional but conversational, avoid corporate speak
- Format: Line breaks between every 1–2 sentences (mobile readability)
- Hook: First two lines must compel "see more" click
- Algorithm: Rewards dwell time, comments, and shares over reactions

**Twitter/X**
- Character limit: 280 (premium: 25,000 for long posts)
- Optimal length: 71–100 characters for highest engagement (Buddy Media)
- Tone: Concise, opinionated, conversational
- Format: One idea per tweet; threads for depth (2–7 tweets optimal)
- Hook: Front-load the most provocative or useful statement
- Algorithm: Rewards replies, quote tweets, and bookmark-to-impression

**Instagram**
- Caption limit: 2,200 characters (~330 words)
- Optimal length: 138–150 characters for feed posts, longer for carousels
- Tone: Authentic, visual-first (caption supports the image)
- Format: First line is the hook (truncated at ~125 characters)
- Algorithm: Rewards saves, shares to DMs, and comment length

**TikTok**
- Caption limit: 4,000 characters
- Optimal length: Under 150 characters (text competes with video)
- Tone: Casual, trend-aware, hook-driven
- Format: Text overlay + spoken hook in first 1–3 seconds is primary
- Algorithm: Rewards watch time, completion rate, shares

### Social Copy Writing Formulas

**The Hook-Value-CTA Formula**
1. Hook: Stop the scroll with a surprising fact, question, or bold claim
2. Value: Deliver the insight, story, or information promised by the hook
3. CTA: Tell the reader what to do next (comment, share, click, save)

**The PAS Formula (Problem-Agitate-Solve)**
1. Problem: Name the pain point in the reader's own language
2. Agitate: Amplify the consequences of not solving it
3. Solve: Present the solution (your content, product, or insight)

**The Open Loop Formula**
Create curiosity gaps that can only be closed by reading further.
- "Most marketers get this wrong (and waste 40% of their budget)."
- "I spent 3 years building the wrong product. Here's what I wish..."
- "The #1 reason startups fail isn't what you think."

### Social Copy Testing

**A/B Testing Variables**
- Hook phrasing (question vs. statement vs. statistic)
- CTA type (ask vs. tell vs. suggest)
- Post length (short vs. medium vs. long)
- Emoji usage (none vs. strategic vs. heavy)
- Formatting (line breaks vs. paragraphs vs. bullet points)

**Metrics by Platform**
| Platform | Primary Metric | Secondary Metric |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|
| LinkedIn | Comments | Click-through rate |
| Twitter/X | Replies | Impressions per follower |
| Instagram | Saves | Shares to DMs |
| TikTok | Watch time % | Shares |

## Section 2: Ad Copy

### Direct Response Copywriting Principles

Ad copy is the purest form of persuasive writing. Every word must
contribute to a single goal: getting the reader to take the next action.

**David Ogilvy's Five Rules for Ad Copy**
1. The headline is 80% of the ad's value (5x more people read headlines
   than body copy)
2. Specificity sells (say "37% increase" not "significant improvement")
3. Promise a benefit (not a feature—benefits are what the reader gets)
4. Include the brand name in the headline (it will be the only thing
   most people read)
5. Test, test, test (never assume—let the market decide)

**Claude Hopkins' Scientific Advertising Principles**
1. Advertising is salesmanship in print (now salesmanship in pixels)
2. The only purpose of an ad is to make a sale (or advance toward one)
3. Use specific facts (people respond to specificity, not generality)
4. Tell the full story (in the space you have, leave nothing important
   unsaid)
5. Track results (unmeasured advertising is wasted advertising)

### Ad Copy Frameworks by Platform

**Google Search Ads**
- Headline 1 (30 chars): Include primary keyword + benefit
- Headline 2 (30 chars): Differentiate or add urgency
- Headline 3 (30 chars): Brand or trust signal
- Description 1 (90 chars): Expand on the benefit, include CTA
- Description 2 (90 chars): Social proof or secondary benefit

**Facebook/Instagram Ads**
- Primary text: First 125 characters critical (above-fold on mobile)
- Headline: 40 characters optimal (appears below image/video)
- Description: 30 characters (often truncated or hidden)
- Structure: Hook (pain/desire) → Bridge (how) → CTA (next step)

**LinkedIn Ads**
- Introductory text: 150 characters visible (600 max)
- Headline: 70 characters max
- Tone: Professional, data-driven, avoid clickbait
- Structure: Industry challenge → Solution insight → Learn more CTA

### Ad Copy Variants to Test

For every ad campaign, create a minimum of 5 copy variants:

**Variant 1: Pain-Point Lead**
"Tired of [problem]? [Product] eliminates [specific pain] so you can
[desired outcome]. Start free."

**Variant 2: Benefit Lead**
"[Quantified benefit] with [Product]. Join [number] [audience] who
already [achieved outcome]."

**Variant 3: Social Proof Lead**
"[Customer name] grew [metric] by [%] using [Product]. See how →"

**Variant 4: Urgency Lead**
"[Limited offer]. Get [benefit] before [deadline]. [Number] spots left."

**Variant 5: Question Lead**
"What if you could [desired outcome] in [timeframe]? [Product] makes
it possible. Try free."

## Section 3: Email Subject Lines

### Subject Line Science

The email subject line is the most consequential piece of short-form
copy. It determines whether the email is opened (30–40% of the email's
total value) or ignored (joining the 75% of emails never opened).

**Optimal Length**
- Desktop: 40–60 characters visible (41 character average display)
- Mobile: 30–40 characters visible (33 character average display)
- Recommendation: Front-load the key message in the first 30 characters

**Subject Line Formulas**

| Formula | Example | When to Use |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| Number + Benefit | "5 ways to cut churn by 20%" | Newsletters, tips |
| Question | "Is your landing page losing leads?" | Re-engagement |
| How-To | "How to write subject lines that convert" | Educational |
| Urgency | "Last chance: 50% off ends tonight" | Promotions |
| Curiosity Gap | "The metric we stopped tracking (and why)" | Thought leadership |
| Personal | "[Name], your Q4 strategy template is ready" | Personalized nurture |
| Social Proof | "Why 10,000 marketers read this every week" | Subscription |
| Announcement | "New: AI-powered content scoring is here" | Product launch |

### Preview Text Optimization

Preview text (the snippet visible after the subject line in inbox views)
is the second most important email copy element. It should extend the
subject line's promise, not repeat it.

- Subject: "Your content audit results are in"
- Bad preview: "Your content audit results are in. Click to view."
- Good preview: "3 pages are hurting your rankings. Here's the fix."

**Technical Implementation**
Preview text is controlled by a hidden `<div>` or `<span>` at the top of
the email HTML body. Without explicit preview text, email clients pull
the first visible text—often "View in browser" or navigation links.

### Subject Line Testing Protocol

- A/B test subject lines on 20% of the list, send winner to remaining 80%
- Test one variable at a time (length, format, personalization, emoji)
- Minimum sample: 1,000 recipients per variant for statistical validity
- Primary metric: Open rate (with caveats—Apple MPP inflates iOS opens)
- Secondary metric: Click-to-open rate (more reliable than open rate)
- Time window: 2–4 hours before declaring winner

## Section 4: Push Notifications

### Push Notification Constraints

Push notifications are the most constrained content format:
- iOS lock screen: ~50 characters visible (110 max)
- Android lock screen: ~60 characters visible (varies by device)
- Web push: 50 characters title, 120 characters body
- User tolerance: Low (users disable push after 3–5 irrelevant sends)

### Push Notification Copy Principles

**Immediate Value**
Every push must justify the interruption. The user has given permission
to be interrupted—violating that trust with low-value messages leads to
opt-out or app deletion.

**Action Specificity**
The push must tell the user exactly what to do and why it matters now.
- Bad: "Check out our new features!"
- Good: "Your report is ready. 3 insights need your attention."

**Personalization**
Personalized push notifications see 4x higher open rates than generic
broadcasts. Use:
- Name personalization (when natural, not forced)
- Behavioral triggers (based on user actions or inactions)
- Contextual relevance (time, location, user state)

### Push Notification Types and Templates

**Transactional Push**
Triggered by user action. Highest engagement, highest tolerance.
- "Your order has shipped. Track delivery →"
- "Payment of $49.99 confirmed. View receipt →"

**Behavioral Push**
Triggered by user behavior patterns. Medium engagement.
- "You left 3 items in your cart. Complete checkout before they sell out."
- "You're 2 days away from your streak. Open the app to keep it alive."

**Re-Engagement Push**
Triggered by inactivity. Lower engagement, use sparingly.
- "We've added 12 new templates since your last visit. Take a look →"
- "[Name], your data insights are waiting. Updated 3 hours ago."

**Promotional Push**
Broadcast to segments. Lowest tolerance. Must be high-value.
- "Flash sale: 40% off annual plans. 24 hours only."
- "New feature: AI content scoring. Try it now →"

## Section 5: Hooks

### The Psychology of Hooks

A hook is the opening element of any content piece—the first sentence,
first 3 seconds, first frame—that captures attention and creates a
reason to continue. Hooks exploit cognitive biases:

**Curiosity Gap (Loewenstein, 1994)**
The brain experiences discomfort when it perceives a gap between what it
knows and what it wants to know. Hooks that create this gap compel
continued consumption to close it.

**Pattern Interrupt**
The brain habituates to expected stimuli and alerts to unexpected ones.
Hooks that break expected patterns (contrarian statements, surprising
data, unexpected juxtapositions) trigger attention.

**Self-Relevance**
The brain prioritizes information relevant to the self. Hooks that
address the reader directly ("You're probably making this mistake") or
reference their identity group trigger engagement.

### Hook Formulas

**The Contrarian Hook**
"Everything you've been told about [topic] is wrong."
"The best [professionals] don't [commonly assumed practice]."

**The Data Hook**
"[Specific number]% of [audience] fail at [task]. Here's why."
"We analyzed [large number] [things]. The results surprised us."

**The Story Hook**
"In 2019, I lost $200,000 because of a single email."
"She was 23, broke, and about to build a $50M company."

**The Question Hook**
"What would you do with 10 extra hours per week?"
"Why do 73% of content marketers feel their strategy is failing?"

**The How-To Hook**
"How to [achieve desired outcome] without [common pain point]."
"The exact process I used to [impressive result] in [timeframe]."

**The Warning Hook**
"Stop doing [common practice] immediately. Here's why."
"If you're still [outdated approach], you're leaving money on the table."

### Testing Hook Effectiveness

**Scroll-Stop Rate**
For social and video: What percentage of users stop scrolling when
they encounter your content? Measured by impression-to-engagement ratio.

**Read-Through Rate**
For blog posts and emails: What percentage of users who read the hook
continue to the next section? Measured by scroll depth analytics.

**Thumb-Stop Ratio**
For video: What percentage of users watch past the first 3 seconds?
Platform analytics provide this directly.

## Summary

Short-form content is constrained writing where every character carries
weight. Social copy must stop the scroll and earn engagement within
platform-specific rules. Ad copy must persuade and convert within
character limits using time-tested direct response principles. Email
subject lines must earn the open in 30–40 visible characters. Push
notifications must justify the interruption in 50 characters or risk
permanent opt-out. Hooks must exploit cognitive biases—curiosity gaps,
pattern interrupts, and self-relevance—to capture attention in the
first moment of contact. Mastery of short-form is the discipline of
saying more with less, tested relentlessly, and optimized by data.
