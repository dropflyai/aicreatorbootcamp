# Multimedia Content — Video Scripts, Podcast Notes, Webinar Content, Infographics, Repurposing 1-to-10

## Overview

Multimedia content extends a brand's reach beyond text into the formats
where modern audiences increasingly consume information: video, audio,
live presentations, and visual data stories. Each format has distinct
production requirements, audience expectations, and engagement
characteristics. More critically, multimedia content enables the 1-to-10
repurposing strategy—creating one substantive piece of content and
transforming it into ten or more derivative assets across formats and
channels. This module covers the craft of writing for multimedia formats
and the operational framework for content repurposing at scale.

## Section 1: Video Scripts

### Video Content Categories

**Educational/Tutorial Videos**
- Duration: 5–15 minutes (YouTube optimal), 60–90 seconds (social)
- Structure: Hook → Context → Step-by-step → Summary → CTA
- Script density: ~150 words per minute of video
- Tone: Instructional, clear, conversational authority

**Thought Leadership Videos**
- Duration: 3–8 minutes (LinkedIn), 10–20 minutes (YouTube)
- Structure: Thesis → Supporting arguments → Implications → CTA
- Script density: ~130 words per minute (slower pacing for emphasis)
- Tone: Authoritative, opinionated, personal

**Product/Demo Videos**
- Duration: 60–120 seconds (landing page), 3–5 minutes (full demo)
- Structure: Problem → Solution → Features in action → Social proof → CTA
- Script density: ~140 words per minute
- Tone: Benefit-focused, energetic, specific

**Customer Story Videos**
- Duration: 2–4 minutes
- Structure: Challenge → Discovery → Implementation → Results → Quote
- Script density: Interview-based (not fully scripted)
- Tone: Authentic, relatable, human

### Video Script Structure

```
[COLD OPEN — 0:00-0:10]
Hook statement or provocative question. Must create curiosity
or promise value within the first 3 seconds.

[TITLE/INTRO — 0:10-0:30]
Brief context: who you are, what this video covers, what the
viewer will learn or gain.

[SECTION 1 — 0:30-3:00]
First main teaching point or argument.
- Visual direction: [describe what should appear on screen]
- B-roll suggestion: [relevant footage or graphics]
- Key callout: [text overlay or highlighted statistic]

[SECTION 2 — 3:00-6:00]
Second main teaching point or argument.
[same substructure as Section 1]

[SECTION 3 — 6:00-9:00]
Third main teaching point or argument.
[same substructure as Section 1]

[SUMMARY — 9:00-9:30]
Recap the three key takeaways in one sentence each.

[CTA — 9:30-10:00]
Tell the viewer exactly what to do next: subscribe, download,
visit a URL, comment with their experience.
```

### Video Script Writing Rules

**Write for the Ear, Not the Eye**
Video scripts must sound natural when spoken. Read every script aloud
before finalizing. Replace written constructions with spoken ones:
- Written: "It is important to note that..."
- Spoken: "Here's the thing—"
- Written: "In the following section, we will examine..."
- Spoken: "Next, let's look at..."

**Front-Load Value**
YouTube's algorithm heavily weights watch time. Viewers who do not
receive value in the first 30 seconds will leave. State the benefit
immediately: "By the end of this video, you'll know exactly how to
[specific outcome]."

**Signal Transitions**
Without visual page breaks, video needs verbal transitions:
- "Now, here's where it gets interesting..."
- "The second thing you need to know is..."
- "But here's the mistake most people make..."

**Include Visual Directions**
A script without visual direction forces the editor to guess. Include
stage directions: [show screenshot], [cut to B-roll of dashboard],
[display statistic as text overlay], [switch to screen recording].

## Section 2: Podcast Notes and Show Planning

### Podcast Content Types

**Interview Podcasts**
- Preparation: Research guest background, identify 5–7 core questions,
  prepare follow-up angles for anticipated responses
- Notes format: Question outline with context notes (not a rigid script)
- Host responsibility: Active listening, follow unexpected threads,
  summarize key points for the audience

**Solo/Monologue Podcasts**
- Preparation: Full outline with key talking points and transitions
- Notes format: Detailed bullet points (not a word-for-word script,
  which sounds stilted when read aloud)
- Structure: Hook → Thesis → 3–5 main points → Summary → CTA

**Panel/Roundtable Podcasts**
- Preparation: Define discussion topics, assign conversation starters,
  prepare moderator transitions and time markers
- Notes format: Topic guide with time allocations and discussion prompts
- Moderator responsibility: Ensure balanced airtime, redirect tangents,
  synthesize diverse viewpoints

### Podcast Show Notes Template

```
Episode [Number]: [Title]

Guest: [Name, Title, Company]
Published: [Date]
Duration: [Minutes]

SUMMARY
[2-3 sentence overview of what this episode covers and why
the listener should care.]

KEY TAKEAWAYS
1. [First major insight or actionable advice]
2. [Second major insight]
3. [Third major insight]

TIMESTAMPS
[00:00] Introduction and guest background
[03:15] [Topic 1 title]
[12:40] [Topic 2 title]
[22:00] [Topic 3 title]
[31:45] Rapid-fire questions
[35:20] Where to find [Guest] and closing

RESOURCES MENTIONED
- [Book/tool/article title] — [URL]
- [Book/tool/article title] — [URL]

TRANSCRIPT
[Full transcript with speaker labels, optimized for readability
and SEO. Edited for clarity—"ums" and false starts removed.]
```

### Podcast SEO Strategy

Podcasts are audio-first but discovered through text. SEO for podcasts
requires:
- Keyword-optimized episode titles (front-load the topic)
- Detailed show notes with target keywords (published as blog posts)
- Full transcripts (massive long-tail keyword coverage)
- Schema markup (PodcastEpisode, PodcastSeries types)
- Chapter markers with descriptive titles (Apple Podcasts, Spotify)

## Section 3: Webinar Content

### Webinar Structure

**Duration**: 45–60 minutes (30 minutes content, 15–30 minutes Q&A)
**Attendee Attention Curve**: Peaks at 5 minutes, drops at 15 minutes,
recovers with interaction, drops sharply after 45 minutes.

```
[0:00-3:00] Welcome and Housekeeping
- Acknowledge attendees, explain Q&A process
- Set expectations for what they will learn

[3:00-5:00] Speaker Introduction
- Credibility statement (not a full bio—highlight relevant expertise)
- Personal connection to the topic

[5:00-10:00] Problem Framing
- Define the challenge the audience faces
- Use data to quantify the problem's impact
- Create urgency for the solution

[10:00-30:00] Core Content (3 Sections)
- Section 1 [10:00-16:00]: First teaching point with example
  → Interactive element: Poll question at 16:00
- Section 2 [16:00-23:00]: Second teaching point with case study
  → Interactive element: Chat question at 23:00
- Section 3 [23:00-30:00]: Third teaching point with framework
  → Interactive element: Quiz or assessment at 30:00

[30:00-35:00] Summary and Framework Recap
- Visual summary slide of all key points
- Downloadable resource offer (slides, template, checklist)

[35:00-37:00] Soft CTA
- Brief mention of relevant product/service that helps with the topic
- Not a hard sell—frame as "if you want to go deeper"

[37:00-55:00] Q&A
- Pre-seeded questions for slow starts
- Prioritize questions that reinforce key points

[55:00-60:00] Close
- Final CTA (next webinar, demo, resource download)
- Thank attendees, provide contact information
```

### Webinar Slide Writing

**One Idea Per Slide**
Slides are visual aids, not documents. Each slide should communicate
a single concept that the speaker elaborates verbally.

**The 6-6-6 Rule**
- Maximum 6 bullet points per slide
- Maximum 6 words per bullet point
- Maximum 6 text-heavy slides in a row before a visual break

**Slide Types for Engagement**
- Data slides: One statistic per slide, large font, minimal annotation
- Framework slides: Visual model or diagram with speaker narration
- Example slides: Screenshot, quote, or case study visual
- Interaction slides: Poll, question, or exercise prompt
- Transition slides: Section title only (gives audience a cognitive break)

## Section 4: Infographics

### Infographic Types

**Statistical Infographics**
Visualize data to make numbers comprehensible and shareable.
Best for: Survey results, benchmark reports, industry statistics.

**Process Infographics**
Visualize a sequential workflow or methodology.
Best for: How-to guides, onboarding flows, decision frameworks.

**Comparison Infographics**
Visualize differences between options, approaches, or time periods.
Best for: Product comparisons, before/after, evolution timelines.

**Geographic Infographics**
Visualize location-based data on maps.
Best for: Regional data, expansion plans, market coverage.

### Infographic Content Brief

```
Title: [Descriptive, keyword-inclusive]
Objective: [What should the viewer understand after seeing this?]
Target Audience: [Who is this for?]
Key Message: [One sentence summary of the insight]
Data Sources: [Cited sources for every data point]

Section 1: [Title]
- Data point: [Specific number + context]
- Visualization type: [Bar chart, pie chart, icon array, etc.]

Section 2: [Title]
- Data point: [Specific number + context]
- Visualization type: [Recommended format]

[Repeat for 4-8 sections]

Footer:
- Sources cited
- Brand logo and URL
- Share CTA
```

### Infographic Writing Rules

- Every data point must have a cited source
- Use round numbers for memorability (say "nearly 75%" not "74.3%")
- Headline the insight, not the data ("3 in 4 Marketers Struggle
  with ROI" not "74.3% of Marketers Report Difficulty")
- Minimize text—let the visualization carry the story
- Include alt text and accessible text versions for screen readers

## Section 5: Content Repurposing — The 1-to-10 Framework

### The Repurposing Principle

Create one substantive "pillar" content piece and systematically
transform it into 10+ derivative assets across formats and channels.
This maximizes content ROI, extends audience reach, and reinforces
messaging through repetition across touchpoints.

### The 1-to-10 Repurposing Map

Starting asset: One 3,000-word blog post or one 45-minute webinar.

```
PILLAR CONTENT (1)
└── Blog post or webinar recording

DERIVATIVE ASSETS (10+)
├── 1. LinkedIn article (condensed version, 800-1,200 words)
├── 2. Twitter/X thread (8-12 tweets, key insights)
├── 3. Instagram carousel (5-8 slides, one insight per slide)
├── 4. Short-form video (60-90 sec, one key takeaway)
├── 5. Email newsletter issue (curated excerpt + link)
├── 6. Podcast episode discussion (expand with commentary)
├── 7. Infographic (visualize the data and framework)
├── 8. SlideShare/PDF (presentation format of key points)
├── 9. Quote graphics (3-5 standalone quotes for social)
├── 10. Community post (discussion prompt based on findings)
└── BONUS: Guest post pitch (angle for external publication)
```

### Repurposing Production Workflow

**Day 1: Publish Pillar Content**
Publish the original long-form piece or host the webinar.

**Day 2-3: Extract Key Assets**
- Pull 5–8 standalone insights or quotes
- Identify the most compelling data points
- Select the most visual framework or model
- Transcribe video/audio content if applicable

**Day 4-7: Produce Derivatives**
- Write social copy variants for each platform
- Create visual assets (carousel slides, quote graphics)
- Edit video clips from webinar or recording
- Draft email newsletter featuring the content

**Day 8-14: Distribute Across Channels**
- Stagger publication across platforms (not all at once)
- Customize each derivative for platform conventions
- Cross-link derivatives back to the pillar content
- Monitor engagement and double down on high-performing formats

**Day 15-30: Extend and Iterate**
- Syndicate to external platforms (Medium, LinkedIn articles)
- Pitch guest post angles derived from the pillar content
- Create follow-up content based on audience questions
- Update the pillar content with new insights from discussions

### Repurposing Quality Control

The primary risk of repurposing is quality degradation. Each derivative
must be a complete, standalone piece of content—not an obvious excerpt
that feels incomplete.

**Quality Checklist for Derivatives**
- Does this work as a standalone piece? (Would someone who never saw
  the original find this valuable?)
- Is it optimized for the target platform's format and conventions?
- Does it link back to the pillar content for deeper exploration?
- Does it maintain the brand voice and quality standards?
- Is it differentiated enough from other derivatives to avoid
  repetition fatigue for followers who see multiple platforms?

## Summary

Multimedia content extends the Content Brain's reach into video, audio,
live, and visual formats. Video scripts must be written for the ear,
front-load value, and include visual direction. Podcast content requires
detailed show notes and transcripts for discoverability. Webinar content
must structure 45–60 minutes around audience attention curves and
interactive elements. Infographics must visualize data with cited
sources and accessible alternatives. The 1-to-10 repurposing framework
transforms a single pillar piece into ten or more derivative assets,
maximizing content ROI across every channel. Multimedia is not optional
—it is where modern audiences live, learn, and decide.
