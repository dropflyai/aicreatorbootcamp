# Persuasive Writing — AIDA, PAS, BAB, Cialdini Principles in Copy

## Overview

Persuasive writing is the art and science of moving readers to action
through words. It is not manipulation — it is the ethical application of
psychological principles to help readers make decisions aligned with their
own interests. Every landing page, email, ad, and CTA is an exercise in
persuasion.

Source: Ogilvy, D. (1985). *Ogilvy on Advertising*.
Source: Cialdini, R. (2006). *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion*.

---

## The Fundamental Principle

David Ogilvy's foundational rule: **"The consumer is not a moron. She is
your wife."** Effective persuasion respects the reader's intelligence. It
provides genuine reasons to act, supported by evidence and delivered with
clarity. Condescension, trickery, and hyperbole are the tools of amateurs.

## Framework 1: AIDA (Attention-Interest-Desire-Action)

The oldest and most universal copywriting framework, attributed to E. St.
Elmo Lewis (1898).

### Structure

**A — Attention**
Capture attention in the first 3 seconds. The reader must stop scrolling.
Techniques:
- Bold, benefit-driven headline
- Startling statistic or counterintuitive claim
- Question that names their problem
- Visual disruption (in design-supported contexts)

Example: "You're losing 23 hours a week to meetings that should be emails."

**I — Interest**
Expand on the attention hook with relevant information. Build the case
that this problem matters and you understand it deeply.
Techniques:
- Elaborate on the problem with specific details
- Share data that quantifies the impact
- Tell a brief relatable story
- Demonstrate empathy ("You've tried X, Y, and Z. None worked.")

Example: "Our research across 500 companies found that the average
knowledge worker spends 31% of their workweek in meetings — and rates
71% of them as unproductive."

**D — Desire**
Transform interest into desire by showing the solution and its benefits.
Focus on outcomes, not features.
Techniques:
- Paint the "after" picture vividly
- Show social proof (testimonials, case studies)
- Quantify the benefit ("Save 10 hours per week")
- Address objections proactively

Example: "Teams using AsyncWork replace 80% of their meetings with
async updates. The average user reclaims 11 hours per week. Here's
what their Tuesdays look like now..."

**A — Action**
Tell the reader exactly what to do next. Remove all friction.
Techniques:
- Single, clear CTA
- Low-commitment first step ("Start free trial" not "Buy now")
- Urgency or scarcity if genuine
- Reassurance ("No credit card required. Cancel anytime.")

Example: "Start your free 14-day trial. No credit card required."

### AIDA Best Applications
- Landing pages
- Product pages
- Long-form sales emails
- Video ad scripts
- Webinar outlines

## Framework 2: PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solve)

A more emotionally driven framework that works by intensifying the
reader's awareness of their pain before presenting relief.

### Structure

**P — Problem**
Name the reader's problem clearly and specifically. Use their language.
Demonstrate that you understand their situation intimately.

Example: "Your content team publishes 4 blog posts a week, but organic
traffic has been flat for 6 months."

**A — Agitate**
Intensify the problem. Show the consequences of inaction. Make the status
quo feel untenable. This is where pathos does its heaviest work.

Example: "Every month that passes, your competitors are building domain
authority you're not. That flat traffic line? It's actually declining
relative to your market. By Q3, you'll be invisible in search results
for your most important keywords."

**S — Solve**
Present your solution as the clear path from agitation to relief. The
relief should feel proportional to the agitation.

Example: "Our content audit identifies exactly which pages are holding
you back, which topics you're missing, and which competitors you can
overtake. In 30 days, you'll have a prioritized content roadmap that
turns flat traffic into a growth curve."

### PAS Ethical Guidelines
- Agitate real problems, not manufactured fears
- The solution must genuinely address the agitated problem
- Never agitate vulnerabilities (health anxiety, financial desperation)
- Agitation should motivate, not paralyze

### PAS Best Applications
- Email subject lines and opening paragraphs
- Social media ad copy
- Short-form landing pages
- Product feature announcements
- Sales outreach emails

## Framework 3: BAB (Before-After-Bridge)

A transformation-focused framework that leverages the contrast between
current state and desired state.

### Structure

**Before** — Describe the reader's current painful reality.
**After** — Paint the picture of their desired future state.
**Bridge** — Show how to get from Before to After (your product/service).

### Example

**Before:** "Right now, your sales team spends 3 hours preparing for
every client call. They dig through CRM notes, search for relevant case
studies, and piece together talking points from scattered documents."

**After:** "Imagine if your reps walked into every call with a one-page
AI-generated brief: the prospect's pain points, relevant case studies,
competitive positioning, and suggested talk track. Preparation time:
2 minutes."

**Bridge:** "SalesBrain analyzes your CRM data, content library, and
call recordings to generate pre-call intelligence automatically.
Set it up in 15 minutes. See the difference on your next call."

### BAB Best Applications
- Case study introductions
- Testimonial frameworks
- Product launch announcements
- Transformation-focused landing pages
- Customer success stories

## Framework 4: The Four Ps (Promise-Picture-Proof-Push)

### Structure

**Promise** — Make a bold, specific, benefit-driven claim.
**Picture** — Help the reader visualize the outcome.
**Proof** — Support the promise with evidence.
**Push** — Deliver the call to action.

### Example

**Promise:** "Double your email open rates in 30 days."
**Picture:** "Imagine checking your campaign dashboard and seeing 45%
open rates where you used to see 22%. Your click-through rates follow.
Your pipeline grows."
**Proof:** "We tested this approach with 200 SaaS companies. Average
open rate improvement: 94%. Here's the data..."
**Push:** "Download the free Subject Line Playbook. Your next send
will prove it works."

## Applying Cialdini's Principles in Copy

### Reciprocity in Copy

Give value before asking for anything:
- Free tools, templates, or calculators
- Ungated educational content
- Generous free tier or trial

**Copy pattern:**
"Here's our complete [resource]. No email required. No strings attached.
We built it because [genuine reason]."

**Anti-pattern:** Offering a "free guide" that requires email, phone,
company name, and budget range. That is not reciprocity; it is a transaction.

### Commitment/Consistency in Copy

Start with small asks, then build:
- "Try it free" -> "Customize your setup" -> "Invite your team"
- Micro-commitments: quizzes, assessments, configuration steps

**Copy pattern:**
"Take the 2-minute assessment to see where you stand."
(Small commitment that leads to larger engagement.)

### Social Proof in Copy

Show that others — especially similar others — have chosen this path:

**Specific > vague:**
- Weak: "Trusted by thousands of companies"
- Strong: "Used by 12,847 marketing teams including Stripe, Notion, and Figma"

**Copy patterns:**
- Customer logos: Show recognizable brands
- Testimonials: Named person, real title, specific result
- Numbers: Users, customers, ratings, reviews
- Awards and recognition: Third-party validation

### Authority in Copy

Demonstrate expertise, do not just claim it:
- "Based on our analysis of 1.2M email campaigns..."
- "According to our research team's 6-month study..."
- "Published in [credible outlet]"
- Expert author bylines with credentials

**Anti-pattern:** "We're the leading platform for..." (self-declared
authority is not authority).

### Liking in Copy

Be human. Be helpful. Be relatable:
- Use conversational tone appropriate to brand voice
- Share behind-the-scenes stories
- Acknowledge mistakes and learnings
- Write with empathy for the reader's situation

**Copy pattern:** "We built this because we had the same problem. Our
team was spending 4 hours a week on [task], and we knew there had to
be a better way."

### Scarcity in Copy

Only use genuine scarcity. Fabricated urgency destroys trust permanently.

**Legitimate scarcity:**
- "Early pricing ends January 31" (and it actually does)
- "12 spots remaining in this cohort" (and there actually are)
- "Beta access for first 500 signups" (real limit)

**Dark pattern (NEVER use):**
- Fake countdown timers that reset
- "Only 2 left!" that never changes
- Artificial urgency with no real deadline

## Persuasive Copy Quality Checklist

Before publishing any persuasive content:

- [ ] Is the core promise specific and verifiable?
- [ ] Does the copy lead with reader benefit, not product features?
- [ ] Is social proof specific (names, numbers, results)?
- [ ] Is the CTA clear, single, and benefit-driven?
- [ ] Does the copy pass the "so what?" test at every paragraph?
- [ ] Are all claims truthful and supportable?
- [ ] Would this copy be effective if a competitor made identical claims?
- [ ] Is scarcity/urgency genuine, not manufactured?
- [ ] Does the reading level match the target audience?
- [ ] Has the copy been read aloud for natural flow?

## Persuasion Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| Feature-first copy | Nobody cares about features; they care about outcomes | Lead with benefit, support with feature |
| Vague claims | "Best-in-class" means nothing | Quantify: "Reduces churn by 34%" |
| No CTA | Reader finishes and does nothing | Every persuasive piece has exactly one CTA |
| Multiple CTAs | Paradox of choice = no choice | One primary CTA per content piece |
| Fake urgency | Trust destroyed permanently | Only genuine deadlines and limits |
| Corporate jargon | "Leverage synergies" = instant bounce | Write like a human talking to a human |
| Ignoring objections | Unaddressed concerns kill conversion | Name and neutralize top 3 objections |
| All sizzle no steak | Hype without substance | Back every claim with evidence |

---

**Persuasion is not about making people want what you have.
It is about showing people that what you have is what they already want.**
