# Content Benchmark Tests -- Scenario-Based Evaluation

Every content scenario below must be evaluated with a structured response.
These benchmarks test real-world content strategy and execution, not generic writing ability.
Model responses show the standard. Your responses must meet or exceed.

---

## HOW TO USE BENCHMARK TESTS

1. Read the scenario completely before formulating a response
2. Draft your response independently
3. Compare against the model response
4. Score each response dimension: Strategy (1-5), Craft (1-5), Impact (1-5)
5. Average must be >= 4.0 to pass

## SCENARIO 1: HEADLINE OUTPERFORMANCE

**Situation:**
The current landing page headline is "Get Started Today." It has a 2.1% conversion rate. You need to write a headline that outperforms it.

**Context:**
- Product: Project management SaaS for agencies
- Primary audience: Agency owners and operations directors
- Key pain: Projects going over budget and over time
- Average deal size: $30K/year
- Current page gets 15K visits/month

**Challenge:**
Write 5 headline alternatives. For each, explain which persuasion principle it leverages and why it should outperform the original.

### Model Response

**Why "Get Started Today" underperforms:**
- No specificity (get started with what?)
- No benefit (what does the reader gain?)
- No audience targeting (who is this for?)
- No urgency that is real (why today?)
- Fails every hook test

**5 Alternatives:**

**1. "Stop Losing $50K Per Project to Scope Creep"**
- Principle: Loss aversion (people are more motivated to avoid loss than to achieve gain)
- Why it works: Quantifies the pain in dollars. Specific. Agency owners immediately relate. "Scope creep" is their exact language (VoC).
- Expected lift: 40-60% over baseline based on loss aversion research

**2. "How 200+ Agencies Deliver Projects On Time and On Budget"**
- Principle: Social proof + Specificity
- Why it works: "200+ agencies" provides credibility. "On time and on budget" are the exact outcomes they want. The "how" creates curiosity.
- Expected lift: 30-50% over baseline

**3. "Your Projects Are Leaking Money. Here Is Where."**
- Principle: Curiosity gap + Problem identification
- Why it works: Makes a bold claim that the reader cannot ignore. "Here is where" promises a specific, actionable revelation. Creates an open loop the reader must close.
- Expected lift: 35-55% over baseline

**4. "The Project Management System Built for Agencies (Not Everyone Else)"**
- Principle: Unity (shared identity) + Exclusivity
- Why it works: Agency owners are tired of tools built for "everyone." This says "we built this for you." The parenthetical creates contrast with competitors.
- Expected lift: 25-40% over baseline

**5. "Deliver Every Project Profitably -- Or See Exactly Why You Didn't"**
- Principle: Commitment/consistency + Transparency
- Why it works: "Every project profitably" sets the aspirational state. "Or see exactly why" shows confidence and transparency. Implies the tool gives visibility either way.
- Expected lift: 30-45% over baseline

**Testing recommendation:**
A/B test headlines 1 and 2 first (strongest predicted lift). Run for minimum 2 weeks or 1,000 conversions per variant before declaring a winner.

## SCENARIO 2: HIGH-TRAFFIC, ZERO-CONVERSION BLOG POST

**Situation:**
A blog post titled "10 Project Management Tips for 2025" gets 10,000 views per month but has generated 0 demo requests, 0 email signups, and 0 pipeline. The post ranks #3 for its target keyword.

**Context:**
- Post is 1,500 words
- Reading time is 6 minutes
- Average time on page: 1:45
- Bounce rate: 78%
- CTA: Small "Request a Demo" button at the bottom
- Content: Generic tips like "communicate clearly" and "use deadlines"

**Challenge:**
Diagnose the problem and rewrite the CTA strategy.

### Model Response

**Diagnosis:**

**Problem 1: Content-intent mismatch**
The keyword "project management tips" attracts TOFU traffic -- people looking for general advice, not ready to buy. The CTA (Request a Demo) is BOFU. The gap between the reader's intent and the desired action is too large. No one reads "10 tips" and thinks "I should schedule a sales meeting."

**Problem 2: Content is generic and undifferentiated**
"Communicate clearly" and "use deadlines" provide no unique value. Readers scan, get nothing new, and leave (hence 1:45 on a 6-minute post). The content fails the originality test.

**Problem 3: CTA placement and relevance**
A single demo CTA at the bottom of a page that 78% of readers bounce from means almost no one sees it. Even those who do see it have no reason to click -- the content did not build toward the CTA.

**Problem 4: No conversion architecture**
There is no secondary CTA, no content upgrade, no email capture. The page has only two outcomes: demo request (too high commitment) or exit.

**Revised CTA Strategy:**

**Step 1: Add a TOFU conversion offer (primary CTA)**
Replace "Request a Demo" with a content upgrade that matches the reader's intent:
- "Download: The Agency Project Profitability Calculator (Free Spreadsheet)"
- Place this at 3 points: after tip #3, after tip #7, and at the bottom
- This captures leads who are interested in the topic but not ready to buy
- Expected conversion: 3-5% of page views (300-500 leads/month)

**Step 2: Add an in-content contextual CTA (secondary)**
After tip #5 (the most actionable tip), insert a contextual callout:
"Want to see how agencies automate this? Watch a 2-minute product walkthrough (no signup required)."
- This bridges TOFU content to product awareness
- Video view is a trackable engagement signal
- Expected engagement: 5-8% click-through

**Step 3: Add an exit-intent popup**
For users about to bounce:
"Before you go -- get the free Agency PM Toolkit (templates, calculators, checklists)."
- Captures bouncing traffic
- Expected capture: 2-3% of bouncers = 150-230 additional leads/month

**Step 4: Rewrite the content for depth and uniqueness**
Replace generic tips with data-backed, specific advice:
- BEFORE: "Communicate clearly with your team"
- AFTER: "Run a 15-minute Monday standup using the 3-3-1 format: 3 priorities, 3 blockers, 1 decision needed. Agencies using this format report 40% fewer missed deadlines (Source: [Study])."

This increases time on page (more depth to consume), reduces bounce (more value), and builds trust (setting up the conversion).

**Expected outcome:**
- 10K views/month x 4% conversion = 400 leads/month (from 0)
- 400 leads x 10% SQL rate = 40 SQLs/month
- 40 SQLs x 25% close rate x $30K = $300K pipeline/month from one blog post

## SCENARIO 3: ENTERPRISE CFO CONTENT BRIEF

**Situation:**
You need to create a content brief for a bottom-of-funnel piece targeting enterprise CFOs who are evaluating your project management platform.

**Context:**
- Product: Enterprise project management platform
- Deal size: $100K-$500K
- Sales cycle: 6-9 months
- The CFO is typically the final decision-maker or key influencer on budget
- Current content library has nothing targeting CFOs specifically
- Sales team reports that deals stall when the CFO asks "what is the ROI?"

**Challenge:**
Create a complete content brief for this piece.

### Model Response

**CONTENT BRIEF: "The CFO's Guide to Project Management ROI: How to Evaluate, Measure, and Justify the Investment"**

**Business Objective:** Decision stage -- arm champions with a tool to get CFO approval. Reduce deal stall rate at CFO review stage.

**Target Persona:**
- Role: Chief Financial Officer or VP Finance
- Company size: 500-5,000 employees
- Priorities: Cost control, predictable margins, capital efficiency, risk reduction
- Language: ROI, TCO, payback period, IRR, margin impact, risk-adjusted return
- Pain: Cannot quantify whether project management tools actually improve profitability
- Objection: "We already have spreadsheets and Jira -- why pay $200K for another tool?"

**Funnel Stage:** BOFU

**Primary Keyword:** "project management ROI" (Vol: 2,400/mo, KD: 35)
**Secondary Keywords:** "project management software ROI calculator," "project management business case," "project management TCO"

**Format:** Long-form guide (3,000-4,000 words) + embedded interactive ROI calculator

**Unique Angle:** Not "why project management matters" (every vendor says that). Instead: "Here is the exact financial model to evaluate any project management investment, including ours. If the numbers do not work, do not buy."

This radical transparency builds trust with CFOs who are allergic to marketing fluff.

**Content Structure:**

1. **Opening hook:** "Your project teams are underperforming by an average of 15-25% on profitability. Here is how to calculate the exact number for your company."
2. **The hidden costs of poor project management** (with industry data and formulas)
   - Scope creep cost formula: (average overrun % x annual project revenue)
   - Resource misallocation cost: (hours wasted x blended rate)
   - Revenue leakage: (projects delivered late x penalty + opportunity cost)
3. **The ROI framework** (3 methods, mirroring how CFOs evaluate investments)
   - Payback period analysis
   - NPV of productivity gains over 3 years
   - Risk-adjusted ROI accounting for implementation cost and adoption risk
4. **Interactive ROI calculator** (reader inputs their numbers, gets personalized output)
5. **Case study: How [Customer] achieved $2.1M in annual savings** (real numbers)
6. **Total Cost of Ownership comparison** (spreadsheets vs. basic tools vs. enterprise platform)
7. **The decision framework** (when to invest, when to wait, when to say no)

**CTAs:**
- Primary: "Run your personalized ROI calculation" (interactive calculator -- captures lead)
- Secondary: "Download the CFO evaluation template (Excel)" (gated asset)
- Tertiary: "See how [Company] saves $2.1M" (links to full case study)

**Persuasion Principles:**
- Authority: Industry data, financial frameworks, CFO language
- Social Proof: Named case study with specific dollar impact
- Reciprocity: Free ROI calculator and evaluation template (massive value before any ask)
- Commitment: Once they run their numbers and see positive ROI, they are psychologically committed

**Distribution Plan:**
- Sales enablement: Every rep uses this in deals where CFO is involved
- LinkedIn: Promoted to CFO/VP Finance personas
- Email: Sent to existing pipeline contacts at CFO level
- ABM: Personalized ads to target account finance teams

**Success Metrics:**
- Calculator completions (lead capture)
- Template downloads
- Sales team usage rate
- Influence on deals where CFO was a blocker
- Pipeline attributed to this asset

## SCENARIO 4: EMAIL SUBJECT LINE OPTIMIZATION

**Situation:**
Your marketing email has a 12% open rate. Industry average is 22%. The current subject line is: "Our Monthly Newsletter -- January Edition."

**Context:**
- Audience: 5,000 marketing managers
- Email contains: 2 blog posts, 1 case study, 1 product update
- Send day: Tuesday, 10am
- The case study is about a customer who increased lead gen by 300%

**Challenge:**
Write 8 alternative subject lines and explain the strategy behind each.

### Model Response

**Why the current subject line fails:**
- "Newsletter" signals low value (readers deprioritize newsletters)
- "January Edition" adds no information
- "Our" makes it about the company, not the reader
- No hook, no benefit, no curiosity

**8 Alternatives:**

**1. "How [Customer Name] grew leads 300% (case study inside)"**
- Strategy: Specificity + Social proof. Leads with the strongest content piece. The number creates curiosity. "Case study inside" sets expectations.

**2. "The lead gen tactic that outperformed everything we tried in Q4"**
- Strategy: Curiosity gap. The reader must open to discover the tactic. "Outperformed everything" signals high value.

**3. "300% more leads -- their exact playbook"**
- Strategy: Extreme brevity + Specificity. Short subject lines stand out in crowded inboxes. "Exact playbook" promises actionable detail.

**4. "You are leaving leads on the table. Here is proof."**
- Strategy: Loss aversion + Challenge. Implies the reader is underperforming. "Here is proof" creates urgency to find out.

**5. "[First Name], one tactic to try this week"**
- Strategy: Personalization + Simplicity. Personal name increases open rates. "One tactic" is low commitment. "This week" creates timeliness.

**6. "What [Customer Name]'s marketing team did differently"**
- Strategy: Curiosity + Social proof. "Did differently" implies a secret or unconventional approach. Named customer adds credibility.

**7. "3 minutes that could change your Q1 results"**
- Strategy: Low time commitment + High value promise. "3 minutes" reduces the barrier to open. "Change your Q1 results" is ambitious.

**8. "Stop reading newsletters. Start reading this."**
- Strategy: Pattern interrupt + Contrast. Acknowledges newsletter fatigue directly. Creates curiosity about what makes this different.

**Testing recommendation:**
- Test 1 vs. 3 (high specificity face-off)
- Test 4 vs. 8 (loss aversion vs. pattern interrupt)
- Send to 4 segments of ~600 each. Winner sends to remaining 2,600.

## SCENARIO 5: CONTENT AUDIT PRIORITIZATION

**Situation:**
You have 200 blog posts. Leadership wants a content audit. You have bandwidth to refresh 20 pieces this quarter.

**Challenge:**
How do you decide which 20 to prioritize?

### Model Response

**Step 1: Pull data for all 200 posts**
For each post, gather:
- Monthly organic traffic (last 90 days)
- Current keyword rankings (position for primary keyword)
- Conversion rate (leads, demos, signups attributed)
- Backlinks (external sites linking to this post)
- Age (date published)
- Content quality score (quick assessment: 1-3 scale)

**Step 2: Categorize into 4 quadrants**

| | High Traffic | Low Traffic |
|---|-------------|-------------|
| **High Conversion** | QUADRANT A: Protect and optimize | QUADRANT B: Promote more aggressively |
| **Low Conversion** | QUADRANT C: Fix the conversion path | QUADRANT D: Evaluate for refresh or removal |

**Step 3: Prioritization framework**

Priority 1 (8 posts): **QUADRANT C -- High traffic, low conversion**
These posts are already getting eyeballs. Fixing the conversion strategy (better CTAs, content upgrades, CTA placement) directly impacts pipeline. Highest ROI effort.

Priority 2 (6 posts): **"Almost ranking" posts -- positions 4-15**
Posts ranking on page 1-2 of Google that could reach top 3 with a refresh. A jump from position 8 to position 3 can increase traffic 5-10x. Look for posts that:
- Rank for high-volume keywords
- Have good backlinks already
- Need content updates to be competitive

Priority 3 (4 posts): **QUADRANT B -- Low traffic, high conversion**
These convert well but nobody sees them. Refresh for SEO optimization, then promote through distribution channels.

Priority 4 (2 posts): **Consolidation targets**
Find 3-4 posts covering the same topic. Merge the best elements into one comprehensive piece. Redirect old URLs. This eliminates keyword cannibalization and creates one strong page.

**What NOT to refresh:**
- Posts with no traffic, no rankings, and no conversion. These are candidates for removal or redirect, not refresh.
- Posts less than 3 months old. Not enough data to judge performance.
- Posts ranking #1 already with good conversion. Do not fix what is not broken.

## SCENARIO 6: COMPETITIVE COMPARISON CONTENT

**Situation:**
Your sales team asks for a "[Your Product] vs. [Competitor]" comparison page. The competitor is well-known and has a larger market share.

**Challenge:**
Create the content strategy for this page. How do you position against a bigger competitor without being negative?

### Model Response

**Strategic approach: Honest broker, not attack piece.**

**Why this matters:** Prospects searching "[Your Product] vs. [Competitor]" are in the decision stage. They want an honest comparison. If you trash the competitor, you lose credibility. If you are too generous to the competitor, you lose the deal.

**Content Structure:**

**1. Lead with the reader's decision, not your product**
Opening: "Choosing between [You] and [Competitor]? Here is an honest comparison to help you decide. We built [Your Product], so we are biased -- but we will be transparent about where each tool excels and where it falls short."

This radical honesty disarms skepticism. The reader now trusts the comparison more.

**2. Feature comparison table (honest)**
Create a clear table. Mark where you win, where they win, and where it is equal. Do NOT fabricate weaknesses for the competitor or overstate your own capabilities.

Where the competitor is stronger: acknowledge it, then explain why it may not matter for the reader's use case.
Where you are stronger: explain why it matters practically, not just as a feature checkbox.

**3. Use case segmentation**
"[Competitor] is the better choice if you need [X]. [Your Product] is the better choice if you need [Y]."
This builds trust and actually qualifies the reader. If they match your ICP, they self-select. If they do not, you saved everyone time.

**4. Customer perspective section**
Include quotes from customers who evaluated both and chose you. Ask them: "What made you choose [Your Product] over [Competitor]?" Use their words, not yours.

**5. Migration section**
"Already using [Competitor]? Here is how to switch." This addresses the switching cost objection and provides a clear path.

**6. CTA Strategy**
- Primary: "See how [Your Product] compares for your specific use case -- get a personalized comparison" (lead capture)
- Secondary: "Talk to a customer who switched from [Competitor]" (social proof conversion)

**What to avoid:**
- FUD (fear, uncertainty, doubt) about the competitor
- Outdated competitor information
- Ignoring competitor strengths that are real
- Claims you cannot substantiate

## SCENARIO 8: THOUGHT LEADERSHIP THAT ACTUALLY LEADS

**Situation:**
The CEO wants to publish a "thought leadership" article. The last one was titled "Why Innovation Matters" and got 47 views.

**Challenge:**
Coach the CEO on what real thought leadership looks like and draft an article outline.

### Model Response

**Coaching the CEO:**

"Thought leadership is not 'opinions from a leader.' It is original thinking that advances the industry conversation. 'Why Innovation Matters' failed because it said what everyone already says. Real thought leadership requires one of these:

1. **A contrarian position you can defend with data.** Not contrarian for shock value -- genuinely arguing against conventional wisdom with evidence.
2. **Original data or research.** Something only you can produce because of your unique position.
3. **A new framework or mental model.** A way of thinking about a problem that others can adopt.
4. **A prediction with reasoning.** Not vague ("AI will change everything") but specific ("By 2027, 40% of project management decisions will be made by AI agents, and here is why").

Which of these can you bring?"

**Draft Outline (assuming the CEO has a contrarian position):**

**Title:** "Your 'Digital Transformation' Is a $10M Bandaid. Here Is What You Should Do Instead."

**Hook:** "I have watched 50+ companies spend $5-15M on 'digital transformation' projects that delivered less than 20% of projected value. The problem is not the technology. The problem is the approach."

**Section 1: The data** -- Share anonymized data from companies you have worked with. Show the gap between transformation promises and results.

**Section 2: The root cause** -- Argue the contrarian position. "Transformation fails because companies automate existing broken processes instead of redesigning for outcomes."

**Section 3: The alternative** -- Present the CEO's framework for how to approach transformation differently. Make it a named model that people can reference.

**Section 4: Proof it works** -- 2-3 examples of companies that did it the CEO's way and succeeded.

**Section 5: The challenge** -- End with a specific call to action. Not "innovate more" but "pick one process this quarter, throw out the old workflow entirely, and redesign from the outcome backward."

## SCENARIO 9: CONTENT BRIEF FOR INTERNAL CHAMPIONS

**Situation:**
Sales says deals stall because the champion cannot articulate your value to internal stakeholders. You need to create content that the champion uses to sell internally.

**Challenge:**
What content pieces do you create?

### Model Response

**The "Champion Toolkit" -- 4 Pieces:**

**1. The One-Page Executive Summary**
- What: Single page with problem, solution, ROI, social proof
- Format: PDF, beautifully designed, shareable via email
- Key feature: CFO section (numbers), COO section (operations), CTO section (technical)
- Written in THEIR voice, not yours: "Here is why I recommend we evaluate [Product]..."

**2. The Internal Presentation Deck (5 slides)**
- Slide 1: The problem we face (with internal data if possible)
- Slide 2: What we evaluated (3 options including do-nothing)
- Slide 3: Why [Product] is the recommendation (comparison matrix)
- Slide 4: Expected ROI and timeline (with their numbers)
- Slide 5: Proposed next steps
- Key feature: Editable. Champion can customize with their internal data.

**3. The Objection Handling Email Templates**
- 3-5 pre-written email templates the champion can send to stakeholders:
  - "To the CFO: the business case" (ROI focused)
  - "To the CTO: technical fit" (integration and security focused)
  - "To the skeptic: addressing concerns" (risk mitigation focused)
- Key feature: Each template is <200 words and addresses one specific concern.

**4. The Quick Reference FAQ**
- 10 questions stakeholders always ask, with concise answers
- "How much does it cost?" "How long to implement?" "Who else uses it?" "What if it does not work?"
- Key feature: Answers are factual, not salesy. Champion can share with confidence.

## SCENARIO 10: REPURPOSING A WEBINAR INTO 10 CONTENT PIECES

**Situation:**
You just ran a 45-minute webinar with 300 attendees on "The Future of Agency Operations." The recording, slides, and Q&A transcript are available.

**Challenge:**
Create 10 distinct content pieces from this single asset.

### Model Response

| # | Content Piece | Source Material | Channel | Effort |
|---|-------------|----------------|---------|--------|
| 1 | Blog post: "5 Key Takeaways from [Webinar Title]" | Key points from presentation | Blog | Medium |
| 2 | Short-form video clip (60 sec): Best insight | Recording highlight | LinkedIn, YouTube Shorts | Low |
| 3 | Short-form video clip (60 sec): Most controversial point | Recording highlight | LinkedIn, X/Twitter | Low |
| 4 | Infographic: Data visualization of key stats shared | Slides + data points | Blog, Pinterest, LinkedIn | Medium |
| 5 | Email: Recap email to registrants who did not attend | Key points + recording link | Email | Low |
| 6 | LinkedIn carousel: 8-slide summary of framework presented | Slides reformatted | LinkedIn | Low |

## SCENARIO 11: CONTENT FOR A PRODUCT NO ONE UNDERSTANDS

**Situation:**
Your product is a "real-time data orchestration platform for event-driven architectures." Nobody outside of your engineering team understands what this means. Your website bounce rate is 85%.

**Challenge:**
Rewrite the product positioning for a non-technical buyer.

### Model Response

**The problem with the current positioning:**
It describes what the product IS, not what it DOES for the buyer. Non-technical buyers do not buy technology. They buy outcomes.

**Step 1: Identify the outcome**
Ask: "What happens when a company uses this successfully?"
Answer (example): "They can react to customer behavior in real-time -- sending the right message, updating the right system, and triggering the right action within milliseconds instead of hours."

**Step 2: Identify the pain**
Ask: "What goes wrong without this?"
Answer: "Companies miss the moment. A customer abandons their cart and the recovery email goes out 6 hours later -- after they have already bought from a competitor."

**Step 3: Rewrite**

BEFORE: "A real-time data orchestration platform for event-driven architectures."

AFTER: "React to every customer moment -- instantly. Connect your systems so the right action happens in milliseconds, not hours. No more missed opportunities."

**Step 4: Homepage structure**
- Hero: The outcome statement (above)
- Section 2: "The problem" -- describe what companies deal with today (the pain, in their words)
- Section 3: "How it works" -- 3 steps, plain language with a simple diagram
- Section 4: "Who uses it" -- 3 use cases with specific outcomes and named customers
- Section 5: CTA -- "See it in action in 2 minutes" (product video, no signup required)

## SCORING SUMMARY

Each scenario response is scored on three dimensions:

| Dimension | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Strategy | 40% | Quality of the thinking, framework, and approach |
| Craft | 35% | Quality of the writing, examples, and execution |
| Impact | 25% | Likelihood of driving measurable business results |

**Pass:** Average >= 4.0 across all dimensions
**Conditional Pass:** Average 3.0-3.9 with no dimension below 3
**Fail:** Average < 3.0 or any dimension below 2
