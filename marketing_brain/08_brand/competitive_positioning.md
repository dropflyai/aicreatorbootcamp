# Competitive Positioning — Obviously Awesome, Category Design, and Messaging

## The Positioning Problem

Most B2B companies believe they have a positioning problem when they actually have
a clarity problem. Their product does something valuable, but they cannot explain
what it is, who it is for, and why it matters in a way that sticks. Buyers encounter
their website, read the messaging, and think: "I still do not understand what makes
you different from the other 15 companies in this space."

April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome" (2019) provides the most actionable positioning
framework for technology companies. This module integrates her methodology with
category design principles and messaging hierarchy development.

## April Dunford: Obviously Awesome Framework

### The Five Components of Positioning

Dunford identifies five components that must be defined in a specific order:

```
1. Competitive Alternatives → 2. Unique Attributes → 3. Value →
4. Target Customer → 5. Market Category
```

**The order matters.** Most companies start with category (we are a CRM) and work
backward. Dunford argues you must start with what you compete against and work
forward to category.

### Component 1: Competitive Alternatives

**Question:** "What would customers use if your product did not exist?"

This is NOT just direct competitors. Competitive alternatives include:
- Direct competitors (same category, same function)
- Adjacent tools (different category, overlapping function)
- Manual processes (spreadsheets, email, meetings)
- Doing nothing (the status quo)
- Internal build (engineering team builds a custom solution)

**Exercise:** List ALL alternatives your best customers considered before choosing
you. Interview 10-15 customers and ask: "Before you found us, how were you handling
this? What did you try first?"

### Component 2: Unique Attributes

**Question:** "What features or capabilities do you have that the alternatives
do not?"

These must be genuinely unique — not "better" or "faster" but things the alternatives
literally cannot do or do not offer. This is the hardest step because most companies
struggle to identify true uniqueness.

**Types of Unique Attributes:**
- Technical capability (architecture, algorithm, data model)
- Integration depth (connections alternatives do not have)
- Data advantage (proprietary data that enables unique insights)
- Experience design (workflow that alternatives do not replicate)
- Business model (pricing or delivery that alternatives do not offer)
- Domain expertise (vertical knowledge embedded in the product)

### Component 3: Value

**Question:** "What value do these unique attributes create for customers?"

Map each unique attribute to a specific value outcome:

| Unique Attribute | Value for Customer |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Real-time conversation analysis | Managers can coach reps on actual behavior, not self-reported |
| Native CRM integration with bi-directional sync | No manual data entry, pipeline always current |
| AI-powered deal risk scoring | Forecast with 90% accuracy, eliminate surprise losses |

### Component 4: Target Customer

**Question:** "Who cares most about the value you deliver?"

Not all customers are equal. Your best-fit customers share characteristics:
- They have the problem your unique attributes solve
- The problem is acute enough to justify investment
- They have the budget and decision process to buy
- They get disproportionate value from your unique strengths

**Target Customer Profile:**
- Company characteristics (size, industry, growth stage, technology maturity)
- Buyer characteristics (title, function, seniority, priorities)
- Situational triggers (what event makes them start looking?)

### Component 5: Market Category

**Question:** "What category helps customers understand your value fastest?"

Category choice is a positioning decision, not a product taxonomy decision.
You choose the category that makes your unique value most obvious:

**Option A: Existing Category**
Position within a known category. Buyers instantly understand what you do.
Risk: You inherit the category's expectations and competitors.

**Option B: Sub-Category**
Create a sub-category within an existing one. "Revenue Intelligence Platform"
within "Sales Technology." Buyers understand the broader context but you own a niche.
Risk: Sub-category may not be recognized yet.

**Option C: New Category**
Create an entirely new category that you define and lead. Requires significant
investment in education but creates enormous competitive advantage.
Risk: Buyers do not know they need this yet.

## Category Design

### What Is Category Design?

Category design, developed by Al Ramadan, Dave Peterson, Christopher Lochhead, and
Kevin Maney in "Play Bigger" (2016), is the practice of creating and developing a
new market category that you define, develop, and dominate. The core thesis: category
kings capture 76% of the total value created in their category.

### The Category Design Process

**Step 1: Identify the Problem the Category Solves**
Not a product problem — a market problem. The problem must be:
- Large enough to justify a new category
- Growing (trend-driven, not static)
- Unaddressed by existing categories

**Step 2: Define the Category**
Create a name and definition that:
- Is memorable and distinctive
- Describes the outcome, not the technology
- Is broad enough for growth but narrow enough for clarity
- Does not sound like existing categories

**Naming Examples:**
| Company | What They Could Be Called | Category They Created |
|---------|-------------------------|---------------------|
| Salesforce | Online CRM | Cloud Computing |
| HubSpot | Marketing Software | Inbound Marketing |
| Gong | Call Recording Tool | Revenue Intelligence |
| Drift | Live Chat | Conversational Marketing |
| Clari | Forecasting Tool | Revenue Operations Platform |

**Step 3: Evangelize the Category**
Category creation requires sustained investment in education:
- Publish the "Point of View" (POV) document articulating the category
- Create content that educates the market about the category problem
- Commission analyst research validating the category
- Host events centered on the category (not your product)
- Build a community around the category
- Get customers to adopt the category language

**Step 4: Dominate the Category**
Once the category is established, position your company as the leader:
- Own the search terms for the category
- Be the most cited source for category information
- Win analyst reports in the category
- Have the most customers publicly associated with the category

## Messaging Hierarchy

### What Is a Messaging Hierarchy?

A messaging hierarchy is a structured document that defines what your company says,
to whom, and at what level of detail. It ensures consistency across all marketing
and sales communications while allowing adaptation for different audiences and
channels.

### Messaging Hierarchy Structure

**Level 1: Category Claim (5 Words)**
The shortest possible description of what you do.
Example: "The Revenue Intelligence Platform"

**Level 2: Positioning Statement (25 Words)**
One sentence that captures who you serve, what you do, and why it matters.
Example: "Gong helps revenue teams win more deals by analyzing every customer
interaction to reveal what is really happening in your pipeline."

**Level 3: Value Pillars (3-4 Pillars, 1-2 Sentences Each)**
The key benefits organized by buyer priority.

| Pillar | Statement | Proof Point |
|--------|----------|------------|
| Win More Deals | Understand exactly what top performers do differently and replicate it across the team | Customers see 27% higher win rates within 6 months |
| Forecast with Confidence | AI-powered deal scoring predicts outcomes with 90%+ accuracy | $2.3B in pipeline managed with <10% forecast variance |
| Coach at Scale | Review every call, identify coaching moments, deliver targeted feedback | Managers coach 3x more effectively using conversation data |

**Level 4: Audience-Specific Messaging**

| Audience | Core Message | Key Benefits | Proof |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| VP Sales | "See every deal as it really is, not as reps wish it were" | Forecast accuracy, deal risk visibility | [Metric] |
| Sales Enablement | "Turn call data into coaching programs that actually work" | Coaching at scale, methodology adoption | [Metric] |
| CRO / Revenue Leader | "The operating system for predictable revenue" | Revenue predictability, team performance | [Metric] |
| IT / Security | "Enterprise-grade security with zero integration friction" | SOC 2, SSO, API-first architecture | [Certification] |

**Level 5: Feature-Level Messaging**
Detailed messaging for each product feature, connected to value pillars.

## Competitive Positioning in Practice

### Battle Card Messaging Section

For each competitor, define:

**Our Positioning Against [Competitor]:**
"When competing against [Competitor], we lead with [unique strength] because
their primary weakness is [specific gap]. Our target audience for competitive
displacement is [specific persona] who values [specific capability] that
[Competitor] cannot match."

### Competitive Messaging Do's and Don'ts

**Do:**
- Lead with your unique strengths, not competitor weaknesses
- Use customer evidence to support claims
- Name specific, verifiable differentiators
- Acknowledge competitor strengths honestly (builds credibility)
- Reframe the evaluation criteria to favor your strengths

**Do Not:**
- Disparage competitors by name in marketing materials
- Make claims you cannot prove with evidence
- Position against a competitor who is significantly larger (elevates them)
- Use FUD (fear, uncertainty, doubt) as a primary tactic
- Ignore competitors entirely (buyers notice and lose trust)

## Positioning Audit

### Annual Positioning Review Checklist

| Question | Assessment |
|----------|-----------|
| Can our sales team articulate our positioning consistently? | [ ] Yes [ ] No |
| Do our top 3 competitors position similarly or differently? | [ ] Similarly [ ] Differently |
| Has our market category changed in the last 12 months? | [ ] Yes [ ] No |
| Do customers describe us the way we want to be described? | [ ] Yes [ ] No |
| Is our positioning reflected consistently across website, sales materials, and social? | [ ] Yes [ ] No |
| Have new competitive alternatives emerged that change our positioning context? | [ ] Yes [ ] No |
| Does our positioning still resonate with our best-fit customers? | [ ] Yes [ ] No |
| Can a new hire understand our positioning within 5 minutes of reading it? | [ ] Yes [ ] No |

If more than 2 answers are "No," it is time for a positioning refresh.

## Messaging Testing

### How to Test Messaging

| Method | Sample Size | Timeline | Best For |
|--------|------------|----------|----------|
| Customer Interviews | 10-15 | 2-3 weeks | Directional validation |
| LinkedIn Ad A/B Test | 10,000+ impressions | 2-4 weeks | Headline and hook testing |
| Landing Page A/B Test | 2,000+ visitors | 4-8 weeks | Value proposition testing |
| Sales Pitch A/B Test | 20+ conversations | 4-8 weeks | Full message testing |
| Win/Loss Analysis | 20+ interviews | Ongoing | Competitive positioning validation |
| Survey (MaxDiff) | 200+ respondents | 2 weeks | Feature/benefit prioritization |

you are spending money to confuse buyers faster. With clear positioning, every
marketing dollar amplifies a consistent, compelling, and differentiated message.**
