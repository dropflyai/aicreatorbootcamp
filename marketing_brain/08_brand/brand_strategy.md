# Brand Strategy — Positioning, Architecture, Equity, and Byron Sharp

## Brand as a Strategic Asset

Brand is not a logo, a color palette, or a tagline. Brand is the sum of every
association, perception, and feeling that exists in a buyer's mind when they
encounter your company name. Brand is the most valuable intangible asset a company
can build — and the hardest to measure, which is why it is chronically under-invested
in B2B organizations obsessed with measurable, short-term performance marketing.

The academic and practitioner evidence is unambiguous: strong brands generate higher
margins, lower customer acquisition costs, greater customer loyalty, and premium
valuation multiples. Kantar BrandZ data shows that strong B2B brands recover from
market downturns 5x faster than weak brands. Yet most B2B companies invest less
than 10% of their marketing budget in brand building.

## Positioning

### What Is Positioning?

Positioning is the act of designing your company's offering and image to occupy a
distinctive place in the mind of the target market. Al Ries and Jack Trout defined
it in "Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind" (1981): positioning is not what you
do to a product; it is what you do to the mind of the prospect.

### The Positioning Statement

**Format:**
"For [target customer] who [need/opportunity], [your brand] is the [category] that
[key benefit] because [reason to believe]."

**Example:**
"For VP Sales at B2B SaaS companies who need to improve forecast accuracy, [Brand]
is the revenue intelligence platform that predicts deal outcomes with 90%+ accuracy
because it analyzes every buyer interaction using AI trained on 200M+ sales conversations."

### Positioning Frameworks

**Framework 1: Category-Based Positioning**
Position within an existing category by claiming a distinctive attribute:
- "The fastest [category]"
- "The most secure [category]"
- "The [category] built for [specific audience]"

**Framework 2: Category Creation**
Create a new category that you define and lead:
- Instead of "CRM" → "Revenue Intelligence Platform"
- Instead of "Email Marketing" → "Customer Engagement Platform"
- Requires significant investment in category education

**Framework 3: Competitive Repositioning**
Define yourself against a known competitor:
- "Unlike [competitor], we [differentiator]"
- Effective when competitor is well-known and your difference is clear
- Risk: gives competitor free awareness

### Positioning Validation

Test your positioning with three questions:
1. **Is it true?** Can you deliver on the claim with evidence?
2. **Is it relevant?** Does the target audience care about this claim?
3. **Is it differentiated?** Could a competitor make the same claim credibly?

If any answer is "no," refine the positioning.

## Brand Architecture

### Brand Architecture Models

| Model | Structure | Example | Best For |
|-------|----------|---------|----------|
| Branded House | Single master brand for all products | Google (Search, Maps, Drive, Cloud) | Strong parent brand, related products |
| House of Brands | Independent brands, parent invisible | P&G (Tide, Pampers, Gillette) | Diverse products, different audiences |
| Endorsed Brand | Sub-brands endorsed by parent | Marriott (Courtyard by Marriott, Ritz-Carlton by Marriott) | Portfolio with brand connection |
| Hybrid | Mix of above | Amazon (Amazon, AWS, Ring, Whole Foods) | Large, diverse companies |

### B2B SaaS Brand Architecture Decisions

**When to use Branded House (recommended for most B2B SaaS):**
- You sell to the same buyer persona across products
- Your brand equity is your primary competitive advantage
- You want to maximize cross-sell through brand recognition
- You are building a platform, not a portfolio of point solutions

**When to use Endorsed or House of Brands:**
- Products serve fundamentally different audiences
- An acquired product has strong existing brand equity
- Product categories are so different that association creates confusion

### Naming Hierarchy

```
Company Brand: [Brand Name]
├── Product Tier Names: [Brand] Pro, [Brand] Enterprise
├── Feature Names: [Descriptive, not branded] Analytics, Pipeline, Coaching
├── Sub-Brand (if needed): [Brand] for Startups, [Brand] for Enterprise
└── Category Claim: The [Category] Platform
```

## Brand Equity Models

### Keller's Brand Equity Model (CBBE)

Kevin Lane Keller's Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) model builds brand equity
in four sequential stages:

```
            ┌────────────────┐
            │   RESONANCE    │  (Brand Loyalty, Community)
            │ "What about    │
            │  you and me?"  │
         ┌──┴────────────────┴──┐
         │     JUDGMENTS &      │  (Perceived Quality, Credibility)
         │     FEELINGS         │  (Warmth, Excitement, Security)
         │ "What about you?"   │
      ┌──┴──────────────────────┴──┐
      │      PERFORMANCE &         │  (Reliability, Effectiveness)
      │      IMAGERY               │  (User Profiles, Purchase Situations)
      │ "What are you?"            │
   ┌──┴────────────────────────────┴──┐
   │           SALIENCE                │  (Brand Awareness, Recognition)
   │ "Who are you?"                    │
   └───────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Building B2B Brand Equity Layer by Layer:**

| Layer | Objective | B2B Tactics |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Salience | Ensure buyers know you exist in the category | Content marketing, SEO, paid awareness, events |
| Performance/Imagery | Define what you do and who it is for | Product messaging, case studies, visual identity |
| Judgments/Feelings | Build perception of quality and trust | Customer reviews, analyst recognition, thought leadership |
| Resonance | Create emotional connection and loyalty | Community, customer advisory board, brand advocacy |

### Aaker's Brand Equity Model

David Aaker identifies five components of brand equity:
1. **Brand Awareness:** Recognition and recall
2. **Perceived Quality:** Buyer's perception of overall quality
3. **Brand Associations:** Mental connections to the brand
4. **Brand Loyalty:** Repeat purchase and advocacy behavior
5. **Proprietary Assets:** Patents, trademarks, channel relationships

## Byron Sharp — How Brands Grow

### Sharp's Evidence-Based Principles

Byron Sharp's "How Brands Grow" (2010, Ehrenberg-Bass Institute) challenged decades
of marketing assumptions with empirical evidence from purchasing data across dozens
of categories. While written primarily about consumer brands, the principles have
significant B2B implications.

### Key Principles

**Principle 1: Growth Comes from Penetration, Not Loyalty**
Brands grow primarily by acquiring new customers, not by getting existing customers
to buy more frequently. The implication for B2B: investing in new logo acquisition
is more important for growth than loyalty programs.

**Principle 2: Double Jeopardy**
Smaller brands have fewer customers AND those customers are slightly less loyal.
Larger brands benefit from both more customers and slightly higher loyalty. The
implication: brand size matters. Bigger brands have structural advantages.

**Principle 3: Mental Availability**
Brands that are more easily recalled in buying situations win more purchases.
Mental availability is increased by consistent, distinctive brand assets and broad
reach (not narrow targeting). The implication: reach more potential buyers more
often with distinctive brand cues.

**Principle 4: Physical Availability**
Brands that are easier to find and buy win more purchases. In B2B, this means:
distribution channels, marketplace presence, self-serve options, and easy procurement.

**Principle 5: Distinctive Assets over Differentiation**
Sharp argues that distinctiveness (being recognizable) matters more than
differentiation (being objectively different). Most products in a category are
functionally similar. What matters is being recognized and recalled. The implication:
invest in consistent visual identity, memorable brand elements, and broad awareness.

### Sharp's Implications for B2B Marketing

| Traditional B2B Belief | Sharp's Evidence | Implication |
|----------------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Focus on your niche | Reach the broadest relevant audience | Do not over-narrow targeting |
| Differentiate on features | Be distinctive (memorable and recognizable) | Invest in visual identity and brand assets |
| Loyalty programs drive growth | Penetration drives growth | Invest in new customer acquisition |
| Target only in-market buyers | Target all category buyers | Brand awareness for future buyers |
| Messaging must be unique | Messaging must be clear and simple | Category entry points, not unique claims |

## Brand Measurement

### Brand Health Metrics

| Metric | Measurement Method | Frequency |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| Aided Awareness | Survey: "Have you heard of [brand]?" | Quarterly |
| Unaided Awareness | Survey: "Name a [category] company" | Quarterly |
| Brand Consideration | Survey: "Would you consider [brand]?" | Quarterly |
| Brand Preference | Survey: "Which [category] would you choose?" | Quarterly |
| NPS | "How likely to recommend?" (0-10) | Quarterly |
| Brand Sentiment | Social listening, review site analysis | Monthly |
| Share of Voice | Media mentions, social mentions vs. competitors | Monthly |
| Branded Search Volume | Google Search Console, Google Trends | Monthly |
| Direct Traffic | Website direct visits (not search, not referral) | Monthly |

### Brand Tracker Survey

Conduct quarterly brand tracking surveys with your target audience (minimum 200
responses per wave for statistical reliability):

**Questions:**
1. "Which companies come to mind when you think about [category]?" (Unaided)
2. "Have you heard of these companies?" [List including yours and competitors] (Aided)
3. "Which of these would you consider purchasing?" (Consideration)
4. "Which of these do you perceive as the best?" (Preference)
5. "How would you describe [your brand] in 3 words?" (Association)
6. "How likely would you be to recommend [brand]?" (NPS)
