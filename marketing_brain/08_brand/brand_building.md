# Brand Building — Long-Term vs. Short-Term, Share of Voice, and Salience

## The Brand Building Imperative

Brand building is the long-term investment in mental availability — ensuring that
when a buyer enters a purchase cycle, your brand is one of the first they think of
and one of the few they seriously consider. It is the most undervalued and
under-invested activity in B2B marketing because its returns are delayed, diffuse,
and difficult to attribute to specific campaigns.

The evidence for brand building is overwhelming. Binet and Field's meta-analysis
of the IPA (Institute of Practitioners in Advertising) database — the largest
database of marketing effectiveness in the world — provides the empirical foundation
for how brand building and performance marketing should be balanced.

## Binet and Field: The 60/40 Rule

### The IPA Effectiveness Research

Les Binet and Peter Field analyzed over 1,000 marketing effectiveness case studies
in the IPA databank to determine what drives business growth. Their landmark
findings, published in "The Long and the Short of It" (2013) and updated in
"Effectiveness in Context" (2018), provide the most robust evidence base for
marketing budget allocation.

### Key Findings

**Finding 1: Brand Building and Sales Activation Serve Different Purposes**

| Dimension | Brand Building | Sales Activation |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| Objective | Create mental availability | Convert existing demand |
| Time Horizon | Long-term (6-12+ months) | Short-term (0-6 months) |
| Mechanism | Emotional priming, memory building | Rational triggers, calls to action |
| Audience | Broad (all category buyers) | Narrow (in-market buyers) |
| Effect Duration | Builds over time, persists for years | Immediate spike, decays quickly |
| Measurement | Brand tracking, share of voice, awareness | Clicks, conversions, pipeline, revenue |

**Finding 2: The Optimal Split Is Approximately 60% Brand / 40% Activation**

This ratio maximizes long-term business growth (profit, market share, pricing power).
Companies that over-invest in activation at the expense of brand see short-term
efficiency gains that mask long-term brand erosion and increasing CAC.

```
                  Long-Term Growth Effect
                         │
                         │        /‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾
                         │       /
                         │      /
                         │     / ← Optimal (~60%)
                         │    /
                         │   /
                         │  /
                         │ /
                         │/
                         └────────────────────── % of Budget on Brand
                         0%                    100%
```

**Finding 3: The Ratio Varies by Context**

Binet and Field's updated research (2018) showed the optimal split varies:

| Context | Brand % | Activation % | Rationale |
|---------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| B2C Mass Market | 60% | 40% | Large audience, emotional decisions |
| B2C Niche | 55% | 45% | Smaller audience, still emotional |
| B2B (General) | 46% | 54% | More rational, higher consideration |
| B2B (Large Enterprise) | 50% | 50% | Longer cycles, trust matters more |
| Online / Digital First | 40% | 60% | Attribution possible, activation measurable |
| New Category / Startup | 65% | 35% | Must build awareness before capture |
| Mature Brand | 45% | 55% | Can harvest existing awareness |

### Applying 60/40 (or 46/54) to B2B SaaS

**Brand Building Activities (40-50% of budget):**
- Content marketing (ungated, thought leadership, podcasts)
- Brand advertising (LinkedIn awareness campaigns, podcast sponsorships)
- Event sponsorships and speaking
- PR and media relations
- Community building
- Social media presence (organic)
- SEO content investment

**Sales Activation Activities (50-60% of budget):**
- Demand generation campaigns (gated content, webinars)
- Paid search (conversion-focused)
- SDR/BDR programs
- ABM campaigns
- Retargeting
- Sales enablement content
- Direct response email campaigns

## Share of Voice (SOV) and Share of Market (SOM)

### The SOV-SOM Relationship

Binet and Field's research also established the relationship between Share of Voice
(how much you spend relative to competitors) and Share of Market (your market share):

**The Excess Share of Voice (ESOV) Principle:**
When SOV exceeds SOM, market share tends to grow. When SOV is below SOM, market
share tends to decline.

```
ESOV = Share of Voice - Share of Market

If ESOV > 0: Brand is investing for growth (share gain expected)
If ESOV = 0: Brand is maintaining position (stable share)
If ESOV < 0: Brand is under-investing (share loss expected)
```

**The Growth Multiplier:**
For every 10 percentage points of ESOV, market share tends to grow by approximately
0.5-0.7 percentage points per year (IPA data, B2B-adjusted).

**Example:**
```
Your SOM: 10%
Your SOV: 18%
ESOV: +8 percentage points

Expected annual share growth: 0.4-0.6 pp
Expected SOM next year: 10.4-10.6%
```

### Measuring Share of Voice in B2B

B2B SOV is harder to measure than B2C (no Nielsen rating equivalents). Proxy metrics:

| SOV Proxy | Measurement | Tool |
|-----------|-------------|------|
| Share of Search | Branded search volume vs. competitors | Google Trends, SEMrush |
| Share of Mentions | Media and social mentions vs. competitors | Sprout Social, Meltwater |
| Share of Impressions | Ad impression share vs. category | LinkedIn Campaign Manager, Google Ads |
| Share of Content | Content volume and ranking vs. competitors | Ahrefs, SEMrush |
| Share of Events | Event presence vs. competitors | Manual tracking |
| Share of Review Volume | Review count on G2/TrustRadius vs. competitors | G2, TrustRadius |

## Brand Salience

### What Is Brand Salience?

Brand salience is the probability that a brand comes to mind in a buying situation.
It is the most important brand metric because it determines whether you make it into
the consideration set. Jenni Romaniuk and Byron Sharp (Ehrenberg-Bass Institute)
define salience as the quantity and quality of memory structures linked to a brand.

### Building Brand Salience

**1. Category Entry Points (CEPs)**
CEPs are the mental triggers that cause a buyer to start thinking about your category:
- Situational: "I am preparing for board meeting" → analytics tool
- Emotional: "I am frustrated with our current vendor" → alternative search
- Functional: "I need to improve forecast accuracy" → revenue intelligence
- Social: "My peer recommended this at the conference" → consideration

**Map your CEPs and ensure your brand is associated with each one.**

**2. Distinctive Brand Assets (DBAs)**
DBAs are the recognizable elements that uniquely identify your brand:
- Logo and wordmark
- Color palette
- Visual style (illustration style, photography treatment)
- Sonic identity (if applicable)
- Tagline or category claim
- Mascot or character (if applicable)
- Typography

**DBA Effectiveness Requirements:**
- Unique: Only your brand uses this element
- Prevalent: It appears across all brand touchpoints
- Famous: Your target audience recognizes it as yours

**3. Consistent Reach**
Brand salience is built through repeated exposure across multiple touchpoints.
Consistency matters more than creativity. A buyer who sees your brand 20 times
over 6 months in consistent visual treatment builds stronger memory structures
than one who sees it twice in brilliant but inconsistent creative.

### The Consideration Set

Research consistently shows that B2B buyers consider 2-4 vendors in any purchase
decision (Gartner). The brands in the initial consideration set win the vast
majority of deals. Brands that must "get on the list" through outbound or competitive
displacement face significantly higher acquisition costs.

**Brand building's primary ROI mechanism:** Ensuring your brand is in the initial
consideration set, which reduces CAC and increases win rates.

### Brand Building ROI

Brand building ROI is measured in:
1. **Decreasing CAC over time** (brand awareness reduces acquisition friction)
2. **Increasing win rate** (brand in consideration set wins more)
3. **Pricing power** (strong brands command 10-20% premium)
4. **Employee acquisition** (strong brands attract better talent)
5. **Investor confidence** (brand equity increases valuation multiples)

### The Brand Tax for Under-Investment

Companies that cut brand investment see a characteristic pattern:
- Year 1: No visible impact (living off existing brand equity)
- Year 2: CAC begins rising 10-20%
- Year 3: Win rates decline, competitive displacement increases
- Year 4+: Significant market share erosion, recovery requires 2-3x the investment that
  was cut

## Brand Campaign Design

### Brand Campaign vs. Performance Campaign

| Element | Brand Campaign | Performance Campaign |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| Objective | Awareness, association, salience | Leads, pipeline, revenue |
| CTA | Soft ("Learn more", "Follow us") | Hard ("Request demo", "Start trial") |
| Audience | Broad (all category buyers) | Narrow (in-market, ICP-fit) |
| Creative | Emotional, storytelling, distinctive | Rational, benefit-focused, direct |
| Measurement | Brand tracking, aided awareness, SOV | CPA, ROAS, pipeline |
| Duration | Ongoing (always-on) | Campaign-based (time-bound) |
| Budget | Consistent spend (no peaks/valleys) | Variable (campaign-driven) |

### Brand Campaign Formats

| Format | Channel | Duration | Investment |
|--------|---------|----------|-----------|
| Video Series | YouTube, LinkedIn, social | 6-12 episodes | $50-200K production |
| Podcast | Apple, Spotify, YouTube | 26+ episodes/year | $30-100K annual |
| Annual Research Report | Website, syndication | Annual publication | $20-50K |
| Thought Leader Program | LinkedIn, conferences | Ongoing | Time + $10-30K |
| Event Sponsorship | Industry conferences | 2-4 events/year | $50-200K per event |
| Brand Advertising | LinkedIn, programmatic | Always-on | $5-20K/month |
| Community | Slack, Discord, Forum | Ongoing | $5-15K/month |

**Brand building is the hardest investment to justify and the most valuable to make.
Short-term performance marketing wins deals. Long-term brand building wins markets.**
