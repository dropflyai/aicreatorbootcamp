# Positioning Strategy -- Owning a Place in the Mind

## Purpose

Positioning is the most consequential marketing decision. It determines how a
brand is perceived relative to alternatives in the minds of the target market.
Every subsequent marketing decision -- messaging, channels, creative, pricing --
flows from positioning. Get positioning wrong and everything downstream fails.

Reference: Ries & Trout, Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind (1981)

---

## What Positioning Is (and Is Not)

**Positioning IS:**
- What you do to the mind of the prospect (Ries & Trout)
- The single most important concept in marketing strategy
- A declaration of who you are FOR and what you are BEST at
- Relative to alternatives (always defined against something)

**Positioning IS NOT:**
- A tagline or slogan (those express positioning, they are not positioning)
- A feature list
- A mission statement
- What you do -- it is what you OWN in the customer's mind

## The Ries & Trout Positioning Framework

### Core Principles

**Principle 1: The Mind is Limited**
People can only hold a small number of brands in any category (typically 2-7).
You must either enter an existing position or create a new one.

**Principle 2: The Mind Hates Confusion**
Simplicity wins. The most powerful positioning is a single word or concept owned
in the mind. Volvo = safety. FedEx = overnight. Salesforce = CRM.

**Principle 3: The Mind Resists Change**
Once positioned, repositioning is extremely difficult. It is easier to create a
new position than to change an existing one.

**Principle 4: The Mind Organizes by Category**
People think in categories. If you are not the leader of an existing category,
create a new one where you can be first.

### Positioning Strategies (Ries & Trout)

**1. Be First (Leader Positioning)**
The first brand to enter a category owns the category position.
- Coca-Cola in cola, Google in search, Kleenex in tissues
- If you are first, DEFEND the position: "The original," "The standard"

**2. Be Second (Against Positioning)**
Position directly against the leader by being the opposite.
- Pepsi vs. Coca-Cola ("The Choice of a New Generation")
- Avis vs. Hertz ("We try harder")
- Mac vs. PC (creative vs. corporate)

**3. Find an Unoccupied Position (Repositioning)**
Identify an attribute or segment the leader does not own.
- High price: "The most expensive, and worth it"
- Low price: "No frills, honest pricing"
- Specific audience: "Built for developers" (when leaders serve everyone)
- Specific use case: "The [category] for [specific context]"

**4. Create a New Category (Reframing)**
If you cannot win in the existing category, create a new one.
- Salesforce did not sell "software" -- they sold "cloud" (No Software logo)
- HubSpot did not sell "marketing tools" -- they sold "inbound marketing"
- This is the foundation of category design

## The April Dunford Positioning Method (Obviously Awesome, 2019)

A practitioner framework for B2B/tech positioning. Five components evaluated
in sequence:

### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives
What would customers do if your product did not exist?
- Not just direct competitors -- include status quo and manual workarounds
- This is your TRUE competitive context
- Example: Competitor might be "Excel spreadsheets," not another SaaS tool

### Step 2: Unique Attributes
What do you have that the alternatives do not?
- Features, capabilities, integrations, data, expertise, approach
- Must be demonstrably true, not aspirational
- Focus on what is truly unique, not merely different

### Step 3: Value (and Proof)
What value do those attributes enable for customers?
- Translate features into outcomes
- Each unique attribute should map to a specific value
- Proof: Case studies, data, benchmarks, testimonials

### Step 4: Target Customer Characteristics
Who cares most about the value you deliver?
- The best positioning targets customers who most value your unique strengths
- Define by: characteristics, context, and urgency of need
- "Desperate" customers are better than "interested" customers

### Step 5: Market Category
What category helps customers understand your value fastest?
Three category strategies:
1. **Existing category:** "We are a [known category] that [differentiator]"
2. **Subcategory:** "We are a [new type] of [known category]"
3. **New category:** "We created [new category] for [audience]"

### Dunford Positioning Canvas

```
+------------------------+----------------------------------+
| Competitive            | What alternatives exist?         |
| Alternatives           |                                  |
+------------------------+----------------------------------+
| Unique Attributes      | What do we have that they        |
|                        | do not?                          |
+------------------------+----------------------------------+
| Value                  | What outcomes does this enable?   |
| (and Proof)            | What evidence supports this?     |
+------------------------+----------------------------------+
| Target Customer        | Who cares most about this value? |
| Characteristics        |                                  |
+------------------------+----------------------------------+
| Market Category        | What category frames us best?    |
+------------------------+----------------------------------+
```

## Category Design (Play Bigger, 2016)

### The Category King Effect
Research by Play Bigger found that the company that defines and leads a new
category captures approximately 76% of the total category economics. Category
kings don't just win market share; they create and own the market.

### The Three Lightning Strikes
Category design requires simultaneous development of:

1. **Company:** The organization and culture that serves the category
2. **Product:** The offering that defines the category experience
3. **Category:** The market context itself (the problem frame)

### Category Design Process

**Step 1: Category Discovery**
- What problem are we really solving?
- How does the world currently frame this problem?
- What would the world look like if this problem were solved?

**Step 2: Category Blueprint (POV)**
Develop a Point of View document:
- Define the problem in a new way
- Explain why now (technology shift, cultural shift, regulatory shift)
- Describe the "promised land" (the future state)
- Explain why you are uniquely positioned to lead

**Step 3: Category Evangelism**
- Educate the market on the new problem frame
- Analyst relations, thought leadership, content
- Speaking, publishing, community building
- The goal is to change how people think about the problem

**Step 4: Category Execution**
- Product embodies the category definition
- Marketing consistently reinforces the category frame
- Sales uses category language, not feature language
- Company makes strategic decisions through the category lens

### Category Design vs. Positioning

| Dimension | Traditional Positioning | Category Design |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Frame | Relative to existing competitors | Creates a new frame |
| Goal | Win in existing category | Create and own new category |
| Timeline | Can be rapid | Takes 2-5 years |
| Risk | Lower (known market) | Higher (unproven market) |
| Reward | Market share | Market ownership |
| When to use | Category exists, clear leaders | No category fits well |

## Positioning Statement Frameworks

### Geoffrey Moore's Positioning Statement
```
For [target customer]
Who [statement of need or opportunity]
[Product name] is a [product category]
That [statement of key benefit]
Unlike [primary competitive alternative]
Our product [statement of primary differentiation]
```

### The XYZ Framework (Simple)
```
We help [X audience] do [Y outcome] through [Z unique approach].
```

### The Onliness Statement (Marty Neumeier)
```
Our [offering] is the only [category]
that [key differentiator]
for [target customer]
who [need or desire]
during [relevant context or occasion]
because [reason to believe].
```

## Positioning Evaluation Criteria

Score any positioning statement on these dimensions:

| Criterion | Question | Score 1-5 |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Relevance | Does the target customer care about this? | |
| Uniqueness | Can only we credibly claim this? | |
| Credibility | Can we prove this is true? | |
| Sustainability | Can this be defended over time? | |
| Clarity | Can a 12-year-old understand it? | |
| Memorability | Will they remember this tomorrow? | |

**Minimum viable score: 4+ on all dimensions.**
**If any dimension scores below 3, the positioning must be reworked.**

## Positioning Traps to Avoid

### Trap 1: Positioning by Features
"We have more features than competitors."
Problem: Features are easily copied. Features do not stick in minds.

### Trap 2: Positioning for Everyone
"We serve all companies of all sizes."
Problem: When you are for everyone, you are for no one.

### Trap 3: Positioning by Technology
"We use AI / blockchain / quantum."
Problem: Technology is a means, not a value. Customers buy outcomes.

### Trap 4: Positioning by Aspiration
"We want to be known for innovation."
Problem: Positioning must be based on current truth, not future hope.

### Trap 5: Internal Positioning
"We are disrupting the industry."
Problem: Positioning lives in the customer's mind, not yours.

## Positioning Validation Process

### Step 1: Internal Alignment Test
Can every team member articulate the positioning consistently?
If not, the positioning is too complex or not well-communicated.

### Step 2: Customer Echo Test
Do customers describe you the way you position yourself?
Interview 10+ customers. If their language does not match, there is a gap.

### Step 3: Competitive Distinctiveness Test
Show your positioning (anonymized) alongside competitors.
Can prospects identify which is yours? Can they see the difference?

### Step 4: Sales Conversation Test
Does positioning help sales conversations or create confusion?
If sales is "going off script," the positioning may not resonate.

### Step 5: Market Test
Do campaigns built on this positioning outperform alternatives?
A/B test positioning-driven messaging vs. feature-driven messaging.

## Repositioning Decision Tree

```
Should we reposition?
  |
  +-- Is our current positioning working? (growing, winning, resonating)
  |     |
  |     +-- Yes --> DO NOT reposition. Strengthen and extend.
  |     |
  |     +-- No --> Continue evaluation
  |
  +-- Why is it failing?
        |
        +-- Market has changed --> Consider category redesign
        |
        +-- Competitors have crowded our position --> Find unoccupied space
        |
        +-- Customers do not understand us --> Clarify (messaging issue, not
        |                                       positioning issue)
        |
        +-- We have outgrown our position --> Expand carefully (sub-brand?)
```
