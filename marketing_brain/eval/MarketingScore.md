# MarketingScore.md -- 8-Dimension Scoring Rubric for Marketing Brain

> Version: 1.0
> Last Updated: 2026-02-03
> Owner: Marketing Brain / CEO Brain
> Purpose: Every output produced by the Marketing Brain is scored across
> eight dimensions. A score of 3 ("Meets Standard") is the minimum for
> shipping. Any dimension scoring 1 triggers an automatic block. Two or
> more dimensions at 1 triggers escalation to the CEO Brain.

---

## How to Use This Rubric

1. After the Marketing Brain produces ANY artifact (positioning doc, campaign
   plan, channel strategy, creative brief, budget allocation, competitive
   analysis), score it across all eight dimensions.
2. Record each dimension score (1, 3, or 5) in the Accountability Protocol.
3. If any dimension scores 1, the artifact MUST NOT ship. Rework is required.
4. If any dimension scores 5, document what made it exceptional.
5. Composite score = sum of all eight. Maximum = 40. Minimum to ship = 24.

## Dimension 1: Positioning Clarity

**What it measures:** Whether the brand and product positioning is distinct,
defensible, and instantly communicable. Can a stranger understand what you do,
for whom, and why it is better in under 10 seconds?

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- Positioning statement is generic ("We help businesses grow").
- No clear differentiation from competitors.
- Positioning changes with every campaign (no consistency).
- Features listed instead of benefits or outcomes.
- "We are the Uber of X" used as actual positioning (lazy analogy, not strategy).
- Target audience described as "everyone" or "businesses."
- Value proposition cannot be stated in one sentence.
- Positioning contradicts actual product capabilities.
- Internal team cannot consistently articulate the positioning.

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- Positioning follows a clear framework: For [target], who [need/pain],
  [product] is a [category] that [key benefit], unlike [alternative],
  we [differentiator].
- Differentiation is based on a genuine, sustainable advantage (not just
  price or a feature that can be copied next quarter).
- Positioning validated with at least 5 target customers (they "get it").
- Positioning stress-tested against the 3 closest competitors.
- Internal team alignment: sales, support, and product can all articulate
  the same positioning.
- Positioning guides what to say AND what NOT to say.

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Positioning creates a new category or reframes an existing one.
- Competitive alternatives explicitly named and the "why not them" is clear.
- Positioning resonates at emotional level, not just rational level.
- Customer language used (not internal jargon or marketing-speak).
- Positioning has been A/B tested with messaging experiments.
- Brand architecture clear: how this product relates to parent brand and
  other products in the portfolio.
- Positioning evolves strategically (not reactively) with market changes.
- "CEO test" passed: the CEO can deliver the positioning impromptu and
  it sounds natural.

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] No positioning document exists.
- [ ] Positioning does not mention a specific target audience.
- [ ] Differentiation claim cannot be substantiated.
- [ ] Sales team and marketing team give different positioning.

### Self-Audit Questions
- If I removed our company name, would this positioning apply to a competitor?
- Can our target customer finish the sentence "I use [product] because..."?
- Is our differentiation something we can defend for 2+ years?
- Would a journalist be able to write one paragraph about us using only
  our positioning?

## Dimension 2: Audience Precision

**What it measures:** Whether the target audience is defined with enough
specificity to make every marketing dollar count. Broad targeting is
expensive and ineffective.

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) not defined.
- Audience described only in demographics ("25-45 year old professionals").
- No behavioral or psychographic segmentation.
- Same message sent to all segments.
- No data on audience size or accessibility.
- Buyer persona is fictional (not based on real customer interviews).
- No distinction between user, buyer, and influencer.
- Targeting so broad that CAC is structurally unsustainable.

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- ICP defined with firmographic (B2B) or behavioral (B2C) criteria.
- At least 3 segments identified with distinct needs and messaging.
- Buyer persona based on real customer interviews (minimum 10).
- Each persona includes: pain points, goals, objections, information sources,
  buying process, decision criteria.
- Segment size estimated (TAM, SAM, SOM).
- Channel preferences mapped per segment (where do they spend attention?).
- Negative persona defined (who is NOT our customer and why).

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Segments validated with behavioral data (not just interviews).
- Predictive scoring model for lead/customer quality.
- Micro-segments identified for hyper-targeted campaigns.
- Audience evolution tracked over time (are segments growing/shrinking?).
- Competitive audience overlap mapped (who else is targeting them?).
- Audience insights shared back to Product Brain to influence roadmap.
- Look-alike audience modeling based on best customers.
- Jobs-to-be-done framework integrated with segmentation.
- Willingness-to-pay data integrated into segment profiles.

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] No ICP defined.
- [ ] Persona based on assumptions, not customer research.
- [ ] No negative persona (no definition of who NOT to target).
- [ ] Same message used for all segments.

### Self-Audit Questions
- If I put our best customer and worst customer in a room, can I articulate
  what makes them different?
- Can I find 10,000 more people exactly like our best customers? How?
- Am I targeting people who CAN buy, or just people who SHOULD buy?
- Is my audience definition tight enough to say "no" to attractive leads
  that do not fit?

## Dimension 3: Funnel Math

**What it measures:** Whether marketing plans are grounded in quantitative
funnel analysis, not wishful thinking. Every campaign must trace a clear
path from spend to revenue.

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- No funnel model exists.
- Marketing plan has no financial projections.
- "Awareness" is the primary goal with no connection to revenue.
- Conversion rates assumed, not measured.
- CAC not calculated or not known.
- LTV not calculated or not known.
- LTV/CAC ratio not computed.
- Budget allocated by gut feel or "what we did last year."
- No attribution model (cannot connect spend to outcomes).

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- Full funnel model: impressions -> clicks -> leads -> MQLs -> SQLs ->
  opportunities -> closed-won (or equivalent B2C funnel).
- Conversion rates measured at each stage with confidence intervals.
- CAC calculated by channel.
- LTV calculated by segment.
- LTV/CAC ratio > 3:1 for paid channels (or justified exception).
- Payback period calculated and within acceptable range.
- Budget allocation driven by funnel math, not precedent.
- Unit economics positive (or within X months of positive with a plan).
- Sensitivity analysis: what if conversion rates drop 20%?

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Cohort-based funnel analysis (funnel metrics tracked by acquisition cohort).
- Marginal CAC vs. average CAC distinguished (what does the NEXT customer
  cost, not the average customer?).
- Channel saturation modeled (diminishing returns at higher spend).
- Funnel bottleneck identified with quantified opportunity.
- Multi-touch attribution model (not just first-touch or last-touch).
- Revenue attribution with hold-out experiments (gold standard).
- Monthly cohort LTV curves showing maturation pattern.
- Scenario modeling: best case, base case, worst case with probabilities.
- Funnel metrics integrated with product metrics (not siloed).

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] No CAC calculation.
- [ ] No LTV calculation.
- [ ] LTV/CAC ratio unknown.
- [ ] Budget approved without funnel model.
- [ ] "Brand awareness" is the sole KPI with no downstream metrics.

### Self-Audit Questions
- If I double the budget, will revenue double? Why or why not?
- Which funnel stage has the biggest drop-off? Can I fix it?
- Am I measuring average CAC when I should be measuring marginal CAC?
- If every paid channel stopped today, how much organic revenue would persist?

## Dimension 4: Channel Strategy

**What it measures:** Whether channels are selected based on audience fit,
unit economics, and scalability -- not just familiarity or trends.

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- Channels selected because "that is what everyone does."
- No channel-market fit analysis (audience is on different channels).
- All budget concentrated in one channel with no diversification plan.
- Channel performance not measured or measured with wrong metrics.
- Organic and paid channels not distinguished in strategy.
- No owned media strategy (entirely dependent on rented platforms).
- Channel fatigue / ad fatigue not monitored.
- No experimentation budget for new channels.

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- Channel selection based on: where target audience spends time + unit
  economics viability + scalability potential.
- Channel-market fit validated with small experiments before major investment.
- Budget allocated proportionally to proven ROI, with 10-20% allocated to
  experimentation.
- Owned (blog, email, community) + earned (PR, word-of-mouth) + paid
  (ads, sponsorships) balanced.
- Channel performance tracked weekly with clear KPIs per channel.
- Diminishing returns detected and budget reallocated accordingly.
- Cross-channel interaction effects understood (channels amplify each other).

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Channel portfolio theory applied (diversification against platform risk).
- First-mover advantage identified in underexploited channels.
- Channel-specific creative optimization (not same ad on every platform).
- Organic flywheel strategy: paid acquisition feeds organic growth mechanism.
- Platform dependency risk assessed and mitigated (not >50% of acquisition
  from a single channel without mitigation plan).
- New channel evaluated quarterly against emerging audience behaviors.
- Attribution experiments: hold-out tests per channel to measure true
  incremental impact.
- Content distribution strategy tailored per channel (not cross-posting).

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] No channel performance data.
- [ ] >70% of budget in a single channel with no diversification plan.
- [ ] Channel selected because "our competitor uses it."
- [ ] No experimentation budget for new channels.

### Self-Audit Questions
- If [primary channel] shut down tomorrow, could we survive?
- Am I investing in channels where my audience IS or where I WISH they were?
- What is the marginal cost of the next customer from each channel?
- Am I measuring channel performance or platform vanity metrics?

## Dimension 5: Creative Quality

**What it measures:** Whether marketing creative (copy, design, video, content)
is compelling, on-brand, and effective at driving the desired action.

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- Creative not aligned with positioning.
- No clear call to action (CTA).
- Creative tested on zero customers before launch.
- Copy is feature-focused, not benefit-focused.
- No visual consistency with brand guidelines.
- Creative is interchangeable with competitor's creative (no distinctiveness).
- One creative asset used for all audiences and channels.
- Headline does not communicate the primary value proposition.
- No emotional hook or relevance trigger.

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- Creative clearly communicates the value proposition within 3 seconds
  (headline test).
- Benefit-led, not feature-led messaging.
- Single, clear CTA that matches the funnel stage.
- A/B tested: at least 2 variations tested for key campaigns.
- Brand consistency: visual identity, tone of voice, messaging hierarchy.
- Channel-optimized: creative adapted for platform specifications and norms.
- Social proof included where applicable (testimonials, logos, numbers).
- Objection handling addressed in the creative or landing page.

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Creative based on customer voice (actual language customers use).
- Multi-variant testing with statistically significant sample sizes.
- Creative performance data feeds into a shared learning repository.
- Emotional resonance tested (not just click-through rates).
- Creative produced in volume for rapid iteration (not precious one-offs).
- Storytelling arc across campaign touchpoints (not isolated ads).
- User-generated content integrated into creative mix.
- Creative wear-out detected and refreshed proactively.
- Personalized creative per segment (not just personalized targeting).

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] No A/B testing of creative.
- [ ] Creative launched without brand guidelines review.
- [ ] CTA missing or unclear.
- [ ] Creative contradicts product positioning.

### Self-Audit Questions
- If I covered the logo, would someone know this is our brand?
- Would I click on this ad if I saw it in my own feed?
- Does the landing page deliver on the promise of the ad?
- Am I measuring creative effectiveness or just production quality?

## Dimension 6: Attribution Rigor

**What it measures:** Whether the Marketing Brain can accurately determine
which activities drive outcomes and which are wasteful.

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- No attribution model.
- Last-click attribution used as sole model (ignores all upper-funnel activity).
- Branded search spend attributed as "new demand generation."
- Organic and paid conflated in reporting.
- Self-reported attribution ("How did you hear about us?") used as sole source.
- Marketing takes credit for sales-sourced pipeline.
- No incrementality testing (cannot distinguish marketing-driven from organic).
- UTM parameters missing or inconsistently applied.

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- Multi-touch attribution model implemented (linear, time-decay, or
  position-based at minimum).
- Branded vs. non-branded paid search separated.
- UTM parameters standardized and enforced across all channels.
- Self-reported attribution collected but not used as sole source.
- Incrementality tested for at least the top-spending channel (hold-out
  experiment or geo-lift).
- Attribution reports distinguish between influenced revenue and sourced
  revenue.
- Data Brain consulted for attribution methodology validation.

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Multi-channel incrementality testing program (geo-lift, matched market,
  or synthetic control for each major channel).
- Media mix modeling (MMM) used alongside multi-touch attribution for
  triangulation.
- Algorithmic attribution (data-driven, Shapley value-based, or Markov chain).
- Holdout experiments run quarterly for top channels.
- Incrementality of branded search measured (what would happen without the
  spend?).
- Attribution model validated against hold-out experiments.
- Full-funnel attribution: content consumption, events, and community
  contributions tracked alongside direct-response activities.
- Attribution data accessible to all stakeholders via self-service dashboard.

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] No attribution model of any kind.
- [ ] Branded search reported as demand generation.
- [ ] No incrementality testing in the past 6 months.
- [ ] Marketing claims credit for 100% of pipeline.

### Self-Audit Questions
- If I turned off all paid marketing, how much revenue would we lose? Do I
  actually know this or am I guessing?
- Am I measuring what marketing CAUSED or what marketing TOUCHED?
- Could I defend our attribution model to a skeptical CFO?
- What percentage of attributed revenue would survive an incrementality test?

## Dimension 7: Growth Sustainability

**What it measures:** Whether growth strategies are sustainable or whether they
rely on unsustainable practices (burning cash, spamming, unsustainable
incentives, misleading promises).

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- Growth entirely dependent on paid acquisition with no organic component.
- CAC trending up quarter-over-quarter with no plan to reverse.
- Retention not measured or declining (leaky bucket).
- Growth tactics rely on dark patterns (misleading copy, deceptive UX,
  confusing unsubscribe flows).
- Referral program that is pure incentive arbitrage (no genuine recommendation).
- Viral coefficient calculated but not sustained beyond initial burst.
- Growth at all costs mentality (no quality or retention filters on acquisition).
- SEO strategy relies on tactics penalizable by search engines.
- Email list bought, not built (compliance and deliverability risk).

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- Organic growth trajectory established or plan to establish it.
- Paid CAC trend stable or declining.
- Retention rate measured and stable or improving.
- At least one compounding growth mechanism identified (content, community,
  SEO, word-of-mouth, network effects).
- Growth quality measured: not just quantity of signups but quality
  (activation rate, retention rate, revenue per customer).
- Compliance with all platform terms of service and advertising regulations.
- Email list built through opt-in with clear value exchange.
- Sustainable content strategy (not just paid distribution of content).

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Multiple compounding growth mechanisms operating simultaneously.
- Viral loop or product-led growth mechanism with measured viral coefficient.
- Community-driven growth with genuine engagement (not just a Discord with
  announcements).
- Paid acquisition used strategically to accelerate organic, not substitute
  for it.
- Growth model includes second-order effects (happy customers generate
  referrals, content, case studies).
- Retention marketing as a growth lever (expansion revenue from existing
  customers exceeds churn).
- Net Revenue Retention > 100%.
- Brand as a growth asset: brand awareness reduces CAC over time (measured).

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] No organic growth strategy.
- [ ] CAC increasing with no plan to address.
- [ ] Retention not measured.
- [ ] Any dark pattern or deceptive growth tactic used.
- [ ] Email compliance violations (CAN-SPAM, GDPR).

### Self-Audit Questions
- If we stopped all paid marketing for 3 months, would the company survive?
- Is our growth rate accelerating or decelerating? Why?
- Are we acquiring customers who stay, or churning through new users?
- Will today's growth strategy still work in 2 years, or are we exploiting
  a window that will close?

## Dimension 8: Competitive Awareness

**What it measures:** Whether the Marketing Brain has a thorough, current
understanding of the competitive landscape and uses it to make better
strategic decisions.

### Score 1 -- Failing (Automatic Block)
- Cannot name the top 3 competitors.
- No competitive analysis document exists.
- Competitive knowledge is anecdotal ("I heard they are doing X").
- Competitor positioning not analyzed.
- Competitor pricing not tracked.
- No win/loss analysis for competitive deals.
- Ignoring indirect competitors and substitutes.
- Competitive response: either "ignore them" or "copy everything."

### Score 3 -- Meets Standard
- Top 5-10 competitors identified and profiled.
- Competitive matrix: features, pricing, positioning, target market.
- Win/loss analysis conducted (at least 10 competitive deals analyzed).
- Competitive positioning mapped (perceptual map or positioning spectrum).
- Competitor messaging monitored (ad library, blog, social media).
- Competitive battlecard maintained and updated quarterly for sales team.
- Indirect competitors and substitutes (including "do nothing") analyzed.

### Score 5 -- Exceptional
- Real-time competitive monitoring system (automated alerts for competitor
  changes: pricing, features, positioning, hiring, funding).
- Competitive intelligence feeds into product roadmap (Product Brain
  collaboration).
- Offensive positioning: proactively shaping the narrative about the category.
- Competitor customer interviews conducted (ethical, from churned competitor
  customers).
- Competitive response playbook: pre-planned responses to likely competitor
  moves.
- Market share estimated and tracked over time.
- Blue ocean analysis: identifying uncontested market space.
- Competitive moat analysis: what makes our position defensible?

### Automatic Failure Conditions
- [ ] Cannot name top 3 competitors.
- [ ] No competitive analysis document.
- [ ] No win/loss analysis.
- [ ] Competitive intelligence is more than 6 months old.

### Self-Audit Questions
- What is our competitor's strongest argument against us?
- If I were a prospect comparing us to [competitor], what would confuse me?
- What competitor move would most threaten our growth? Do we have a response?
- Are we competing on dimensions that matter to customers or to us?

## Composite Scoring Table

| Composite Score | Rating | Action |
|-----------------|--------|--------|
| 36-40 | World-Class | Document as exemplar. Share methodology. |
| 30-35 | Strong | Ship with confidence. Minor improvements optional. |
| 24-29 | Meets Standard | Ship. Address weak dimensions next iteration. |
| 16-23 | Below Standard | Do NOT ship. Rework weakest dimensions. |
| 8-15 | Critical Failure | Full rework. Escalate to CEO Brain. |

---

## Escalation Triggers

1. **Positioning Clarity scores 1** -> All downstream marketing is built on
   a broken foundation. Full stop. Repositioning sprint required.
2. **Funnel Math scores 1** -> Marketing is spending without accountability.
   Budget freeze until funnel model is built.
3. **Attribution Rigor scores 1** -> Cannot trust any ROI claims. Implement
   basic attribution before next campaign.
4. **Growth Sustainability scores 1** -> Unsustainable growth practices risk
   regulatory, reputational, or financial damage. CEO Brain escalation.
5. **Competitive Awareness scores 1 for 2+ consecutive artifacts** ->
   Competitive deep-dive sprint before next strategy decision.
6. **Any two dimensions score 1 simultaneously** -> Escalate to CEO Brain.
   Marketing Brain capacity or competence review.
7. **Creative Quality scores 1 on customer-facing campaign** -> Campaign
   paused. Brand damage assessment required.

## Domain-Specific Failure Mode Checklist

Before scoring, check for these common marketing failure modes:

### Strategic Failures
- [ ] Marketing strategy disconnected from product strategy.
- [ ] Brand promise exceeds product reality (trust debt).
- [ ] Market positioning copied from a competitor.
- [ ] Strategy changes every quarter without learning.

### Execution Failures
- [ ] Campaign launched without tracking / UTM parameters.
- [ ] Landing page does not match ad creative.
- [ ] Email sent to wrong segment.
- [ ] A/B test run without proper sample size.
- [ ] Budget overspent on underperforming channel.

### Measurement Failures
- [ ] Vanity metrics reported as KPIs.
- [ ] Attribution model not validated with incrementality tests.
- [ ] Cohort effects ignored (treating all customers as equal).
- [ ] Seasonality not accounted for in performance analysis.

---

## Annual Calibration

Every quarter, the Marketing Brain must:

1. Review all artifacts scored in the previous quarter.
2. Identify the dimension with the lowest average score.
3. Benchmark against industry best practices for that dimension.
4. Recalibrate scoring criteria based on market evolution.
5. Archive scoring data for longitudinal analysis.
6. Update competitive profiles and positioning as market shifts.

---

*Marketing without measurement is guessing with a budget. Every claim must
be defensible. Every dollar must be accountable.*
