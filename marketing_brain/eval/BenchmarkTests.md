## How to Use These Benchmarks

1. Present the scenario to the Marketing Brain without the model answer.
2. Score the response against the model answer using the MarketingScore rubric.
3. A passing response must address ALL key points in the model answer.
4. A superior response adds considerations not covered.
5. Record results in the Accountability Protocol.
6. Re-test quarterly.

## Test 1: Rising CAC Diagnosis

**Scenario:** Customer Acquisition Cost has increased 40% over 6 months
across ALL paid channels (Google Ads, Meta, LinkedIn). The marketing team
says "CPMs are going up industry-wide." Diagnose the problem and propose
solutions.

**Model Answer:**

"CPMs are going up" is a symptom explanation, not a diagnosis. A 40% CAC
increase across ALL channels simultaneously suggests systemic issues beyond
platform costs.

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. DECOMPOSE CAC: CAC = Spend / Customers. But also: CAC = CPM / (CTR x
   Conversion Rate). Which component is driving the increase?
   - If CPM is up but CTR and conversion are stable: market cost increase.
   - If CTR is declining: creative fatigue or audience saturation.
   - If conversion is declining: landing page degradation, product-market
     fit shift, or targeting degradation.
2. AUDIENCE SATURATION: After 6 months, you may have exhausted the most
   receptive audience. The remaining audience is harder to convert. Check:
   - Frequency metrics: are you showing ads to the same people too often?
   - Audience overlap between channels: are Google and Meta targeting the
     same people?
   - Have you expanded targeting to less qualified audiences?
3. CREATIVE FATIGUE: Even the best creative wears out. Check:
   - When was creative last refreshed?
   - CTR trend over time (declining CTR = creative fatigue signal).
   - Frequency x CTR interaction (high frequency + declining CTR = fatigue).
4. COMPETITIVE DYNAMICS: If ALL channels are up, competitors may be bidding
   more aggressively. Check:
   - Auction insights (Google) / competitive overlap metrics.
   - Are new competitors entering the space?
   - Is it a seasonal effect (end of fiscal year, Q4 ad spending surge)?
5. PRODUCT-MARKET FIT SHIFT: If conversion rates are declining across all
   channels AND creative is fresh AND audiences are not saturated, the
   problem may not be marketing at all. The market's appetite for the
   product may have shifted.
6. SOLUTIONS (prioritized):
   - IMMEDIATE: Refresh creative across all channels. Test fundamentally
     different messaging angles, not just new images.
   - SHORT-TERM: Reallocate budget to highest-efficiency channels. Cut
     lowest-performing channels entirely rather than spreading budget thin.
   - MEDIUM-TERM: Invest in organic/owned channels to reduce dependence
     on paid (content, SEO, community, referral program).
   - STRATEGIC: Re-evaluate ICP. Are you targeting the right audience?
     Maybe the best audience is not on these platforms.
   - MEASUREMENT: Conduct incrementality tests on each channel. Some may be
     showing inflated attribution (especially branded search).
7. DO NOT: Simply increase budget to compensate. This accelerates the problem.

**Automatic fail if:** Response accepts "CPMs are going up" as the full
explanation, or proposes only budget increases as the solution.

## Test 2: Entering a Market with Entrenched Competitors

**Scenario:** You are launching a project management tool in a market with
3 entrenched competitors (Asana, Monday.com, Linear). Each has strong brand
awareness and tens of millions in marketing budget. You have $500K annual
marketing budget. How do you position and go to market?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. DO NOT COMPETE HEAD-ON: $500K vs. tens of millions means you cannot win
   on awareness in the general market. You must find asymmetric strategies.
2. POSITIONING STRATEGY:
   - Find the underserved niche that incumbents ignore or serve poorly.
   - Incumbents serve the broad market. You serve a specific vertical, use
     case, or persona with extreme depth.
   - Example niches: project management for agencies, for hardware teams,
     for remote-first teams, for solo consultants, for regulated industries.
   - Position as the specialist, not the generalist. "Built for X" not
     "Better for everyone."
3. DIFFERENTIATION:
   - Cannot differentiate on features alone (incumbents will match).
   - Differentiate on: specific workflow fit, opinionated design philosophy,
     pricing model, integration depth with niche tools, community, or values.
   - The differentiator must be something incumbents CANNOT or WILL NOT copy
     because it contradicts their broad-market strategy.
4. GO-TO-MARKET STRATEGY:
   - **Community-first:** Build in the communities where the niche lives
     (specific Slack groups, Reddit communities, industry forums).
   - **Content dominance in the niche:** Own SEO for niche-specific long-tail
     keywords. Incumbents target broad keywords. You target "[niche] project
     management."
   - **Product-led growth:** Free tier with viral mechanics (collaboration
     inherently spreads the tool).
   - **Partnerships:** Integrate with niche-specific tools that incumbents
     do not prioritize.
   - **Proof points:** Get 10 lighthouse customers in the niche who will
     publicly advocate.
5. WHAT NOT TO DO:
   - Do not run Google Ads on "project management software" (you will lose
     the auction to Asana's budget).
   - Do not try to match incumbent feature sets.
   - Do not position against all 3 competitors simultaneously.
   - Do not pursue enterprise sales with a $500K budget.
6. MEASUREMENT:
   - Primary: revenue from target niche.
   - Secondary: share of voice in niche community, NPS within niche.
   - Do NOT measure total market share (irrelevant at this stage).

**Automatic fail if:** Response proposes a broad-market strategy competing
head-on with incumbents, or allocates the $500K across general paid channels.

## Test 3: Brand Awareness High, Conversion Low

**Scenario:** Unaided brand awareness in your category is 35% (strong for
a startup). However, your demo request conversion rate from website visitors
is 1.2% (below the 2-3% industry benchmark). Diagnose.

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. HIGH AWARENESS + LOW CONVERSION = POSITIONING OR EXPERIENCE PROBLEM:
   People know you exist but are not compelled to act. This is not a
   top-of-funnel problem.
2. DIAGNOSIS FRAMEWORK (check in order):
   - **Positioning mismatch:** People know the brand but misunderstand what
     it does. They arrive at the website with wrong expectations.
     - Check: bounce rate by traffic source. High bounce = wrong expectations.
     - Check: on-site search queries (what are visitors looking for?).
     - Check: exit pages (where do they leave?).
   - **Website experience:** The website fails to convert interest into action.
     - Check: page load speed (every second reduces conversion by ~7%).
     - Check: clarity of value proposition above the fold.
     - Check: CTA visibility and friction (how many steps to request demo?).
     - Check: mobile experience (if >50% mobile traffic, mobile conversion
       is critical).
     - Check: social proof (testimonials, logos, case studies).
   - **Audience quality:** High awareness may include people who are aware
     but are not in the target market.
     - Check: what percentage of aware audience matches ICP?
     - Check: awareness source (if driven by viral content that attracted
       wrong audience, awareness is vanity).
   - **Trust deficit:** Awareness without trust. They know you but do not
     believe you can deliver.
     - Check: review sites (G2, Capterra, TrustPilot) for sentiment.
     - Check: competitive comparison content (are competitors winning the
       evaluation?).
     - Check: security/compliance concerns (especially enterprise buyers).
   - **Buying stage mismatch:** Website optimized for bottom-of-funnel
     (demo request) but most visitors are top-of-funnel (researching).
     - Offer intermediate CTAs (free trial, resource download, newsletter)
       for earlier-stage visitors.
3. INVESTIGATION PLAN:
   - Heatmap and session recording analysis on key pages.
   - User survey on website: "What almost stopped you from requesting a demo?"
   - A/B test the demo request page (reduce friction, change CTA copy,
     add social proof).
   - Segment conversion rate by traffic source (which aware-audience sources
     convert best?).
4. COMMON FIX: Often the issue is a mismatch between awareness-building
   content and website experience. The content promises one thing, the
   website presents another.

**Automatic fail if:** Response focuses on increasing awareness further, or
does not systematically diagnose the conversion gap.

## Test 4: Channel-Market Fit Assessment

**Scenario:** Your B2B SaaS product targets VPs of Engineering at companies
with 200-1000 employees. You have been spending 60% of your budget on
LinkedIn Ads. Results are declining. Propose an alternative channel strategy.

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. WHY LINKEDIN MAY BE DECLINING:
   - Audience saturation: the VP Engineering audience at 200-1000 employee
     companies is finite. LinkedIn targeting has exposed most of them.
   - Ad blindness: VPs of Engineering are heavily targeted on LinkedIn.
     They have learned to ignore ads.
   - CPMs rising: LinkedIn is the most expensive social platform per
     impression. Diminishing returns hit faster.
   - LinkedIn serves awareness, not always conversion for technical buyers.
2. ALTERNATIVE CHANNELS for VP Engineering audience:
   - **Content/SEO:** VPs of Engineering read technical content. Invest in
     high-quality engineering blog posts, technical case studies, and
     thought leadership on topics THEY care about (not your product).
     Target informational keywords they search for.
   - **Developer/Engineering communities:** Hacker News, specific subreddits,
     dev.to, Stack Overflow. Organic presence, not ads. Sponsor community
     events and newsletters.
   - **Podcasts:** Sponsor or appear on engineering leadership podcasts.
     Niche but highly targeted and trusted.
   - **Events and conferences:** Technical conferences where VPs of
     Engineering attend (not SaaS conferences). Speaking slots > booth
     presence.
   - **Partner/integration channel:** If your product integrates with tools
     VPs of Engineering already use, co-market with those platforms.
   - **Direct outreach:** ABM (Account-Based Marketing) for the most
     valuable accounts. Personalized, not spray-and-pray.
   - **Referral/word-of-mouth:** VPs of Engineering trust peer
     recommendations above all else. Customer advocacy program.
3. CHANNEL EVALUATION FRAMEWORK:
   - For each alternative: estimate audience reach, expected CAC, scalability,
     and measurement capability.
   - Test with small budget before major allocation.
   - Give channels appropriate ramp time (content/SEO needs 6+ months).
4. DO NOT:
   - Simply shift the 60% to another paid platform (same problem will repeat).
   - Abandon LinkedIn entirely (it still has value, just not at 60% allocation).
   - Over-index on channels where VP Engineering is not the actual audience
     (TikTok, Instagram, etc.).
5. REALLOCATION PROPOSAL:
   - LinkedIn: 25% (reduced, focused on retargeting and ABM lists only).
   - Content/SEO: 25% (long-term investment, 6-12 month payoff).
   - Community/events: 20% (medium-term, relationship-based).
   - ABM/direct outreach: 15% (high-intent, targeted).
   - Experimentation: 15% (new channels, referral program, partnerships).

**Automatic fail if:** Response simply reallocates from LinkedIn to another
paid social platform, or does not consider the unique media consumption
habits of VP Engineering audience.

## Test 5: Marketing Attribution Conflict

**Scenario:** Your CFO says marketing-attributed revenue is $10M but finance
only counts $6M in actual marketing-sourced revenue. The CFO wants to cut
your budget by 40%. How do you respond?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. UNDERSTAND THE GAP: The $4M difference likely comes from different
   attribution definitions:
   - Marketing's $10M: multi-touch attribution where marketing touched the
     account at any point (influenced revenue).
   - Finance's $6M: first-touch or last-touch attribution where marketing
     was the originating source (sourced revenue).
   - Both numbers can be "correct" under their definitions.
2. DO NOT ARGUE ABOUT WHOSE NUMBER IS RIGHT: This is a definitional problem,
   not a factual dispute. Arguing makes marketing look defensive.
3. INSTEAD, PROPOSE ALIGNMENT:
   - Acknowledge that influenced revenue ($10M) and sourced revenue ($6M)
     measure different things.
   - Agree on a SHARED metric for budget decisions. Options:
     a. Use sourced revenue for budget allocation, influenced revenue for
        understanding total impact.
     b. Use a blended metric that gives partial credit.
     c. Best: use incrementality testing to determine TRUE marketing impact.
4. INCREMENTALITY IS THE TIE-BREAKER:
   - Propose geographic hold-out tests or channel-level hold-outs.
   - "Let us pause marketing in 3 cities for 4 weeks and measure the revenue
     impact. That will tell us the true incremental contribution."
   - This settles the argument with evidence, not attribution models.
5. DEFEND AGAINST THE BUDGET CUT:
   - Even at $6M sourced, if marketing spend is $2M, the ROI is 3:1.
     Is that acceptable?
   - Model the impact of a 40% budget cut: which channels would be cut?
     What is the projected revenue loss? (Usually, cutting budget reduces
     revenue more than proportionally due to losing frequency and reach
     thresholds.)
   - Present a scenario analysis: Budget at current level, -20%, -40%.
     Show projected revenue for each.
6. LONG-TERM FIX:
   - Implement a single attribution framework agreed upon by marketing
     and finance.
   - Regular calibration with incrementality tests.
   - Joint reporting: one dashboard that both teams trust.

**Automatic fail if:** Response argues that the $10M number is "right" and
the CFO is wrong, or does not propose incrementality testing as the objective
resolution.

## Test 6: Content Marketing ROI Justification

**Scenario:** You have been investing $300K/year in content marketing (blog,
guides, webinars). The CEO asks "what is the ROI?" Direct attribution shows
content influenced 50 leads last year, resulting in $150K revenue. By direct
math, the ROI is -50%. How do you respond?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. CONTENT MARKETING ROI CANNOT BE MEASURED BY DIRECT ATTRIBUTION ALONE:
   Content operates across the entire funnel and has compounding effects
   that direct attribution misses.
2. WHAT DIRECT ATTRIBUTION MISSES:
   - **SEO value:** Content drives organic traffic. What is the equivalent
     paid search cost for that traffic? If content drives 50,000 organic
     visits/year and paid search costs $10/click, the SEO equivalent value
     is $500K.
   - **Brand building:** Content consumed during the research phase influences
     brand perception. Buyers who read 3+ pieces of content before contacting
     sales convert at a higher rate and have higher ACV.
   - **Sales enablement:** How many times did sales share a blog post or
     guide with a prospect? This is content-influenced but not content-attributed.
   - **Compounding returns:** Blog content published 2 years ago still
     drives traffic today. Unlike paid ads (which stop working when you
     stop paying), content appreciates.
3. COMPREHENSIVE ROI MODEL:
   - Direct attribution: $150K (what we can prove).
   - SEO equivalent value: $X (what we would pay in ads for the same traffic).
   - Sales-influenced pipeline: survey sales team on content usage in deals.
   - Conversion rate lift: compare conversion rate of prospects who consumed
     content vs. those who did not.
   - Long-tail traffic value: project forward based on content aging curve.
4. WHAT TO CHANGE (acknowledge the valid concern):
   - $150K in directly attributable revenue from $300K spend IS concerning.
   - Audit content performance: which pieces drive results? Which do not?
   - 80/20 rule: likely 20% of content drives 80% of results. Double down
     on what works.
   - Add clearer CTAs to content (without being spammy).
   - Implement content scoring: track content consumption as a lead scoring
     factor.
5. PRESENT THE FULL PICTURE:
   - "Direct attribution shows $150K. But content also delivers $X in SEO
     equivalent value, influences $Y in pipeline per sales team feedback,
     and increases conversion rates by Z% for content-consuming prospects.
     Total estimated impact is $[sum], making the ROI [positive number]."
   - Be honest about what is measured vs. estimated.

**Automatic fail if:** Response accepts the -50% ROI at face value, or
cannot articulate the non-directly-attributable value of content.

## Test 7: Pricing Page Optimization

**Scenario:** Your pricing page gets 15,000 visitors/month but only 3% click
"Start Free Trial" and only 0.5% click "Talk to Sales." The rest leave.
Diagnose and fix.

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. 96.5% EXIT RATE FROM PRICING PAGE IS NOT UNUSUAL but can be improved.
   The pricing page is the highest-intent page on the site. Every
   percentage point improvement has major revenue impact.
2. DIAGNOSE WHY VISITORS LEAVE:
   - **Sticker shock:** Price is higher than expected. Check: are visitors
     coming from content that set a different expectation?
   - **Confusion:** Too many plans, unclear differentiation between tiers.
     Check: heatmaps showing where visitors hover/click.
   - **Missing information:** Visitors need more detail before committing.
     Check: do they navigate to other pages after pricing? Which ones?
   - **Comparison shopping:** They are evaluating options and need to see
     competitors too. Natural behavior, not a problem to solve entirely.
   - **Wrong visitors:** Some percentage are not in the ICP. Check: traffic
     source analysis for pricing page.
   - **Trust deficit:** No social proof, no security badges, no money-back
     guarantee on the pricing page.
3. OPTIMIZATION FRAMEWORK:
   - **Reduce plans:** If >3 plans (excluding enterprise), consider
     consolidating. Decision fatigue reduces conversion.
   - **Highlight the recommended plan:** Visual emphasis on the most popular
     or best-value plan.
   - **Feature comparison table:** Clear, scannable comparison of what each
     plan includes.
   - **Address objections on the page:** FAQ section addressing pricing
     concerns.
   - **Social proof ON the pricing page:** customer logos, testimonials,
     "10,000 teams trust us."
   - **Risk reduction:** Free trial, money-back guarantee, "cancel anytime."
   - **Pricing anchoring:** Show annual pricing (lower per-month) next to
     monthly pricing.
   - **Calculator tool:** "Estimate your cost" for usage-based pricing.
4. TESTING PLAN:
   - Prioritize tests by expected impact and ease of implementation.
   - Test 1: Simplify plan presentation (reduce cognitive load).
   - Test 2: Add social proof elements (build trust).
   - Test 3: Test CTA copy (not just "Start Free Trial" -- try "Start
     Building Free" or "Try Free for 14 Days").
   - Test 4: Test layout (horizontal vs. vertical plan comparison).
5. MEASUREMENT:
   - Primary: trial starts + sales conversations as percentage of
     pricing page visitors.
   - Secondary: plan selection distribution (are people choosing the
     right plan?).
   - Guardrail: trial-to-paid conversion rate (do not optimize for junk
     trials).
   - Qualitative: exit survey on pricing page ("What, if anything, almost
     stopped you?").

**Automatic fail if:** Response proposes only lowering prices, or does
not systematically diagnose the multiple potential causes.

## Test 8: Category Creation

**Scenario:** Your product does not fit neatly into an existing category.
Analysts do not cover your space. Customers describe you differently. Should
you create a new category, and if so, how?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. CATEGORY CREATION IS HIGH-RISK, HIGH-REWARD:
   - Reward: category leader gets disproportionate market share and premium
     pricing. If you define the category, you define the buying criteria.
   - Risk: customers buy in categories. If yours does not exist, you need
     to educate the market before you can sell. This costs time and money.
2. WHEN TO CREATE A CATEGORY (must meet criteria):
   - Existing categories genuinely do not fit (not just "we want to seem
     unique").
   - There is a real, named pain point that existing categories do not address.
   - You have the budget and patience for a multi-year education effort.
   - The market is large enough to justify a new category (not a niche).
   - You have credibility to lead the conversation (customers, data, thought
     leadership).
3. HOW TO CREATE A CATEGORY (if decision is yes):
   - **Name it:** The category name must be intuitive (self-explanatory),
     memorable, and searchable. Do NOT use invented jargon.
   - **Define the problem:** Before talking about your product, establish
     that the PROBLEM exists and is underserved.
   - **Publish the manifesto:** A thought leadership piece that names the
     category, defines the problem, and describes the ideal solution
     (which happens to be your product).
   - **Recruit allies:** Analysts, customers, partners, and even competitors
     who validate that this category is real.
   - **Create content:** Own the search results for the category term.
     Blog posts, guides, comparisons, "What is [category]?" content.
   - **Analyst relations:** Brief Gartner, Forrester, IDC. Get them to
     recognize the category.
   - **Customer proof:** Case studies using category language. Customers
     saying "we needed [category] and found [product]."
4. THE ALTERNATIVE: Attach to an existing category with a modifier.
   - "The [existing category] for [specific audience]"
   - "Modern [existing category]" or "AI-powered [existing category]"
   - Lower risk: customers already understand the category. Higher risk:
     you are compared to incumbents on their terms.
5. MEASUREMENT:
   - Search volume for the category term (should grow over time).
   - Share of voice for the category term.
   - Analyst recognition.
   - Customer adoption of category language in their own descriptions.
   - Win rate against competitors (should be higher in deals where the
     customer uses your category framing).

**Automatic fail if:** Response says "always create a category" or "never
create a category" without analyzing the specific conditions, or proposes
a category name that is jargon rather than intuitive.

## Test 9: Crisis Communications

**Scenario:** A customer posts a viral tweet (2M impressions) claiming your
product lost their data. Your engineering team confirms it was a user error,
not a product bug. How do you respond?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. THE TRUTH DOES NOT MATTER AS MUCH AS THE PERCEPTION (in the short term):
   2M impressions means thousands of potential customers now associate your
   brand with data loss. Whether it was user error is almost irrelevant to
   the PR damage.
2. IMMEDIATE RESPONSE (within 2 hours):
   - Acknowledge the situation publicly. Do NOT ignore it.
   - Express empathy: "We take data safety incredibly seriously. We are sorry
     for the experience [customer name] had."
   - Do NOT blame the customer publicly. Even if it was user error, saying
     "they did it wrong" looks defensive and hostile.
   - State that you are investigating and will provide an update.
3. DIRECT OUTREACH (within 1 hour):
   - Contact the customer directly (DM, email, phone).
   - Offer to help them recover / resolve the situation regardless of cause.
   - If you resolve it well, they may post a positive update.
4. PUBLIC FOLLOW-UP (within 24 hours):
   - Provide a transparent explanation of what happened (without technical
     blame language).
   - Frame it constructively: "We identified that the UX for [action] was
     not clear enough. We are improving it to prevent this for all users."
   - Announce concrete improvement: "We have added [safeguard] to prevent
     data loss scenarios."
5. TURNING CRISIS INTO OPPORTUNITY:
   - Publish a blog post about your data safety practices.
   - Announce a product improvement that prevents the issue.
   - This shows: we listen, we respond, we improve. This BUILDS trust.
6. INTERNAL ACTIONS:
   - Product Brain: Is the UX genuinely confusing? If user error was this
     easy, it is a product problem even if technically correct.
   - Engineering Brain: Are there safeguards (confirmation dialogs, undo,
     backups) that should exist?
   - Marketing Brain: Monitor sentiment for 2 weeks. Prepare responses for
     follow-up questions.
7. WHAT NOT TO DO:
   - Do not ignore it.
   - Do not delete comments or block the user.
   - Do not post a legalistic "user error" response.
   - Do not respond with corporate-speak.
   - Do not respond emotionally or sarcastically.

**Automatic fail if:** Response focuses on proving the customer wrong, or
does not address the perception management problem separately from the
factual question.

## Test 10: Referral Program Design

**Scenario:** Your product has high NPS (65) but no referral program.
Design a referral program that generates measurable growth without
destroying unit economics.

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. HIGH NPS IS A PREREQUISITE, NOT A GUARANTEE: People recommend products
   they love, but they need a nudge and a mechanism to do so conveniently.
2. PROGRAM DESIGN:
   - **Trigger timing:** When to ask for referrals (after positive outcomes,
     not after signup). Identify "moments of delight" in the product.
   - **Incentive structure:** Both sides must benefit (two-sided incentive).
     Options:
     - Product credits (account credit, extra features, extended trial).
     - Monetary rewards (gift cards, discounts on next bill).
     - Status rewards (exclusive features, early access).
     - Charitable donations (in their name).
   - **Incentive sizing:** Must be less than CAC for the referred customer
     (otherwise, just spend on ads). Typically 20-50% of CAC.
   - **Mechanics:** Make sharing friction-free (one-click share link,
     pre-written message they can customize, multiple sharing channels).
3. FRAUD PREVENTION:
   - Self-referral detection (same device, same IP, same payment method).
   - Minimum activity threshold before reward is paid (referred user must
     activate/convert).
   - Rate limiting (cap referrals per person to prevent gaming).
   - Reward timing: pay AFTER the referred user completes a qualifying action,
     not at signup.
4. MEASUREMENT:
   - Referral rate: % of users who refer at least one person.
   - Referral conversion rate: % of referred users who convert.
   - Cost per referred customer vs. other channel CAC.
   - Referred customer quality: retention, LTV compared to non-referred.
   - Viral coefficient (K-factor): referrals per user x conversion rate.
   - K > 1 means organic viral growth. K > 0.3 is still valuable.
5. TESTING PLAN:
   - Start with a segment (highest NPS users, longest tenure).
   - Test incentive types (product credits vs. monetary vs. status).
   - Test trigger timing (post-signup vs. post-outcome vs. periodic prompt).
   - Test sharing channels (email vs. social vs. direct link).
6. SUSTAINABILITY:
   - Monitor for incentive saturation (do rewards stop being motivating?).
   - Separate intrinsic motivation (genuine recommendation) from extrinsic
     (reward-driven). Over-incentivizing can undermine genuine advocacy.
   - Evolve the program based on data, not just launch and forget.

**Automatic fail if:** Response proposes a referral program without fraud
prevention, without measurement framework, or with incentives that exceed CAC.

## Test 11: Multi-Product Marketing Architecture

**Scenario:** Your company now has 3 products: the original SaaS tool, an
API platform, and a mobile app. Each has a different target audience. How do
you structure the marketing?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. BRAND ARCHITECTURE DECISION: Before marketing tactics, define the brand
   relationship between products:
   - **Branded house:** One brand, product descriptions (Google -> Google
     Docs, Google Sheets). Simplest but risks dilution.
   - **House of brands:** Separate brands per product (P&G -> Tide, Pampers).
     Maximum flexibility but expensive to build each brand.
   - **Endorsed brand:** Parent brand endorses products (Marriott -> Courtyard
     by Marriott). Balance of credibility and independence.
   - Choose based on: audience overlap, positioning overlap, and portfolio
     strategy.
2. IF AUDIENCES OVERLAP:
   - Cross-sell is a major opportunity. Marketing to existing customers for
     new products has lowest CAC.
   - Unified messaging: position the suite as a comprehensive solution.
   - Customer journey mapping across products (which product do they enter
     with? Where do they expand?).
3. IF AUDIENCES DO NOT OVERLAP:
   - Separate marketing strategies per product.
   - Shared brand platform but distinct messaging.
   - Separate websites or clearly delineated sections.
   - Separate budgets and P&Ls for accountability.
4. ORGANIZATIONAL STRUCTURE:
   - Shared: brand guidelines, design system, marketing ops, analytics.
   - Dedicated: product marketers for each product (specialists who
     understand the audience deeply).
   - Leadership: VP/CMO who manages the portfolio, not just individual
     products.
5. MEASUREMENT:
   - Per-product CAC, LTV, funnel metrics.
   - Cross-product metrics: expansion rate, multi-product retention premium.
   - Portfolio metrics: total customer value across products.
   - Cannibalization tracking: does one product reduce another's revenue?
6. COMMON PITFALLS:
   - Treating all products the same in messaging (confuses audiences).
   - Not having dedicated product marketing (generic messages for all).
   - Website that is confusing because it tries to serve all audiences
     at once.
   - Over-optimizing one product's marketing at the expense of others.

**Automatic fail if:** Response does not address brand architecture, or
treats all 3 products as having the same marketing strategy.

## Test 12: Marketing During Recession

**Scenario:** The economy is entering a recession. Your CFO wants to cut
marketing budget by 50%. Your competitors are also cutting. What do you
recommend?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. HISTORICAL EVIDENCE: Companies that maintain or increase marketing during
   recessions gain market share that persists long after recovery (Harvard
   Business Review, multiple studies). Competitors cutting spend creates
   opportunity.
2. DO NOT ACCEPT A BLANKET 50% CUT: Instead, propose a strategic reallocation.
3. WHAT TO CUT:
   - Channels with worst unit economics (highest CAC, longest payback).
   - "Brand awareness" spending without clear ROI measurement.
   - Events and sponsorships with unproven ROI.
   - Any channel where incrementality has not been tested.
4. WHAT TO PROTECT:
   - Highest-ROI channels (prove ROI with data to justify protection).
   - Customer retention marketing (keeping existing customers is cheaper
     than acquiring new ones, especially in a recession).
   - Content/SEO (zero marginal cost once created, compounds over time).
   - Competitive share-of-voice (if competitors cut, your share of voice
     increases at lower cost).
5. WHAT TO INCREASE:
   - Paid channels where competitor departure reduces CPMs (you get more
     reach for less money).
   - Customer success and expansion marketing (grow revenue from existing
     base).
   - Case studies and ROI calculators (buyers in recessions need stronger
     ROI justification).
   - Messaging that emphasizes cost savings, efficiency, ROI (align with
     recessionary buying psychology).
6. NEGOTIATE WITH THE CFO:
   - "I can cut $X from low-ROI activities. I need to protect $Y in
     high-ROI activities. Here is the projected revenue impact of each
     scenario."
   - Offer more frequent reporting (monthly instead of quarterly) in
     exchange for protecting budget.
   - Propose a "trigger-based" budget: automatically increase spend if
     metrics exceed targets, decrease if they fall below.
7. ADJUST MESSAGING:
   - Recession buyers have different psychology: more risk-averse, longer
     sales cycles, more stakeholders in decisions, emphasis on ROI.
   - Adjust content: ROI calculators, total cost of ownership comparisons,
     efficiency gains, cost reduction positioning.

**Automatic fail if:** Response accepts the 50% cut without proposing
strategic reallocation, or does not leverage the competitive opportunity
of reduced competitor spending.

## Test 13: Product Launch Sequencing

**Scenario:** You are launching a major new product. You have 8 weeks.
Design the launch sequence from pre-launch to post-launch, including
all marketing activities.

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. PRE-LAUNCH (Weeks 1-4):
   - **Week 1-2: Internal alignment**
     - Positioning finalized and tested.
     - Sales team trained (battlecard, demo script, FAQ).
     - Support team trained (knowledge base, common issues).
     - Customer success team briefed (expansion opportunities).
   - **Week 2-3: Content production**
     - Launch blog post written and reviewed.
     - Product demo video produced.
     - Customer testimonials/beta stories collected.
     - Press release drafted.
     - Social media content calendar created.
     - Email sequences built (announcement, nurture, sales follow-up).
     - Landing page built and tested.
   - **Week 3-4: Seeding**
     - Beta users activated for testimonials and reviews.
     - Analysts briefed (Gartner, Forrester if relevant).
     - Press/influencer embargo briefings.
     - "Coming soon" teaser to email list.
     - Product Hunt scheduled (if relevant).
2. LAUNCH WEEK (Week 5):
   - **Day 1: Big bang**
     - Blog post, press release, social media, email blast.
     - CEO/founder social posts.
     - Product Hunt launch (if B2C/prosumer).
     - Paid social boost on launch content.
   - **Day 2-3: Amplification**
     - Employee advocacy (team shares).
     - Community engagement (respond to every comment).
     - Influencer/partner amplification.
     - Webinar or live demo event.
   - **Day 4-5: Follow-up**
     - Additional content angles (use cases, technical deep-dives).
     - Customer stories published.
     - Re-target launch page visitors.
3. POST-LAUNCH (Weeks 6-8):
   - **Week 6: Sustain**
     - Performance analysis (what is working?).
     - Optimize: double down on highest-performing content/channels.
     - Second wave of content (deeper topics, comparisons, tutorials).
   - **Week 7: Expand**
     - Launch paid campaigns targeting new audiences.
     - Partner co-marketing activations.
     - Sales follow-up cadence activated.
   - **Week 8: Review**
     - Full launch retrospective.
     - Metrics vs. targets analysis.
     - Lessons learned documented.
     - Transition from "launch mode" to "sustain mode."
4. MEASUREMENT:
   - Pre-launch: waitlist signups, press placements secured.
   - Launch: traffic, signups, trials, demo requests, media coverage.
   - Post-launch: activation rate, revenue impact, awareness lift.
5. RISK MITIGATION:
   - Technical launch issues: engineering on standby for launch day.
   - Negative reception: crisis communication plan ready.
   - Low traction: backup amplification plan (additional spend, additional
     content, additional outreach).

**Automatic fail if:** Response skips pre-launch preparation, does not
include internal alignment, or treats launch as a single-day event rather
than a multi-week sequence.

## Test 14: Marketing Org Structure

**Scenario:** You are hired as the first VP of Marketing at a Series B
startup ($15M ARR, 100 employees). You have budget to hire 5 marketers.
How do you structure the team?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. FIRST: Diagnose what the company needs before hiring.
   - What is working organically? (Do not hire to replace what is working.)
   - Where are the biggest gaps? (Usually: demand gen, content, or product
     marketing.)
   - What is the sales motion? (PLG vs. sales-led dictates marketing structure.)
2. RECOMMENDED FIRST 5 HIRES (for sales-led motion):
   - **Hire 1: Product Marketing Manager** -- Positioning, messaging, sales
     enablement, competitive intelligence. The most critical hire because
     everything else depends on messaging clarity.
   - **Hire 2: Demand Generation Manager** -- Paid acquisition, lead nurture,
     funnel optimization. Drives pipeline.
   - **Hire 3: Content Marketer** -- Blog, SEO, thought leadership, guides.
     Long-term compounding asset.
   - **Hire 4: Marketing Operations** -- Martech stack, attribution, reporting,
     email automation. Enables measurement.
   - **Hire 5: Designer or Creative Generalist** -- Visual content, ad
     creative, landing pages. Accelerates all other functions.
3. FOR PLG MOTION, ADJUST:
   - Hire 1: Growth/PLG marketer (onboarding, activation, self-serve funnel).
   - Hire 2: Product Marketing Manager.
   - Hire 3: Content Marketer (product-led content, templates, resources).
   - Hire 4: Marketing Operations.
   - Hire 5: Community Manager or Developer Advocate (if developer audience).
4. WHAT TO OUTSOURCE:
   - PR (agency until volume justifies in-house).
   - Paid media management (agency or freelancer for execution, in-house
     for strategy).
   - Video production (project-based).
   - Design overflow (contractors).
5. SEQUENCING:
   - Hire product marketer FIRST (foundations before execution).
   - Hire demand gen SECOND (revenue pipeline urgency).
   - Content and ops can parallel (weeks 4-8).
   - Creative last (can use contractors short-term).
6. MEASUREMENT FOR THE TEAM:
   - Team-level: pipeline sourced, pipeline influenced, revenue attributed.
   - Individual: role-specific KPIs (PMM: win rate, message testing results;
     Demand Gen: MQLs, CAC, pipeline; Content: organic traffic, content-
     sourced pipeline; Ops: data quality, attribution coverage).

**Automatic fail if:** Response hires 5 demand gen specialists without
product marketing or content, or does not consider the sales motion (PLG
vs. sales-led) in the hiring plan.

## Test 15: International Marketing Adaptation

**Scenario:** Your US-based product is expanding to the UK and Brazil
simultaneously. What marketing considerations must be addressed beyond
translation?

**Model Answer:**

**Key points that must be addressed:**
1. TRANSLATION IS THE SMALLEST PART OF INTERNATIONAL MARKETING:
   - UK: Same language, completely different business culture, different
     media landscape, different regulations.
   - Brazil: Different language, different culture, different digital
     platforms, different payment preferences, different regulatory environment.
2. MARKET-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS:
   **UK:**
   - GDPR compliance (stricter than US, even post-Brexit).
   - ASA (Advertising Standards Authority) regulations for advertising claims.
   - British English: "colour" not "color," "mobile" not "cell phone," but
     more importantly, TONE. British marketing is typically more understated,
     less hyperbolic than US marketing. "The best product ever" would feel
     crass.
   - Media landscape: LinkedIn is strong for B2B, but publications differ
     (The Register, TechCrunch UK, Computer Weekly).
   - Pricing: GBP, not USD. Price sensitivity may differ.
   - Cultural references: US case studies and references may not resonate.
     Need UK-specific proof points.
   **Brazil:**
   - Portuguese (Brazilian), not Spanish. Not just translation -- localization.
   - WhatsApp is the dominant communication channel (far more than US).
     Marketing via WhatsApp is expected and effective.
   - Boleto bancario and PIX as payment methods (credit card penetration
     is lower).
   - Social media: Instagram and YouTube are massive. TikTok growing fast.
   - Regulatory: LGPD (data protection law similar to GDPR).
   - Business culture: relationships matter more. Direct selling approach
     may not work.
   - Pricing: must account for purchasing power parity. US pricing is often
     unaffordable in Brazil.
   - Tax: complex tax structure affects pricing.
3. LOCALIZATION BEYOND LANGUAGE:
   - Testimonials and case studies from local customers.
   - Local team or partners for credibility.
   - Local events and communities.
   - Date formats (DD/MM/YYYY in both UK and Brazil, not MM/DD/YYYY).
   - Currency display and conversion.
   - Imagery: reflect local diversity and cultural norms.
4. CHANNEL STRATEGY DIFFERENCES:
   - What works in the US may not work in UK/Brazil. Research local channel
     landscape before assuming.
   - Influencer ecosystem is different in each market.
   - SEO: separate domain/subdirectory strategy for local search.
   - Paid: different platforms, different costs, different creative norms.
5. GO-TO-MARKET RECOMMENDATION:
   - Sequence, do not simultaneous-launch. UK is lower effort (language
     overlap, cultural proximity). Start there, learn, then Brazil.
   - For each market: start with the smallest viable segment, prove PMF,
     then expand.
   - Hire or partner locally: a US team cannot effectively run Brazilian
     marketing remotely without local expertise.
6. MEASUREMENT:
   - Separate dashboards per market.
   - Do NOT compare US metrics to UK/Brazil metrics directly.
   - Set market-specific baselines and targets.

**Automatic fail if:** Response treats international expansion as "translate
the website and run the same ads," or does not address regulatory, cultural,
and channel differences for each specific market.
