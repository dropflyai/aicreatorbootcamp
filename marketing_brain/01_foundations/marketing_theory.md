# Marketing Theory -- Foundational Frameworks

## Purpose

This module codifies the foundational theories that underpin all marketing
decisions. Every recommendation, campaign, and strategy produced by the Marketing
Brain must trace back to one or more of these frameworks. Theory without
application is academic; application without theory is guesswork.

---

## The Marketing Concept Evolution

### Production Era (pre-1920s)
"Build it and they will come." Focus on manufacturing efficiency.
Assumption: Demand exceeds supply.

### Sales Era (1920s-1950s)
"Sell what we make." Focus on persuasion and distribution.
Assumption: Competition requires aggressive selling.

### Marketing Era (1950s-present)
"Make what we can sell." Focus on customer needs.
Assumption: Long-term success requires customer orientation.
Reference: Drucker, The Practice of Management (1954).

### Relationship Era (1990s-present)
"Build lasting customer relationships." Focus on lifetime value.
Assumption: Retention is more profitable than acquisition.
Reference: Reichheld, The Loyalty Effect (1996).

### Digital/Data Era (2010s-present)
"Personalize at scale." Focus on data-driven, omnichannel experiences.
Assumption: Technology enables 1:1 marketing at mass scale.

## The Marketing Mix: 4Ps and 7Ps

### McCarthy's 4Ps (1960)

**Product**
- Core benefit: What problem does it solve?
- Actual product: Features, quality, design, brand
- Augmented product: Warranty, support, delivery, installation
- Decision tree: What does the customer need? --> What features deliver that?
  --> How does our product compare to alternatives?

**Price**
- Pricing strategies: cost-plus, value-based, competitive, penetration, skimming
- Price elasticity: % change in demand / % change in price
- Psychological pricing: anchoring, charm pricing ($9.99), decoy effect
- Decision tree: What is the perceived value? --> What are competitors charging?
  --> What is our cost floor? --> What pricing strategy fits our GTM?

**Place (Distribution)**
- Direct vs. indirect channels
- Channel intensity: intensive, selective, exclusive
- Digital distribution: app stores, marketplaces, direct web
- Decision tree: Where does our customer buy? --> What channels reach them?
  --> What margins do channels require? --> What control do we need?

**Promotion**
- Advertising, sales promotion, public relations, personal selling, direct marketing
- Push (trade promotion, sales force) vs. pull (consumer advertising, content)
- Integrated Marketing Communications (IMC): consistent message across all channels
- Decision tree: Who is our audience? --> What message resonates? --> Which
  channels reach them? --> What frequency is needed?

### Booms & Bitner's Extended 7Ps (1981) -- For Services

**People**
- Every person who interacts with customers IS marketing
- Employee training, culture, customer service quality
- In SaaS: support team, onboarding specialists, CSMs

**Process**
- The systems and procedures that deliver the service
- Customer journey mapping, service blueprinting
- In SaaS: onboarding flow, support ticketing, billing

**Physical Evidence**
- Tangible cues that signal quality in intangible services
- In digital: UI quality, testimonials, case studies, certifications
- Trust signals: logos, reviews, security badges

## STP Framework (Kotler)

The foundational strategic marketing framework. MUST be completed before any
tactical execution.

### Step 1: Segmentation

Divide the total market into distinct groups of buyers with different needs,
characteristics, or behaviors.

**Segmentation Variables:**

| Type | B2C Variables | B2B Variables |
|------|---------------|---------------|
| Geographic | Region, city size, climate | HQ location, operating regions |
| Demographic | Age, income, education, occupation | Industry, company size, revenue |
| Psychographic | Lifestyle, values, personality | Company culture, risk tolerance |
| Behavioral | Usage rate, loyalty, benefits sought | Purchase process, buying criteria |

**Effective Segments Must Be:**
- Measurable: Size and purchasing power can be quantified
- Accessible: Can be reached through available channels
- Substantial: Large enough to be profitable
- Differentiable: Respond differently to different marketing mixes
- Actionable: Can design effective programs for them

### Step 2: Targeting

Evaluate segment attractiveness and select one or more target segments.

**Targeting Strategies:**
1. Undifferentiated (Mass): One offer for entire market (rare in modern marketing)
2. Differentiated (Segmented): Different offers for different segments
3. Concentrated (Niche): Focus on one or few segments deeply
4. Micromarketing: Tailored to specific individuals or locations

**Segment Evaluation Matrix:**

```
                    Segment Attractiveness
                    Low         High
Competitive    Strong  | Protect   | Invest heavily |
Position       Weak    | Divest    | Selective invest|
```

### Step 3: Positioning

Design the offering and image to occupy a distinct place in the target segment's
mind relative to competing offerings.

**Positioning Statement Template (Moore):**
"For [target segment] who [need/opportunity], [product name] is a [category]
that [key benefit]. Unlike [primary alternative], our product [primary
differentiation]."

## Marketing Myopia (Levitt, HBR 1960)

Theodore Levitt's foundational warning: companies fail when they define their
business too narrowly around their product rather than the customer need they serve.

### Classic Examples
| Company Defined As | Should Have Defined As |
|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Railroad company | Transportation company |
| Movie studio | Entertainment company |
| Typewriter manufacturer | Information processing company |

### Modern Application
| Narrow Definition | Customer-Need Definition |
|-------------------|--------------------------|
| "We sell CRM software" | "We help companies grow revenue through better customer relationships" |
| "We provide cloud storage" | "We enable seamless collaboration across distributed teams" |
| "We make project management tools" | "We help teams ship work faster with less friction" |

### Myopia Test
Ask: "If our product disappeared tomorrow, what need would go unmet?"
The answer to that question is your real market definition.

## Brand Equity (Aaker, 1991)

David Aaker's brand equity model defines five dimensions of brand value:

### 1. Brand Awareness
- Recognition: Can they identify the brand when prompted?
- Recall: Does the brand come to mind unprompted in the category?
- Top-of-mind: Is it the first brand recalled?
- Measurement: Aided/unaided awareness surveys

### 2. Perceived Quality
- Not objective quality -- the customer's subjective perception
- Drives purchase decisions and premium pricing
- Measurement: Quality perception surveys, willingness-to-pay studies

### 3. Brand Associations
- Everything linked to the brand in the customer's memory
- Functional attributes, emotional benefits, brand personality
- Measurement: Brand association mapping, projective techniques

### 4. Brand Loyalty
- Behavioral loyalty (repeat purchase) vs. attitudinal loyalty (preference)
- Loyalty ladder: non-customer --> price-sensitive --> habitual --> committed
- Measurement: Repeat purchase rate, NPS, switching intention

### 5. Proprietary Brand Assets
- Patents, trademarks, channel relationships
- Legal and structural barriers to competition
- Measurement: IP portfolio valuation, distribution coverage

### Brand Equity Pyramid (Keller CBBE Model, 2001)

```
           /  Resonance  \          <-- Intense, active loyalty
          / Judgments|Feelings \     <-- Rational and emotional evaluation
         / Performance|Imagery  \   <-- What brand IS and MEANS
        /      Salience          \  <-- Deep, broad brand awareness
```

**Building brand equity requires ascending the pyramid in order.**

## Diffusion of Innovation (Rogers, 1962)

How new products and ideas spread through populations.

### Adoption Curve

```
  Innovators   Early       Early      Late       Laggards
   (2.5%)    Adopters   Majority   Majority     (16%)
              (13.5%)    (34%)      (34%)

    |-------|---------|----------|----------|---------|
    ^                 ^
    |                 |
  Launch          "The Chasm"
                  (Moore, 1991)
```

### The Chasm (Geoffrey Moore)
The critical gap between early adopters and early majority:
- Early adopters buy vision; early majority buys proven solutions
- Crossing requires: whole product, reference customers, pragmatist messaging
- Strategy: dominate a niche beachhead, then expand

### Marketing Implications by Segment
| Segment | Messaging | Channel | Pricing |
|---------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Innovators | Technology-forward, novel | Tech press, communities | Premium OK |
| Early Adopters | Vision, competitive advantage | Industry events, thought leadership | Value-based |
| Early Majority | Proven, reliable, complete | Mainstream media, referrals | Competitive |
| Late Majority | Standard, easy, risk-free | Mass channels, bundling | Discount |
| Laggards | "Everyone else already has this" | Default/embedded | Low cost |

## The Ansoff Matrix (1957)

Growth strategy decision framework:

```
                 Existing Products    New Products
Existing     |  Market Penetration | Product Development |
Markets      |  (lowest risk)      | (medium risk)       |
             |---------------------|---------------------|
New          |  Market Development | Diversification     |
Markets      |  (medium risk)      | (highest risk)      |
```

**Decision Tree:**
1. Can we grow with existing products in existing markets? --> Market penetration
2. Can we serve existing customers with new products? --> Product development
3. Can we bring existing products to new markets? --> Market development
4. Only if 1-3 exhausted --> Consider diversification

## Porter's Five Forces (Applied to Marketing)

While a competitive strategy framework (MBA Brain domain), marketers must
understand competitive dynamics:

1. **Threat of new entrants** --> Impacts positioning durability
2. **Bargaining power of buyers** --> Impacts pricing and messaging
3. **Bargaining power of suppliers** --> Impacts channel strategy
4. **Threat of substitutes** --> Impacts category definition
5. **Competitive rivalry** --> Impacts differentiation requirements

**Marketing Application:** Higher competitive intensity demands stronger
positioning, more distinctive brand assets, and clearer differentiation.

## Decision Framework: Which Theory to Apply?

```
START: What marketing decision?
  |
  +-- Defining our market? --> Levitt's Marketing Myopia test
  |
  +-- Who to target? --> STP Framework (Kotler)
  |
  +-- How to grow? --> Ansoff Matrix
  |
  +-- What to offer/how? --> Marketing Mix (4Ps/7Ps)
  |
  +-- Building brand value? --> Aaker's Brand Equity / Keller CBBE
  |
  +-- Launching new product? --> Rogers Diffusion + Moore's Chasm
  |
  +-- Competitive response? --> Porter's Five Forces
```
