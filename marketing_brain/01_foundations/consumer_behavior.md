# Consumer Behavior -- Purchase Psychology and Decision-Making

## Purpose

This module codifies the science of how and why consumers make purchasing
decisions. All marketing execution -- messaging, creative, offers, UX -- must
be grounded in an understanding of human decision-making. Marketing that ignores
behavioral science is marketing that relies on luck.

---

## The Consumer Decision Process (Engel-Blackwell-Miniard Model)

### Five-Stage Model

```
1. Need Recognition --> 2. Information Search --> 3. Evaluation of Alternatives
        --> 4. Purchase Decision --> 5. Post-Purchase Behavior
```

### Stage 1: Need Recognition
The gap between current state and desired state. Triggered by:
- Internal stimuli (hunger, frustration, aspiration)
- External stimuli (advertising, social proof, life events)

**Marketing Implication:** Demand creation targets this stage. Content marketing,
brand advertising, and category education all work to trigger need recognition.

### Stage 2: Information Search
- Internal search: Memory, past experience, brand familiarity
- External search: Online reviews, comparison sites, social media, peers
- The "messy middle" (Google, 2020): Consumers loop between exploration
  (expanding options) and evaluation (narrowing options)

**Marketing Implication:** SEO, content marketing, and brand salience determine
whether your brand enters the consideration set. Byron Sharp: brands that are
easier to think of and easier to find win.

### Stage 3: Evaluation of Alternatives
- Consideration set: The subset of known brands actually evaluated (typically 2-5)
- Evaluative criteria: Price, features, brand, reviews, availability
- Decision rules: Compensatory (trade-offs) vs. non-compensatory (elimination)

**Marketing Implication:** Competitive differentiation and positioning determine
whether you survive evaluation. Clear, distinctive positioning is essential.

### Stage 4: Purchase Decision
- Intention does not always equal purchase
- Intervening factors: price changes, peer opinions, situational factors
- Channel friction: checkout complexity, payment options, delivery time

**Marketing Implication:** CRO, checkout optimization, and reducing friction at
the point of purchase. Every additional step loses 5-20% of buyers.

### Stage 5: Post-Purchase Behavior
- Cognitive dissonance: Buyer's remorse, especially for high-involvement purchases
- Satisfaction = Performance vs. Expectations (Oliver's Expectation-Confirmation)
- Delighted customers become advocates; disappointed customers become detractors

**Marketing Implication:** Onboarding, customer success, NPS programs, and
lifecycle marketing. Post-purchase experience drives retention and referral.

## Dual-Process Theory (Kahneman, 2011)

Daniel Kahneman's System 1 / System 2 framework is foundational for understanding
marketing influence.

### System 1 (Fast, Automatic, Intuitive)
- Operates automatically with little effort
- Relies on heuristics, associations, emotions
- Handles 95%+ of daily decisions
- Susceptible to cognitive biases

**Marketing Applications:**
- Brand recognition and distinctive assets (Sharp)
- Emotional advertising that creates associations
- Simple, clear messaging (avoid cognitive load)
- Default options and choice architecture

### System 2 (Slow, Deliberate, Analytical)
- Requires conscious effort and attention
- Handles complex calculations and comparisons
- Engaged for high-involvement, high-risk decisions
- Easily fatigued

**Marketing Applications:**
- Detailed product comparisons for high-involvement purchases
- Case studies and ROI calculators (B2B)
- Rational arguments for considered purchases
- Feature comparison tables

### Decision Framework: System 1 vs. System 2 Marketing

```
                High Involvement         Low Involvement
High Risk    | System 2 dominant       | Mixed processing    |
             | Rational messaging      | Heuristic + rational|
             | Case studies, ROI proof  | Social proof + specs|
             |-------------------------|---------------------|
Low Risk     | Mixed processing        | System 1 dominant   |
             | Emotional + rational    | Brand, habit, emotion|
             | Reviews + brand trust   | Distinctive assets  |
```

## Cognitive Biases in Marketing

### Biases That Affect Purchase Decisions

**Anchoring (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974)**
The first piece of information encountered serves as a reference point.
- Application: Show the highest-priced option first
- Application: Display original price crossed out next to sale price
- Application: "Starting at $X" anchors below the expected price

**Social Proof (Cialdini, 1984)**
People follow the actions of others, especially under uncertainty.
- Application: Customer counts ("Join 50,000+ companies")
- Application: Testimonials and reviews
- Application: "Most popular" badges on pricing tiers
- Application: Real-time activity ("12 people viewing this now")

**Scarcity (Cialdini, 1984)**
Items seem more valuable when they are limited or dwindling.
- Application: Limited-time offers and countdown timers
- Application: "Only 3 left in stock"
- Application: Exclusive access and waitlists
- Warning: Manufactured scarcity damages trust when discovered

**Loss Aversion (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)**
Losses feel roughly 2x as painful as equivalent gains feel pleasurable.
- Application: "Don't miss out" framing vs. "Get the benefit"
- Application: Free trials (removing something feels like loss)
- Application: Risk reversal (money-back guarantees)

**Reciprocity (Cialdini, 1984)**
People feel obligated to return favors and gestures.
- Application: Free content, tools, templates (content marketing)
- Application: Free trials and freemium products
- Application: Personalized gifts or experiences

**Authority (Cialdini, 1984)**
People defer to experts and credible sources.
- Application: Expert endorsements and certifications
- Application: Research citations and data
- Application: Industry analyst recognition (Gartner, Forrester)

**Commitment and Consistency (Cialdini, 1984)**
People align future actions with past commitments.
- Application: Small initial commitments (free trial, newsletter signup)
- Application: Progressive profiling (ask for more info gradually)
- Application: Public commitments increase follow-through

**The Decoy Effect (Ariely, 2008)**
Adding an inferior third option makes one of the original two more attractive.
- Application: Three-tier pricing where the middle tier is the target
- Example: Small ($5), Medium ($12), Large ($14) -- Large looks like best value

**Status Quo Bias**
People prefer the current state of affairs; change requires effort.
- Application: Default selections (opt-out vs. opt-in)
- Application: Auto-renewal and subscription models
- Application: "Recommended" and "Most Popular" defaults

**The Endowment Effect (Thaler, 1980)**
People overvalue things they already possess.
- Application: Free trials create a sense of ownership
- Application: Customization during trial increases switching cost
- Application: "Your personalized dashboard" language

## Motivation Theory Applied to Marketing

### Maslow's Hierarchy (Adapted for Marketing)

```
         Self-Actualization
        (premium, aspirational brands)
       /                              \
      Esteem (status, luxury brands)
     /                                \
    Belonging (community, social brands)
   /                                    \
  Safety (insurance, security, trust signals)
 /                                        \
Physiological (food, shelter, basic needs)
```

**Application:** Map your product to the need level it serves.
Products can serve multiple levels simultaneously.

### Jobs to Be Done (Christensen, HBS)
Customers "hire" products to do a job. Three job dimensions:
1. Functional: The practical task to accomplish
2. Emotional: How the customer wants to feel
3. Social: How the customer wants to be perceived

**JTBD Interview Template:**
- "When did you first start looking for a solution?"
- "What were you trying to accomplish?"
- "What alternatives did you consider?"
- "What made you choose [this product]?"
- "What would have to be true for you to switch?"

## The Fogg Behavior Model (BJ Fogg, Stanford)

Behavior = Motivation * Ability * Prompt (B = MAP)

### When behavior does not occur, diagnose:

```
Is Motivation high?
  |
  +-- No --> Increase motivation (emotional appeal, social proof, incentives)
  |
  +-- Yes --> Is Ability high (is it easy)?
                |
                +-- No --> Reduce friction (simplify, remove steps, pre-fill)
                |
                +-- Yes --> Is there a Prompt?
                              |
                              +-- No --> Add a trigger (CTA, notification, email)
                              |
                              +-- Yes --> Check timing of prompt
```

### Application to Conversion Optimization
| Funnel Stage | Primary Lever |
|-------------|---------------|
| Awareness | Motivation (why should I care?) |
| Consideration | Ability (how easy is it to learn more?) |
| Decision | Prompt (clear CTA at the right moment) |
| Purchase | Ability (frictionless checkout) |
| Retention | Motivation + Prompt (re-engagement) |

## The Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo, 1986)

Two routes to persuasion:

### Central Route
- High involvement, high motivation
- Careful processing of message arguments
- Attitude change is durable and predictive of behavior
- Use when: B2B enterprise sales, high-consideration purchases

### Peripheral Route
- Low involvement, low motivation
- Relies on cues: source attractiveness, number of arguments, social proof
- Attitude change is temporary and less predictive
- Use when: FMCG, impulse purchases, low-cost products

### Matching Persuasion Route to Context

```
Is the buyer highly involved in this purchase?
  |
  +-- Yes --> Central Route
  |           - Strong arguments, evidence, data
  |           - Case studies, white papers, ROI proof
  |           - Expert endorsements, detailed comparisons
  |
  +-- No --> Peripheral Route
              - Celebrity/influencer endorsements
              - Visual appeal, emotional resonance
              - Social proof, popularity cues
              - Simple, memorable messaging
```

## Behavioral Segmentation Framework

Beyond demographics, segment by behavior:

### RFM Analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary)
| Segment | Recency | Frequency | Monetary | Strategy |
|---------|---------|-----------|----------|----------|
| Champions | Recent | Often | High | Reward, upsell |
| Loyal | Recent | Often | Medium | Grow share of wallet |
| Potential | Recent | Low | Low | Nurture, educate |
| At Risk | Not recent | Was often | Was high | Win-back campaigns |
| Lost | Long ago | Was often | Was high | Reactivation or let go |

### Engagement Scoring
- Active (weekly usage): Retention and expansion focus
- Engaged (monthly usage): Activation and habit-building focus
- Passive (quarterly usage): Re-engagement campaigns
- Dormant (no usage 90+ days): Win-back or deprecation

## Choice Architecture (Thaler & Sunstein, Nudge, 2008)

### Principles for Designing Decision Environments

1. **Defaults matter most.** Pre-selected options are chosen 70-90% of the time.
   Application: Default to annual billing, recommended plan, opt-in.

2. **Reduce choice overload.** Hick's Law: decision time increases with options.
   Application: 3-4 pricing tiers maximum. Curate, do not overwhelm.

3. **Make the right choice easy.** Friction is the enemy of conversion.
   Application: One-click purchase, pre-filled forms, saved payment methods.

4. **Provide feedback.** People need confirmation that they are making progress.
   Application: Progress bars, confirmation screens, success messages.

5. **Expect errors.** Design for the mistake path, not just the happy path.
   Application: Forgiving forms, clear error messages, easy undo.

## Cultural and Contextual Factors

### Hofstede's Cultural Dimensions (Applied to Marketing)
- Individualism vs. collectivism: "Be unique" vs. "Join the community"
- Uncertainty avoidance: Risk-taking messaging vs. trust/safety messaging
- Power distance: Aspirational/luxury positioning vs. egalitarian messaging

### Situational Factors
- Purchase urgency: Time pressure increases reliance on heuristics
- Social context: Group purchases vs. individual decisions
- Mood: Positive mood increases peripheral processing
- Physical environment: In-store vs. online decision contexts
