# Qualitative Methods

Comprehensive guide to qualitative research methodologies, from in-depth interviews through ethnographic inquiry, including design, execution, and quality standards.

---

## 1. In-Depth Interviews (IDI)

### 1.1 Definition and Purpose

In-depth interviews are one-on-one conversations between a researcher and a participant, designed to explore the participant's experiences, perspectives, motivations, and decision-making processes in rich detail. IDIs are the foundational qualitative method for understanding the "why" behind behavior.

**When to use IDIs:**
- Exploring sensitive or personal topics where group dynamics would inhibit disclosure.
- Understanding complex decision processes that require sustained probing.
- Studying executive or expert perspectives where group scheduling is impractical.
- Generating deep insight from a small number of information-rich cases.

### 1.2 Interview Guide Design

The interview guide is the researcher's roadmap, not a script. It organizes topics and key questions while allowing flexibility to follow emergent themes.

**Structure of an effective guide:**

**Opening block (5 minutes):**
- Introduction and rapport building.
- Explain the purpose, format, confidentiality, and recording.
- Obtain consent (verbal if written consent was pre-collected).
- "Before we start, do you have any questions?"

**Context block (10 minutes):**
- Background questions to understand the participant's world.
- "Tell me about your role and what a typical day looks like."
- "Walk me through how you currently handle [topic area]."

**Core exploration blocks (30-40 minutes):**
- 3-5 topic areas with 2-3 primary questions each.
- Each question has prepared probes for depth.
- Questions ordered from broad to specific, non-threatening to challenging.
- Critical incident technique: "Think of a specific time when..."
- Process mapping: "Walk me through step by step..."
- Emotional probing: "How did that make you feel? What was going through your mind?"

**Projection block (5-10 minutes):**
- Future-oriented or hypothetical questions.
- "If you could change one thing about..."
- "Imagine the ideal solution..."

**Closing block (5 minutes):**
- "Is there anything I haven't asked that you think is important?"
- "Of everything we discussed, what stands out as most significant to you?"
- Thank participant and explain next steps.

### 1.3 Probing Techniques

Probing is the skill that separates amateur from expert interviewers. The goal is to go deeper without leading.

**Silent probe:** Pause for 5-7 seconds after a response. The social discomfort of silence often prompts elaboration. This is the most underused and most powerful technique.

**Echo probe:** Repeat the last few words with a rising tone. "You said it was frustrating?" This signals interest without directing.

**Elaboration probe:** "Tell me more about that." "Can you unpack that a bit?" "What do you mean by that?"

**Specific example probe:** "Can you give me a concrete example?" "Walk me through the last time that happened."

**Contrast probe:** "How is that different from [alternative]?" "What would happen if [opposite scenario]?"

**Emotional probe:** "How did that feel?" "What was your reaction?" "What was going through your mind at that moment?"

**Timeline probe:** "When did that start?" "What happened before/after?"

**Quantification probe:** "How often does that happen?" "On a scale of 1-10, how [important/frustrating/likely]?"

### 1.4 Building Rapport

Rapport is the foundation of quality interview data. Without trust, participants give surface-level, socially desirable responses.

**Techniques:**
- Mirror the participant's energy level and communication style.
- Demonstrate genuine curiosity. Lean in. Make eye contact. Nod.
- Validate without agreeing. "That makes sense." Not "I totally agree."
- Share minimal personal information to humanize yourself without centering your experience.
- Never judge, correct, or contradict. Even when participants say something factually wrong, note it internally but do not challenge in the moment.
- Use the participant's name naturally.
- Match vocabulary to the participant's level. No jargon unless they use it first.

## 2. Focus Groups

### 2.1 When to Use Focus Groups

Focus groups leverage group dynamics to generate insights that individual interviews might miss. Social interaction stimulates ideas, reveals shared language, exposes disagreements, and produces emergent themes through participant-to-participant dialogue.

**Best for:** Generating ideas, exploring shared experiences, understanding social norms and language, testing concepts and messages, exploring areas where group interaction adds value.

**Not appropriate for:** Sensitive topics, individual decision processes, topics where conformity pressure would suppress honest responses, executive research, usability testing.

### 2.2 Focus Group Design

**Size:** 6-8 participants per group. Fewer than 5 limits interaction; more than 10 prevents everyone from contributing.

**Composition:** Homogeneous within groups (similar characteristics so participants feel comfortable sharing), heterogeneous across groups (different segments to capture diverse perspectives). Never mix decision-makers with their subordinates, or customers with prospects.

**Number of groups:** Minimum 2 groups per segment to distinguish group-specific dynamics from genuine findings. 3-4 groups per segment for robust analysis.

**Duration:** 60-90 minutes. Beyond 90 minutes, energy and contribution quality decline.

### 2.3 Moderation Skills

**Opening:** Establish ground rules (everyone speaks, no wrong answers, one at a time, we want disagreement). Use an ice-breaker activity to reduce anxiety.

**During discussion:**
- Ensure balanced participation. Redirect dominators: "Thank you, [name]. I'd love to hear from others. [Quiet person], what do you think?"
- Manage tangents: "That's interesting. Let me park that and come back to our main topic."
- Probe disagreements: "I notice some different perspectives here. Can we explore that?"
- Use activities (card sorts, collages, ranking exercises) to break monotony and generate discussion.

**Expert moderator vs research moderator:** Focus groups require active facilitation skills beyond interview skills. Group dynamics, energy management, and conflict resolution are additional competencies.

## 3. Ethnographic Research

### 3.1 Ethnography in Applied Research

Adapted from anthropology, ethnographic research involves prolonged immersion in the participant's environment to understand culture, context, and behavior as they naturally occur. In applied research, ethnography is typically shorter and more focused than academic anthropology but retains the core principles of naturalistic observation and cultural interpretation.

**Duration in applied research:** 2-8 hours per participant/site (mini-ethnography) to 1-4 weeks (traditional ethnography).

### 3.2 Ethnographic Fieldwork

**Preparation:** Define the cultural domain and research questions. Negotiate access. Establish trust with gatekeepers. Plan recording methods (field notes, photography, video). Obtain ethical approval.

**In the field:**
- Observe without judging. Record what you see, not what you interpret.
- Ask naive questions. "Why do you do it that way?" (even if you think you know).
- Follow the participant's lead. Their priorities reveal what matters.
- Document artifacts: tools, documents, physical spaces, signs, workarounds.
- Note the unsaid: what topics are avoided, what behavior is hidden, what contradictions exist between stated values and observed actions.

**After the field:**
- Expand field notes immediately (within 24 hours). Memory degrades rapidly.
- Distinguish observation (what you saw) from inference (what you think it means).
- Identify patterns, contradictions, and surprises.
- Conduct follow-up interviews to validate observations.

### 3.3 Cultural Probes

Self-documentation kits given to participants to capture their world in their own terms. Kits may include disposable cameras, diaries, maps to annotate, collage materials, and prompts. Cultural probes generate inspirational data for design rather than systematic research data.

## 4. Grounded Theory

### 4.1 Overview

Developed by Glaser and Strauss (1967), grounded theory is a systematic methodology for developing theory from data. Rather than testing a pre-existing hypothesis, the researcher iteratively collects and analyzes data, allowing theory to emerge from the data itself.

### 4.2 Core Process

**Open coding:** Read data line by line. Label concepts. Break data into discrete chunks. Generate initial codes directly from the data, not from pre-existing frameworks.

**Constant comparison:** Compare each new data instance to existing codes. Similar instances strengthen a code; different instances create new codes or refine existing ones.

**Axial coding:** Identify relationships between codes. Group codes into categories. Develop category properties and dimensions. Explore causal conditions, context, strategies, and consequences.

**Selective coding:** Identify the core category that integrates all other categories. Develop a theoretical narrative that explains the phenomenon. The core category is the central theme around which all other categories revolve.

**Theoretical sampling:** Data collection is guided by the emerging theory. As patterns emerge, seek participants or contexts that will help refine, validate, or challenge the developing theory.

**Theoretical saturation:** Data collection continues until new data no longer generates new codes or categories. Saturation is a judgment call, not a statistical calculation.

## 5. Phenomenological Research

### 5.1 Purpose

Phenomenology seeks to understand the lived experience of a phenomenon from the perspective of those who experience it. It asks: "What is it like to experience X?" The goal is to describe the essence of the experience, not to explain or theorize.

### 5.2 Husserl's Descriptive Phenomenology

The researcher brackets (sets aside) their own assumptions and biases (epoché) to encounter the phenomenon as the participant experiences it. Data is collected through deep, open-ended interviews (typically 60-120 minutes). Analysis identifies significant statements, meaning units, and textural/structural descriptions.

### 5.3 Heidegger's Interpretive Phenomenology (IPA)

Interpretive Phenomenological Analysis acknowledges that the researcher cannot fully bracket their own experience. Instead, interpretation is a legitimate part of understanding. The researcher engages in a "double hermeneutic": the participant interprets their experience, and the researcher interprets that interpretation.

**IPA is particularly useful for:** Studying life transitions, identity experiences, chronic conditions, pivotal decisions, emotional responses to technology.

## 6. Narrative Analysis

### 6.1 Overview

Narrative analysis examines the stories people tell to understand how they make sense of their experiences. The structure, content, and performance of narratives reveal identity, values, power dynamics, and cultural meanings.

### 6.2 Elements of Narrative Analysis

**Structural analysis:** How is the story organized? Beginning, middle, end. Orientation, complicating action, evaluation, resolution, coda (Labov model).

**Thematic analysis of narratives:** What themes recur across stories? What values and beliefs are embedded?

**Performative analysis:** How is the story told? What is the audience? What identity is being constructed?

### 6.3 Application in Research

Customer journey narratives reveal emotional highs and lows, critical moments, and attribution of blame or credit. Brand narratives reveal how customers incorporate products into their identity stories. Switching narratives reveal the tipping points that drive customer churn.

## 7. Recording and Transcription

### 7.1 Recording Best Practices

- Record audio at minimum; video when body language or context matters.
- Use dedicated recording equipment (not just laptop microphone). External USB microphones or recording apps with backup.
- Always have a backup recording method.
- Test equipment before every session.
- Obtain explicit consent for recording. Explain storage and deletion policies.

### 7.2 Transcription Standards

**Verbatim transcription:** Word-for-word, including filler words (um, uh), false starts, repetitions, and pauses. Required for discourse analysis and detailed coding.

**Clean verbatim:** Minor filler words removed, false starts cleaned up, but meaning preserved exactly. Standard for thematic analysis and most applied research.

**Summary transcription:** Key points summarized rather than transcribed verbatim. Loses nuance. Acceptable only for supplementary data.

### 7.3 Transcription Services

**Human transcription:** Rev ($1.50-$2.00/min), GoTranscript, TranscribeMe. Higher accuracy, especially for accents and technical terminology. Turnaround: 12-48 hours.

**AI transcription:** Otter.ai, Whisper (OpenAI), Descript, Trint. Lower cost ($0.00-$0.25/min), faster turnaround (minutes), but requires manual review and correction. Accuracy: 85-95% depending on audio quality and accents.

**Best practice:** Use AI transcription for initial draft, then human-review sections used as evidence in reports. Critical quotes must be verified against the recording.

## 8. Qualitative Research Quality Standards

### 8.1 Lincoln and Guba's Trustworthiness Criteria

| Quantitative Criterion | Qualitative Equivalent | Strategy |
|----------------------|----------------------|----------|
| Internal validity | Credibility | Prolonged engagement, triangulation, member checking, peer debriefing |
| External validity | Transferability | Thick description, purposive sampling |
| Reliability | Dependability | Audit trail, reflexive journal, inquiry audit |
| Objectivity | Confirmability | Audit trail, triangulation, reflexivity |

### 8.2 Reflexivity

The researcher's position, biases, and assumptions inevitably influence data collection and interpretation. Reflexivity involves systematically examining and documenting how the researcher's own perspective shapes the research. Maintain a reflexive journal throughout the study.

## 9. Qualitative Methods Selection Guide

| Question Type | Recommended Method | Sample Size |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Individual experience in depth | IDI | 8-20 |
| Shared norms, language, ideas | Focus groups | 3-6 groups |
| Behavior in natural context | Ethnography | 5-15 sites |
| Theory development from data | Grounded theory | 20-30+ |
| Essence of lived experience | Phenomenology (IPA) | 6-15 |
| How people make meaning | Narrative analysis | 5-25 |
| Sensitive or stigmatized topics | IDI (never focus groups) | 8-20 |

## 10. Qualitative Methods Quality Checklist

- [ ] Method matches the research question type
- [ ] Interview guide or observation protocol is designed and pilot tested
- [ ] Sampling is purposive and justified
- [ ] Consent and ethical protocols are in place
- [ ] Recording and transcription standards are defined
- [ ] Analysis method is specified (thematic, grounded theory, IPA, etc.)
- [ ] Trustworthiness strategies are implemented (member checking, triangulation)
- [ ] Reflexivity is documented
- [ ] Findings are grounded in data (supported by quotes/observations)
- [ ] Limitations of the qualitative approach are acknowledged
