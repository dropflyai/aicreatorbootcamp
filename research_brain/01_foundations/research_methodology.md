# Research Methodology

Comprehensive framework for designing and executing rigorous research across market, user, and competitive domains.

---

## 1. Research Paradigms

### 1.1 Positivism

Positivism asserts that objective knowledge is obtainable through systematic observation and measurement. Reality exists independently of the observer. Research under this paradigm seeks generalizable laws through hypothesis testing, controlled experiments, and statistical analysis. The researcher maintains detachment. Data is quantitative. Results are replicable.

**Strengths:** High reliability, statistical generalizability, clear cause-and-effect identification.
**Weaknesses:** Oversimplifies human behavior, ignores context, cannot capture meaning or motivation.
**Best for:** A/B testing, pricing experiments, conversion rate optimization, large-scale surveys.

### 1.2 Interpretivism

Interpretivism holds that reality is socially constructed and subjective. Multiple valid interpretations exist for any phenomenon. Research under this paradigm seeks deep understanding of meaning, context, and lived experience. The researcher is an active participant in knowledge construction.

**Strengths:** Rich contextual understanding, captures nuance and motivation, reveals the "why."
**Weaknesses:** Limited generalizability, researcher bias risk, time-intensive, difficult to replicate.
**Best for:** User interviews, ethnographic studies, diary studies, exploratory research.

### 1.3 Pragmatism

Pragmatism rejects the forced choice between paradigms. The research question dictates the method. Truth is what works. This paradigm embraces mixed methods, combining quantitative and qualitative approaches within a single study. Most applied business research operates within pragmatism.

**Strengths:** Flexible, practical, addresses real-world complexity, produces actionable findings.
**Weaknesses:** May lack philosophical coherence, risk of superficial mixing without integration.
**Best for:** Most business and product research, strategic studies, market entry analysis.

## 2. Quantitative vs Qualitative vs Mixed Methods

### 2.1 Quantitative Research

Collects numerical data analyzed through statistical methods. Answers "how much," "how many," "how often." Requires structured instruments (surveys, analytics, experiments). Sample sizes must support statistical inference. Results are expressed in metrics, correlations, and significance tests.

**Design types:** Experimental, quasi-experimental, correlational, descriptive survey.
**Analysis:** Descriptive statistics, inferential statistics, regression, ANOVA, factor analysis.
**Quality criteria:** Validity, reliability, objectivity, generalizability.

### 2.2 Qualitative Research

Collects non-numerical data (words, observations, artifacts) analyzed through interpretive methods. Answers "why," "how," "what does it mean." Uses unstructured or semi-structured instruments. Sample sizes are small but deep. Results are expressed in themes, patterns, and narratives.

**Design types:** Phenomenological, grounded theory, ethnographic, case study, narrative.
**Analysis:** Thematic analysis, content analysis, discourse analysis, constant comparative.
**Quality criteria:** Credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability (Lincoln & Guba).

### 2.3 Mixed Methods

Combines quantitative and qualitative within a single study or program of research. Three core designs exist:

- **Convergent parallel:** Collect both simultaneously, merge during analysis.
- **Explanatory sequential:** Quantitative first, then qualitative to explain results.
- **Exploratory sequential:** Qualitative first, then quantitative to test findings at scale.

Integration is the critical differentiator. Simply doing both is not mixed methods; the data streams must inform each other at analysis or interpretation stages.

## 3. Research Design

### 3.1 Exploratory Research

**Purpose:** Generate hypotheses, discover patterns, understand a new domain.
**When:** Entering a new market, investigating an unfamiliar user segment, early-stage product discovery.
**Methods:** Open-ended interviews, focus groups, ethnography, literature review, secondary data analysis.
**Output:** Themes, hypotheses, conceptual models, research questions for further investigation.
**Risk:** May not produce immediately actionable findings; requires follow-up research to validate.

### 3.2 Descriptive Research

**Purpose:** Characterize a population, market, or phenomenon accurately.
**When:** Sizing a market, profiling user segments, benchmarking satisfaction, auditing competitors.
**Methods:** Surveys, observational studies, content analysis, secondary data synthesis.
**Output:** Distributions, frequencies, profiles, benchmarks, segment descriptions.
**Risk:** Describes but does not explain; correlation is not causation.

### 3.3 Causal Research

**Purpose:** Establish cause-and-effect relationships between variables.
**When:** Testing whether a price change affects conversion, whether a feature drives retention.
**Methods:** Experiments (A/B tests, randomized controlled trials), quasi-experiments, difference-in-differences.
**Output:** Causal estimates, treatment effects, confidence intervals.
**Risk:** Requires rigorous control; confounding variables threaten internal validity.

## 4. Validity

### 4.1 Internal Validity

The degree to which the study establishes a trustworthy cause-and-effect relationship. Threats include: selection bias, maturation, history effects, testing effects, instrumentation changes, regression to the mean, attrition, diffusion of treatment.

**Mitigation:** Random assignment, control groups, blinding, pre-registration, manipulation checks.

### 4.2 External Validity

The degree to which findings generalize beyond the study context to other populations, settings, and times. Threats include: sample specificity, setting artificiality, temporal specificity, treatment variation irrelevance.

**Mitigation:** Representative sampling, multi-site studies, replication across contexts.

### 4.3 Construct Validity

The degree to which the study measures what it claims to measure. Threats include: inadequate operationalization, mono-method bias, mono-operation bias, hypothesis guessing, evaluation apprehension.

**Mitigation:** Multiple measures, validated instruments, pilot testing, convergent and discriminant evidence.

### 4.4 Statistical Conclusion Validity

The degree to which conclusions about covariation are accurate. Threats include: low statistical power, violated assumptions, fishing/p-hacking, unreliable measures, restricted range, heterogeneity of units.

**Mitigation:** Power analysis, assumption checking, pre-registration, appropriate effect size reporting.

## 5. Reliability

### 5.1 Test-Retest Reliability

Consistency of results when the same instrument is administered to the same participants at two time points. Measured by Pearson correlation or intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC). Target: r > 0.70 for exploratory, r > 0.80 for applied research.

### 5.2 Inter-Rater Reliability

Agreement between two or more independent raters coding the same data. Measured by Cohen's kappa (two raters) or Fleiss' kappa (multiple raters). Benchmarks: 0.41-0.60 moderate, 0.61-0.80 substantial, 0.81-1.00 almost perfect (Landis & Koch).

### 5.3 Internal Consistency

Degree to which items within a scale measure the same construct. Measured by Cronbach's alpha or McDonald's omega. Target: alpha > 0.70 for research, alpha > 0.80 for high-stakes decisions. Split-half reliability as alternative.

## 6. Sampling Theory

### 6.1 Probability Sampling

Every member of the population has a known, non-zero chance of selection. Enables statistical inference to the population.

- **Simple random sampling:** Equal probability for all members. Gold standard but often impractical.
- **Stratified sampling:** Population divided into strata, random sample from each. Reduces variance.
- **Cluster sampling:** Population divided into clusters, random clusters selected. Cost-efficient for geographic dispersion.
- **Systematic sampling:** Every kth element. Simple but risk of periodicity bias.

### 6.2 Non-Probability Sampling

Selection based on accessibility, judgment, or purpose. Cannot generalize statistically but appropriate for qualitative and exploratory research.

- **Convenience sampling:** Most accessible participants. Highest bias risk.
- **Purposive sampling:** Deliberately selected for specific characteristics. Essential for qualitative depth.
- **Snowball sampling:** Participants recruit others. Useful for hard-to-reach populations.
- **Quota sampling:** Non-random selection within predefined quotas. Approximates stratification.

### 6.3 Sample Size Calculations

For quantitative research, sample size depends on four factors:
1. **Effect size** (d, r, or odds ratio): Smallest meaningful difference.
2. **Significance level** (alpha): Typically 0.05.
3. **Statistical power** (1 - beta): Typically 0.80 (80%).
4. **Variability** (standard deviation): From pilot data or literature.

Formula for two-group comparison: n = (Z_alpha/2 + Z_beta)^2 * 2 * sigma^2 / delta^2

For surveys, the standard formula incorporates population size, confidence level, and margin of error: n = (Z^2 * p * (1-p)) / e^2, adjusted for finite population.

### 6.4 Power Analysis

Power is the probability of detecting an effect that actually exists. Underpowered studies waste resources and produce unreliable results. Conduct a priori power analysis before data collection using G*Power, R (pwr package), or manual calculation.

**Common minimum sample sizes by method:**
- Two-group comparison (medium effect): ~64 per group
- Correlation (medium effect): ~85 total
- Regression (medium effect, 5 predictors): ~92 total
- Chi-square (medium effect): ~88 total

## 7. Research Ethics

### 7.1 IRB and Ethical Review

Institutional Review Boards review research involving human subjects. While formal IRB review is typically academic, the principles apply to all research: beneficence (do good), non-maleficence (do no harm), autonomy (respect for persons), justice (fair distribution of burdens and benefits).

### 7.2 Informed Consent

Participants must understand what participation entails before agreeing. Elements: purpose, procedures, duration, risks, benefits, confidentiality protections, right to withdraw, contact information. Consent forms must be written in plain language. Special provisions for vulnerable populations.

### 7.3 Deception and Debrief

Deception is sometimes necessary (e.g., pricing experiments where knowing the true purpose would change behavior). Acceptable only when: no alternative exists, the scientific value justifies it, participants are fully debriefed afterward, no lasting harm results.

### 7.4 Data Privacy and Protection

Research data must comply with applicable regulations (GDPR, CCPA, etc.). Principles: data minimization, purpose limitation, storage limitation, anonymization or pseudonymization, secure storage, clear retention and destruction policies. PII requires enhanced protections.

## 8. Choosing the Right Methodology

| Research Question Type | Recommended Approach | Example |
|----------------------|---------------------|---------|
| What is happening? | Descriptive / Quantitative | Market sizing, usage analytics |
| Why is it happening? | Exploratory / Qualitative | User interviews, ethnography |
| Will X cause Y? | Causal / Experimental | A/B tests, pricing experiments |
| How much does X matter? | Correlational / Quantitative | Survey + regression |
| What should we build? | Mixed Methods (exploratory sequential) | Interviews then concept test survey |
| Is the solution working? | Mixed Methods (convergent) | Usability test + satisfaction survey |

## 9. Research Quality Checklist

- [ ] Research question is clearly defined and answerable
- [ ] Paradigm and methodology are appropriate for the question
- [ ] Design addresses relevant validity threats
- [ ] Sampling strategy matches the research purpose
- [ ] Sample size is justified (power analysis for quantitative)
- [ ] Instruments are reliable and valid
- [ ] Ethical considerations are addressed
- [ ] Analysis plan is specified before data collection
- [ ] Limitations are acknowledged
- [ ] Findings are presented with appropriate confidence

## 10. Common Methodological Errors

1. **Convenience sampling masquerading as representative:** Using whoever is available then generalizing broadly.
2. **Leading questions in surveys:** Embedding assumptions that bias responses.
3. **Confirmation bias in analysis:** Seeking evidence that supports pre-existing beliefs.
4. **Underpowered studies:** Sample too small to detect real effects.
5. **HARKing:** Hypothesizing After Results are Known, presenting post-hoc findings as a priori.
6. **Ignoring non-response bias:** Assuming non-respondents are similar to respondents.
7. **Conflating correlation and causation:** Drawing causal conclusions from observational data.
8. **p-hacking:** Running multiple analyses until a significant result appears.
