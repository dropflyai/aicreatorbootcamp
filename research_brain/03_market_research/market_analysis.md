## 1. Porter's Five Forces

Michael Porter's Five Forces framework (1979, updated 2008) analyzes the competitive intensity and attractiveness of an industry. It remains the foundational tool for industry-level analysis, though it must be supplemented with dynamic and ecosystem perspectives.

### 1.1 Threat of New Entrants

New entrants bring new capacity, desire to gain market share, and often substantial resources. The threat depends on barriers to entry and the expected retaliation from existing competitors.

**Barriers to entry:**
- **Economies of scale:** Incumbents operate at lower per-unit costs. New entrants must either enter at scale (capital-intensive, risky) or accept cost disadvantage.
- **Network effects:** Value increases with user count. Platforms with strong network effects (marketplaces, social networks) create winner-take-most dynamics.
- **Switching costs:** Customer investment in learning, integration, data migration, and workflow customization. High switching costs insulate incumbents.
- **Capital requirements:** Initial investment needed to compete. Declining in software (cloud infrastructure lowers entry cost) but increasing in hardware, biotech, and regulated industries.
- **Incumbency advantages independent of scale:** Brand recognition, proprietary technology, access to raw materials, favorable locations, learning curve advantages.
- **Regulatory barriers:** Licensing, compliance requirements, data residency rules, industry-specific regulations.
- **Access to distribution channels:** Established relationships with resellers, app stores, integration partners.

**Assessment questions:** How easily can a well-funded startup enter this market? What would it cost? How long before they become competitive? How would incumbents respond?

### 1.2 Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Suppliers are powerful when they can raise prices or reduce quality without losing business. In software, key suppliers include cloud infrastructure providers (AWS, GCP, Azure), API providers, data providers, and talent (developers, researchers).

**Factors increasing supplier power:** Few suppliers, high switching costs, differentiated inputs, no substitutes, supplier can forward-integrate, industry is not an important customer.

### 1.3 Bargaining Power of Buyers

Buyers are powerful when they can demand lower prices, higher quality, or more services. In B2B SaaS, buyer power varies dramatically by segment: enterprise buyers have significant leverage; SMBs individually have little but collectively can drive commoditization.

**Factors increasing buyer power:** Few buyers (concentrated demand), standardized products, low switching costs, buyer can backward-integrate, price-sensitive buyers, full information about alternatives.

### 1.4 Threat of Substitute Products

Substitutes are products from different industries that fulfill the same need. Spreadsheets substitute for specialized software. Video calls substitute for business travel. AI-generated content substitutes for human writers.

**Assessment:** Identify substitutes by asking "What else could the customer use to solve this problem?" Evaluate substitute attractiveness on price-performance ratio and switching costs.

### 1.5 Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

The intensity of competition among current players. Fierce rivalry erodes profitability through price wars, advertising battles, product enhancements, and service improvements.

**Factors intensifying rivalry:** Many competitors of similar size, slow industry growth, high fixed costs (pressure to fill capacity), low differentiation, high exit barriers, diverse competitors (different strategies and origins).

### 1.6 Applying Five Forces

Rate each force as Low/Medium/High. Industries where all five forces are weak (low threat of entry, low buyer/supplier power, few substitutes, mild rivalry) offer the highest profit potential. Industries where multiple forces are strong require careful positioning or structural change.

## 2. PESTEL Analysis

Macro-environmental analysis examining six categories of external forces that shape industry conditions.

### 2.1 Political Factors

Government stability, trade policy, tax policy, labor laws, environmental regulations, political risk, government subsidies, lobbying landscape. For technology: data sovereignty requirements, encryption regulations, content moderation laws, antitrust enforcement.

### 2.2 Economic Factors

GDP growth, inflation rates, interest rates, exchange rates, unemployment, consumer confidence, disposable income, business investment cycles. For SaaS: IT budget trends, CFO spending priorities, procurement cycle changes, recession resilience of the category.

### 2.3 Social Factors

Demographics (aging population, urbanization, household formation), cultural attitudes, education levels, lifestyle changes, health consciousness, social mobility, work patterns (remote work, gig economy). For technology: digital literacy, privacy attitudes, AI acceptance, sustainability expectations.

### 2.4 Technological Factors

R&D spending, technology infrastructure, automation rates, innovation diffusion, patent activity, technology transfer, digital transformation maturity. For software: AI/ML advancement, API economy growth, low-code/no-code trend, edge computing, quantum computing timeline.

### 2.5 Environmental Factors

Climate change impacts, sustainability regulations, carbon footprint requirements, resource scarcity, environmental consciousness. For technology: data center energy consumption, electronic waste, green computing, ESG reporting requirements.

### 2.6 Legal Factors

Antitrust/competition law, data protection regulations (GDPR, CCPA, emerging global frameworks), employment law, intellectual property protection, consumer protection, industry-specific regulations (HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOX).

### 2.7 PESTEL Application

For each factor: (1) Identify the specific trend or force. (2) Assess its impact on the industry (positive, negative, neutral). (3) Assess its likelihood and timeframe. (4) Determine strategic implications for your business.

## 3. Value Chain Analysis

Porter's value chain decomposes industry activities to identify where value is created and where profit pools exist.

### 3.1 Primary Activities

**Inbound logistics:** Acquiring inputs. In software: recruiting talent, procuring cloud infrastructure, obtaining data.
**Operations:** Transforming inputs into outputs. In software: product development, quality assurance, deployment.
**Outbound logistics:** Delivering outputs. In software: distribution (app stores, web, API), content delivery, customer onboarding.
**Marketing and sales:** Creating awareness and closing deals. In software: demand generation, sales process, partnerships.
**Service:** Post-sale support. In software: customer success, technical support, training.

### 3.2 Support Activities

**Firm infrastructure:** Management, finance, legal, compliance.
**Human resource management:** Recruiting, training, compensation, culture.
**Technology development:** R&D, platform maintenance, innovation.
**Procurement:** Vendor management, contract negotiation, sourcing.

### 3.3 Industry Value Chain Mapping

Map the entire industry value chain from raw inputs to end consumer. Identify which stages capture the most value (profit pools). Look for stages being disrupted, disintermediated, or re-bundled.

## 4. Market Structure Analysis

### 4.1 Market Structure Types

**Perfect competition:** Many sellers, homogeneous products, free entry/exit, perfect information. Theoretical ideal; rarely exists in practice. Closest examples: commodity markets.

**Monopolistic competition:** Many sellers, differentiated products, relatively free entry/exit. Most consumer markets and many B2B software categories. Competition on features, brand, and customer experience rather than price alone.

**Oligopoly:** Few dominant sellers, significant barriers to entry, interdependent pricing. Cloud infrastructure (AWS, Azure, GCP), mobile OS (iOS, Android), search (Google), social (Meta). Game theory applies; competitors react to each other's moves.

**Monopoly:** Single seller, very high barriers to entry. Rare in technology due to innovation cycles, but temporary monopolies exist through network effects, patents, or regulatory protection.

### 4.2 Concentration Metrics

**Concentration ratio (CRn):** Market share of the top n firms. CR4 > 60% suggests oligopoly.
**Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI):** Sum of squared market shares of all firms. HHI < 1,500: competitive. 1,500-2,500: moderately concentrated. > 2,500: highly concentrated. Used by DOJ for antitrust analysis.

### 4.3 Market Structure Dynamics

Markets do not maintain structure permanently. Fragmented markets consolidate through M&A and winner-take-most dynamics. Concentrated markets fragment through disruption and regulatory intervention. Mapping the direction of structural change is as important as assessing current structure.

## 5. Industry Lifecycle Stage

### 5.1 The Industry Lifecycle Model

**Introduction:** New technology, few competitors, high uncertainty, rapid product evolution, early adopter customers, high costs, low/negative profitability. Growth rate: accelerating but from small base.

**Growth:** Demand increasing rapidly, new competitors entering, product standardizing, distribution expanding, customer base broadening. Growth rate: 20%+ annually. Focus: market share capture.

**Maturity:** Growth slowing, market saturated, competition intensifies on price and service, consolidation through M&A, process innovation over product innovation. Growth rate: GDP-level. Focus: efficiency and retention.

**Decline:** Demand decreasing due to substitution or structural change. Competitors exiting, overcapacity, price erosion. Focus: cash extraction, niche defense, or exit.

### 5.2 Strategic Implications by Stage

| Stage | Priority | Key Metrics | Common Mistakes |
|-------|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Introduction | Product-market fit | Engagement, retention, NPS | Premature scaling |
| Growth | Market share capture | Revenue growth, CAC, expansion | Ignoring unit economics |
| Maturity | Efficiency, retention | NRR, margins, market share | Under-investing in innovation |
| Decline | Cash flow optimization | Free cash flow, cost reduction | Over-investing in a shrinking market |

## 6. Disruptive Innovation Theory

### 6.1 Christensen's Framework

Clayton Christensen's theory (The Innovator's Dilemma, 1997) explains how incumbents lose to seemingly inferior products. Disruption occurs when a new entrant targets the low end of the market (or non-consumers) with a simpler, cheaper, more accessible product, then improves until it satisfies mainstream requirements.

**Key concepts:**
- **Sustaining innovation:** Improvement along dimensions that mainstream customers value. Incumbents excel at this.
- **Disruptive innovation:** Initially worse on traditional metrics but better on accessibility, simplicity, or cost. Incumbents ignore it until it is too late.
- **Performance overshoot:** When incumbent products exceed what most customers need, creating an opening for "good enough" alternatives.

### 6.2 Disruption Patterns

**Low-end disruption:** Targets overserved customers who are paying for features they do not use. Example: Budget airlines vs full-service carriers.

**New-market disruption:** Targets non-consumers who previously could not access the solution. Example: Cloud computing enabling startups that could not afford data centers.

### 6.3 Assessing Disruption Risk

- Is there performance overshoot in the market? Are customers paying for capabilities they do not use?
- Are there non-consumers who would use a simpler, cheaper version?
- Is a new technology enabling a 10x cost reduction or accessibility improvement?
- Are incumbents dismissing the new entrant as "not a real competitor"?

## 7. Jobs-to-Be-Done Market Definition

### 7.1 JTBD Market Definition

Traditional market definition based on product categories or demographics often leads to myopic analysis. Jobs-to-Be-Done (Christensen, Ulwick) defines markets by the job the customer is trying to accomplish.

**Example:** The "commuting market" includes cars, public transit, bicycles, e-scooters, remote work software, and relocation services. Product-category analysis would treat these as separate markets; JTBD analysis reveals they all compete for the same job.

### 7.2 Job Mapping

Deconstruct the core job into steps: Define what needs to be done, locate inputs, prepare, confirm readiness, execute, monitor, modify, conclude. At each step, identify desired outcomes (metrics customers use to measure success). Underserved outcomes represent innovation opportunities.

## 8. Category Design

### 8.1 Defining a New Category

Category design (Play Bigger framework) involves creating and dominating a new market category rather than competing in an existing one. The category creator educates the market, defines the language, and establishes the evaluation criteria.

**Category creation signals:** Existing categories do not capture your value proposition. Customers struggle to describe what you do using existing vocabulary. No analyst report covers your space. You consistently lose deals because buyers do not understand the category.

### 8.2 Category Design Process

1. **Identify the category:** Name the problem space in a way that positions your solution as the natural leader.
2. **Define the category narrative:** Articulate why the old way is broken and why the new category is inevitable.
3. **Educate the market:** Content marketing, analyst briefings, events, customer success stories.
4. **Establish evaluation criteria:** Define the features and capabilities that matter in this category (biased toward your strengths).
