# Glossary -- Research Brain

## Research Methodology

**A/B Testing (Split Testing)**: A controlled experiment comparing two variants (A and B) to determine which performs better on a defined metric. Participants are randomly assigned to conditions. Requires sufficient sample size and duration to achieve statistical significance.

**Bias**: Systematic error in research design, data collection, or analysis that distorts findings away from the true value. Common types: selection bias, response bias, confirmation bias, survivorship bias, recall bias, social desirability bias, anchoring bias.

**Blind Study**: A study where participants (single-blind) or both participants and researchers (double-blind) do not know which condition/group they are in. Reduces expectation effects and researcher bias.

**Confidence Interval (CI)**: A range of values within which the true population parameter is estimated to fall, with a specified probability (typically 95%). A 95% CI means that if the study were repeated many times, 95% of calculated intervals would contain the true value.

**Confounding Variable (Confounder)**: A variable that influences both the independent and dependent variables, creating a spurious association. Failure to control for confounders threatens internal validity.

**Construct Validity**: The degree to which a test or measure actually assesses the theoretical construct it claims to measure. Example: Does a "customer satisfaction" survey actually measure satisfaction, or does it measure politeness?

**Control Group**: In experimental design, the group that does not receive the treatment or intervention. Provides a baseline against which the treatment group is compared.

**Correlation**: A statistical relationship between two variables. Correlation does not imply causation. Pearson's r ranges from -1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive). Zero indicates no linear relationship.

**Cross-sectional Study**: A study that collects data at a single point in time. Provides a snapshot but cannot establish temporal ordering or causation.

**Dependent Variable (DV)**: The outcome variable that the researcher measures. It is expected to change in response to manipulation of the independent variable.

**Effect Size**: A quantitative measure of the magnitude of a phenomenon. Unlike p-values, effect sizes indicate practical significance. Common measures: Cohen's d, odds ratio, correlation coefficient, eta-squared.

**External Validity**: The extent to which findings generalize beyond the specific study to other populations, settings, times, and measures. Threatened by non-representative samples and artificial settings.

**Generalizability**: The degree to which research findings can be applied to populations or contexts beyond the specific study sample and conditions.

**Hypothesis**: A testable prediction about the relationship between variables. The null hypothesis (H0) states no effect; the alternative hypothesis (H1) states the expected effect.

**Independent Variable (IV)**: The variable that the researcher manipulates or observes to determine its effect on the dependent variable.

**Informed Consent**: The process of providing participants with complete information about a study's purpose, procedures, risks, and benefits, and obtaining their voluntary agreement to participate.

**Internal Validity**: The degree to which a study establishes a causal relationship between the independent and dependent variables, ruling out alternative explanations.

**IRB (Institutional Review Board)**: A committee that reviews research involving human subjects to ensure ethical standards are met. Even when formal IRB review is not required, the principles (beneficence, justice, respect for persons) should guide all human research.

**Likert Scale**: A psychometric scale commonly used in survey research. Typically 5 or 7 points ranging from "Strongly Disagree" to "Strongly Agree." Named after Rensis Likert (1932). Properly, individual items are Likert-type items; a Likert scale is the composite of multiple items.

**Longitudinal Study**: A study that collects data from the same subjects over an extended period. Enables analysis of change over time and stronger causal inference than cross-sectional designs.

**Margin of Error (MOE)**: The range of values above and below a sample statistic within which the true population parameter is likely to fall. For proportions: MOE = z * sqrt(p(1-p)/n). Commonly reported as +/- percentage points.

**Mean**: The arithmetic average of a set of values. Sensitive to outliers. For skewed distributions, the median may be more representative.

**Median**: The middle value when data is ordered. More robust to outliers than the mean. Preferred for income data and other skewed distributions.

**Mixed Methods**: A research approach that combines qualitative and quantitative methods in a single study or program of research. Sequential (one method informs the next) or concurrent (both methods simultaneously).

**Null Hypothesis (H0)**: The default assumption that there is no effect, no difference, or no relationship. Statistical tests assess whether observed data are inconsistent with the null hypothesis.

**Operationalization**: The process of defining how a theoretical construct will be measured or manipulated in a specific study. Example: "user satisfaction" operationalized as "NPS score on a 0-10 scale."

**P-value**: The probability of obtaining results as extreme as the observed results, assuming the null hypothesis is true. A p-value below 0.05 is conventionally considered "statistically significant," but this threshold is arbitrary and should be interpreted in context with effect size.

**Population**: The complete group of individuals or items that the researcher wants to draw conclusions about. The sample is a subset of the population.

**Power (Statistical Power)**: The probability that a study will detect an effect when one truly exists. Conventionally set at 0.80 (80%). Power depends on effect size, sample size, alpha level, and measurement precision.

**Qualitative Research**: Research that collects and analyzes non-numerical data (words, images, observations) to understand concepts, opinions, or experiences. Provides depth and context. Methods include interviews, ethnography, and thematic analysis.

**Quantitative Research**: Research that collects and analyzes numerical data to identify patterns, test hypotheses, and make predictions. Provides breadth and generalizability. Methods include surveys, experiments, and statistical analysis.

**Quasi-experiment**: An experiment that lacks random assignment to conditions. Common in field settings where randomization is impractical. Weaker causal inference than true experiments.

**Reliability**: The consistency of a measure. A reliable measure produces similar results under consistent conditions. Types: test-retest reliability, inter-rater reliability, internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha).

**Replication**: Repeating a study to verify findings. Direct replication uses the same methods; conceptual replication uses different methods to test the same hypothesis. The replication crisis in social science underscores the importance of designing replicable studies.

**Sample**: A subset of the population selected for study. The sample should be representative of the population to support generalization.

**Sample Size**: The number of observations or participants in a study. Larger samples generally produce more precise estimates and greater statistical power. Sample size should be determined a priori based on expected effect size, desired power, and alpha level.

**Sampling Frame**: The list or other device that defines the population from which a sample is drawn. The gap between the sampling frame and the true population is a source of coverage error.

**Standard Deviation (SD)**: A measure of the dispersion of data points around the mean. Approximately 68% of data falls within 1 SD of the mean, 95% within 2 SDs (for normally distributed data).

**Statistical Significance**: The likelihood that an observed result is not due to chance. Conventionally determined by a p-value below 0.05. Statistical significance does not imply practical significance or importance.

**Triangulation**: Using multiple methods, data sources, analysts, or theories to study the same phenomenon. Convergent findings across approaches increase confidence in conclusions.

**Type I Error (False Positive)**: Rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true. Concluding an effect exists when it does not. The alpha level (typically 0.05) sets the acceptable probability of Type I error.

**Type II Error (False Negative)**: Failing to reject the null hypothesis when it is actually false. Missing a real effect. The beta level (1 - power) determines the probability of Type II error.

**Validity**: The degree to which a measure, study, or conclusion is sound. Multiple types: construct, internal, external, face, content, criterion, ecological.

## User Research

**Affinity Mapping (Affinity Diagramming)**: A synthesis technique where observations, quotes, and data points are written on individual notes and grouped into clusters by thematic similarity. Bottom-up grouping reveals patterns. Originated in KJ Method (Jiro Kawakita).

**Card Sorting**: A technique for understanding how users categorize information. Open card sorts let users create categories; closed card sorts use predefined categories. Informs information architecture.

**Concept Testing**: Evaluating user response to a product concept before it is built. Uses descriptions, sketches, or low-fidelity prototypes to gauge interest, comprehension, and perceived value.

**Contextual Inquiry**: A semi-structured interview method conducted in the user's natural environment while they perform tasks. Based on four principles: context, partnership, interpretation, and focus. Developed by Beyer and Holtzblatt (1998).

**Diary Study**: A longitudinal research method where participants self-report their behaviors, experiences, and thoughts over time. Captures in-context data that interviews may miss. Typical duration: 1-4 weeks.

**Ethnography**: Immersive, observational research conducted in participants' natural settings over extended periods. Aims to understand culture, practices, and meaning from the insider perspective.

**Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)**: A framework that defines customer needs in terms of the "job" they are trying to accomplish. Originated with Christensen, Ulwick, and Moesta. A "job" is functional, emotional, and social. "People don't want a quarter-inch drill; they want a quarter-inch hole."

**Mental Model**: A user's internal representation of how a system works. Research uncovers mental models to design systems that match user expectations. Mismatches between mental models and system models cause usability problems.

**Persona**: A fictional but evidence-based representation of a key user segment. Effective personas are grounded in research data, not assumptions. Include goals, behaviors, pain points, and context. Originated with Alan Cooper (1999).

**Probe (Interview Probe)**: A follow-up question or prompt used to elicit deeper information. Types: clarification probes ("What do you mean by that?"), elaboration probes ("Tell me more"), and contrast probes ("How does that compare to X?").

**Think-Aloud Protocol**: A usability testing technique where participants verbalize their thoughts while performing tasks. Concurrent think-aloud (during) or retrospective (after). Reveals cognitive processes and decision points.

**Tree Testing**: A usability technique for evaluating the findability of topics in a website's information architecture. Participants are given tasks and navigate a text-only hierarchy to find the correct location.

**Usability Testing**: Evaluating a product by testing it with representative users. Participants attempt tasks while observers note difficulties and errors. Can be moderated or unmoderated, in-person or remote. 5 participants detect approximately 80% of usability problems (Nielsen, 2000).

## Market Research

**Battlecard**: A competitive intelligence document that provides sales teams with key differentiators, objection handlers, and talking points for competing against a specific competitor.

**Bottom-up Market Sizing**: Estimating market size by building from unit-level data (number of potential customers x average revenue per customer). More precise but more data-intensive than top-down.

**CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate)**: The mean annual growth rate over a specified time period longer than one year. CAGR = (Ending Value / Beginning Value)^(1/n) - 1.

**Market Segmentation**: Dividing a market into distinct subgroups of customers who have different needs, characteristics, or behaviors. Segments should be measurable, accessible, substantial, differentiable, and actionable.

**Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: A customer loyalty metric based on the question "How likely are you to recommend [product] to a friend or colleague?" on a 0-10 scale. Score = % Promoters (9-10) minus % Detractors (0-6).

**PESTEL Analysis**: A framework for analyzing macro-environmental factors: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal. Used to identify external forces that affect an industry or market.

**Porter's Five Forces**: A framework for analyzing industry competitive intensity: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitutes, and rivalry among existing competitors. Developed by Michael Porter (1979).

**SAM (Serviceable Available Market)**: The portion of TAM that a company can reach with its specific business model, channels, and geographic focus.

**SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)**: The portion of SAM that a company can realistically capture given its current resources, competition, and go-to-market strategy.

**SWOT Analysis**: A framework analyzing internal Strengths and Weaknesses and external Opportunities and Threats. Most effective when findings are evidence-based rather than speculative.

**TAM (Total Addressable Market)**: The total revenue opportunity available if a product achieved 100% market share. Represents the maximum possible demand.

**Top-down Market Sizing**: Estimating market size by starting with broad industry data and narrowing to the relevant segment. Faster but less precise than bottom-up.

**Win/Loss Analysis**: Systematic post-decision research with prospects who chose or rejected a product. Reveals competitive strengths, weaknesses, and decision criteria through structured interviews.

## Survey Methodology

**Acquiescence Bias (Yea-saying)**: The tendency for respondents to agree with statements regardless of content. Mitigated by mixing positively and negatively worded items.

**Cognitive Pretesting**: Testing survey questions with a small sample to identify comprehension problems, ambiguity, and response difficulties before full deployment. Methods: think-aloud, probing, paraphrasing.

**Coverage Error**: The gap between the target population and the sampling frame. If the frame excludes portions of the population, the sample cannot represent them.

**Dillman's Tailored Design Method (TDM)**: A comprehensive approach to survey design that applies social exchange theory to maximize response rates and minimize error. Emphasizes respondent trust, rewards, and cost reduction. Reference: Dillman, Smyth, and Christian (2014).

**Item Non-response**: When respondents skip individual questions. Can introduce bias if non-response is systematic (e.g., high-income respondents skip income questions).

**Measurement Error**: The difference between a measured value and the true value. Sources include question wording, response scales, mode effects, and respondent cognitive limitations.

**Non-response Bias**: Systematic difference between respondents and non-respondents. If those who did not respond differ from those who did, results may not represent the target population.

**Response Rate**: The percentage of the sample that completes the survey. Low response rates increase the risk of non-response bias. Response rate = completed surveys / eligible sample.

**Sampling Error**: The natural variation between a sample statistic and the true population parameter. Decreases with larger sample sizes. Quantified by the margin of error.

**Scale Anchoring**: Providing labels or reference points on response scales to ensure consistent interpretation across respondents.

**Skip Logic (Branching)**: Survey logic that directs respondents to different questions based on their previous answers. Reduces respondent burden and improves data quality.

**Total Survey Error (TSE)**: The framework encompassing all sources of error in a survey: coverage error, sampling error, non-response error, measurement error. Minimizing TSE is more important than maximizing any single quality dimension.

## Qualitative Research

**Axial Coding**: The second stage of grounded theory coding, where categories identified in open coding are related to each other. Identifies relationships between categories and subcategories.

**Constant Comparison**: A grounded theory technique where each new piece of data is compared with previously coded data. Ensures emerging codes and categories are grounded in the data.

**Grounded Theory**: A systematic methodology for developing theory that is "grounded" in data. Involves iterative data collection and analysis through open, axial, and selective coding. Originated with Glaser and Strauss (1967).

**In Vivo Coding**: Using participants' own words as code labels. Preserves the participant's voice and ensures codes are grounded in lived experience.

**Member Checking**: Sharing findings or interpretations with participants to verify accuracy. A technique for establishing credibility in qualitative research.

**Open Coding**: The first stage of grounded theory coding. Breaking down data into discrete concepts and labeling them. Generates initial codes from the data without imposing preexisting categories.

**Saturation (Theoretical Saturation)**: The point in data collection when new data no longer reveals new themes or categories. Indicates sufficient data has been collected. Typically reached at 12-20 interviews for relatively homogeneous populations.

**Selective Coding**: The third stage of grounded theory coding. Identifying the core category that integrates all other categories into a unified theoretical framework.

**Thematic Analysis**: A method for identifying, analyzing, and reporting patterns (themes) within data. Six phases: familiarization, initial coding, searching for themes, reviewing themes, defining themes, writing up. Framework by Braun and Clarke (2006).

**Thick Description**: Detailed, contextualized description that captures not just behaviors but their context and meaning. Concept from Clifford Geertz (1973). Essential for transferability in qualitative research.
