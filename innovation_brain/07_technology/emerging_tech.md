# Emerging Technology -- Evaluation and Strategic Assessment

## Overview

Emerging technologies represent the raw material of transformational innovation. The
challenge is not identifying emerging technologies -- there is no shortage of hype.
The challenge is evaluating which technologies are real, which are relevant to your
strategy, and when to invest. This module provides rigorous frameworks for technology
evaluation, including hype cycle positioning, technology readiness levels, and proof
of concept design. It also surveys the current frontier technologies with sober
assessments of where they actually matter.

---

## Technology Evaluation Framework

### The Three Questions

Before investing in any emerging technology, answer three questions:

```
1. IS IT REAL?
   - Does the technology actually work outside the lab?
   - What is the Technology Readiness Level (TRL)?
   - Are there credible demonstrations, not just papers?
   - What are the known limitations and failure modes?

2. IS IT RELEVANT?
   - Does it solve a problem our customers have?
   - Does it align with our innovation thesis?
   - Does it create competitive advantage we can sustain?
   - Is the timing right (not too early, not too late)?

3. IS IT READY?
   - Can we integrate it with our existing systems?
   - Do we have (or can we acquire) the talent to use it?
   - Are the supporting ecosystem and infrastructure mature?
   - What is the total cost of adoption (not just the technology cost)?
```

## Hype Cycle Positioning (Gartner)

### Using the Hype Cycle as a Strategic Tool

```
Expectations
    ^
    |        Peak of
    |       Inflated
    |      Expectations
    |        /\
    |       /  \      KEY: Where a technology sits on the hype cycle
    |      /    \     determines the STRATEGY, not just the timing.
    |     /      \
    |    /        \    Slope of         Plateau of
    |   /          \  Enlightenment    Productivity
    |  /            \    /            ___________
    | / Innovation   \ /            /
    |/ Trigger        V            /
    |              Trough of
    |             Disillusionment
    +---------------------------------------------> Time
```

### Strategic Response by Hype Cycle Phase

| Phase | What Is Happening | Strategic Response | Risk Profile |
|-------|------------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Innovation Trigger | Technology demonstrated; no products | Research & monitor; academic partnerships | Very low investment, very high uncertainty |
| Peak of Inflated Expectations | Media hype; vendor proliferation; early VC | Be skeptical; validate with POC; avoid overspending | High capital risk from overinvestment |
| Trough of Disillusionment | Failures reported; interest wanes; shakeout | Best time to invest if fundamentals are sound | Contrarian opportunity; requires conviction |
| Slope of Enlightenment | Real use cases emerge; 2nd/3rd gen products | Build and deploy; product-market fit clearer | Competition increasing; time to act |
| Plateau of Productivity | Mainstream adoption; clear ROI | Optimize and scale; differentiate on execution | Commoditization risk; differentiation narrowing |

### Hype Cycle Evaluation Checklist

```

  - Number of credible production deployments: ____
```

## Technology Readiness Levels (TRL 1-9)

### The TRL Scale

Originally developed by NASA, TRLs provide an objective measure of technology maturity:

```
TRL 9: Actual system proven in operational environment (full deployment)
TRL 8: System complete and qualified (production-ready)
TRL 7: System prototype demonstration in operational environment
TRL 6: System/subsystem model or prototype demonstrated in relevant environment
TRL 5: Component validation in relevant environment
TRL 4: Component validation in laboratory environment
TRL 3: Analytical and experimental proof of concept
TRL 2: Technology concept formulated
TRL 1: Basic principles observed and reported

RESEARCH -------- DEVELOPMENT -------- DEPLOYMENT
(TRL 1-3)         (TRL 4-6)           (TRL 7-9)
```

### TRL Assessment Framework

| TRL | Evidence Required | Innovation Brain Action | Investment Level |
|-----|-------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| 1-2 | Published research, theoretical basis | Monitor through academic partnerships | $0 (scouting only) |
| 3 | Lab demonstration of key principle | Small exploratory research project | $10-50K |
| 4 | Component works in lab conditions | Internal POC with dedicated team | $50-200K |
| 5 | Component works in realistic conditions | Pilot planning; cross-functional team | $200K-500K |
| 6 | Prototype demonstrated in relevant environment | Venture investment; dedicated team | $500K-2M |
| 7 | System prototype in operational environment | Scale planning; production readiness | $2-10M |
| 8-9 | Production system qualified and operational | Full deployment | $10M+ |

## Proof of Concept (POC) Design

### POC Design Methodology

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
| PROOF OF CONCEPT DESIGN                                           |
|                                                                  |
| Technology: ________________________________________________     |
| TRL: ____   Target TRL after POC: ____                          |
|                                                                  |
| Technical hypothesis:                                            |
| "We believe [technology] can [specific capability]               |
|  at [performance level] within [constraints]."                   |
|                                                                  |
| Scope:                                                           |
|   IN scope: _________________________________________________   |
|   OUT of scope: _____________________________________________   |
|                                                                  |
| Success criteria:                                                |
|   Performance: ____________  Threshold: ______                   |
|   Accuracy: ______________  Threshold: ______                    |
|   Latency: ______________  Threshold: ______                     |
|   Cost: _________________  Threshold: ______                     |
|                                                                  |
| Team: _______________________________________________________    |
| Duration: _____ weeks   Budget: $______                          |
|                                                                  |
| If successful: _________________________________________________ |
| If unsuccessful: _______________________________________________ |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
```

### POC Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Correction |
|-------------|-------------|-----------|
| POC becomes product | Scope creep; never-ending POC | Time-box strictly (2-4 weeks) |
| Too many variables | Cannot determine what worked | Test one variable at a time |
| No success criteria | Cannot objectively evaluate | Define thresholds BEFORE starting |
| Happy path only | Does not test real-world conditions | Include edge cases and failure modes |
| No comparison baseline | Cannot quantify improvement | Measure current state first |
| Vendor-run POC | Vendor optimizes for their demo, not your reality | Run internally with your data |

## Pilot Design for Emerging Technology

### From POC to Pilot

```
POC (Can it work?)
  |
  +--> NO --> Stop or pivot technology approach
  |
  +--> YES --> PILOT (Can it work in our environment?)
                |
                +--> Define pilot scope (single business unit, limited users)
                +--> Define duration (60-90 days typically)
                +--> Define success metrics (business outcomes, not just tech metrics)
                +--> Assign pilot owner and team
                +--> Plan data collection and analysis
                +--> Define Go/No-Go criteria
                |
                +--> NO --> Stop, iterate, or expand POC
                |
                +--> YES --> SCALE (Can we deploy broadly?)
```

## Current Frontier Technologies

### AI / Large Language Models

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| TRL | 7-8 (production-ready for many use cases) |
| Hype cycle | Slope of Enlightenment / early Plateau |
| Where it actually matters | Content generation, code assistance, customer support, data analysis, search, summarization |
| Where it is overhyped | AGI timelines, replacing all knowledge workers, autonomous decision-making |
| Strategic recommendation | INVEST NOW. Competitive advantage accrues to early deployers. Focus on augmentation, not replacement. |
| Key risks | Hallucination, bias, data privacy, regulatory uncertainty, cost at scale |

### Spatial Computing (AR/VR/XR)

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| TRL | 6-7 (hardware improving, software ecosystem developing) |
| Hype cycle | Trough of Disillusionment (post-Meta pivot) |
| Where it actually matters | Training/simulation, industrial design, remote collaboration, gaming |
| Where it is overhyped | Metaverse replacing physical interaction, mass consumer adoption |
| Strategic recommendation | MONITOR and PILOT for specific industrial/training use cases. Mass consumer adoption is 5+ years out. |
| Key risks | Hardware cost, user comfort, content creation cost, limited developer ecosystem |

### Quantum Computing

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| TRL | 3-5 (depends on application; most are far from production) |
| Hype cycle | Peak of Inflated Expectations |
| Where it actually matters | Cryptography, drug discovery, materials science, optimization problems, financial modeling |
| Where it is overhyped | General-purpose computing replacement, near-term enterprise applications |
| Strategic recommendation | MONITOR through academic partnerships. No production investment for most companies. Prepare for quantum-safe cryptography. |
| Key risks | Decoherence, error rates, extreme cooling requirements, very limited real-world applications today |

### Biotech / Synthetic Biology

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| TRL | 4-7 (varies widely by application) |
| Hype cycle | Slope of Enlightenment (for specific applications) |
| Where it actually matters | Drug development, agriculture, materials, food production, diagnostics |
| Where it is overhyped | General programmability of biology, timeline for complex applications |
| Strategic recommendation | INVEST if in relevant industries (pharma, agriculture, materials). Monitor otherwise. |

### Robotics and Autonomous Systems

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| TRL | 5-8 (mature for manufacturing; developing for general purpose) |
| Hype cycle | Slope of Enlightenment (industrial); Peak (humanoid/general) |
| Where it actually matters | Manufacturing, logistics, agriculture, hazardous environments, healthcare |
| Where it is overhyped | General-purpose humanoid robots, full self-driving (L5) |
| Strategic recommendation | INVEST for specific industrial applications. PILOT for logistics. MONITOR for general-purpose. |

### Brain-Computer Interfaces

| Dimension | Assessment |
|-----------|-----------|
| TRL | 3-5 (medical applications advancing; consumer far behind) |
| Hype cycle | Innovation Trigger to Peak |
| Where it actually matters | Medical applications (paralysis, epilepsy, neurological disorders) |
| Where it is overhyped | Consumer applications, "thought control" of devices, timeline to mass adoption |
| Strategic recommendation | MONITOR only. Research partnerships if in healthcare. Not relevant for most companies for 10+ years. |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Hype is not signal**: Most hyped technologies fail or take 10+ years to mature.
2. **TRL provides objective assessment**: Use it to cut through vendor and media noise.
3. **POC must be time-boxed and criteria-driven**: Or it becomes an endless science project.
4. **The trough is the best time to invest**: Contrarian timing creates competitive advantage.
5. **AI/LLM is the most actionable frontier technology today**: Most companies should invest now.
6. **Technology evaluation must be continuous**: What was irrelevant last year may be critical this year.
