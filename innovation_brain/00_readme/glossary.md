# Glossary --- Innovation Brain

## Canonical Innovation Terminology

This glossary defines terms as used within the Innovation Brain. All modules
reference these definitions. When terminology conflicts arise between sources,
the definitions below are authoritative within this brain.

---

## A

**Absorptive Capacity**
An organization's ability to recognize, assimilate, and apply new external
knowledge (Cohen & Levinthal, 1990). Determines how effectively an organization
can leverage technology scouting, open innovation, and academic partnerships.
Measured by R&D investment intensity, knowledge diversity, and prior related
knowledge.

**Ambidextrous Organization**
An organizational structure that simultaneously pursues exploitation of existing
competencies and exploration of new opportunities (O'Reilly & Tushman, 2004).
Achieved through structural separation (dedicated innovation units), contextual
ambidexterity (individuals switching between modes), or sequential ambidexterity
(alternating organizational focus).

**Assumption Mapping**
A technique for identifying and prioritizing the assumptions underlying an
innovation hypothesis (Bland & Osterwalder, 2019). Assumptions are mapped on a
2x2 matrix: importance (how critical to success) vs. evidence (how validated).
The riskiest assumptions --- high importance, low evidence --- are tested first.

---

## B

**Build-Measure-Learn Loop**
The fundamental feedback loop of the Lean Startup (Ries, 2011). Begins with
building a Minimum Viable Product, measuring its impact with actionable metrics,
and learning whether to pivot or persevere. The goal is to minimize total time
through the loop while maximizing validated learning per cycle.

**Business Model Canvas (BMC)**
A strategic management template for documenting existing or developing new
business models (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010). Nine building blocks: Customer
Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams,
Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships, Cost Structure.

---

## C

**Concierge MVP**
A minimum viable product where the founders manually deliver the value
proposition to customers rather than building automated technology (Ries, 2011).
Used to validate demand and understand customer needs before investing in
technology development. Contrasted with Wizard of Oz MVP where the customer
believes the technology exists.

**Corporate Venture Capital (CVC)**
Strategic investment by established corporations in external startups to gain
access to innovation, technology, or market intelligence (Chesbrough, 2002).
Distinguished from financial VC by strategic objectives: window on technology,
demand stimulation, ecosystem building.

**Creative Destruction**
Joseph Schumpeter's concept (1942) describing the process by which innovation
renders existing products, processes, and business models obsolete. The
entrepreneur is the agent of creative destruction, creating new economic
structures while destroying old ones. Fundamental to understanding why
established firms resist radical innovation.

**Crossing the Chasm**
Geoffrey Moore's framework (1991) describing the gap between early adopters and
the early majority in technology adoption. The chasm exists because early
adopters value novelty and disruption while the early majority values
reliability, references, and whole product solutions. Most technology ventures
fail in the chasm.

---

## D

**Design Thinking**
A human-centered innovation methodology developed at IDEO and Stanford's d.school
(Brown, 2009; Kelley & Kelley, 2013). Five phases: Empathize (understand users),
Define (frame the problem), Ideate (generate solutions), Prototype (make ideas
tangible), Test (validate with users). Emphasizes iteration, empathy, and
bias-toward-action.

**Discovery-Driven Planning**
A planning approach for highly uncertain ventures (McGrath & MacMillan, 2009).
Instead of projecting forward from assumptions, works backward from required
outcomes to identify the assumptions that must prove true. Converts assumptions
into checkpoints that trigger invest/pivot/kill decisions.

**Disruptive Innovation**
A process by which smaller companies with fewer resources successfully challenge
established businesses (Christensen, 1997). Disruption occurs from below (low-end
disruption targeting overserved customers) or from the side (new-market disruption
creating entirely new consumption). Distinguished from sustaining innovation which
improves existing products along established performance dimensions.

---

## E

**Effectuation**
An entrepreneurial decision-making logic described by Sarasvathy (2001).
Instead of starting with a goal and finding means (causation), effectuation
starts with available means and discovers possible goals. Principles: bird-in-hand
(start with what you have), affordable loss (limit downside), crazy quilt
(co-create with stakeholders), lemonade (leverage surprises), pilot-in-the-plane
(focus on controllable aspects).

**Experiment**
In innovation context: a structured test designed to validate or invalidate a
specific assumption (Thomke, 2003). Distinct from exploration or research.
An experiment has a hypothesis, measurable outcome, success/failure criteria
defined in advance, and a bounded time frame. Types: A/B test, concierge,
Wizard of Oz, smoke test, landing page, crowdfunding, pre-sale.

---

## F

**First Principles Thinking**
A reasoning approach that breaks down complex problems to their most fundamental
truths and reasons up from there (attributed to Aristotle; popularized in
innovation by Elon Musk). Contrasted with reasoning by analogy. Useful for
identifying non-obvious innovation opportunities by questioning inherited
assumptions.

**Fidelity Spectrum**
The range of prototype resolution from low-fidelity (paper sketches, verbal
descriptions) to high-fidelity (functional software, physical prototypes).
The appropriate fidelity level depends on what assumption is being tested. Lower
fidelity is faster and cheaper; higher fidelity yields more realistic user
behavior data.

---

## G-H

**Gartner Hype Cycle**
A graphical representation of technology maturity (Fenn & Raskino, 2008). Five
phases: Innovation Trigger, Peak of Inflated Expectations, Trough of
Disillusionment, Slope of Enlightenment, Plateau of Productivity. Used in
technology scouting to calibrate timing of technology adoption investments.

**Horizon Framework (Three Horizons)**
McKinsey's framework (Baghai, Coley & White, 1999) for managing innovation
portfolios across time horizons. Horizon 1: defend and extend current business
(0-12 months). Horizon 2: build emerging businesses (12-36 months). Horizon 3:
create viable options for future businesses (36-72 months). Balanced portfolio
allocates resources across all three.

---

## I

**Incremental Innovation**
Improvements to existing products, services, or processes that enhance
performance along dimensions valued by current customers. Low risk, low reward.
Constitutes the majority of most organizations' innovation activity. Necessary
but insufficient for long-term competitive advantage.

**Innovation Accounting**
A framework for measuring progress in innovation when traditional financial
metrics are misleading (Ries, 2011; Viki, 2017). Metrics include: number of
experiments run, assumptions validated/invalidated, learning velocity, time to
validated learning, customer discovery conversations completed, pivots per
dollar spent.

**Innovation Portfolio**
The collection of all innovation initiatives across an organization, managed as
a portfolio analogous to a financial investment portfolio. Balances risk/reward
across horizons, innovation types, and time-to-impact. Prevents over-concentration
in any single category.

**Innovation Theater**
Innovation activities that create the appearance of innovation without generating
actual value (Viki, 2017). Examples: hackathons with no follow-through,
innovation labs with no budget authority, ideation sessions with no validation
pipeline. The Innovation Brain actively detects and prevents innovation theater.

**Intrapreneurship**
Entrepreneurial behavior within an established organization (Pinchot, 1985).
Intrapreneurs drive innovation using corporate resources while operating with
startup-like autonomy. Requires executive sponsorship, ring-fenced resources,
and protection from corporate immune response.

---

## J-K

**Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD)**
A framework for understanding customer motivation (Christensen et al., 2016;
Ulwick, 2005). Customers "hire" products to do "jobs" in their lives. Innovation
opportunities exist where jobs are important but poorly served. Three dimensions:
functional jobs (practical tasks), emotional jobs (feelings sought), social jobs
(how others perceive).

**Kill Criteria**
Pre-defined conditions that trigger termination of an innovation initiative.
Established before work begins to prevent sunk-cost escalation. Examples:
"If we cannot achieve X% conversion in Y weeks with Z budget, we kill this
initiative." Must be specific, measurable, and agreed upon by stakeholders.

---

## L

**Lateral Thinking**
A creative problem-solving approach developed by Edward de Bono (1970). Instead
of logical, sequential reasoning (vertical thinking), lateral thinking seeks
solutions through indirect, creative approaches: provocation, random entry,
challenge of assumptions. Complementary to analytical methods.

**Lean Canvas**
A one-page business plan adapted from the Business Model Canvas for
early-stage ventures (Maurya, 2012). Nine blocks: Problem, Customer Segments,
Unique Value Proposition, Solution, Channels, Revenue Streams, Cost Structure,
Key Metrics, Unfair Advantage. Designed for iteration, not permanence.

**Lean Startup**
A methodology for developing businesses and products through validated learning,
scientific experimentation, and iterative product releases (Ries, 2011). Core
principles: entrepreneurs are everywhere, entrepreneurship is management,
validated learning, build-measure-learn, innovation accounting.

**Learning Velocity**
The rate at which an organization converts uncertainty into knowledge through
experimentation. Measured as validated learnings per unit time or per unit cost.
A leading indicator of innovation success. Organizations with higher learning
velocity reach product-market fit faster and waste fewer resources on invalidated
assumptions.

---

## M-N

**Minimum Viable Product (MVP)**
The version of a new product that allows a team to collect the maximum amount of
validated learning about customers with the least effort (Ries, 2011). Not a
minimal product --- it is the minimum experiment needed to test a critical
hypothesis. Types: landing page, concierge, Wizard of Oz, single-feature,
crowdfunding campaign.

**Minimum Viable Concept (MVC)**
A pre-MVP artifact used to test desirability before building anything. Can be a
storyboard, concept video, mockup, or verbal pitch. Tests whether the value
proposition resonates before investing in any product development.

---

## O

**Open Innovation**
A paradigm that assumes organizations can and should use external ideas as well
as internal ideas to advance their innovation (Chesbrough, 2003). Models:
outside-in (sourcing external innovation), inside-out (licensing internal
innovations to others), coupled (co-creation with partners). Contrasted with
closed innovation where all R&D is internal.

---

## P

**Pivot**
A structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis
about the product, strategy, or engine of growth (Ries, 2011). Not a failure
--- a pivot is a learning-driven adaptation. Types: zoom-in, zoom-out, customer
segment, customer need, platform, business architecture, value capture, engine
of growth, channel, technology.

**Psychological Safety**
A shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking (Edmondson,
1999). In innovation contexts, psychological safety enables experimentation by
reducing fear of failure, encouraging dissent, and supporting learning from
mistakes. Without it, innovation programs generate conformity, not creativity.

---

## R

**Radical Innovation**
Innovation that introduces products or services with substantially different
performance attributes compared to existing offerings, often creating new
markets or fundamentally altering existing ones. High risk, high reward.
Contrasted with incremental innovation. Examples: smartphone (vs. feature
phone), streaming video (vs. DVD rental).

**Riskiest Assumption Test (RAT)**
An experiment specifically designed to test the assumption most likely to
invalidate the entire innovation hypothesis (Bland, 2019). Tests are ordered
by risk: the assumption that, if wrong, would make everything else irrelevant.
Prevents teams from testing easy assumptions while ignoring existential ones.

---

## S

**SCAMPER**
An ideation technique using seven prompts to transform existing concepts
(Eberle, 1971): Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify/Magnify, Put to other uses,
Eliminate, Reverse/Rearrange. Systematic approach to generating variations on
existing products, services, or processes.

**Stage-Gate Process**
A structured product development methodology where projects pass through a
series of stages separated by decision gates (Cooper, 1990). At each gate,
a cross-functional team evaluates whether the project should proceed, be
recycled, or be killed based on predefined criteria and deliverables.

**Sustaining Innovation**
Innovation that improves the performance of established products along the
dimensions of performance that mainstream customers historically value
(Christensen, 1997). Can be incremental or radical in nature. The key
distinction from disruptive innovation is that sustaining innovation targets
existing customers and value networks.

---

## T

**Technology Readiness Level (TRL)**
A measurement system originally developed by NASA to assess technology maturity
from basic research (TRL 1) to proven operational system (TRL 9). Used in
technology scouting to evaluate how close an emerging technology is to
commercial deployment. Scale: 1-3 (research), 4-6 (development), 7-9
(deployment).

**TRIZ**
A Russian-originated systematic methodology for innovation and invention
(Altshuller, 1946-1985). Based on analysis of 400,000+ patents to identify
universal principles of invention. 40 inventive principles, contradiction
matrix, substance-field analysis. Moves invention from random creativity to
systematic problem-solving.

---

## V-W

**Validated Learning**
The process of demonstrating empirically that a team has discovered valuable
truths about a startup's present and future business prospects (Ries, 2011).
Distinct from anecdotal learning or confirmation bias. Requires measurable
experiments with pre-defined success criteria.

**Value Proposition Canvas**
A tool for understanding the fit between customer needs and product offerings
(Osterwalder et al., 2014). Customer side: jobs, pains, gains. Product side:
products/services, pain relievers, gain creators. Fit exists when products
address important jobs, relieve significant pains, and create meaningful gains.

**Weak Signal**
An early indicator of a potentially important future change that is currently
ambiguous and difficult to interpret (Ansoff, 1975). Technology scouting and
futures thinking depend on detecting and interpreting weak signals before they
become obvious trends. Examples: niche academic papers, edge-case user behavior,
regulatory discussions.

**Wizard of Oz MVP**
A type of minimum viable product where the customer-facing product appears fully
functional but is actually operated manually behind the scenes (Ries, 2011).
Named after the Wizard of Oz who projected an illusion of power from behind a
curtain. Tests demand and user experience before building technology. Distinct
from concierge MVP where the customer knows they are receiving manual service.

## Innovation Type Matrix (Quick Reference)

| Type | Risk | Reward | Market | Technology |
|------|------|--------|--------|------------|
| Incremental | Low | Low | Existing | Existing |
| Sustaining | Medium | Medium | Existing | New or improved |
| Disruptive (low-end) | High | High | Overserved in existing | Simpler/cheaper |
| Disruptive (new-market) | High | High | Non-consumers | Enables new use |
| Radical | Very High | Very High | New | New |
