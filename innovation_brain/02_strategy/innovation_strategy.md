# Innovation Strategy -- Design and Execution

## Overview

Innovation strategy is the deliberate allocation of resources, capabilities, and
attention toward the creation of new value. Without strategy, innovation becomes
theater -- activity without direction, experimentation without learning, investment
without return. A coherent innovation strategy aligns innovation efforts with
corporate strategy, manages the innovation portfolio across horizons, and creates
the governance structures that enable disciplined innovation.

This module establishes how to design, resource, govern, and measure innovation strategy.

---

## Alignment with Corporate Strategy

### The Strategy-Innovation Cascade

```
Corporate Strategy
  "Where do we compete and how do we win?"
       |
       v
Business Unit Strategy
  "What is our competitive advantage in this market?"
       |
       v
Innovation Strategy
  "What new value must we create to sustain/extend competitive advantage?"
       |
       v
Innovation Portfolio
  "What specific bets are we making across H1/H2/H3?"
       |
       v
Innovation Execution
  "How do we run experiments, build ventures, launch products?"
```

### Strategic Innovation Questions

Every innovation strategy must answer five questions:

1. **How much** will innovation contribute to our growth targets?
2. **Where** will we focus innovation efforts (markets, technologies, segments)?
3. **What type** of innovation will we pursue (incremental, adjacent, transformational)?
4. **How** will we source innovation (internal R&D, partnerships, acquisition)?
5. **How** will we organize for innovation (ambidexterity, dedicated units, distributed)?

## The Innovation Thesis

### Definition

An innovation thesis is a concise statement of belief about where the world is going
and how the company will create value in that future. It serves as a strategic filter
for all innovation investments.

### Innovation Thesis Structure

```
We believe that [fundamental shift in the world]
will create [specific opportunity or threat]
for [target customer/market segment].
We will [specific innovation approach]
to capture [specific value].
Our unfair advantage is [what we uniquely bring].
We will know this is working when [measurable indicator].
```

### Examples of Innovation Theses

| Company | Innovation Thesis (Simplified) |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Tesla | Electrification + software will disrupt automotive. First-mover in premium EV. |
| Amazon | Every purchase will happen online. Invest in logistics and selection infinity. |
| Stripe | Every company will need payments infrastructure. Make it developer-simple. |
| Netflix | Entertainment will shift to streaming. Own content and distribution. |

### Using the Innovation Thesis

- All innovation proposals must connect to the thesis
- The thesis is reviewed annually and updated when fundamental assumptions change
- The thesis provides a "north star" that prevents innovation drift
- Teams can independently make decisions that align with the thesis

### Portfolio Balance Assessment

| Indicator | Healthy Portfolio | Unhealthy Portfolio |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| H1:H2:H3 ratio | ~70:20:10 (varies by industry) | 100:0:0 or 0:0:100 |
| Revenue from new products | 20-30% from products < 3 yrs old | < 10% or declining |
| Kill rate | Actively killing underperformers | Keeping everything alive |
| Graduation rate | H3 --> H2 --> H1 pipeline flowing | No ideas graduating |
| Time horizon diversity | Short, medium, and long-term bets | All same time horizon |

## Resource Allocation for Innovation

### Dedicated vs. Shared Resources

| Model | Description | Pros | Cons |
|-------|-------------|------|------|
| Dedicated innovation unit | Separate team, budget, space | Focus, speed, culture | Isolation, resentment, scaling |
| Distributed innovation | Innovation embedded in all teams | Integration, scale | Dilution, conflict with BAU |
| Hybrid | Central innovation team + distributed | Balance of focus and scale | Complexity, coordination cost |
| Innovation time (20% time) | Fraction of everyone's time | Broad participation | Low accountability, fragmented |

### Innovation Budget Framework

```
Total Innovation Budget
  |
  +--> Core R&D (70%)
  |     |
  |     +--> Product improvements (40%)
  |     +--> Process improvements (20%)
  |     +--> Technical debt / platform (10%)
  |
  +--> Growth Innovation (20%)
  |     |
  |     +--> Adjacent market exploration (10%)
  |     +--> New capability building (5%)
  |     +--> Partnership/acquisition scouting (5%)
  |
  +--> Transformational Innovation (10%)
        |
        +--> Moonshot projects (5%)
        +--> Venture building (3%)
        +--> External ventures / CVC (2%)
```

### Resource Allocation Traps

1. **Peanut butter spreading**: Allocating too little to too many projects
2. **Pet project protection**: Keeping alive projects that should be killed
3. **Core cannibalization fear**: Under-investing in H2/H3 to protect H1
4. **Innovation tourism**: Visiting innovation without committing resources
5. **Metric mismatch**: Applying H1 metrics to H3 investments

## Innovation Metrics

### Metrics by Innovation Stage

| Stage | Metric Category | Example Metrics |
|-------|----------------|-----------------|
| Input | Resources invested | R&D spend as % revenue, headcount, time allocation |
| Activity | Innovation activity | Ideas generated, experiments run, patents filed |
| Output | Innovation results | New products launched, revenue from new products |
| Impact | Strategic value | Market share change, new market entry, platform value |
| Culture | Innovation health | Employee innovation survey, idea participation rate |

### Leading vs. Lagging Innovation Metrics

```
LEADING INDICATORS                LAGGING INDICATORS
(Predict future innovation)       (Measure past innovation)

- # of experiments running        - Revenue from new products
- Idea pipeline volume            - Market share in new segments
- Cross-functional collaboration  - Patent portfolio value
- Speed of experimentation        - Innovation ROI
- Kill rate (disciplined pruning) - Time to market
- External partnership activity   - Customer satisfaction with new features
```

### Innovation Accounting (Ries)

For transformational innovation, traditional financial metrics fail. Innovation
accounting provides alternative progress measures:

1. **Learning velocity**: How fast are we reducing uncertainty?
2. **Assumption validation rate**: How many critical assumptions have we tested?
3. **Pivot rate**: How quickly do we change direction when evidence demands it?
4. **Customer development progress**: How far along the problem-solution-PMF journey?
5. **Cost per learning**: How efficiently do we generate validated insights?

## Build vs. Buy vs. Partner for Innovation

### Decision Framework

```
START
  |
  v
Is this innovation core to our competitive advantage?
  |
  +--> YES --> Do we have the capability to build it?
  |             |
  |             +--> YES --> BUILD internally
  |             +--> NO --> Can we acquire the capability?
  |                          |
  |                          +--> YES --> BUY (acquire company/team)
  |                          +--> NO --> PARTNER (strategic alliance)
  |
  +--> NO --> Is it available on the market?
               |
               +--> YES --> BUY (license/procure)
               +--> NO --> PARTNER or IGNORE
```

### Build vs. Buy vs. Partner Comparison

| Factor | Build | Buy (Acquire) | Partner |
|--------|-------|---------------|---------|
| Speed | Slow (12-36 months) | Fast (3-6 months) | Medium (6-12 months) |
| Cost | Variable, ongoing | High upfront, integration cost | Shared, ongoing |
| Control | Full | Full (post-integration) | Shared |
| Risk | Execution risk | Integration risk | Alignment risk |
| IP ownership | Full | Full | Shared or licensed |
| Cultural fit | Natural | Often difficult | Must be managed |
| Best when | Core competency, unique needs | Speed critical, talent scarce | Non-core, complementary strengths |

## Innovation Governance

### Governance Structure

```
+---------------------------+
|    Innovation Board       |    Quarterly: Portfolio review, resource allocation,
|    (CEO + C-suite)        |    strategic alignment, kill decisions
+---------------------------+
            |
+---------------------------+
|   Innovation Council      |    Monthly: Pipeline review, stage-gate decisions,
|   (VPs + Innovation Lead) |    cross-functional coordination
+---------------------------+
            |
+---------------------------+
|   Innovation Teams        |    Weekly: Sprint execution, experiment results,
|   (Cross-functional)      |    pivot/persevere decisions
+---------------------------+
```

### Stage-Gate Governance

| Gate | Decision | Evidence Required | Decision Makers |
|------|----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Gate 0: Idea Screen | Worth exploring? | Problem statement, initial evidence | Innovation Council |
| Gate 1: Concept Approval | Worth testing? | Customer evidence, feasibility assessment | Innovation Council |
| Gate 2: Validation Approval | Worth building? | Experiment results, business case draft | Innovation Board |
| Gate 3: Launch Approval | Worth scaling? | Product-market fit evidence, unit economics | Innovation Board |
| Gate 4: Scale Approval | Worth full investment? | Traction metrics, growth model | CEO + Board |

### Kill Criteria

Projects should be killed when:
- Core assumptions are falsified and no viable pivot exists
- Market timing has fundamentally shifted
- Resource requirements exceed available budget by > 3x
- Team cannot be staffed with appropriate talent
- Competitive landscape makes success improbable
- Strategic alignment has changed (thesis updated)

## Time Horizons for Innovation Bets

| Horizon | Time Frame | Expected Outcome | Management Approach |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| H1: Core | 0-12 months | Revenue improvement | Traditional PM, OKRs |
| H2: Adjacent | 12-36 months | New revenue stream | Lean startup, agile |
| H3: Transformational | 36-72 months | Strategic option | Venture model, options thinking |
| Beyond H3 | 5-10+ years | Intellectual foundation | Research partnerships, futures thinking |

### Options Thinking for H3 Innovation

Transformational innovation should be managed as a portfolio of real options:
- Each H3 project is an option to invest further, not a commitment
- Options have value even if never exercised (learning value)
- Invest small to buy the option; invest big only when uncertainty reduces
- Manage the portfolio, not individual projects

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Strategy before execution**: Innovation without strategy is waste.
2. **Thesis drives focus**: A clear innovation thesis filters all investments.
3. **Portfolio over projects**: Manage the innovation portfolio, not individual ideas.
4. **Metrics must match maturity**: H1 metrics on H3 projects kill innovation.
5. **Governance enables, not blocks**: Stage gates accelerate good ideas and kill bad ones.
6. **Build-buy-partner is strategic**: Source innovation based on competitive advantage logic.
