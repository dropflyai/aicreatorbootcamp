# Innovation Types -- Taxonomy and Classification

## Overview

Not all innovations are created equal. The type of innovation determines the
appropriate strategy, resource allocation, organizational structure, risk tolerance,
and success metrics. Misclassifying innovation type is one of the most common and
costly errors in innovation management -- applying incremental management to
transformational innovation (or vice versa) virtually guarantees failure.

This module establishes the complete taxonomy of innovation types used throughout
the Innovation Brain.

## The Innovation Ambition Matrix (Nagji & Tuff, 2012)

### Three Levels of Innovation Ambition

```

                      |    new markets)

                      |        new areas)
                      |          20%
                      |

                      |     (optimize existing)
                      |          70%
                      |
                      +---------------------------->
                    Existing               New to
                    Markets         Where to Play
                                    (Markets)
```

### The 70-20-10 Innovation Portfolio

| Type | Budget Allocation | Expected Return | Management Style |
|------|------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Core/Incremental | ~70% | Predictable, near-term | Traditional project management |
| Adjacent | ~20% | Moderate risk, mid-term | Lean/agile management |
| Transformational | ~10% | High risk, long-term | Venture/exploration management |

**Critical insight**: Companies that allocate 100% to core innovation eventually stagnate.
Companies that allocate 100% to transformational innovation burn cash without revenue.
The 70-20-10 ratio is a starting heuristic -- the actual ratio should reflect industry
dynamics, competitive position, and strategic intent.

### Return Profile by Type

Research by Nagji and Tuff found that the return profile inverts the allocation:
- Core innovations (70% of investment) generate ~10% of long-term revenue growth
- Adjacent innovations (20% of investment) generate ~20% of long-term revenue growth
- Transformational innovations (10% of investment) generate ~70% of long-term revenue growth

This asymmetry is why transformational innovation must be pursued despite its risk.

## The Four Types of Innovation (Henderson & Clark, 1990)

### Architectural vs. Component Innovation

```
                   Core Concepts
                   Reinforced        Overturned
                 +------------------+------------------+
    Linkages     | INCREMENTAL      | MODULAR          |
    Between      | (better          | (new core        |
    Components   |  components,     |  technology,     |
    Unchanged    |  same            |  same            |
                 |  architecture)   |  architecture)   |
                 +------------------+------------------+
    Linkages     | ARCHITECTURAL    | RADICAL          |
    Between      | (same            | (new core        |
    Components   |  components,     |  technology,     |
    Changed      |  new             |  new             |
                 |  architecture)   |  architecture)   |
                 +------------------+------------------+
```

| Innovation Type | Example | Risk Level | Organizational Challenge |
|----------------|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| Incremental | iPhone camera upgrades year over year | Low | Operational efficiency |
| Modular | Swap gas engine for electric (same car architecture) | Medium | Component expertise |
| Architectural | iPhone (recombined existing technologies) | High | Organizational structure |
| Radical | Internet, CRISPR, quantum computing | Very High | Everything changes |

**Key insight**: Architectural innovation is the most dangerous for incumbents because
it looks incremental (same components) but requires reorganizing internal relationships.
This is why established firms consistently fail at architectural innovation.

## Product Innovation

### Definition
Creating new products or significantly improving existing products that deliver
new value to customers.

### Product Innovation Spectrum

```
Incremental        Enhancement       Next-Gen          New Category
Improvement        Extension         Product           Creation
    |                  |                |                  |
    v                  v                v                  v
Bug fixes,         New features,    Complete           Product that
quality            line             redesign,          defines a
improvements,      extensions,      new platform,      new market
cost reduction     variants         tech migration     category
    |                  |                |                  |
 Low Risk           Medium Risk      High Risk         Very High Risk
 High certainty     Moderate         Low certainty     Extreme
 of outcome         certainty        of outcome        uncertainty
```

### Metrics for Product Innovation
- Revenue from new products (% of total revenue from products < 3 years old)
- Time to market (concept to launch)
- Customer adoption rate
- Product-market fit indicators (retention, NPS, usage frequency)

## Process Innovation

### Definition
New or significantly improved methods of production, delivery, or support that
reduce cost, improve quality, or increase speed.

### Process Innovation Categories

1. **Automation**: Replacing manual steps with technology
2. **Elimination**: Removing non-value-adding steps (Lean waste removal)
3. **Integration**: Combining previously separate processes
4. **Standardization**: Creating repeatable, consistent processes
5. **Digitization**: Converting physical processes to digital

### Process vs. Product Innovation Dynamics

| Dimension | Product Innovation | Process Innovation |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Visibility | External, customer-facing | Internal, often invisible |
| Timing | Dominates early in industry lifecycle | Dominates as industry matures |
| Competitive impact | Differentiation advantage | Cost/quality advantage |
| Imitation speed | Medium (reverse engineering) | Slow (embedded in organization) |
| Measurement | Revenue growth | Cost reduction, efficiency |

**Utterback-Abernathy model**: Industries progress through a lifecycle where product
innovation dominates early (fluid phase), then process innovation takes over
(transitional phase), and finally both stabilize (specific phase).

## Business Model Innovation

### Definition
Fundamental changes to how value is created, delivered, and captured. Business model
innovation changes the logic of the business, not just its products or processes.

### Business Model Canvas Elements Subject to Innovation

```
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
|                    BUSINESS MODEL CANVAS                          |
|                                                                  |
|  Key Partners | Key Activities | Value Prop  | Customer     |    |
|               |                |             | Relationships|    |
|               | Key Resources  |             | Channels     |    |
|               |                |             |              |    |
|  Cost Structure                | Revenue Streams              |  |
+------------------------------------------------------------------+
Any element can be innovated. The most powerful innovations change
multiple elements simultaneously.
```

### Business Model Innovation Patterns

| Pattern | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Freemium | Free basic + paid premium | Spotify, Slack |
| Platform/Marketplace | Connect buyers and sellers | Airbnb, Uber |
| Subscription | Recurring revenue, access model | Netflix, SaaS |
| Razor-and-blade | Cheap hardware, expensive consumables | Printers, Keurig |
| Reverse razor-and-blade | Expensive hardware, cheap/free content | Apple, gaming consoles |
| Asset-light | Own the platform, not the assets | Airbnb, Uber |
| Direct-to-consumer | Bypass intermediaries | Warby Parker, Dollar Shave Club |
| Open source | Free product, paid services | Red Hat, MongoDB |
| Pay-per-use | Usage-based pricing | AWS, Twilio |

## Experience Innovation

### Definition
Innovation in how customers interact with, perceive, and derive meaning from products
and services. Experience innovation transforms the emotional and functional journey.

### Experience Innovation Dimensions

1. **Sensory**: How it looks, sounds, feels, smells, tastes
2. **Affective**: How it makes customers feel emotionally
3. **Cognitive**: How it engages thinking and problem-solving
4. **Behavioral**: How it changes what customers do
5. **Relational**: How it connects customers to others

### Pine & Gilmore Experience Economy

```
Economic Offering Progression:

Commodities --> Goods --> Services --> Experiences --> Transformations
(extract)    (make)   (deliver)    (stage)         (guide)
(fungible)   (tangible)(intangible)(memorable)     (effectual)
(natural)    (standard)(customized)(personal)      (individual)
(lowest $)   (low $)  (medium $)  (high $)        (highest $)
```

```
|  Research --> Dev     |      |   Ideas flow IN      |
|    |                  |      |    |   |   |   |     |

```

### Open Innovation Modes

| Mode | Direction | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| Outside-in | External --> Internal | Technology scouting, licensing-in, acquisition, crowdsourcing |
| Inside-out | Internal --> External | Licensing-out, spin-offs, open-sourcing |
| Coupled | Bidirectional | Joint ventures, partnerships, ecosystems |

## Frugal Innovation (Radjou & Prabhu, 2015)

Innovation under severe resource constraints. Creating more value with fewer resources.

### Principles of Frugal Innovation
1. **Seek opportunity in adversity**: Constraints breed creativity
2. **Do more with less**: Hyper-efficient resource use
3. **Think and act flexibly**: Agile, adaptive, rapid iteration
4. **Keep it simple**: Reduce complexity, increase accessibility
5. **Include the margin**: Design for underserved populations
6. **Follow your heart**: Purpose-driven innovation

### Examples
- Aravind Eye Care: $30 cataract surgery (vs. $3,000 in the US)
- M-Pesa: Mobile banking without smartphones
- Tata Nano: $2,000 car (attempting to serve new market)

---

## Reverse Innovation (Govindarajan & Trimble, 2012)

Innovation that originates in emerging markets and flows to developed markets --
the reverse of traditional diffusion patterns.

### Mechanism
1. Innovate for resource-constrained emerging market
2. Develop affordable, accessible, simple solution
3. Discover that the innovation also serves unmet needs in developed markets
4. Scale globally from the bottom up

### Example
GE Healthcare portable ultrasound: developed for rural China ($15K vs. $100K),
later adopted by US emergency rooms and ambulances.

## Platform Innovation

### Definition
Creating infrastructure upon which other innovations can be built. Platforms
amplify innovation by enabling ecosystem participants to create value.

### Platform Types

| Platform Type | Value Creation | Example |
|--------------|---------------|---------|
| Transaction platform | Connects buyers/sellers | App Store, Airbnb |
| Innovation platform | Enables third-party development | AWS, Android |
| Integrated platform | Both transaction and innovation | Apple iOS ecosystem |
| Investment platform | Holds companies, active strategy | Berkshire, Alphabet |

### Platform vs. Pipeline

```
PIPELINE (linear)            PLATFORM (networked)
Supplier --> Firm --> Customer    Producer <--> Platform <--> Consumer
Value flows one direction        Value flows multi-directionally
Firm creates all value           Ecosystem creates value
Scales linearly                  Scales exponentially (network effects)
```

## Innovation vs. Invention

A critical distinction that is frequently confused:

| Dimension | Invention | Innovation |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Definition | Creation of something new | Successful application of invention to create value |
| Output | Patent, prototype, discovery | Product, service, business, adoption |
| Requirement | Technical novelty | Market success |
| Measure | "Does it work?" | "Do people use it and pay for it?" |
| Risk | Technical risk | Market risk + technical risk + execution risk |
| Responsibility | R&D, labs, inventors | Entire organization |

**Invention + Commercialization = Innovation**

Most inventions never become innovations. The Innovation Brain focuses on the
full journey from insight to impact, not just the creation of novelty.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Portfolio across types**: The 70-20-10 split balances risk and return.
2. **Architectural innovation is the stealth killer**: Same parts, new relationships.
3. **Open innovation is not optional**: The best ideas may be outside your walls.
4. **Innovation without adoption is just invention**: Diffusion is the measure.
5. **Platform beats pipeline**: Ecosystem value creation scales exponentially.
6. **Frugal and reverse innovation expand the playing field**: Constraints breed creativity.
