# Intrapreneurship -- Entrepreneurship Inside Organizations

## Overview

Intrapreneurship is the practice of applying entrepreneurial thinking, behavior, and
methods within an established organization. Gifford Pinchot III coined the term in 1985
to describe employees who act as entrepreneurs inside their companies. Unlike corporate
venture building (which is top-down and structured), intrapreneurship is bottom-up and
organic -- it harnesses the innovative energy of employees throughout the organization.
This module covers program design, incentive structures, protection mechanisms, and
success metrics for intrapreneurship.

## Innovation Time Programs

### Google 20% Time

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Concept | Engineers spend 20% of time on self-directed projects |
| Origin | Google (2004), inspired by 3M 15% rule (1948) |
| Famous products | Gmail, Google News, AdSense |
| Current status | Largely discontinued in practice (though officially available) |
| Key lesson | Works when culture genuinely supports it; fails when managers penalize it |

### 3M 15% Rule

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| Concept | Employees may spend 15% of time on projects of their choosing |
| Origin | William McKnight, 3M Chairman (1948) |
| Famous products | Post-it Notes, Scotchgard |
| Duration | 75+ years and still active |
| Key lesson | Sustained commitment over decades creates a culture of innovation |

### Innovation Time Design Principles

```
EFFECTIVE Innovation Time                INEFFECTIVE Innovation Time

+ Explicit permission from leadership    - Lip service without real support
+ Protected time (not "on top of" work)  - Expected to be done in spare time
+ Access to resources (tools, budgets)   - No budget for materials/prototyping
+ Showcase events (demo days)            - Ideas disappear into a void
+ Path to investment for promising ideas - No connection to business pipeline
+ Manager buy-in and support             - Managers penalize time spent
+ Clear IP and credit policies           - Company claims all IP with no credit
```

### Innovation Time Metrics

| Metric | Target | Measurement |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Participation rate | > 30% of eligible employees | HR tracking |
| Ideas generated | 2+ per participant per quarter | Idea platform |
| Ideas progressed | 10% of ideas receive further investment | Stage-gate tracking |
| Products launched | 1+ per year from innovation time | Product tracking |
| Employee satisfaction | Innovation-related satisfaction > 4/5 | Survey |

## Hackathons

### Hackathon Design

```
PRE-HACKATHON (2-4 weeks before)
  - Define themes aligned with strategic priorities
  - Open registration (cross-functional teams encouraged)
  - Provide challenge briefs, data sets, APIs
  - Recruit judges (executives, customers, experts)

HACKATHON (24-72 hours)
  Day 1: Team formation, problem definition, ideation
  Day 2: Building, prototyping, testing
  Day 3: Polish, prepare pitch, demo

POST-HACKATHON (critical -- often neglected)
  Week 1: Judging, prizes, internal communication
  Week 2-4: Top 3 projects receive $5-20K seed funding
  Month 2-3: Funded teams get dedicated time to continue
  Month 4-6: Stage-gate review -- invest more or sunset
```

### Hackathon Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Kills Innovation | Fix |
|-------------|----------------------|-----|
| No follow-through | Best ideas die after the event | Funded continuation program |
| Non-strategic themes | Fun projects that do not matter | Align themes with company strategy |
| Same teams always win | Clique formation, others disengage | Team randomization, mentorship |
| Judges value polish over substance | Rewards presentation skill, not innovation | Structured evaluation rubric |
| No executive participation | Signals that leadership does not care | Require C-suite judging and attendance |
| Weekend events only | Excludes parents, caregivers, etc. | During work hours or flexible timing |

## Skunkworks Projects

### Definition and Origin

The term "skunkworks" originated at Lockheed Martin's Advanced Development Programs
division, led by Kelly Johnson during World War II. A skunkworks is a small,
autonomous team working on a radical project with minimal bureaucracy.

### Kelly Johnson's 14 Rules (Adapted)

1. The project manager must have practically complete control of the program
2. Strong but small project offices must be provided
3. The number of people connected to the project must be restricted
4. Simple drawing and reporting system with a minimum of reports
5. Monthly cost reviews, not surprise audits
6. The contractor must be delegated the authority to test their own product
7. Mutual trust between the organization and the project team
8. Access must be strictly controlled to maintain security and focus
9. Performance goals and compensation must be achievable
10. Timely funding so the project is not held hostage to budget cycles
11. Mutual trust between the project team and the parent organization
12. Outside testing must be coordinated early
13. Access to the best available tools and technology
14. Proposals should be brief and to the point

### When to Use Skunkworks

| Use Skunkworks When | Do NOT Use Skunkworks When |
|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| Speed is critical (competitive threat) | The innovation requires broad organizational change |
| The project is radical / high-risk | The innovation is incremental |
| Organizational politics would kill it | Broad stakeholder input is essential |
| A small team can build a prototype | Scale requires many teams from day one |
| Secrecy matters (competitive advantage) | Open collaboration is more valuable |

## Incentive Design for Intrapreneurs

### The Incentive Challenge

Intrapreneurs take career risk (time away from core job, potential failure) for
organizational benefit. Without proper incentives, the rational employee avoids
innovation:

```
Risk-Reward Matrix for Employees:

                    SUCCESS                    FAILURE
Core Job:    Promotion, bonus, status    Performance issues (clear)
Innovation:  Credit goes to company,     Career damage, wasted time,
             maybe a bonus               "didn't deliver on core job"

Without intervention, the rational choice is always: stay in core job.
```

### Incentive Options

| Incentive | Description | Effectiveness |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| Phantom equity | Virtual equity in the venture (cash bonus at exit/milestone) | High |
| Innovation bonus | Cash bonus for successful innovation outcomes | Medium |
| Career advancement | Innovation track as a recognized career path | High |
| Time allocation | Protected time for innovation work | Medium |
| Recognition | Awards, internal visibility, executive access | Medium |
| External exposure | Conference speaking, publications, patents with name | Medium |
| Team autonomy | Independence, reduced bureaucracy, choice of tools | High |

### Incentive Design Principles

1. **Reward learning, not just outcomes**: Failed experiments that generate insights deserve recognition
2. **Protect against career damage**: Innovation failure should not appear on performance reviews
3. **Make innovation a career path**: Not a detour from the "real" career track
4. **Align individual and organizational incentives**: Share upside, not just risk
5. **Recognize publicly**: Visibility is a powerful motivator for intrapreneurs

### Protection Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Implementation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Executive air cover | Dedicated C-suite sponsor who actively defends |

## Success Metrics for Intrapreneurship Programs

### Program-Level Metrics

| Metric | Description | Target |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Participation rate | % of employees who submit ideas or participate | > 20% |
| Idea velocity | Number of ideas submitted per month | Increasing quarter over quarter |
| Conversion rate | % of ideas that receive exploration funding | 5-15% |
| Venture success rate | % of funded ventures that achieve PMF | 10-20% |
| Revenue from new ventures | Revenue attributed to intrapreneurship program | Increasing annually |
| Time to market | Average time from idea to market for successful ventures | Decreasing |
| Employee retention | Retention of intrapreneurs vs. general population | Higher |
| Innovation culture score | Employee survey on innovation climate | > 4.0/5.0 |
| Cost per innovation | Total program cost / successful innovations launched | Decreasing |

### Balancing Metrics

```

- Innovation time used    - Pilots launched           - Patents filed

  collaborations                                     - Customer satisfaction improvement
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Bottom-up innovation requires top-down support**: Intrapreneurship programs fail without executive commitment.
2. **Follow-through is everything**: Hackathons without funding paths are innovation theater.
3. **Protect against antibodies**: Internal startups need explicit protection mechanisms.
4. **Incentives must align risk and reward**: If failure is penalized, nobody will innovate.
5. **Measure the program, not just projects**: Track participation, conversion, and culture shift.
6. **Innovation time works only when it is truly protected**: "20% time" that comes from personal time is exploitation.
