# Glossary of Business and Economics Terms

## Purpose

This glossary provides precise definitions for 100+ key terms used across the MBA Brain. Definitions are organized alphabetically and draw from standard academic and practitioner usage. Cross-references point to relevant modules.

---

## A

**Adverse Selection** — A market failure where one party has private information that the other does not, leading to inefficient outcomes. In insurance, high-risk individuals are more likely to purchase coverage. (Akerlof, 1970) [Module 03, theory/microeconomics]

**Agency Costs** — The sum of monitoring costs, bonding costs, and residual loss arising from the separation of ownership and control. (Jensen & Meckling, 1976) [Module 09, theory/organizational_economics]

**Alpha (Jensen's Alpha)** — The risk-adjusted abnormal return of an investment relative to a benchmark. Positive alpha indicates outperformance. [Module 05, theory/finance_theory]

**Annual Recurring Revenue (ARR)** — The annualized value of recurring subscription revenue, normalized for contract length. ARR = MRR x 12. [Module 04]

**Arbitrage** — Simultaneous purchase and sale of equivalent assets to profit from price differences, with no net investment and no risk. [Module 05]

**Asset Specificity** — The degree to which an asset is specialized to a particular transaction or relationship. Higher specificity creates bilateral dependency and hold-up risk. (Williamson, 1985) [Module 08, theory/organizational_economics]

---

## B

**BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)** — The best outcome achievable without reaching a deal with the current counterparty. Determines negotiation walk-away point. (Fisher & Ury, 1981) [Module 10]

**Bayesian Updating** — Revising probability estimates as new evidence arrives, using Bayes' theorem: P(H|E) = P(E|H)P(H) / P(E). [Module 02, theory/econometrics]

**Beta** — A measure of systematic risk; the sensitivity of an asset's returns to market returns. Beta = Cov(R_i, R_m) / Var(R_m). [Module 05, theory/finance_theory]

**Bounded Rationality** — The concept that decision-makers are rational within the limits of their information, cognitive capacity, and available time. (Simon, 1947) [Module 01, theory/organizational_economics]

**Burn Rate** — The rate at which a company consumes cash, typically measured monthly. Gross burn = total cash spent; net burn = total cash spent minus revenue. [Module 12]

**Business Model** — The architecture of value creation, delivery, and capture mechanisms employed by a firm. (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010) [Module 06]

---

## C

**CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model)** — E[R_i] = r_f + beta_i(E[R_m] - r_f). The expected return of an asset equals the risk-free rate plus a risk premium proportional to beta. (Sharpe, 1964; Lintner, 1965) [Module 05, theory/finance_theory]

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)** — Total cost of acquiring a new customer, including marketing, sales, and onboarding expenses. CAC = total acquisition spend / number of new customers acquired. [Module 04, Module 07]

**Cash Flow Statement** — Financial statement reporting cash inflows and outflows from operating, investing, and financing activities over a period. [Module 04]

**Churn Rate** — The percentage of customers or revenue lost over a given period. Customer churn = lost customers / starting customers. Revenue churn (net) accounts for expansion revenue. [Module 04]

**Coase Theorem** — If property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are zero, bargaining will produce an efficient outcome regardless of initial rights allocation. (Coase, 1960) [theory/microeconomics]

**Cohort Analysis** — Analyzing groups of users who share a common characteristic (e.g., signup month) to track behavior over time. Essential for understanding retention and LTV evolution. [Module 04]

**Competitive Advantage** — A firm's ability to produce goods or services more effectively than competitors, generating superior economic returns. May be temporary or sustainable. [Module 06]

**Contribution Margin** — Revenue minus variable costs, expressed in absolute terms or as a percentage. Measures incremental profitability per unit. [Module 04]

**Convertible Note** — A short-term debt instrument that converts to equity upon a triggering event (typically a qualified financing round), with a discount rate and/or valuation cap. [Module 05]

**Cost of Capital** — The return required by investors (debt and equity holders) to provide capital to the firm. See WACC. [Module 05]

---

## D

**DCF (Discounted Cash Flow)** — Valuation method that estimates the present value of expected future cash flows, discounted at the appropriate cost of capital. [Module 05, theory/finance_theory]

**Deadweight Loss** — The net loss of total surplus (consumer + producer) from a market inefficiency, such as monopoly pricing or taxation. [theory/microeconomics]

**Default Alive vs. Default Dead** — A startup is "default alive" if, at current revenue growth rate and expense levels, it will reach profitability before running out of cash. (Paul Graham) [Module 12]

**Demand Elasticity** — The percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in price. Elastic: |E| > 1; Inelastic: |E| < 1. [Module 03]

**Discount Rate** — The rate used to convert future cash flows to present value. Reflects the time value of money and risk. [Module 05]

**Disruption** — A process by which smaller companies with fewer resources successfully challenge established incumbents, typically by targeting overlooked segments with simpler, cheaper offerings. (Christensen, 1997) [Module 06]

**DuPont Analysis** — Decomposition of ROE into three components: profit margin x asset turnover x equity multiplier. ROE = (Net Income/Revenue) x (Revenue/Assets) x (Assets/Equity). [Module 04]

---

## E

**EBITDA** — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. A proxy for operating cash flow, widely used in valuation multiples. [Module 04]

**Economies of Scale** — Cost advantages that arise from increased output. Average cost decreases as production volume increases, due to fixed cost spreading and operational efficiencies. [Module 03, Module 08]

**Economies of Scope** — Cost advantages from producing multiple products jointly rather than separately, due to shared resources, capabilities, or distribution. [Module 06]

**Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)** — Asset prices reflect available information. Weak form (past prices), semi-strong (all public info), strong (all info including private). (Fama, 1970) [theory/finance_theory]

**Equity Premium** — The excess return of the stock market over the risk-free rate, historically approximately 5-7% per year in the US. [theory/finance_theory]

**EV (Enterprise Value)** — Market cap + total debt - cash. Represents the total value of a business to all capital providers. [Module 05]

**Externality** — A cost or benefit imposed on a third party not involved in a transaction. Negative externality: pollution. Positive externality: vaccination. [theory/microeconomics]

---

## F-G

**FCFF (Free Cash Flow to Firm)** — EBIT(1-tax) + Depreciation - CapEx - Change in Working Capital. Cash available to all capital providers. [Module 05]

**Flywheel Effect** — A self-reinforcing cycle where each component of a business strengthens the others, creating compounding advantage over time. (Collins) [Module 06]

**Game Theory** — The formal study of strategic interaction among rational agents. (von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944; Nash, 1950) [Module 03, theory/game_theory_formal]

**GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles)** — The standard framework for financial accounting in the United States, issued by FASB. [Module 04]

**Giffen Good** — A good for which demand increases when its price rises, due to a dominating income effect. Theoretically possible, empirically rare. [theory/microeconomics]

**Gross Margin** — (Revenue - COGS) / Revenue. Measures the percentage of revenue retained after direct production costs. [Module 04]

---

## H-I

**Hold-Up Problem** — When one party makes a relationship-specific investment, the counterparty can exploit this dependency to extract better terms. Anticipation of hold-up leads to underinvestment. (Williamson, 1985) [theory/organizational_economics]

**Hurdle Rate** — The minimum acceptable rate of return on an investment, typically equal to the cost of capital or WACC. [Module 05]

**IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards)** — Global accounting standards issued by the IASB, used in most countries outside the United States. [Module 04]

**Incentive Compatibility** — A contract or mechanism is incentive compatible if each participant's best strategy is to act truthfully/as intended. [Module 01, theory/game_theory_formal]

**Internal Rate of Return (IRR)** — The discount rate that makes the NPV of a project's cash flows equal to zero. Higher IRR indicates more attractive investment. [Module 05]

---

## J-L

**Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)** — Framework asserting that customers "hire" products to complete functional, social, and emotional jobs. Focus on the job, not the demographic. (Christensen, Ulwick) [Module 11]

**Kano Model** — Framework categorizing product features into must-have (basic), performance (linear), and delight (excitement) categories based on customer satisfaction curves. [Module 11]

**Lean Startup** — Methodology emphasizing validated learning through rapid build-measure-learn cycles, minimum viable products, and pivoting. (Ries, 2011) [Module 12]

**Lerner Index** — L = (P - MC) / P = 1/|epsilon|. Measures market power as the percentage markup over marginal cost. [theory/microeconomics]

**Liquidation Preference** — The right of preferred shareholders to receive a specified return before common shareholders in a liquidation event. Typically 1x non-participating. [Module 05]

**LTV (Customer Lifetime Value)** — The total net revenue expected from a customer over their entire relationship. LTV = ARPU x Gross Margin / Churn Rate (simplified). [Module 04, Module 07]

**LTV:CAC Ratio** — LTV divided by CAC. Measures return on customer acquisition investment. Target: 3:1 or higher for SaaS businesses. [Module 04]

---

## M

**Marginal Cost** — The additional cost of producing one more unit. MC = dC/dQ. [Module 03, theory/microeconomics]

**Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS)** — The rate at which a consumer is willing to trade one good for another while maintaining constant utility. MRS = MU_x / MU_y. [theory/microeconomics]

**Moat** — A sustainable competitive advantage that protects a business from competition. Types include network effects, switching costs, economies of scale, brand, and regulatory barriers. (Buffett) [Module 06]

**Modigliani-Miller Theorem** — In perfect markets, firm value is independent of capital structure (Proposition I). Cost of equity increases linearly with leverage (Proposition II). (1958) [theory/finance_theory]

**Moral Hazard** — Post-contractual behavior change when one party's actions, unobservable to the other, affect outcomes. (Holmstrom, 1979) [theory/organizational_economics]

**Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)** — The total predictable recurring revenue normalized to a monthly amount. MRR = number of subscribers x ARPU. [Module 04]

---

## N-O

**Nash Equilibrium** — A strategy profile where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing strategy. (Nash, 1950) [Module 03, theory/game_theory_formal]

**NDR / NRR (Net Dollar/Revenue Retention)** — Revenue retained from existing customers including expansion, contraction, and churn. NDR > 100% means expansion exceeds churn. [Module 04]

**Net Present Value (NPV)** — The sum of all future cash flows discounted to present value, minus the initial investment. Accept projects with NPV > 0. [Module 05]

**Network Effects** — A product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Direct: same-side (phone network). Indirect: cross-side (marketplace). [Module 03, Module 06]

**Opportunity Cost** — The value of the best alternative foregone when making a choice. [Module 03]

---

## P

**Pareto Efficiency** — An allocation where no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off. [theory/microeconomics]

**Payback Period** — The time required to recover the initial investment through cash flows. For CAC: months to recover acquisition cost. [Module 04]

**Pecking Order Theory** — Firms prefer internal financing, then debt, then equity, due to asymmetric information costs. (Myers & Majluf, 1984) [theory/finance_theory]

**Porter's Five Forces** — Framework analyzing industry attractiveness: threat of entry, supplier power, buyer power, substitutes, and rivalry. (Porter, 1979) [Module 06]

**Price Elasticity of Demand** — E_d = (%change Q) / (%change P). Measures demand sensitivity to price changes. [Module 03]

**Principal-Agent Problem** — The challenge of motivating an agent to act in the principal's interest when interests diverge and actions are unobservable. [Module 01, theory/organizational_economics]

**Prospect Theory** — Decision-making model incorporating loss aversion, reference dependence, and probability weighting. (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979) [theory/finance_theory]

---

## R

**Real Options** — The application of financial options theory to real investment decisions, valuing managerial flexibility to delay, expand, or abandon projects. [Module 05, theory/finance_theory]

**Regression to the Mean** — The statistical tendency for extreme observations to be followed by observations closer to the average. [Module 02]

**Revenue Recognition (ASC 606)** — The five-step model for recognizing revenue: identify contract, identify performance obligations, determine transaction price, allocate price, recognize revenue when obligations are satisfied. [Module 04]

**Risk Premium** — The additional return demanded for bearing risk above the risk-free rate. Equity risk premium = E[R_m] - r_f. [Module 05]

**Rule of 40** — A SaaS benchmark: revenue growth rate (%) + profit margin (%) should exceed 40%. Balances growth against profitability. [Module 04]

**Runway** — The number of months a company can operate before exhausting cash. Runway = cash balance / monthly net burn. [Module 12]

---

## S

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)** — A convertible instrument that converts to equity upon a triggering event, with no interest or maturity date. Simpler than convertible notes. (Y Combinator) [Module 05]

**Selection Bias** — Systematic error from non-random selection of subjects, causing the sample to be unrepresentative of the population. [Module 02, theory/econometrics]

**Sharpe Ratio** — (E[R_p] - r_f) / sigma_p. Measures excess return per unit of total risk. [Module 05, theory/finance_theory]

**Signaling** — Costly actions taken by informed parties to credibly convey private information. (Spence, 1973) [Module 07, theory/microeconomics]

**Slutsky Equation** — Decomposes price effect into substitution and income effects. [theory/microeconomics]

**Survivorship Bias** — The error of analyzing only surviving entities (successful companies, winning funds) while ignoring those that failed, leading to overly optimistic conclusions. [Module 02]

**Switching Costs** — The costs a customer incurs when changing suppliers, including financial, procedural, and relational costs. Higher switching costs create competitive moats. [Module 06]

---

## T-U

**Terminal Value** — The value of a business beyond the explicit forecast period in a DCF analysis. Calculated via Gordon Growth Model (perpetuity) or exit multiple method. [Module 05]

**Total Addressable Market (TAM)** — The total revenue opportunity for a product or service assuming 100% market share. [Module 06, Module 12]

**Transaction Costs** — The costs of using the market mechanism: search costs, bargaining costs, enforcement costs. (Coase, 1937; Williamson, 1985) [theory/organizational_economics]

**Type 1 / Type 2 Decisions** — Irreversible (one-way door) vs. reversible (two-way door) decisions. Type 1 decisions deserve deep analysis; Type 2 decisions should move fast. (Bezos) [Module 01]

**Unit Economics** — The revenues and costs associated with a single unit of business (one customer, one transaction, one product). Determines scalability. [Module 04]

---

## V-Z

**Value Chain** — The full range of activities required to bring a product from conception to delivery. Analyzing the value chain identifies opportunities for competitive advantage. (Porter, 1985) [Module 06]

**Venture Capital Power Law** — VC fund returns are dominated by a small number of extreme winners. Fund economics require targeting 10x+ returns on individual investments. [Module 05, Module 12]

**VRIO Framework** — Evaluates resources/capabilities for competitive advantage: Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Organized to capture value. (Barney, 1991) [Module 06]

**WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)** — The blended cost of capital across debt and equity: WACC = (E/(D+E))r_E + (D/(D+E))r_D(1-t). Used as discount rate in DCF valuation. [Module 05, theory/finance_theory]

**Winner's Curse** — In common-value auctions, the winning bidder tends to have the most optimistic estimate and thus overpays. [theory/game_theory_formal]

**Working Capital** — Current assets minus current liabilities. Measures short-term liquidity and operational efficiency. [Module 04]

**ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)** — The range between parties' reservation prices where a mutually beneficial deal is possible. (Fisher & Ury) [Module 10]
