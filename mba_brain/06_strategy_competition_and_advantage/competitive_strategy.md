## 1. Porter's Five Forces

*Citation: Porter, M. E. (1980). Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors. Free Press.*

### 1.1 The Framework

Industry profitability is determined by five competitive forces:

**1. Threat of New Entrants**
- Barriers to entry: economies of scale, capital requirements, switching costs, access to distribution, government policy, network effects
- Higher barriers = higher industry profitability

**2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers**
- Concentrated supplier base, high switching costs, differentiated inputs, threat of forward integration
- Powerful suppliers capture more value

**3. Bargaining Power of Buyers**
- Concentrated buyers, low switching costs, commodity products, backward integration threat
- Powerful buyers push prices down

**4. Threat of Substitutes**
- Products from other industries that serve the same need
- High substitute availability caps industry profitability

**5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors**
- Number and balance of competitors, industry growth rate, fixed costs, product differentiation, exit barriers
- Intense rivalry erodes profitability

### 1.2 Formal Analysis Template

For each force, rate intensity (Low/Medium/High) and identify specific drivers:

```
Force               Intensity   Key Drivers
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
Threat of Entry     Medium      Moderate capital requirements, some network effects
Supplier Power      Low         Fragmented suppliers, low switching costs
Buyer Power         High        Concentrated buyers, commodity product
Substitute Threat   Medium      Adjacent technologies could serve similar needs
Rivalry             High        5 similar-sized competitors, slow growth
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
Overall: Moderate-Low profitability potential
```

## 2. Generic Strategies

*Citation: Porter, M. E. (1985). Competitive Advantage: Creating and Sustaining Superior Performance. Free Press.*

### 2.1 Three Generic Strategies

```
                    Competitive Advantage
                    Lower Cost        Differentiation
Competitive   Broad    Cost Leadership   Differentiation
Scope         Narrow   Cost Focus        Differentiation Focus
```

**Cost leadership:** Achieve lowest cost position in the industry. Requires scale, process efficiency, tight cost control. Risk: competitors imitate, technology changes cost structure.

**Differentiation:** Offer unique value that buyers prize, commanding premium prices. Requires innovation, brand, customer intimacy. Risk: cost premium exceeds willingness to pay.

**Focus:** Target a narrow segment with either cost or differentiation advantage. Requires deep understanding of segment needs. Risk: segment is not large enough, or broad competitors enter.

### 2.2 The Stuck in the Middle Problem

Porter argued that firms must choose one strategy — trying to be both low-cost and differentiated leads to "stuck in the middle" with inferior performance.

**Critique:** Some firms successfully combine low cost and differentiation (e.g., IKEA, Southwest Airlines, Amazon). Technology can enable both simultaneously (digitalization reduces costs while enabling personalization).

## 3. Value Chain Analysis

*Citation: Porter, M. E. (1985). Competitive Advantage. Free Press.*

### 3.1 Primary Activities

```
Inbound      ->  Operations  ->  Outbound     ->  Marketing  ->  Service
Logistics                        Logistics         & Sales
```

### 3.2 Support Activities

- Firm infrastructure (management, finance, legal)
- Human resource management
- Technology development
- Procurement

### 3.3 Analysis Method

For each activity, assess:
1. What is the cost of this activity?
2. What value does it create for the customer?
3. Is our performance better or worse than competitors?
4. Can this activity be a source of advantage?

Competitive advantage comes from performing activities differently or performing different activities than rivals.

## 4. Resource-Based View (RBV)

### 4.1 VRIO Framework

*Citation: Barney, J. B. (1991). Firm resources and sustained competitive advantage. Journal of Management, 17(1), 99-120.*

A resource or capability generates sustained competitive advantage if it is:

| Criterion | Question | If No |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| **V**aluable | Does it enable the firm to exploit opportunities or neutralize threats? | Competitive disadvantage |
| **R**are | Is it controlled by few firms? | Competitive parity |
| **I**nimitable | Is it costly to obtain or replicate? | Temporary advantage |
| **O**rganized | Is the firm organized to capture value from this resource? | Unexploited potential |

Only resources that pass all four criteria produce **sustained competitive advantage**.

### 4.2 Sources of Inimitability

*Citation: Barney, J. B. (1991). Firm resources and sustained competitive advantage. Journal of Management, 17(1), 99-120.*

1. **Path dependence:** The resource was developed through a unique historical process that cannot be replicated
2. **Causal ambiguity:** Competitors cannot identify which resources generate the advantage
3. **Social complexity:** The resource is embedded in complex social relationships (culture, trust, routines)
4. **Legal protection:** Patents, trademarks, copyrights

## 5. Dynamic Capabilities

*Citation: Teece, D. J., Pisano, G., & Shuen, A. (1997). Dynamic capabilities and strategic management. Strategic Management Journal, 18(7), 509-533.*

### 5.1 Definition

Dynamic capabilities are the firm's ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences to address rapidly changing environments.

While RBV explains advantage from existing resources, dynamic capabilities explain how firms create, extend, and modify their resource base over time.

### 5.2 Three Categories

1. **Sensing:** Identifying and assessing opportunities and threats in the environment
2. **Seizing:** Mobilizing resources to capture value from opportunities
3. **Transforming:** Continuous renewal — reconfiguring assets and organizational structures

### 5.3 Microfoundations

| Capability | Microfoundations |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Sensing | R&D, market research, scanning technologies, external networks |
| Seizing | Business model design, investment decisions, organizational architecture |
| Transforming | Knowledge management, corporate governance, decentralization |

## 6. Blue Ocean Strategy

*Citation: Kim, W. C., & Mauborgne, R. (2005). Blue Ocean Strategy. Harvard Business School Press.*

### 6.1 Red Ocean vs. Blue Ocean

| Dimension | Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| Market space | Existing industries | New/uncontested market |
| Competition | Beat the competition | Make competition irrelevant |
| Demand | Exploit existing demand | Create and capture new demand |
| Value-cost | Choose value OR cost | Pursue value AND cost |
| Strategy | Align with differentiation or low-cost | Break the value-cost trade-off |

### 6.2 Strategy Canvas and Four Actions Framework

**Eliminate:** Which factors the industry takes for granted should be eliminated?
**Reduce:** Which factors should be reduced below the industry standard?
**Raise:** Which factors should be raised above the industry standard?
**Create:** Which factors should the industry has never offered should be created?

## 7. Disruption Theory

*Citation: Christensen, C. M. (1997). The Innovator's Dilemma. Harvard Business School Press.*

### 7.1 The Mechanism of Disruption

1. Incumbents serve mainstream customers with improving products
2. Entrants target overlooked segments (low-end or new market) with simpler, cheaper solutions
3. Entrants improve over time, eventually serving mainstream customers
4. Incumbents are displaced — not because they failed to innovate, but because they rationally focused on their best customers

### 7.2 Why Rational Incumbents Fail

The innovator's dilemma: incumbents rationally allocate resources to high-margin customers and proven markets. Disruptive innovations initially serve less profitable segments — so they are rationally ignored until it's too late.

**Formal mechanism:**
- Incumbent's sustaining trajectory overshoots customer needs
- Disruptive trajectory, though initially inferior, improves to "good enough" for mainstream
- By the time the incumbent responds, the disrupter has captured the market

### 7.3 Critique and Limitations

*Citation: King, A. A., & Baatartogtokh, B. (2015). How useful is the theory of disruptive innovation? MIT Sloan Management Review, 57(1), 77.*

- Many cited examples of "disruption" don't fit the theory precisely
- Incumbents sometimes successfully respond to disruption
- Not all low-end innovation is disruptive
- The theory is better at explaining the past than predicting the future

## 8. Integration: Strategy Assessment Protocol

For any strategic situation, apply multiple lenses:

1. **Industry analysis** (Five Forces): Is this an attractive industry?
2. **Competitive position** (Generic strategies, Value chain): Where does our advantage come from?
3. **Resource audit** (VRIO): Are our advantages sustainable?
4. **Dynamic capabilities assessment:** Can we adapt as the environment changes?
5. **Disruption scan:** Are there disruptive threats we're ignoring?
6. **Blue ocean exploration:** Can we create uncontested market space?
