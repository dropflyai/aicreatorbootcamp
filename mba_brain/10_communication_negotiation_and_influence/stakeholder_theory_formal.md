## 1. Stakeholder Identification and Salience

### 1.1 Mitchell, Agle & Wood Stakeholder Typology

*Citation: Mitchell, R. K., Agle, B. R., & Wood, D. J. (1997). Toward a theory of stakeholder identification and salience: Defining the principle of who and what really counts. Academy of Management Review, 22(4), 853-886.*

Stakeholder salience depends on three attributes:

1. **Power:** The stakeholder's ability to influence the organization (coercive, utilitarian, or normative power)
2. **Legitimacy:** The perceived validity of the stakeholder's claim on the organization
3. **Urgency:** The degree to which the stakeholder's claim demands immediate attention

### 1.2 Stakeholder Classification

| Type | Attributes | Priority | Example |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|
| Definitive | Power + Legitimacy + Urgency | Highest | Key investors, regulators with deadlines |
| Dominant | Power + Legitimacy | High | Board members, major customers |
| Dangerous | Power + Urgency | Handle carefully | Hostile acquirers, activist investors |
| Dependent | Legitimacy + Urgency | Support needed | Employees in crisis, affected communities |
| Dormant | Power only | Monitor | Government agencies, large potential partners |
| Discretionary | Legitimacy only | Low urgency | Industry associations, academic advisors |
| Demanding | Urgency only | Lowest | Vocal complainers without power or legitimacy |

## 2. Power-Interest Grid

### 2.1 The Framework

```
High Power
          |
  Keep    |    Manage
  Satisfied|    Closely
          |
----------|----------
          |
  Monitor |    Keep
  (Minimum|    Informed
  Effort) |
          |
Low Power
     Low Interest      High Interest
```

### 2.2 Communication Strategy by Quadrant

| Quadrant | Strategy | Frequency | Depth |
|----------|----------|-----------|-------|
| High Power, High Interest | Manage closely | Weekly/biweekly | Deep, proactive |
| High Power, Low Interest | Keep satisfied | Monthly/quarterly | Summary, key decisions |
| Low Power, High Interest | Keep informed | Regular updates | Transparent, responsive |
| Low Power, Low Interest | Monitor | As needed | Minimal, reactive |

## 3. Stakeholder Theory — Freeman's Framework

*Citation: Freeman, R. E. (1984). Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach. Pitman.*

### 3.1 Core Proposition

The purpose of a firm is to create value for all stakeholders, not just shareholders. Effective management requires balancing the interests of customers, employees, suppliers, communities, and investors.

### 3.2 Stakeholder vs. Shareholder Primacy

| Dimension | Shareholder Primacy | Stakeholder Theory |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------------|
| Objective | Maximize shareholder value | Create value for all stakeholders |
| Origin | Friedman (1970) | Freeman (1984) |
| Time horizon | Often short-term focused | Longer-term, relationship-oriented |
| Risk | Neglects non-shareholder interests | Potential diffusion of accountability |
| Legal basis | Fiduciary duty to shareholders | Broader responsibility claims |

### 3.3 Practical Integration

Most effective operators integrate both perspectives:
- Shareholders provide capital and bear financial risk (their interests matter)
- Ignoring other stakeholders creates long-term risks (employee turnover, regulatory action, customer backlash)
- Sustainable shareholder value creation requires stakeholder satisfaction

## 4. Board Management

### 4.1 Board Governance Theory

*Citation: Jensen, M. C. (1993). The modern industrial revolution, exit, and the failure of internal control systems. Journal of Finance, 48(3), 831-880.*

**Agency theory perspective:** The board monitors management on behalf of shareholders, mitigating agency costs.

**Resource dependence perspective** (Pfeffer & Salancik, 1978): The board provides access to resources, legitimacy, and advice.

### 4.2 Board Communication Best Practices

**Before meetings:**
- Send materials 5-7 days in advance
- Highlight key decisions needed (not just information)
- No surprises — share bad news early

**During meetings:**
- Lead with strategy and key decisions, not operational details
- Present options with recommendations and rationale
- Manage time ruthlessly

**Between meetings:**
- Regular 1:1 updates with key board members
- Call immediately for major events
- Leverage board members' networks proactively

### 4.3 Investor Communication Cadence

| Update Type | Frequency | Content |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Board meeting | Quarterly | Full financial review, strategy, key decisions |
| Investor update | Monthly | KPIs, milestones, asks |
| Ad hoc | As needed | Major events |

## 5. Crisis Stakeholder Communication

*Citation: Coombs, W. T. (2007). Protecting organization reputations during a crisis. Corporate Reputation Review, 10(3), 163-176.*

### 5.1 Situational Crisis Communication Theory (SCCT)

SCCT matches crisis response strategies to the level of organizational responsibility:

| Crisis Type | Responsibility | Strategy |
|-------------|---------------|----------|
| Natural disaster, rumor | Minimal | Deny or diminish |
| Technical error, challenge | Low | Diminish (excuse, justification) |
| Human error, organizational misdeed | High | Rebuild (apology, compensation) |

### 5.2 Crisis Communication Protocol

1. **Acknowledge immediately** — Silence signals guilt or incompetence
2. **State what you know and don't know** — Honesty preserves credibility
3. **Describe the response** — What actions are being taken
4. **Commit to updates** — Specify when you will provide more information
5. **Take responsibility where appropriate** — Accept accountability
