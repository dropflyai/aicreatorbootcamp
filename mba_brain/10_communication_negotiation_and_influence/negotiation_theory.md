## 1. Principled Negotiation

*Citation: Fisher, R., Ury, W., & Patton, B. (1981). Getting to Yes: Negotiating Agreement Without Giving In. Houghton Mifflin.*

### 1.1 Four Principles

1. **Separate the people from the problem:** Negotiate on substance, not emotions. Address relationship issues directly, not through substantive concessions.

2. **Focus on interests, not positions:** Positions are what people say they want. Interests are why they want it. Multiple positions can satisfy the same interest.

3. **Invent options for mutual gain:** Before deciding, brainstorm creative solutions that expand the pie. Joint gains are available when parties value things differently.

4. **Insist on objective criteria:** Use standards, benchmarks, precedents, or fair processes to evaluate options. Avoids contests of will.

### 1.2 BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)

Your BATNA is your walk-away option — what you do if no deal is reached. It is the single most important factor determining your negotiation power.

**BATNA improves your position because:**
- A strong BATNA means you can walk away (credible threat)
- You should never accept a deal worse than your BATNA
- Improving your BATNA improves your negotiation outcome

**BATNA analysis:**

```
1. List all alternatives if this negotiation fails
2. Evaluate each alternative realistically
3. Select the best alternative = your BATNA
4. Estimate the counterparty's BATNA
5. If your BATNA > their offer, walk away
6. If their BATNA is weak, you have leverage
```

### 1.3 ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

```
Your reservation price (minimum acceptable) ──────── ZOPA ──────── Their reservation price
```

A deal is possible only if a ZOPA exists (your minimum is less than their maximum). The negotiation determines where within the ZOPA the agreement falls.

## 2. Integrative vs. Distributive Bargaining

### 2.1 Distributive Bargaining (Fixed Pie)

One party's gain is the other's loss. Tactics: anchoring, commitment, deadline pressure, information asymmetry.

**When to use:** Single-issue negotiations, one-time transactions, no ongoing relationship.

### 2.2 Integrative Bargaining (Expanding the Pie)

Parties have different preferences, allowing trades that make both better off.

**Conditions for integration:**
- Multiple issues (tradeoffs possible)
- Different priorities (you value X more, they value Y more)
- Different risk preferences (one party accepts risk the other avoids)
- Different time preferences (one values now, other values later)

**Technique — Logrolling:** Trade concessions on issues where you care less for concessions on issues where you care more.

## 3. Schelling Focal Points

*Citation: Schelling, T. C. (1960). The Strategy of Conflict. Harvard University Press.*

### 3.1 Concept

In coordination problems without communication, people tend to converge on "focal points" — solutions that seem natural, special, or obvious based on shared cultural context.

**Examples:**
- Asked to pick a meeting spot in New York, people often say "Grand Central Terminal, noon"
- Asked to split $100, people usually say 50-50
- In price negotiations, round numbers serve as focal points

### 3.2 Application to Negotiation

- **Anchoring** works partly through focal point effects — the first number mentioned becomes a reference
- **Fairness norms** (50-50 split, market rate) serve as focal points
- **Precedent** creates focal points — "the standard deal in this industry is X"

## 4. Cialdini's Principles of Influence

*Citation: Cialdini, R. B. (1984). Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion. Harper Business.*

### 4.1 Six Principles

| Principle | Description | Negotiation Application |
|-----------|------------|----------------------|
| **Reciprocity** | People feel obligated to return favors | Make concessions first to trigger reciprocal concessions |
| **Commitment** | People act consistently with prior commitments | Get small agreements early; they anchor larger ones |
| **Social proof** | People follow others' behavior under uncertainty | Reference comparable deals, industry norms |
| **Authority** | People defer to credible experts | Cite expert opinions, data, credentials |
| **Liking** | People agree with those they like | Build rapport before negotiating substance |
| **Scarcity** | People value what's scarce | Create legitimate time pressure, limited availability |

### 4.2 Ethical Boundaries

These principles describe psychological reality. Using them ethically means:
- Providing genuine value (reciprocity through real concessions)
- Making honest commitments
- Citing accurate social proof
- Representing genuine authority
- Building authentic rapport
- Communicating real scarcity

Manipulative application (false scarcity, fake social proof) destroys trust and long-term relationships.

## 5. Commitment and Escalation

### 5.1 Escalation of Commitment

*Citation: Staw, B. M. (1976). Knee-deep in the big muddy: A study of escalating commitment to a chosen course of action. Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 16(1), 27-44.*

People tend to increase commitment to a failing course of action because:
- Sunk cost fallacy (invested too much to quit)
- Self-justification (admitting failure is psychologically costly)
- Completion bias (desire to finish what was started)
- Reputation concerns (perceived as indecisive if reversing)

### 5.2 De-escalation Strategies

1. Set kill criteria before starting (Module 01)
2. Separate the decision to continue from the original decision
3. Assign different people to evaluate continuation
4. Frame the decision as "starting fresh" — would we begin this today?
5. Normalize pivoting and strategic retreat

## 6. Advanced Negotiation Tactics

### 6.1 Anchoring

The first number in a negotiation disproportionately influences the outcome. Research shows that even random anchors (spinning a roulette wheel) affect subsequent estimates.

**Rules for anchoring:**
- Make the first offer when you have good information about the ZOPA
- Let them anchor first when you have poor information
- Your anchor should be ambitious but defensible
- Support your anchor with rationale (not arbitrary)

### 6.2 Multiple Equivalent Simultaneous Offers (MESOs)

Present 2-3 packages that are equally acceptable to you but structured differently. This:
- Signals flexibility and good faith
- Reveals counterparty preferences through their reactions
- Creates opportunities for integrative solutions

### 6.3 Contingent Agreements

When parties disagree about future outcomes, use contingent agreements:
"If revenue exceeds $X, the earn-out is $Y; if below $X, the earn-out is $Z."

This resolves optimism bias and creates value from differences in beliefs.
