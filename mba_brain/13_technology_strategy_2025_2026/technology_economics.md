## 1. Technology Adoption Dynamics

### 1.1 Diffusion of Innovations

*Citation: Rogers, E. M. (1962). Diffusion of Innovations. Free Press.*

Adoption follows a normal distribution (bell curve), with cumulative adoption following an S-curve:

```
Adopter Categories:
Innovators (2.5%) -> Early Adopters (13.5%) -> Early Majority (34%) -> Late Majority (34%) -> Laggards (16%)
```

### 1.2 Crossing the Chasm

*Citation: Moore, G. A. (1991). Crossing the Chasm. Harper Business.*

A "chasm" exists between early adopters and the early majority. Early adopters buy for innovation and vision; the early majority buy for productivity and proven solutions.

**Chasm-crossing strategy:**
1. Focus on a narrow beachhead segment
2. Deliver a "whole product" (not just technology, but complete solution)
3. Use pragmatist references and social proof
4. Position against the market alternative, not the technology alternative

## 2. Platform Economics

### 2.1 Winner-Take-All Dynamics

Conditions for winner-take-all markets:
- Strong network effects (direct or indirect)
- High switching costs
- Low multi-homing (users stick to one platform)
- Economies of scale in supply

**When winner-take-all does NOT apply:**
- Weak network effects
- Low switching costs
- Geographic/cultural segmentation
- Regulatory barriers to monopoly

### 2.2 Platform Governance

*Citation: Boudreau, K. J., & Hagiu, A. (2009). Platform rules: Multi-sided platforms as regulators. In A. Gawer (Ed.), Platforms, Markets and Innovation. Edward Elgar.*

Platform owners must balance:
- **Openness vs. control:** More openness attracts developers but reduces quality control
- **Subsidize vs. charge:** Pricing across platform sides to maximize adoption
- **Standardization vs. variety:** Standards enable quality but reduce innovation

## 3. Technology S-Curves and Disruption

### 3.1 Technology S-Curves

*Citation: Foster, R. (1986). Innovation: The Attacker's Advantage. Summit Books.*

Performance of any technology follows an S-curve: slow initial improvement, rapid advancement, then diminishing returns.

```
Performance
     |                     * * * *
     |                  *
     |               *
     |            *
     |          *
     |        *
     |      *
     |   * *
     | * *
     |*
     └────────────────────── R&D Effort
```

When a technology approaches its performance limit, a new S-curve (new technology) begins its slow phase, eventually surpassing the old.

### 3.2 Disruption and S-Curve Transitions

Incumbents are most vulnerable during S-curve transitions because:
- They have invested heavily in the old technology (sunk costs)
- Their processes are optimized for the old technology
- Their customers may initially prefer the old technology
- The new technology starts inferior on established performance metrics
