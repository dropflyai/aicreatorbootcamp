# Annotated Reading List — 50+ Canonical Business Books

## Purpose

An annotated bibliography of essential business books organized by topic. Each entry includes the full citation, a brief summary, the key takeaway, and the MBA Brain module it most directly supports.

---

## Strategy and Competition

### 1. Competitive Strategy — Michael E. Porter (1980)
*Porter, M. E. (1980). Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors. Free Press.*
The foundational text on industry analysis. Introduces the Five Forces framework for evaluating industry attractiveness and three generic strategies (cost leadership, differentiation, focus).
**Key takeaway:** Industry structure determines profitability. Understand the five forces before entering any market. [Module 06]

### 2. Competitive Advantage — Michael E. Porter (1985)
*Porter, M. E. (1985). Competitive Advantage: Creating and Sustaining Superior Performance. Free Press.*
Introduces the value chain concept. Competitive advantage comes from performing activities differently or performing different activities than rivals.
**Key takeaway:** Strategy is about the entire system of activities, not individual best practices. [Module 06]

### 3. Good to Great — Jim Collins (2001)
*Collins, J. (2001). Good to Great: Why Some Companies Make the Leap... and Others Don't. Harper Business.*
Matched-pair study of companies that achieved sustained outperformance. Identifies Level 5 leadership, first who then what, hedgehog concept, flywheel.
**Key takeaway:** Disciplined people + disciplined thought + disciplined action = sustained results. [Module 09]

### 4. The Innovator's Dilemma — Clayton Christensen (1997)
*Christensen, C. M. (1997). The Innovator's Dilemma. Harvard Business School Press.*
Explains why great companies fail when disruptive technologies emerge. Incumbents rationally focus on existing customers, creating opportunity for disruptors.
**Key takeaway:** Disruption comes from below — watch for simpler, cheaper alternatives serving overlooked segments. [Module 06]

### 5. Blue Ocean Strategy — W. Chan Kim & Renee Mauborgne (2005)
*Kim, W. C., & Mauborgne, R. (2005). Blue Ocean Strategy. Harvard Business School Press.*
Argues for creating uncontested market space rather than competing in existing industries. Introduces the strategy canvas and four actions framework.
**Key takeaway:** The best competitive strategy may be to make competition irrelevant. [Module 06]

### 6. 7 Powers — Hamilton Helmer (2016)
*Helmer, H. (2016). 7 Powers: The Foundations of Business Strategy. Deep Strategy LLC.*
Identifies seven sources of durable competitive advantage: scale economies, network effects, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power.
**Key takeaway:** Strategy is the study of power — the set of conditions creating the potential for persistent significant differential returns. [Module 06]

### 7. Playing to Win — A.G. Lafley & Roger Martin (2013)
*Lafley, A. G., & Martin, R. L. (2013). Playing to Win: How Strategy Really Works. Harvard Business Review Press.*
Strategy as five interconnected choices: winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems.
**Key takeaway:** Strategy requires making explicit, integrated choices — not just setting goals. [Module 06]

## Entrepreneurship and Startups

### 8. The Lean Startup — Eric Ries (2011)
*Ries, E. (2011). The Lean Startup. Crown Business.*
Applies lean manufacturing principles to startups. Build-Measure-Learn loop, MVP, validated learning, pivot or persevere.
**Key takeaway:** Startups are experiments. Minimize time through the learning loop. [Module 12]

### 9. Zero to One — Peter Thiel (2014)
*Thiel, P. (2014). Zero to One: Notes on Startups, or How to Build the Future. Crown Business.*
Argues for creating monopolies through technology innovation rather than competing in existing markets. Contrarian thinking, last mover advantage, distribution.
**Key takeaway:** The most important question is not what you believe that everyone else agrees with, but what important truth do few people agree with you on? [Module 12]

### 10. The Four Steps to the Epiphany — Steve Blank (2005)
*Blank, S. (2005). The Four Steps to the Epiphany. K&S Ranch.*
Customer Development methodology: discovery, validation, creation, building. Separates search phase from execution phase.
**Key takeaway:** Get out of the building. Talk to customers before building anything. [Module 12]

### 11. The Hard Thing About Hard Things — Ben Horowitz (2014)
*Horowitz, B. (2014). The Hard Thing About Hard Things. Harper Business.*
Practical advice on the toughest challenges of running a startup: layoffs, firing friends, managing psychology, wartime vs. peacetime CEO.
**Key takeaway:** There are no easy answers in business. The job of a CEO is to make the decisions no one else can. [Module 09]

### 12. Crossing the Chasm — Geoffrey Moore (1991)
*Moore, G. A. (1991). Crossing the Chasm. Harper Business.*
Explains the gap between early adopters and the early majority in technology adoption. Focus on a beachhead, deliver a whole product.
**Key takeaway:** The chasm is crossed one niche at a time. [Module 13]

## Finance and Valuation

### 13. The Intelligent Investor — Benjamin Graham (1949)
*Graham, B. (1949). The Intelligent Investor. Harper & Brothers.*
The bible of value investing. Introduces margin of safety, Mr. Market, and the distinction between investment and speculation.
**Key takeaway:** The market is a voting machine in the short run and a weighing machine in the long run. [Module 05, theory]

### 14. Valuation — McKinsey & Company (2020, 7th ed.)
*Koller, T., Goedhart, M., & Wessels, D. (2020). Valuation: Measuring and Managing the Value of Companies. Wiley.*
The definitive DCF valuation textbook. ROIC, growth, WACC, terminal value, multiples.
**Key takeaway:** Value is created when companies invest capital at returns exceeding their cost of capital. [Module 05]

### 15. Venture Deals — Brad Feld & Jason Mendelson (2019, 4th ed.)
*Feld, B., & Mendelson, J. (2019). Venture Deals: Be Smarter Than Your Lawyer and Venture Capitalist. Wiley.*
Comprehensive guide to venture capital term sheets, negotiation, and fundraising mechanics.
**Key takeaway:** Understand every term in a term sheet before signing. Economics and control are the two things that matter. [Module 05]

### 16. Secrets of Sand Hill Road — Scott Kupor (2019)
*Kupor, S. (2019). Secrets of Sand Hill Road: Venture Capital and How to Get It. Portfolio/Penguin.*
Insider's view of VC fund economics, LP/GP relationships, and what VCs actually optimize for.
**Key takeaway:** Understand your investor's incentives — fund economics drive their behavior. [Module 05]

## Marketing and Growth

### 17. Positioning — Al Ries & Jack Trout (1981)
*Ries, A., & Trout, J. (1981). Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind. McGraw-Hill.*
The foundational text on positioning. It's not about what you do to the product, but what you do to the mind of the prospect.
**Key takeaway:** Be first in a category, or create a new category you can be first in. [Module 07]

### 18. Influence — Robert Cialdini (1984)
*Cialdini, R. B. (1984). Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion. Harper Business.*
Six principles of persuasion: reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof, authority, liking, scarcity.
**Key takeaway:** Persuasion follows predictable patterns that can be applied (ethically) to marketing, sales, and negotiation. [Module 10]

### 19. Competing Against Luck — Clayton Christensen et al. (2016)
*Christensen, C. M., Hall, T., Dillon, K., & Duncan, D. S. (2016). Competing Against Luck. Harper Business.*
Full articulation of Jobs to Be Done theory applied to innovation. Customers hire products to make progress in their lives.
**Key takeaway:** Understand the job, not the customer demographics. [Module 11]

### 20. Hacking Growth — Sean Ellis & Morgan Brown (2017)
*Ellis, S., & Brown, M. (2017). Hacking Growth. Currency.*
Systematic approach to growth through rapid experimentation across the customer journey.
**Key takeaway:** Growth is a systematic process, not a collection of tricks. [Module 07]

### 21. Play Bigger — Ramadan, Peterson, Lochhead & Maney (2016)
*Ramadan, A., et al. (2016). Play Bigger. Harper Business.*
Category design as a strategy discipline. Category kings capture 76% of market capitalization.
**Key takeaway:** Design the category, not just the product. [Module 07]

## Leadership and Management

### 22. High Output Management — Andy Grove (1983)
*Grove, A. S. (1983). High Output Management. Random House.*
Management as a high-leverage activity. Meetings, decisions, planning, performance reviews as managerial tools.
**Key takeaway:** A manager's output is the output of the teams under their supervision. [Module 09]

### 23. The Fearless Organization — Amy Edmondson (2019)
*Edmondson, A. C. (2019). The Fearless Organization: Creating Psychological Safety in the Workplace. Wiley.*
Research-backed case for psychological safety as the foundation of high-performing teams.
**Key takeaway:** Psychological safety is not about being nice; it's about enabling candor and learning. [Module 09]

### 24. Thinking, Fast and Slow — Daniel Kahneman (2011)
*Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, Fast and Slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux.*
Two systems of thinking: System 1 (fast, intuitive, error-prone) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, effortful). Catalogues cognitive biases.
**Key takeaway:** We are systematically irrational in predictable ways. Design decisions to account for biases. [Module 02]

### 25. Poor Charlie's Almanack — Charlie Munger (2005)
*Munger, C. T. (2005). Poor Charlie's Almanack. Walsworth.*
Mental models, multidisciplinary thinking, psychology of human misjudgment. The latticework of mental models approach.
**Key takeaway:** Build a latticework of models from multiple disciplines. Avoid man-with-a-hammer syndrome. [Module 01]

## Operations and Execution

### 26. The Goal — Eliyahu Goldratt (1984)
*Goldratt, E. M. (1984). The Goal: A Process of Ongoing Improvement. North River Press.*
Introduces Theory of Constraints through a novel format. Every system has a bottleneck that determines throughput.
**Key takeaway:** Optimizing anything other than the constraint is an illusion of improvement. [Module 08]

### 27. The Toyota Way — Jeffrey Liker (2004)
*Liker, J. K. (2004). The Toyota Way: 14 Management Principles from the World's Greatest Manufacturer. McGraw-Hill.*
14 principles of the Toyota Production System, grouped into philosophy, process, people, and problem-solving.
**Key takeaway:** Continuous improvement and respect for people are inseparable. [Module 08]

### 28. Lean Thinking — Womack & Jones (1996)
*Womack, J. P., & Jones, D. T. (1996). Lean Thinking. Simon & Schuster.*
Five lean principles: identify value, map the value stream, create flow, establish pull, pursue perfection.
**Key takeaway:** Most activities are waste. Ruthlessly eliminate everything that doesn't create value for the customer. [Module 08]

## Product Management

### 29. Inspired — Marty Cagan (2018, 2nd ed.)
*Cagan, M. (2018). Inspired: How to Create Tech Products Customers Love. Wiley.*
Empowered product teams, continuous discovery, product-market fit. How the best companies build products.
**Key takeaway:** The job of product is to discover solutions that are valuable, usable, feasible, and viable. [Module 11]

### 30. Continuous Discovery Habits — Teresa Torres (2021)
*Torres, T. (2021). Continuous Discovery Habits. Product Talk LLC.*
Opportunity Solution Trees, weekly discovery cadence, assumption testing.
**Key takeaway:** Make discovery a continuous habit, not a phase. [Module 11]

## Negotiation

### 31. Getting to Yes — Fisher, Ury & Patton (1981)
*Fisher, R., Ury, W., & Patton, B. (1981). Getting to Yes. Houghton Mifflin.*
Principled negotiation: separate people from problem, focus on interests, invent options, use objective criteria.
**Key takeaway:** Negotiate on interests, not positions. Your BATNA is your greatest source of power. [Module 10]

### 32. Negotiation Genius — Malhotra & Bazerman (2007)
*Malhotra, D., & Bazerman, M. H. (2007). Negotiation Genius. Bantam.*
Advanced negotiation strategies: investigative negotiation, dealing with liars, negotiating from weakness.
**Key takeaway:** The best negotiators create value before claiming it. [Module 10]

## Economics and Decision-Making

### 33. Microeconomic Theory — Mas-Colell, Whinston & Green (1995)
*Mas-Colell, A., Whinston, M. D., & Green, J. R. (1995). Microeconomic Theory. Oxford University Press.*
The definitive graduate microeconomics textbook. Consumer theory, producer theory, general equilibrium, game theory, mechanism design.
**Key takeaway:** The rigorous foundations for all economic reasoning about markets and behavior. [theory/microeconomics]

### 34. Game Theory — Fudenberg & Tirole (1991)
*Fudenberg, D., & Tirole, J. (1991). Game Theory. MIT Press.*
Comprehensive graduate game theory. Normal and extensive form, equilibrium refinements, repeated games, mechanism design.
**Key takeaway:** Strategic interaction requires reasoning about what others will do in response to your actions. [theory/game_theory_formal]

### 35. Mostly Harmless Econometrics — Angrist & Pischke (2009)
*Angrist, J. D., & Pischke, J.-S. (2009). Mostly Harmless Econometrics. Princeton University Press.*
Modern causal inference methods: regression, IV, DiD, RDD. The "credibility revolution" in empirical economics.
**Key takeaway:** Correlation is not causation. Design your analysis to isolate causal effects. [theory/econometrics]

## Behavioral Science and Psychology

### 36. Predictably Irrational — Dan Ariely (2008)
*Ariely, D. (2008). Predictably Irrational. Harper Collins.*
Systematic cognitive biases affecting economic decisions: anchoring, the power of free, social norms vs. market norms.
**Key takeaway:** People are predictably irrational in ways that affect pricing, marketing, and product design. [Module 02]

### 37. Nudge — Thaler & Sunstein (2008)
*Thaler, R. H., & Sunstein, C. R. (2008). Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness. Yale University Press.*
Choice architecture and libertarian paternalism. How the design of choices affects behavior.
**Key takeaway:** Default options, framing, and choice architecture are powerful tools for behavior change. [Module 07]

### 38. Superforecasting — Philip Tetlock (2015)
*Tetlock, P. E., & Gardner, D. (2015). Superforecasting: The Art and Science of Prediction. Crown.*
What makes some forecasters dramatically better: probabilistic thinking, updating beliefs, intellectual humility.
**Key takeaway:** Good forecasting is a skill that can be learned through calibration and deliberate practice. [Module 02]

## Technology and Innovation

### 39. The Lean Product Playbook — Dan Olsen (2015)
*Olsen, D. (2015). The Lean Product Playbook. Wiley.*
Step-by-step process for achieving product-market fit. Target customer, underserved needs, value proposition, feature set, UX, MVP.
**Key takeaway:** Product-market fit is a systematic, repeatable process. [Module 11]

### 40. Platform Revolution — Parker, Van Alstyne & Choudary (2016)
*Parker, G. G., Van Alstyne, M. W., & Choudary, S. P. (2016). Platform Revolution. W. W. Norton.*
How platform businesses transform industries. Network effects, platform governance, launch strategies.
**Key takeaway:** Platforms beat pipelines by enabling and governing value-creating interactions. [Module 06]

## Finance Theory (Academic)

### 41. Asset Pricing — John Cochrane (2005)
*Cochrane, J. H. (2005). Asset Pricing (Revised ed.). Princeton University Press.*
Unified framework for asset pricing based on the stochastic discount factor. CAPM, factor models, consumption-based models.
**Key takeaway:** All of asset pricing reduces to one equation: price = E[mx]. [theory/finance_theory]

### 42. The Theory of Corporate Finance — Jean Tirole (2006)
*Tirole, J. (2006). The Theory of Corporate Finance. Princeton University Press.*
Contract-theoretic approach to corporate finance. Moral hazard, adverse selection, corporate governance, capital structure.
**Key takeaway:** Corporate finance is fundamentally about contracts under asymmetric information. [theory/finance_theory]

## Additional Essential Reads

### 43. Antifragile — Nassim Nicholas Taleb (2012)
Systems that gain from disorder. Build optionality, embrace uncertainty, avoid fragility. [Module 01]

### 44. The Strategy of Conflict — Thomas Schelling (1960)
Foundational game theory applied to real-world strategy. Focal points, commitment, deterrence. [Module 10, theory]

### 45. The Alchemy of Finance — George Soros (1987)
Reflexivity in financial markets. Beliefs affect fundamentals, creating feedback loops. [Module 01]

### 46. Only the Paranoid Survive — Andy Grove (1996)
Strategic inflection points and how to navigate technological discontinuities. [Module 13]

### 47. The Mythical Man-Month — Fred Brooks (1975)
Adding manpower to a late project makes it later. Communication overhead scales quadratically. [Module 08]

### 48. Measure What Matters — John Doerr (2018)
OKR (Objectives and Key Results) framework for goal-setting and execution. [Module 08]

### 49. Built to Last — Collins & Porras (1994)
Visionary companies preserve core ideology while stimulating progress. Big Hairy Audacious Goals (BHAGs). [Module 06]

### 50. The Outsiders — William Thorndike (2012)
Eight unconventional CEOs who outperformed by focusing on capital allocation rather than operations. [Module 05]

## Reading Recommendations by Role

### For Founders
Must-read first 5: The Lean Startup, Zero to One, The Hard Thing About Hard Things, High Output Management, Venture Deals

### For Product Managers
Must-read first 5: Inspired, Competing Against Luck, Crossing the Chasm, Continuous Discovery Habits, The Lean Product Playbook

### For Finance/Strategy Roles
Must-read first 5: Competitive Strategy, Valuation (McKinsey), The Intelligent Investor, 7 Powers, Playing to Win

### For Operations Leaders
Must-read first 5: The Goal, High Output Management, Lean Thinking, Measure What Matters, The Toyota Way
