## 1. Situational Leadership

*Citation: Hersey, P., & Blanchard, K. H. (1969). Life cycle theory of leadership. Training & Development Journal, 23(5), 26-34.*

### 1.1 The Model

Leadership style should match the maturity/development level of the follower:

| Follower Development | Leader Style | Behavior |
|---------------------|-------------|----------|
| D1: Low competence, high commitment | S1: Directing | High task, low relationship |
| D2: Some competence, low commitment | S2: Coaching | High task, high relationship |
| D3: Moderate-high competence, variable commitment | S3: Supporting | Low task, high relationship |
| D4: High competence, high commitment | S4: Delegating | Low task, low relationship |

### 1.2 Application

A new hire (D1) needs clear direction and structure. An experienced, committed team member (D4) needs autonomy and minimal oversight. Applying the wrong style — micromanaging experts or giving autonomy to novices — creates frustration and poor performance.

## 2. Transformational Leadership

*Citation: Burns, J. M. (1978). Leadership. Harper & Row.*
*Citation: Bass, B. M. (1985). Leadership and Performance Beyond Expectations. Free Press.*

### 2.1 Four Components (Bass's Model)

**Idealized influence:** Leader serves as a role model, demonstrating high ethical standards and commitment.

**Inspirational motivation:** Leader articulates a compelling vision that inspires and motivates followers.

**Intellectual stimulation:** Leader encourages innovation, questions assumptions, and reframes problems.

**Individualized consideration:** Leader attends to each follower's needs, acting as mentor and coach.

### 2.2 Transformational vs. Transactional

| Dimension | Transactional | Transformational |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Basis | Exchange (reward for performance) | Inspiration (intrinsic motivation) |
| Focus | Short-term results | Long-term vision and growth |
| Mechanism | Contingent rewards, management by exception | Vision, role modeling, intellectual challenge |
| Follower response | Compliance | Commitment |

### 2.3 Evidence

Meta-analyses show transformational leadership is positively correlated with follower satisfaction, motivation, and performance (Judge & Piccolo, 2004). Effect sizes are moderate but consistent across cultures and contexts.

*Citation: Judge, T. A., & Piccolo, R. F. (2004). Transformational and transactional leadership: A meta-analytic test of their relative validity. Journal of Applied Psychology, 89(5), 755-768.*

## 3. Servant Leadership

*Citation: Greenleaf, R. K. (1970). The Servant as Leader. Robert K. Greenleaf Center.*

### 3.1 Core Principle

The servant-leader's primary motivation is to serve others. Leadership is a means of service, not an end in itself. The test: "Do those served grow as persons? Do they, while being served, become healthier, wiser, freer, more autonomous?"

### 3.2 Key Practices

1. **Listening** — Deep, active listening as the foundation of all other practices
2. **Empathy** — Understanding others' perspectives and experiences
3. **Healing** — Supporting wholeness and well-being
4. **Awareness** — Self-awareness and organizational awareness
5. **Persuasion** — Influencing through dialogue, not positional authority
6. **Conceptualization** — Thinking beyond day-to-day operations
7. **Foresight** — Anticipating likely outcomes and consequences
8. **Stewardship** — Holding the organization in trust for the greater good
9. **Commitment to growth** — Investing in each person's development
10. **Building community** — Creating belonging and shared purpose

## 4. Level 5 Leadership

*Citation: Collins, J. (2001). Good to Great. Harper Business.*

### 4.1 The Hierarchy

Collins identified five levels of leadership capability:

```
Level 5: Executive        — Builds enduring greatness through humility + will
Level 4: Effective Leader — Catalyzes commitment to a compelling vision
Level 3: Competent Manager — Organizes people and resources efficiently
Level 2: Contributing Team Member — Contributes to team objectives
Level 1: Highly Capable Individual — Productive through talent and knowledge
```

### 4.2 Level 5 Paradox

Level 5 leaders combine:
- **Personal humility:** Modest, self-effacing, deflects credit to others
- **Professional will:** Fierce resolve, unwavering commitment to results

They attribute success to their team and external factors, but take personal responsibility for failures.

### 4.3 Critique

Collins's methodology (matched-pair comparative analysis of companies) has been criticized for survivorship bias and post-hoc rationalization. The correlation between Level 5 traits and outcomes may not be causal. Nevertheless, the framework provides useful aspirational guidance.

## 5. Psychological Safety

*Citation: Edmondson, A. C. (1999). Psychological safety and learning behavior in work teams. Administrative Science Quarterly, 44(2), 350-383.*

### 5.1 Definition

Psychological safety is the shared belief that the team is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes.

### 5.2 Why It Matters

Google's Project Aristotle found psychological safety was the single most important factor distinguishing high-performing teams from low-performing ones — more important than dependability, structure, meaning, or impact.

### 5.3 Building Psychological Safety

| Practice | What It Looks Like |
|----------|-------------------|
| Frame work as learning | "We're trying something new; mistakes are expected" |
| Acknowledge fallibility | "I may miss something — I need your input" |
| Model curiosity | Ask questions rather than making statements |
| Respond productively to bad news | Thank people who surface problems early |
| Separate people from problems | Critique ideas, not individuals |
| Normalize failure | Share own mistakes openly; conduct blameless postmortems |

### 5.4 Psychological Safety is Not

- Comfort (it often involves productive discomfort)
- Permission to avoid accountability
- An absence of standards or expectations
- Unconditional agreement with the leader

High psychological safety + high accountability = the learning zone.

## 6. Motivation Theory

### 6.1 Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

*Citation: Herzberg, F. (1959). The Motivation to Work. Wiley.*

**Hygiene factors** (prevent dissatisfaction but don't motivate):
- Salary, job security, working conditions, company policies, supervision, relationships

**Motivators** (create satisfaction and motivation):
- Achievement, recognition, work itself, responsibility, advancement, growth

**Implication:** Fixing hygiene factors (e.g., raising salary) removes dissatisfaction but doesn't create motivation. To motivate, provide meaningful work, recognition, and growth opportunities.

### 6.2 Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

*Citation: Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (1985). Intrinsic Motivation and Self-Determination in Human Behavior. Springer.*

Three innate psychological needs drive intrinsic motivation:

1. **Autonomy:** The desire to be the causal agent in one's own life — to have choice and control
2. **Competence:** The desire to master one's environment — to feel effective and capable
3. **Relatedness:** The desire to connect with others — to belong and feel valued

**When these needs are satisfied:** Intrinsic motivation, creativity, and well-being increase.
**When these needs are thwarted:** Motivation decreases, performance declines, turnover increases.

### 6.3 Designing Motivating Work

| SDT Need | Job Design Practices |
|----------|---------------------|
| Autonomy | Choice in how, when, where to work; ownership of outcomes |
| Competence | Challenging but achievable goals; feedback; skill development |
| Relatedness | Team membership; shared purpose; social connection |

### 6.4 Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Motivation

**Extrinsic:** Behavior driven by external rewards or punishments (bonuses, promotions, fear of termination).

**Intrinsic:** Behavior driven by internal satisfaction from the activity itself (interest, mastery, purpose).

**The overjustification effect** (Deci, 1971): Adding extrinsic rewards to already-intrinsically-motivated activities can reduce intrinsic motivation. Paying people for what they love to do can make them love it less.

**Practical balance:** Use extrinsic rewards for tasks that are inherently uninteresting. Protect intrinsic motivation for creative, complex, meaningful work.

## 7. Integration: Leadership for Operators

### 7.1 Situational Application

| Situation | Theory | Application |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| New team member | Situational (S1/S2) | More directive, more coaching |
| Expert team | Situational (S3/S4) | Delegate, support |
| Culture building | Transformational | Vision, values, role modeling |
| Day-to-day execution | Transactional | Clear goals, feedback, rewards |
| Innovation challenges | Psychological safety | Create space for risk-taking |
| Scaling leadership | Level 5 | Humility + fierce resolve |
| Retention | SDT | Autonomy, competence, relatedness |
