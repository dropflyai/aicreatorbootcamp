# Scaling Operations — Frameworks for Growth Without Breaking

## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**
- When and how to add process vs. maintain judgment-based decisions
- How to identify and manage organizational debt
- When to automate vs. keep human decision-making
- How to build quality management systems that scale

---

## 1. Scaling Frameworks

### 1.1 The Greiner Growth Model

*Citation: Greiner, L. E. (1972). Evolution and revolution as organizations grow. Harvard Business Review, 50(4), 37-46.*

Organizations grow through alternating phases of evolution (smooth growth) and revolution (crisis):

```
Phase 1: Growth through Creativity    -> Crisis of Leadership
Phase 2: Growth through Direction     -> Crisis of Autonomy
Phase 3: Growth through Delegation    -> Crisis of Control
Phase 4: Growth through Coordination  -> Crisis of Red Tape
Phase 5: Growth through Collaboration -> Crisis of Internal Growth
```

Each phase requires different management approaches. Solutions that enable one phase create the crisis of the next.

### 1.2 The Three Horizons of Scaling

| Horizon | Focus | Operational Mode |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| H1: Survive (0-10 customers) | Find product-market fit | Everything is manual, founders do everything |
| H2: Stabilize (10-100 customers) | Repeatability | Document processes, hire specialists, basic tooling |
| H3: Scale (100-1000+ customers) | Efficiency and leverage | Automate, build systems, optimize |

## 2. Organizational Debt

### 2.1 Definition

Organizational debt is the accumulation of suboptimal structures, processes, and cultural patterns that were expedient in the short term but create drag at scale.

**Types of organizational debt:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| Process debt | Missing or ad-hoc processes | No onboarding process; every new hire has different experience |
| Structural debt | Wrong org structure for current needs | Still organized by function when product teams are needed |
| Cultural debt | Norms that don't scale | "Everyone has access to everything" at 200 people |
| People debt | Wrong people in wrong roles | Early generalists in specialist roles |
| Technical debt | Shortcuts in systems | Spreadsheets replacing proper tools |

### 2.2 Managing Organizational Debt

1. **Audit regularly:** At each doubling of headcount, review structures, processes, and tools
2. **Pay down proactively:** Don't wait for crisis; allocate 10-20% of leadership time to org health
3. **Accept some debt strategically:** Not all debt is bad — speed matters in early stages
4. **Distinguish reversible from irreversible:** Cultural debt compounds and is hard to reverse; process debt is relatively easy to fix

## 3. Process vs. Judgment Decisions

### 3.1 Classification Framework

| Characteristic | Process Decision | Judgment Decision |
|---------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Frequency | High (daily/weekly) | Low (monthly/quarterly) |
| Complexity | Low to moderate | High |
| Stakes | Low to moderate | High |
| Information | Structured, available | Ambiguous, incomplete |
| Reversibility | High | Low |

### 3.2 Decision Governance

```
                  Low Stakes          High Stakes
High Frequency    Automate            Standardize with exceptions
Low Frequency     Delegate            Executive judgment
```

**Automate:** Rules-based, no human intervention needed. Examples: invoice processing, routine approvals, status updates.

**Standardize with exceptions:** Clear process for the 80% case, escalation path for exceptions. Examples: hiring process, pricing approvals, customer escalations.

**Delegate:** Empower individuals to decide within guidelines. Examples: small expenditures, feature prioritization within a sprint.

**Executive judgment:** Requires senior leadership input. Examples: entering a new market, major partnerships, organizational restructuring.

## 4. Automation vs. Human Decision Points

### 4.1 Automation Decision Matrix

```
                      Low Cognitive Complexity    High Cognitive Complexity
High Volume           AUTOMATE                    AUGMENT (AI-assist)
Low Volume            STANDARDIZE                 KEEP HUMAN
```

### 4.2 The Automation Ladder

1. **Document** the process (write it down)
2. **Standardize** the process (make it consistent)
3. **Measure** the process (track performance)
4. **Template** the process (reduce setup time)
5. **Partially automate** (automate routine steps, keep human judgment for complex steps)
6. **Fully automate** (no human intervention for the standard path)
7. **Self-optimize** (system improves itself based on outcomes)

### 4.3 When NOT to Automate

- When the process is still evolving (automate too early and you freeze a bad process)
- When edge cases are common and require nuanced judgment
- When the cost of errors is very high
- When human relationships are critical (sales, customer success)
- When you don't understand the process well enough to codify it

## 5. Quality Management Systems

### 5.1 The Quality-Speed Tradeoff (and When It's False)

Common belief: higher quality requires more time. This is often false.

**Where quality and speed align:**
- Investing in prevention (code reviews, testing) reduces rework time
- Clear requirements reduce iterations
- Good tools reduce cycle time AND improve quality

**Where the tradeoff is real:**
- Additional testing/validation adds time
- More reviews add approval time
- Higher-quality materials cost more

### 5.2 Quality Framework for Scaling

| Stage | Quality Approach |
|-------|-----------------|
| Pre-PMF (0-10 customers) | Quality = speed of learning. Ship fast, talk to users, iterate. |
| Early growth (10-100) | Introduce basic quality gates: code review, customer feedback loops |
| Scaling (100-1K) | Formal QA, automated testing, SLAs, incident management |
| At scale (1K+) | ISO/SOC frameworks, audit trails, continuous monitoring |

### 5.3 Continuous Improvement

**Kaizen (continuous improvement):**
Small, incremental improvements made continuously by everyone in the organization.

**PDCA cycle (Deming):**
Plan -> Do -> Check -> Act -> (repeat)

1. **Plan:** Identify improvement opportunity, set hypothesis
2. **Do:** Implement small-scale change
3. **Check:** Measure results against hypothesis
4. **Act:** If improvement confirmed, standardize; if not, analyze and try again
