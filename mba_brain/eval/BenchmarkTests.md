# MBA Benchmark Tests -- 20 PhD-Level Business and Strategy Questions

Diagnostic assessment to evaluate strategic thinking depth. These questions test beyond textbook knowledge into applied judgment, synthesis, and second-order reasoning. Use these to calibrate MBA Brain output quality.

---

## How to Use

1. Answer each question in 300-500 words.
2. Compare against the expected answer framework (not exact wording).
3. Score each answer 1-5 using the rubric at the bottom.
4. Calculate total score. Minimum passing: 70/100 (average 3.5/question).
5. Questions scoring below 3 indicate areas needing development.

## Strategy and Competitive Advantage

### Q1: Moat Durability Analysis

**Question:** Amazon's competitive moat includes scale economies, logistics infrastructure, Prime membership lock-in, AWS, and marketplace network effects. Rank these moat components by durability (which will last longest?) and explain your reasoning. Which moat component is most vulnerable and what would threaten it?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Network effects (marketplace) and switching costs (Prime + ecosystem) are most durable because they compound with scale and are self-reinforcing.
- AWS has a strong moat but is vulnerable to multi-cloud strategies and commodity pricing.
- Logistics infrastructure is durable but requires massive ongoing capex and is replicable by well-funded competitors (Walmart, Shopify logistics).
- Scale economies alone are the weakest moat: they can be matched by competitors who achieve sufficient scale.
- The most vulnerable is AWS because enterprise cloud is increasingly multi-cloud, pricing pressure from GCP/Azure is persistent, and AI workloads may shift the competitive landscape.
- Should discuss feedback loops between moat components (Prime drives marketplace volume drives logistics efficiency drives Prime value).

### Q2: Strategic Inflection Points

**Question:** Andy Grove described strategic inflection points as "10X changes" that fundamentally alter the competitive landscape. Identify a current (2024-2026) strategic inflection point in a specific industry. Analyze: (a) what is the 10X force, (b) who are the winners and losers, (c) what should incumbents do, and (d) what should new entrants do.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Should identify a genuine inflection point (AI in knowledge work, AI in software development, GLP-1 drugs in healthcare/food, etc.) not a hype cycle.
- 10X force must be quantified or evidenced (cost reduction, capability expansion, behavior change).
- Winner/loser analysis must include non-obvious second-order effects (not just "AI companies win").
- Incumbent strategy must account for the Innovator's Dilemma (cannibalization fear, existing customer lock-in, organizational inertia).
- New entrant strategy must identify the wedge (underserved segment, new job-to-be-done, fundamentally different cost structure).
- Bonus: discusses timing -- too early is indistinguishable from wrong.

### Q3: Platform Strategy Tradeoffs

**Question:** You are building a two-sided marketplace (e.g., connecting service providers with consumers). You face the chicken-and-egg problem. Design a launch strategy that solves the cold start problem. Then explain: at what point does the marketplace become self-sustaining, and what metrics tell you that you have crossed that threshold?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Must choose a side to subsidize first and explain why (usually supply side, but depends on context).
- Should discuss single-player mode (value for one side even without the other).
- Launch strategy should be narrow: one geography, one vertical, one use case.
- Should reference liquidity (enough supply that demand is satisfied quickly enough to retain).
- Self-sustaining threshold: organic supply growth exceeds churn, organic demand growth exceeds churn, take rate supports CAC.
- Metrics: match rate, time-to-match, repeat usage rate, organic supply sign-ups, NPS by cohort.
- Should discuss the risk of disintermediation and how to prevent it.

## Finance and Valuation

### Q4: Capital Allocation Under Uncertainty

**Question:** You are CEO of a profitable SaaS company ($20M ARR, 25% growth, 15% FCF margin). You have $10M in cash. Three options: (A) invest in R&D to build an AI product (uncertain payoff, 18-month timeline), (B) acquire a smaller competitor ($8M price, $3M ARR, 10% growth), (C) return cash to shareholders via buyback. Analyze each option with a framework and recommend one.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Should apply a capital allocation framework (hurdle rate, IRR comparison, risk-adjusted returns).
- Option A: high uncertainty, high potential upside. Should estimate scenarios with probabilities. Key risk is execution and market timing.
- Option B: quantifiable return. Should calculate implied valuation multiple, accretion/dilution, integration risk, strategic fit. $8M for $3M ARR = ~2.7x revenue multiple -- reasonable for 10% growth SaaS.
- Option C: signals lack of investment opportunities. Only appropriate if growth is mature and reinvestment ROI is below cost of capital.
- Recommendation should depend on stated assumptions about AI product TAM, execution capability, and competitive dynamics.
- Must discuss optionality value (Option A creates optionality, Option B creates scale, Option C creates nothing).
- Best answers use Kelly Criterion thinking or expected value with downside protection.

### Q5: Unit Economics Diagnosis

**Question:** A D2C e-commerce brand has: AOV $85, gross margin 60%, CAC $45, repeat purchase rate 2.3x/year, average customer lifespan 2.1 years, monthly churn 4%. They are burning $200K/month and have 8 months of runway. Diagnose the health of this business. What would you fix first and why?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Calculate LTV: AOV ($85) x Gross Margin (60%) x Purchases/Year (2.3) x Lifespan (2.1) = ~$246
- LTV:CAC = $246:$45 = 5.5:1 (healthy)
- Payback period: $45 / ($85 x 0.60 x 2.3/12) = ~4.7 months (acceptable)
- Monthly churn 4% implies average lifespan of 25 months -- but stated lifespan is 2.1 years (25.2 months). Consistent.
- If unit economics are healthy (LTV:CAC 5.5:1), burn is likely from fixed costs, marketing spend scaling, or inventory.
- First diagnosis: is CAC increasing? Are they scaling acquisition into less efficient channels?
- Fix first: with 8 months of runway, focus on reaching cash flow breakeven. Cut unprofitable channels. Improve contribution margin. Consider fundraising or credit facility.
- Must address the tension between growth and burn.

### Q6: Fundraising Strategy

**Question:** A Series A startup ($2M ARR, 120% NRR, 15% month-over-month growth) is approached by two VCs: (A) offers $10M at $40M pre-money with 1x non-participating preferred and a board seat, (B) offers $15M at $50M pre-money with 1x participating preferred, 2x liquidation preference, and two board seats. Which offer should the founder take and why?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Offer A: $10M for 20% dilution. Clean terms (non-participating, single liquidation preference, one board seat).
- Offer B: $15M for 23% dilution. Dirty terms (participating preferred means double-dipping, 2x liquidation preference means first $30M goes to investors, two board seats gives investors board control with 3 seats).
- Must calculate payout scenarios at different exit values ($50M, $100M, $200M, $500M) to show how participating preferred and 2x liquidation preference dramatically change founder economics.
- At $100M exit: Offer A founder gets ~$80M; Offer B founder gets significantly less due to 2x liquidation ($30M) + participation.
- Offer A is almost always better unless the company desperately needs the extra $5M to survive.
- Board dynamics: two board seats from one investor creates governance risk.
- Best answers discuss signaling effects, relationship quality, and value-add beyond capital.

## Operations and Execution

### Q7: Scaling Bottleneck Analysis

**Question:** A SaaS startup has grown from 100 to 1,000 customers in 12 months. Customer support tickets are up 15x (not 10x). Engineering velocity has dropped 40%. Employee NPS went from +60 to +15. Revenue grew 8x. Diagnose what is happening and design a 90-day plan to stabilize.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Support growing 15x vs. 10x customer growth = support load per customer is increasing. This means onboarding is poor, product UX has issues, or documentation is inadequate. Not just a hiring problem.
- Engineering velocity dropping = technical debt, context switching, interrupt-driven work (likely pulled into support), or organizational scaling problems (too many meetings, unclear priorities).
- Employee NPS collapse = burnout, unclear direction, broken processes, or culture dilution from rapid hiring.
- Revenue 8x with 10x customers = ARPU decreased by ~20%. Possible downmarket drift or discounting.
- 90-day plan should address: (1) immediate support triage (categorize tickets, identify top 5 root causes, fix product issues causing tickets), (2) engineering focus (protect engineering time, reduce context switching, establish sprint discipline), (3) people (skip-level 1:1s to understand concerns, address burnout, clarify priorities), (4) process (implement lightweight operating cadence, weekly metrics review, quarterly planning).
- Must prioritize: you cannot fix everything at once. Stop the bleeding first.

### Q8: Process Design for Quality

**Question:** Design a quality assurance process for a financial services startup that processes 50,000 transactions daily. The process must catch errors before they affect customers, scale with volume, and not slow down transaction processing below 200ms SLA. Consider both automated and human-in-the-loop elements.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Tiered approach: automated rules engine for known patterns (real-time), ML anomaly detection for unknown patterns (near-real-time), human review for flagged transactions (async).
- Real-time layer: rule-based validation (field format, amount limits, duplicate detection, velocity checks). Must execute in < 50ms to leave headroom for SLA.
- Near-real-time layer: ML model scoring for fraud/error probability. Transactions above threshold are flagged but not blocked. Executed async within 5 minutes.
- Human review: queue of flagged transactions prioritized by risk score and dollar amount. SLA for human review (4 hours for high-value, 24 hours for low).
- Feedback loop: human review decisions feed back into ML model and rules engine. False positive rate tracked and minimized.
- Monitoring: real-time dashboard showing error rates, flag rates, human review backlog, SLA compliance.
- Scaling: automated layers scale horizontally. Human review scales with hiring and tooling efficiency.
- Must address regulatory requirements (SOX, PCI-DSS if applicable).

## Marketing and Growth

### Q9: Growth Loop Design

**Question:** Design a growth loop (not a funnel) for a B2B SaaS product that provides project management for agencies. The loop must be self-reinforcing, measurable, and create compounding returns. Explain the mechanics, identify the key metric at each step, and describe what would break the loop.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Growth loop (not funnel): output of one cycle becomes input of next cycle.
- Example loop: Agency uses tool -> invites client to shared workspace -> client sees value -> client's other agencies are invited -> new agency adopts tool.
- Each step has a conversion metric: invite rate, client activation rate, cross-pollination rate, new agency activation rate.
- Compounding mechanism: each new agency brings N clients, each client works with M agencies, creating MxN expansion.
- Key metric: viral coefficient (K-factor). K > 1 means self-sustaining growth.
- What breaks the loop: poor onboarding (clients don't engage with workspace), privacy concerns (clients don't want agencies to see each other), competitive tools already embedded at client organizations.
- Must distinguish between viral growth (users invite users) and word-of-mouth (users tell people who independently sign up).

### Q10: Pricing Strategy

**Question:** You have a developer tool SaaS with three customer segments: individual developers (50K potential users, WTP $0-20/mo), startups (10K potential companies, WTP $50-200/mo), and enterprise (500 potential companies, WTP $500-5000/mo). Design a pricing strategy including packaging, feature gating, and pricing model. Justify your decisions.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Three-tier pricing aligned with segments: Free/Developer, Team/Startup, Enterprise.
- Free tier: generous enough to be useful (not crippled), creates habit and switching costs. Gated by usage (seats, projects, API calls) not features.
- Team tier: $49-99/mo per workspace. Unlocks collaboration features (the natural upgrade trigger). Usage-based component for compute/API.
- Enterprise: custom pricing ($500-5000/mo). Unlocks SSO, audit logs, SLA, dedicated support, compliance features.
- Pricing model: per-seat with usage component (predictable base + upside from expansion).
- Must discuss: why not pure usage-based (unpredictable revenue), why not flat rate (leaves money on table for enterprise), reverse trial vs. freemium.
- Should calculate potential revenue: Free (50K x $0 + conversion rate to paid), Team (10K x conversion rate x ~$75 ARPU), Enterprise (500 x win rate x ~$2000 ARPU).
- Feature gating philosophy: gate on collaboration and compliance, not on core functionality.

## Leadership and Organization

### Q11: Organizational Design

**Question:** A 40-person startup is reorganizing from functional teams (engineering, design, sales, marketing) to cross-functional product teams. Design the new org structure, define team composition, explain the tradeoffs vs. functional structure, and describe how to manage the transition without losing productivity.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Cross-functional pods: each team has engineering, design, product, and optionally data. Each pod owns a product area end-to-end.
- Shared services: platform engineering, design systems, and infrastructure remain centralized to avoid duplication.
- Pod size: 5-8 people (two-pizza team). At 40 people: 4-5 product pods + shared services.
- Tradeoffs: gains (ownership, speed, customer focus, reduced handoffs). Losses (specialization depth, knowledge sharing across functions, potential duplication, career path clarity for specialists).
- Transition plan: (1) define pods and missions, (2) let people express preferences, (3) ensure each pod has senior leadership, (4) run functional guilds for knowledge sharing (weekly engineering guild, design guild), (5) phase transition over 4-6 weeks with parallel operation period.
- Must address: how do specialists (e.g., only 2 designers for 5 pods) get allocated? Matrix reporting with functional manager for career development and pod lead for daily work.
- Risks: pods become silos, inconsistent quality across pods, specialists feel isolated.

### Q12: Crisis Communication

**Question:** Your startup's database was breached. 50,000 users' email addresses and hashed passwords were exposed (no payment data). You have confirmed the breach 2 hours ago. The vulnerability is patched. Write a crisis communication plan: who do you tell, in what order, what do you say, and what do you NOT say? Include the actual customer notification email.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Order: (1) legal counsel, (2) board/investors, (3) employees, (4) affected users, (5) regulators (if required by jurisdiction), (6) media (if story breaks). All within 24-72 hours depending on jurisdiction.
- Customer email must include: what happened (factual, not speculative), what data was affected, what was NOT affected (payment data safe), what you are doing about it, what they should do (change passwords), how to contact you.
- What NOT to say: do not minimize ("minor incident"), do not speculate on attacker identity, do not make promises you cannot keep ("this will never happen again"), do not blame third parties.
- Tone: honest, direct, accountable, not panicked. Take responsibility.
- Must address: regulatory notification requirements (GDPR 72-hour rule, state breach notification laws), credit monitoring offer (probably not needed since no financial data), password reset force (yes, force all affected users to reset).
- Internal communication: all-hands addressing what happened, what the company is doing, and FAQ for employees who will get questions from customers.
- Post-crisis: publish transparency report within 30 days, detail security improvements made.

## Analytics and Decision-Making

### Q13: Metric Design and Goodhart's Law

**Question:** A sales team is incentivized on number of deals closed per quarter. Describe five specific ways this metric will be gamed (Goodhart's Law), and design a better incentive system that aligns individual salesperson behavior with company goals. Your system must be simple enough to explain in 2 minutes.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Gaming mechanisms: (1) closing small/easy deals instead of strategic accounts, (2) offering excessive discounts to close faster, (3) pulling forward deals from next quarter (sandbagging/hockey stick), (4) overselling features to close (creating churn), (5) cherry-picking leads and hoarding pipeline.
- Better system design: balanced scorecard approach. Example: 60% revenue (not deals), 20% net revenue retention (accounts closed 12+ months ago still paying), 20% pipeline quality (deal size relative to ICP, accurate forecasting).
- Must be simple: "You are paid on revenue you close, health of accounts you have closed, and quality of your pipeline."
- Should discuss: why not just revenue (encourages discounting for large deals), why include retention (aligns with customer success), why pipeline quality (prevents sandbagging and gaming).
- Quarterly vs. annual: blend both. Quarterly accelerators for hitting targets, annual component for retention.
- Must address: what happens when you change incentives (expect pushback, grandfather existing deals, communicate why change is happening).

### Q14: Experiment Design

**Question:** Your product team believes changing the onboarding flow from a 7-step wizard to a 3-step minimal flow will increase activation rate (defined as completing first core action within 7 days). Design a rigorous experiment to test this hypothesis. Include: hypothesis, success metrics, guardrail metrics, sample size calculation approach, duration, and decision criteria.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Hypothesis: "Reducing onboarding from 7 steps to 3 steps will increase 7-day activation rate by at least 5 percentage points (from current 35% to 40%)."
- Primary metric: 7-day activation rate.
- Secondary metrics: time to first core action, onboarding completion rate, 30-day retention.
- Guardrail metrics (must not degrade): 30-day retention, support ticket rate, feature adoption breadth (ensuring users don't miss important setup).
- Sample size: based on MDE (minimum detectable effect), current conversion rate, desired power (80%), significance level (5%). For 35% base rate and 5pp MDE, approximately 1,500 users per group.
- Duration: enough to collect required sample size + at least 1 full week to account for day-of-week effects. Likely 2-4 weeks depending on signup volume.
- Randomization: user-level, not session-level. Consistent experience per user.
- Decision criteria: statistically significant improvement in activation (p < 0.05) AND no statistically significant degradation in guardrail metrics AND practical significance (effect size is worth the engineering effort).
- Must address: novelty effects (new flow gets attention because it is new), learning effects, and potential segment differences (new users vs. returning users who abandoned previous onboarding).

### Q15: Causal Inference

**Question:** Your data shows that customers who use your mobile app have 3x higher retention than customers who only use the web app. The product team wants to invest $2M in making mobile the primary experience and pushing all users to mobile. What questions would you ask before approving this investment? What analyses would you run to determine if the relationship is causal?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Core issue: correlation vs. causation. Mobile users may be more engaged BECAUSE they are more committed customers, not because mobile makes them more committed.
- Selection bias: users who download a mobile app are already more invested. They would likely have high retention regardless of platform.
- Questions: What is the retention of users who have both mobile and web vs. web only? What happens to retention when web-only users adopt mobile (before/after analysis)? What are the demographics/segments of mobile vs. web users? Is mobile retention high because of push notifications (an engagement feature, not the app itself)?
- Causal analysis approaches: (1) natural experiment -- did any event cause a sudden increase in mobile adoption that was not correlated with user quality? (2) Instrumental variable -- find something that affects mobile adoption but not retention directly (e.g., app store featuring). (3) Propensity score matching -- match mobile and web users on observable characteristics and compare retention. (4) RCT -- randomly prompt a subset of web users to download mobile and measure retention change.
- Must discuss: even if mobile does cause better retention, is $2M the right investment? What is the expected retention improvement, and does it justify the cost? What is the opportunity cost?

## Entrepreneurship and Ventures

### Q16: Market Entry Timing

**Question:** You are evaluating whether to enter the "AI for legal document review" market in 2025. The market has: 5 funded startups (largest raised Series B), no dominant player yet, large incumbents (Thomson Reuters, LexisNexis) have announced AI features, law firms are conservative adopters. Analyze the timing. Is it too early, too late, or just right? What would make you change your answer?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Framework for timing: technology readiness, market readiness (customer willingness), competitive dynamics, regulatory environment.
- Arguments for "right time": LLM technology is proven for document tasks, no dominant player yet (market share is up for grabs), incumbent AI features are likely shallow (bolt-on, not native).
- Arguments for "too early": law firms are conservative (long sales cycles, procurement complexity), regulatory uncertainty around AI in legal, hallucination risk in high-stakes legal work.
- Arguments for "too late": 5 funded startups means others have identified the opportunity. First mover advantage in enterprise is real (switching costs are high). Incumbents have distribution advantage.
- Assessment: likely the right time IF you have a differentiated wedge (specific practice area, specific firm size, specific document type). Wrong time for a generic "AI legal" platform.
- What changes the answer: (1) incumbent ships a genuinely good product (too late), (2) major legal AI error causes a lawsuit (too early -- market freezes), (3) regulation mandates AI disclosure in legal work (opportunity or threat depending on approach).

### Q17: Business Model Innovation

**Question:** Traditional gyms make money from memberships (high churn, overselling capacity, punishing usage). Peloton made money from hardware + subscription. Design a third business model for fitness that creates better alignment between the company's revenue and the customer's health outcomes. Explain the incentive structure and why it is superior.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Key insight: traditional gyms profit when customers do NOT use the service. This is fundamentally misaligned.
- A better model aligns revenue with usage and outcomes.
- Possible models: (1) Pay-per-outcome: insurance-subsidized fitness where health improvements (measurable via wearables) reduce insurance premiums and gym captures a share. (2) Employer-sponsored wellness with outcome-based pricing. (3) "Fitness as a service" with flexible per-session pricing + coaching, where the company earns more when you work out more. (4) Community-based model with social accountability and revenue share with successful members.
- Must analyze incentive alignment: does the company make MORE money when the customer gets healthier? If yes, interests are aligned.
- Must address practical challenges: measurement difficulty, selection bias (healthy people buy health products), privacy concerns with health data, regulatory issues.
- Superior because: (1) reduces churn (customers see results and stay), (2) enables premium pricing (paying for outcomes, not access), (3) creates word-of-mouth (results are visible), (4) enables insurance/employer subsidies (reduces CAC).

## Ethics and Governance

### Q18: Ethical Dilemma Analysis

**Question:** Your AI-powered hiring tool reduces time-to-hire by 60% and has been validated to not discriminate on race or gender in aggregate. However, investigation reveals it systematically disadvantages candidates from non-traditional backgrounds (bootcamp graduates, career changers, self-taught developers) because it was trained on data from successful hires who were predominantly CS graduates from top universities. What do you do?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- This is a proxy discrimination problem. The model is not discriminating on protected classes directly, but the proxy variables (education, career path) correlate with socioeconomic status, race, and age.
- Immediate action: flag the issue, do not hide it. Conduct impact analysis: how many candidates were disadvantaged? Were any rejected who would have been strong hires?
- Options: (1) retrain the model with more diverse training data and success metrics, (2) add manual review for candidates flagged as "low match" to catch false negatives, (3) redesign success criteria to include job performance outcomes not just hire-to-hire similarity, (4) remove or reduce weight of education/background features.
- Must discuss: the legal landscape (EEOC guidance on AI hiring, NYC Local Law 144), the business case for diverse hiring (innovation, market representation), and the difference between "aggregate fairness" and "subgroup fairness."
- Best answers discuss that "validated to not discriminate in aggregate" can mask subgroup discrimination, and that fairness metrics are contested (demographic parity vs. equal opportunity vs. calibration).
- Transparency: communicate findings to customers (employers using the tool), publish bias audit results, commit to ongoing monitoring.

### Q19: Stakeholder Conflict Resolution

**Question:** You are CEO. Your largest customer (30% of revenue) demands a feature that would require storing user data in a way that violates your privacy policy. Your VP of Sales says losing this customer would require layoffs. Your VP of Engineering says the architecture change would take 6 months and create security risks. Your VP of Legal says the privacy policy change could expose the company to regulatory fines. What is your decision framework and what do you decide?

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Framework: (1) identify all stakeholders and their interests, (2) assess reversibility (one-way door), (3) evaluate second-order effects, (4) apply ethical reasoning, (5) define decision criteria.
- Key insight: 30% revenue concentration is the real problem, not the feature request. This customer has too much leverage.
- Analysis: complying creates regulatory risk, security risk, and precedent (other customers will demand similar exceptions). Not complying risks 30% revenue loss and layoffs.
- Decision: do NOT comply with the request as stated. Instead: (1) explore alternative technical solutions that meet the customer's need without violating privacy policy, (2) begin immediate revenue diversification to reduce concentration, (3) be transparent with the customer about constraints and offer alternatives, (4) if customer insists, negotiate a contractual framework where the customer assumes liability for the data handling.
- Must address: the short-term pain (potential revenue loss) vs. long-term risk (regulatory fines, loss of trust, security breach). This is a one-way door if you change privacy policies.
- Best answers discuss customer concentration as a strategic risk and include a diversification plan.

### Q20: Long-Term Value Creation

**Question:** Contrast the 20-year strategic trajectories of Amazon and WeWork. Both pursued aggressive growth with negative margins. Why did Amazon's strategy create durable value while WeWork's did not? Extract three generalizable principles for when negative-margin growth is rational vs. irrational.

**Expected Answer Framework:**
- Amazon: negative margins were investing in infrastructure (fulfillment, AWS, Prime) that created increasing returns to scale. Each dollar invested reduced future marginal costs. Unit economics improved with scale. Flywheel effect: lower prices -> more customers -> more sellers -> more selection -> lower prices.
- WeWork: negative margins were subsidizing leases and growth that did NOT create increasing returns to scale. Each new location had similar cost structure to the last. No network effects (my use of WeWork does not make your experience better). Unit economics did not improve with scale -- they were structurally negative.
- Three principles:
  1. **Invest in decreasing marginal costs, not constant ones.** Amazon's logistics costs per unit decreased with volume. WeWork's lease costs per desk did not.
  2. **Negative margins must build durable assets or network effects.** Amazon built warehouses, technology, and customer habit. WeWork built... more leases (an obligation, not an asset).
  3. **Unit economics must have a credible path to positive at scale.** Amazon's contribution margin per order improved. WeWork's contribution margin per desk was structurally challenged by long-term lease obligations and premium buildout costs.
- Bonus: discuss the role of narrative vs. fundamentals. Both told growth stories, but Amazon's was backed by improving unit economics data while WeWork's required perpetual capital infusion.
- Additional principle: network effects test -- does each new customer/user/node make the product more valuable for existing users? Amazon marketplace: yes. WeWork: no.
