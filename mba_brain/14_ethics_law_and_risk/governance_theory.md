## 1. Agency Theory and Governance

### 1.1 The Separation of Ownership and Control

*Citation: Berle, A. A., & Means, G. C. (1932). The Modern Corporation and Private Property. Harcourt, Brace & World.*

In public corporations, ownership (shareholders) is separated from control (management). This creates the classic agency problem: managers may pursue their own interests at shareholders' expense.

### 1.2 Jensen-Meckling Agency Framework Applied to Governance

*Citation: Jensen, M. C., & Meckling, W. H. (1976). Theory of the firm. Journal of Financial Economics, 3(4), 305-360.*

**Governance mechanisms to align interests:**

| Mechanism | How It Works | Limitation |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| Board monitoring | Directors oversee management | Information asymmetry, capture |
| Executive compensation | Equity-based pay aligns with shareholder value | Can create short-termism |
| Market for corporate control | Hostile takeovers discipline bad management | Costly, disruptive |
| Debt covenants | Lenders impose financial discipline | May restrict value-creating investments |
| Auditing | External verification of financial statements | Costly, can be gamed |
| Shareholder activism | Large shareholders monitor directly | Free-rider problem among small shareholders |

### 1.3 Jensen's Free Cash Flow Theory

*Citation: Jensen, M. C. (1986). Agency costs of free cash flow, corporate finance, and takeovers. American Economic Review, 76(2), 323-329.*

Managers of firms with substantial free cash flow and limited growth opportunities are tempted to waste cash on empire-building (unprofitable acquisitions, excessive perks).

**Solution:** Debt forces managers to pay out cash as interest, reducing the discretionary resources available for value-destroying activity.

## 2. Corporate Governance Models

### 2.1 Anglo-American Model (Outsider System)

- Dispersed ownership
- Active capital markets
- Board as monitor of management
- Hostile takeovers as disciplining mechanism
- Focus on shareholder value

### 2.2 Continental European/Japanese Model (Insider System)

- Concentrated ownership (banks, families, cross-holdings)
- Relationship-based financing
- Board includes stakeholder representatives (employees in Germany)
- Fewer hostile takeovers
- Broader stakeholder orientation

## 3. Risk Governance

### 3.1 Enterprise Risk Management (ERM)

*Citation: Committee of Sponsoring Organizations (COSO). (2017). Enterprise Risk Management - Integrating with Strategy and Performance.*

**Risk categories:**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Strategic | Competitive disruption, market shifts, M&A failures |
| Operational | Process failures, supply chain disruption, IT outages |
| Financial | Liquidity crises, credit risk, currency fluctuations |
| Compliance | Regulatory violations, data breaches, legal liability |
| Reputational | Public relations crises, ethical failures, brand damage |

### 3.2 Risk Assessment Framework

```
Risk Score = Probability x Impact x (1 - Mitigation Effectiveness)
```

| Probability | Impact | Risk Level |
|------------|--------|------------|
| High (>50%) | High (existential) | Critical |
| High | Medium | High |
| Medium (10-50%) | High | High |
| Medium | Medium | Medium |
| Low (<10%) | High | Medium |
| Low | Medium | Low |
