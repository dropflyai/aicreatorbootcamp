# ESG Theory and Sustainable Business Economics

## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**
- How to evaluate ESG factors through an economic lens
- How sustainability relates to firm value
- How to avoid greenwashing while creating genuine impact

## 1. The Economic Case for ESG

### 1.1 Stakeholder Theory and ESG

*Citation: Freeman, R. E. (1984). Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach. Pitman.*

ESG factors represent stakeholder interests that affect long-term firm value:
- **Environmental:** Resource efficiency, emissions, waste (affects operating costs, regulatory risk)
- **Social:** Labor practices, community impact, diversity (affects talent, reputation, productivity)
- **Governance:** Board structure, executive compensation, transparency (affects agency costs, investor confidence)

### 1.2 ESG and Financial Performance

*Citation: Friede, G., Busch, T., & Bassen, A. (2015). ESG and financial performance: Aggregated evidence from more than 2000 empirical studies. Journal of Sustainable Finance & Investment, 5(4), 210-233.*

Meta-analysis of 2,200+ studies found:
- ~90% of studies show a non-negative relationship between ESG and financial performance
- ~60% show a positive relationship
- The relationship is strongest for governance factors and weakest for environmental factors
- Causality direction remains debated (do good firms do ESG, or does ESG make firms good?)

### 1.3 Channel Mechanisms

| ESG Factor | Value Creation Channel | Value Destruction if Ignored |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------------------|
| Carbon emissions | Regulatory compliance, energy cost reduction | Carbon taxes, stranded assets |
| Water usage | Operational continuity | Supply disruption, community opposition |
| Labor practices | Talent attraction, productivity | Turnover, lawsuits, reputation damage |
| Data privacy | Customer trust | Fines (GDPR: up to 4% of global revenue), brand damage |
| Board diversity | Better decision-making, reduced groupthink | Regulatory pressure, investor activism |
| Executive compensation | Alignment with long-term value | Short-termism, public backlash |

## 2. Carbon Economics

### 2.1 Social Cost of Carbon

The social cost of carbon (SCC) estimates the economic damage from emitting one additional ton of CO2:

```
SCC = Present value of future damages from 1 ton of CO2 emitted today
```

Estimates vary widely: $50-200+/ton CO2 (EPA, Stern Review, academic estimates).

### 2.2 Carbon Pricing Mechanisms

| Mechanism | How It Works | Operator Impact |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Carbon tax | Fixed price per ton of CO2 | Predictable cost; incentivizes reduction |
| Cap-and-trade | Government sets emission cap; firms trade permits | Market-determined price; uncertainty |
| Internal carbon price | Company self-imposes shadow price on emissions | Decision-making tool; not mandatory |
| Border carbon adjustment | Tariff on imports from countries without carbon pricing | Affects international supply chains |

### 2.3 Stranded Asset Risk

*Citation: Carbon Tracker Initiative. (2013). Unburnable Carbon.*

If global warming is limited to 2 degrees C, a significant portion of fossil fuel reserves cannot be burned — making them "stranded assets" with zero economic value. This risk extends to infrastructure, real estate in vulnerable areas, and businesses dependent on carbon-intensive supply chains.

## 3. Reporting Frameworks

### 3.1 Major Frameworks

| Framework | Focus | Audience |
|-----------|-------|----------|
| GRI (Global Reporting Initiative) | Broad sustainability impacts | All stakeholders |
| SASB (now ISSB) | Financially material sustainability factors | Investors |
| TCFD | Climate-related financial risks | Investors, regulators |
| CDP | Environmental disclosure (carbon, water, forests) | Investors, customers |
| B Corp | Holistic social and environmental performance | Consumers, talent |

### 3.2 Greenwashing Risks

| Greenwashing Tactic | Detection |
|--------------------|-----------|
| Vague claims ("eco-friendly") | No specific metrics or targets |
| Cherry-picking positive data | Ignoring material negative impacts |
| Misleading certifications | Certifications not from credible third parties |
| Carbon offset reliance | Offsets without reducing actual emissions |
| Scope 1/2 reporting only | Ignoring Scope 3 (supply chain) emissions |
