# Positioning Theory — Academic and Practitioner Foundations

## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**
- How should we position our product in the market?
- What category should we create or compete in?
- How do we differentiate meaningfully to our target segment?

---

## 1. Ries & Trout Positioning Theory

*Citation: Ries, A., & Trout, J. (1981). Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind. McGraw-Hill.*

### 1.1 Core Principles

Positioning is not what you do to a product — it is what you do to the mind of the prospect. The goal is to occupy a distinct position in the customer's mental map of the category.

**Key laws:**
1. **Law of leadership:** It's better to be first than it is to be better. The first brand in a category owns the category position.
2. **Law of the category:** If you can't be first in a category, create a new category you can be first in.
3. **Law of the mind:** It's not about being first in the market, but first in the mind.
4. **Law of focus:** The most powerful concept in marketing is owning a word in the prospect's mind. (Volvo = safety, FedEx = overnight)
5. **Law of exclusivity:** Two companies cannot own the same word in the prospect's mind.

### 1.2 Positioning Statement Framework

```
For [target customer]
who [statement of need or opportunity],
[product name] is a [product category]
that [key benefit/reason to buy].
Unlike [competitive alternative],
our product [key differentiator].
```

**Example:**
For enterprise sales teams who struggle with CRM adoption, ProductX is a conversational CRM that automatically captures deal data from email and calendar. Unlike Salesforce, our product requires zero manual data entry.

## 2. Category Design

*Citation: Ramadan, A., Peterson, D., Lochhead, C., & Maney, K. (2016). Play Bigger: How Pirates, Dreamers, and Innovators Create and Dominate Markets. Harper Business.*

### 2.1 The Category Design Thesis

The companies that create and dominate new categories capture the majority of value. Category kings typically capture 76% of the total market capitalization in their category.

### 2.2 The Category Creation Process

1. **Define the problem:** Not the solution, but the problem that the category addresses. Frame the problem in a way that makes your solution the natural answer.
2. **Name the category:** Create a memorable, descriptive name that you own. (E.g., "cloud computing," "customer success platform," "no-code")
3. **Frame the point of view:** Develop a strong category narrative — why the world needs this category now.
4. **Lightning strike:** Coordinated launch that conditions the market to understand the new category.

### 2.3 Category vs. Brand

Category > Brand. If you own the category, you can afford an average brand. If you own a great brand in someone else's category, you're playing catch-up.

## 3. STP Framework

### 3.1 Segmentation

Dividing the market into distinct groups of customers with different needs, characteristics, or behaviors.

**Segmentation bases:**

| Base | Variables | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| Demographic | Age, income, company size, industry | "Mid-market SaaS companies, 50-500 employees" |
| Geographic | Region, country, urban/rural | "Tier 1 US cities" |
| Psychographic | Values, lifestyle, personality | "Innovation-oriented, risk-tolerant CTOs" |
| Behavioral | Usage rate, loyalty, benefits sought | "Heavy users who value reliability over features" |
| Needs-based | Specific problems to solve | "Teams that need async communication" |

### 3.2 Targeting

Evaluating each segment's attractiveness and selecting which to serve.

**Segment evaluation criteria:**
- Size and growth potential
- Profitability and willingness to pay
- Competitive intensity
- Accessibility (can we reach them efficiently?)
- Strategic fit with our capabilities

**Targeting strategies:**
- **Undifferentiated:** One offer for entire market (mass market)
- **Differentiated:** Different offers for different segments
- **Concentrated:** Focus on one segment (niche)
- **Micromarketing:** Individual-level customization

### 3.3 Positioning

Creating a distinct, valued position in the target customer's mind relative to competitors.

**Positioning dimensions:**
- Product attributes (fastest, simplest, most reliable)
- Benefits (saves time, reduces risk, increases revenue)
- Use occasion (for enterprise, for developers, for teams)
- User category (for marketers who hate complexity)
- Against a competitor (we're the anti-Salesforce)
- Product class (we're not a CRM, we're a revenue platform)

## 4. Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

### 4.1 Christensen's JTBD Theory

*Citation: Christensen, C. M., Hall, T., Dillon, K., & Duncan, D. S. (2016). Competing Against Luck. Harper Business.*

Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to do a job. The job is the fundamental unit of analysis for innovation.

**Job structure:**
- **Functional job:** The practical task the customer needs accomplished
- **Emotional job:** How the customer wants to feel
- **Social job:** How the customer wants to be perceived by others

**Example — Milkshake study:**
People "hire" morning milkshakes to make a boring commute interesting and keep them full until lunch. The competitor is not other milkshakes — it's bananas, bagels, donuts, and boredom.

### 4.2 Ulwick's Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI)

*Citation: Ulwick, A. W. (2005). What Customers Want. McGraw-Hill.*

**Method:**
1. Identify the job to be done
2. Map the job process (steps the customer goes through)
3. Identify desired outcomes (metrics the customer uses to measure success)
4. Quantify importance and satisfaction for each outcome
5. Find underserved outcomes (high importance, low satisfaction)
6. Innovate to address underserved outcomes

**Opportunity scoring:**

```
Opportunity = Importance + max(Importance - Satisfaction, 0)
```

High opportunity scores indicate unmet needs — the best targets for innovation.

## 5. Perceptual Mapping

### 5.1 Method

Plot brands on a two-dimensional map based on customer perceptions of key attributes.

```
              High Quality
                   |
    Premium        |        Performance
    Brands         |        Brands
                   |
Low Price ─────────┼───────── High Price
                   |
    Budget         |        Overpriced
    Brands         |        (empty quadrant)
                   |
              Low Quality
```

### 5.2 Application

1. Identify the two most important dimensions in your category
2. Plot competitors based on customer research
3. Identify "white space" — positions not occupied by competitors
4. Determine if white space represents real customer demand or an empty position for good reason

## 6. Brand Positioning Statement

### 6.1 Components

A complete brand positioning must address:

1. **Target audience:** Precisely who this is for
2. **Frame of reference:** The category or competitive set
3. **Point of difference:** The unique, valued benefit
4. **Reason to believe:** Evidence that the claim is credible

### 6.2 Testing Positioning

| Test | Question | Pass Criteria |
|------|----------|--------------|
| Relevance | Does the target care about this difference? | Strong "yes" from target interviews |
| Differentiation | Is this truly distinct from competitors? | Cannot be easily claimed by competitors |
| Credibility | Can we deliver on this promise? | Supported by evidence or capability |
| Sustainability | Can competitors match this over time? | Structural advantage (not just marketing) |
| Communicability | Can we explain this simply? | Understood in one sentence |
