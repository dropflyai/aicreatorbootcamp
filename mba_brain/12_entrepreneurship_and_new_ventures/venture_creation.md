## What This Enables

**Decisions it helps make:**

## 1. Lean Startup Methodology

*Citation: Ries, E. (2011). The Lean Startup. Crown Business.*

### 1.1 The Build-Measure-Learn Loop

```
Ideas -> BUILD -> Product -> MEASURE -> Data -> LEARN -> Ideas
         (MVP)              (Metrics)          (Validated learning)
```

The goal is to minimize the total time through this loop. Speed of learning determines speed of progress.

### 1.2 Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

The version of the product that allows maximum validated learning with minimum effort.

**MVP types by fidelity:**

| Type | Effort | Learning | Example |
|------|--------|----------|---------|
| Landing page | Hours | Demand signal | Measure email signups for described product |
| Concierge | Days | Deep, qualitative | Deliver service manually to a few customers |
| Wizard of Oz | Days-weeks | Behavior at scale | Automated front-end, manual back-end |
| Single-feature | Weeks | Core value test | Build only the one feature that matters most |
| Piecemeal | Days | Feasibility + demand | Assemble from existing tools (no-code, APIs) |

### 1.3 Pivots

A structured course correction designed to test a new fundamental hypothesis about the product, business model, or growth engine.

**Pivot taxonomy** (Ries, 2011):

| Pivot Type | What Changes | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Zoom-in | Feature becomes the product | Slack (from gaming company) |
| Zoom-out | Product becomes a feature of larger product | Expanding scope |
| Customer segment | Same product, different market | B2C to B2B |
| Customer need | Same customers, different problem | Instagram (Burbn -> photos) |
| Platform | App -> platform (or vice versa) | Shopify (store -> platform) |
| Business architecture | High margin/low volume to low margin/high volume | Enterprise to self-serve |
| Value capture | Revenue model change | Free to freemium to paid |
| Channel | Distribution method change | Direct to channel partners |
| Technology | New technology, same solution | Rebuild on new infrastructure |

## 2. Customer Development

*Citation: Blank, S. (2005). The Four Steps to the Epiphany. K&S Ranch.*

### 2.1 The Four Steps

```
Search Phase:
1. Customer Discovery -> Understand the problem and validate hypotheses
2. Customer Validation -> Validate the business model (repeatable sales process)

Execution Phase:
3. Customer Creation -> Create end-user demand (marketing and sales scale)
4. Company Building -> Transition from search to execution (scaling operations)
```

### 2.2 Customer Discovery Process

1. **State hypotheses:** Business Model Canvas as a set of testable hypotheses
2. **Test the problem:** Do customers have the problem you think they have?
3. **Test the solution:** Does your solution solve the problem in a way customers value?
4. **Verify or pivot:** Do the data support your hypotheses, or should you pivot?

### 2.3 Evidence Hierarchy

| Evidence Type | Strength | What It Proves |
|--------------|----------|---------------|
| They paid you | Strongest | Willingness to pay for this solution |
| Letter of intent / commitment | Strong | Serious interest |
| They used the MVP | Moderate | Product delivers value |
| They said they would use it | Weak | Interest, not commitment |
| They said it's a problem | Weakest | Problem exists, not that your solution works |

## 3. Effectuation Theory

*Citation: Sarasvathy, S. D. (2001). Causation and effectuation: Toward a theoretical shift from economic inevitability to entrepreneurial contingency. Academy of Management Review, 26(2), 243-263.*

### 3.1 Causation vs. Effectuation

| Dimension | Causation (Traditional) | Effectuation (Entrepreneurial) |
|-----------|------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Starting point | Goal/opportunity | Available means (who I am, what I know, whom I know) |
| Decision logic | Expected return | Affordable loss |
| Attitude to others | Competitive | Partnership (crazy quilt) |
| Attitude to uncertainty | Predict and plan | Leverage surprises (lemonade principle) |
| Worldview | Future is predictable | Future is co-created (pilot-in-the-plane) |

### 3.2 Five Principles of Effectuation

1. **Bird-in-hand:** Start with what you have (your means), not what you want (your goals)
2. **Affordable loss:** Invest only what you can afford to lose, not what you expect to gain
3. **Crazy quilt:** Build partnerships with self-selecting stakeholders who commit resources
4. **Lemonade:** Embrace surprises as opportunities, not disruptions
5. **Pilot-in-the-plane:** Focus on controllable aspects of the future rather than predicting uncontrollable ones

### 3.3 When to Use Effectuation vs. Causation

| Context | Approach |
|---------|----------|
| High uncertainty, new markets | Effectuation |
| Known markets, clear goals | Causation |
| Early-stage exploration | Effectuation |
| Scaling proven model | Causation |
| Resource-constrained | Effectuation |
| Resource-abundant | Either |

## 4. Bricolage

*Citation: Baker, T., & Nelson, R. E. (2005). Creating something from nothing: Resource construction through entrepreneurial bricolage. Administrative Science Quarterly, 50(3), 329-366.*

### 4.1 Concept

Bricolage is "making do by applying combinations of resources at hand to new problems and opportunities." Entrepreneurs don't wait for ideal resources — they improvise with what's available.

**Three elements:**
1. Making do (taking action rather than planning)
2. Combining existing resources for new purposes
3. Using resources at hand (not waiting for the "right" ones)

### 4.2 Application

Startups practice bricolage when they:
- Use no-code tools to build MVPs instead of custom engineering
- Recruit advisors instead of full-time hires
- Repurpose existing infrastructure for new use cases
- Combine free tools creatively (Zapier + Airtable + Stripe)

## 5. Disciplined Entrepreneurship

*Citation: Aulet, B. (2013). Disciplined Entrepreneurship: 24 Steps to a Successful Startup. Wiley.*

### 5.1 The 24-Step Framework (Grouped)

**Who is your customer?**
1. Market segmentation
2. Select a beachhead market
3. Build an end-user profile
4. Calculate TAM for beachhead
5. Profile the persona
6. Full life cycle use case

**What can you do for your customer?**
7. High-level product specification
8. Quantify the value proposition
9. Identify next 10 customers
10. Define your core
11. Chart competitive position

**How does your customer acquire your product?**
12. Determine the customer's Decision-Making Unit (DMU)
13. Map the process to acquire a customer
14. Calculate TAM for follow-on markets
15. Design a business model
16. Set pricing framework

**How do you make money?**
17. Calculate LTV per customer
18. Map the sales process
19. Calculate CAC
20. Identify key assumptions
21. Test key assumptions
22. Define minimum viable business product (MVBP)
23. Show that the dogs will eat the dog food
24. Develop a product plan

## 6. Startup Archetypes

### 6.1 By Innovation Type

| Archetype | Innovation Source | Example |
|-----------|-----------------|---------|
| Technology-push | New technology enables new capabilities | Deep tech, biotech |
| Market-pull | Unmet customer need drives development | Customer-discovered products |
| Business model | Same product/technology, new business model | Dollar Shave Club, Airbnb |
| Platform | Creates a new marketplace or ecosystem | Uber, Shopify |
| Regulatory | Policy change creates opportunity | Fintech after banking deregulation |
