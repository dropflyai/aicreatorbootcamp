# Social Media Brain -- Glossary

## Core Terminology

This glossary defines key terms used throughout the Social Media Brain. All modules reference these definitions. Terms are organized by domain for efficient lookup.

---

## Platform & Algorithm Terms

**Algorithm**
The proprietary ranking system each platform uses to determine which content appears in a user's feed. Algorithms evaluate signals such as engagement velocity, relevance, recency, and relationship strength to personalize content delivery. Each platform's algorithm operates differently and is continuously updated.

**Algorithmic Reach**
The number of unique users who see a piece of content through algorithmic distribution (as opposed to paid distribution or direct profile visits). Also called organic reach. This metric has declined across most platforms as competition for feed space increases and platforms monetize through advertising.

**API (Application Programming Interface)**
A set of protocols allowing third-party applications to interact with a social platform's data and functionality. Social APIs enable scheduling, analytics, and content management through external tools. Rate limits and access tiers vary by platform and partnership level.

**Content Graph**
A recommendation model (used notably by TikTok) that surfaces content based on content similarity and user behavior patterns rather than social connections. Contrasts with the social graph model used by platforms like Facebook, where content from connections is prioritized.

**Engagement Velocity**
The rate at which a piece of content accumulates engagement (likes, comments, shares, saves) in the period immediately following publication. High engagement velocity signals quality to algorithms, triggering broader distribution. Critical in the first 30-60 minutes on most platforms.

**Feed**
The primary content consumption interface on a social platform. Can be algorithmically sorted (Instagram, TikTok, LinkedIn) or chronologically sorted (optional on X). The feed is the primary battleground for organic content distribution.

**For You Page (FYP)**
TikTok's primary content discovery feed, driven by the content graph algorithm. The FYP surfaces content from any creator (not just followed accounts) based on predicted user interest, making it the primary distribution mechanism for TikTok content.

**Hashtag**
A metadata tag prefixed with # that categorizes content and enables discoverability. Hashtag strategy varies significantly by platform: high volume on Instagram (up to 30), minimal on LinkedIn (3-5), strategic on TikTok (trending + niche), largely deprecated on X.

**Impression**
A single instance of content being displayed to a user. One user can generate multiple impressions of the same content. Impressions measure content exposure frequency; reach measures unique exposure. The impression-to-reach ratio indicates content recirculation.

**Organic**
Content distribution achieved without paid promotion. Organic reach is determined by algorithmic ranking, follower engagement, and content virality. Organic social is the foundation of social media strategy; paid social amplifies and extends it.

**Reach**
The number of unique users who have seen a piece of content. Distinguished from impressions (total views, including repeat views by the same user). Reach is the foundational awareness metric in social media measurement.

**Shadow Ban**
An unconfirmed practice where platforms allegedly reduce content distribution without notifying the creator. While platforms generally deny shadow banning, observable patterns include sudden reach drops, content not appearing in hashtag searches, and reduced discoverability. More accurately described as algorithmic suppression.

**Social Graph**
The network of connections between users on a platform. Platforms like Facebook and LinkedIn heavily weight the social graph in content distribution, meaning content from people you know is prioritized. Contrasts with content graph models.

## Engagement & Community Terms

**Comment-to-Like Ratio**
The proportion of comments relative to likes on a piece of content. A higher ratio indicates deeper engagement and more meaningful audience interaction. Comments require more effort than likes, making this a quality signal for both algorithms and strategists.

**Community Guidelines**
A published set of rules governing acceptable behavior within a brand's social media community. Includes content standards, interaction expectations, moderation policies, and consequences for violations. Essential for maintaining a healthy brand community.

**Dark Social**
Content sharing that occurs through private channels (DMs, email, messaging apps) and cannot be tracked by standard analytics. Estimated to account for 80%+ of social sharing (RadiumOne research). Represents a significant blind spot in social measurement.

**Engagement Rate**
The percentage of an audience that interacts with content. Calculated as: (Total Engagements / Reach or Followers) x 100. Industry benchmarks vary by platform and audience size. The most commonly cited social media performance metric.

**Engagement Rate Formulas:**
- **By Reach:** (Likes + Comments + Shares + Saves) / Reach x 100 -- most accurate
- **By Followers:** (Likes + Comments + Shares + Saves) / Followers x 100 -- most common
- **By Impressions:** (Likes + Comments + Shares + Saves) / Impressions x 100 -- media-focused

**Lurker**
A community member who consumes content but does not actively engage (like, comment, share). Research suggests 90% of online community members are lurkers (Nielsen's 90-9-1 rule). Lurkers are still valuable audience members -- they read, absorb, and may convert.

**Parasocial Relationship**
A one-sided relationship where an audience member develops a sense of intimacy and connection with a media figure (creator, brand persona) who is unaware of their existence. Coined by Horton and Wohl (1956). Understanding parasocial dynamics is critical for influencer marketing effectiveness and community building.

**Response Rate**
The percentage of inbound messages or comments that receive a response from the brand. Measured as: (Messages Responded To / Total Inbound Messages) x 100. Sprout Social Index benchmarks average response rates at 35-40% across industries.

**Response Time**
The average time between a user's inbound message/comment and the brand's response. Sprout Social data indicates 79% of consumers expect a response within 24 hours on social; 40% expect a response within 1 hour. A key community management KPI.

**Save Rate**
The percentage of viewers who save a piece of content for later reference. Saves signal high-value content to algorithms and indicate the content has reference or aspirational value beyond momentary entertainment. Particularly important on Instagram.

**Sentiment**
The emotional tone of social mentions and conversations about a brand, product, or topic. Measured as positive, negative, or neutral (sometimes with granularity: very positive, somewhat positive, etc.). Automated sentiment analysis uses NLP; manual coding provides higher accuracy.

**Share of Voice (SOV)**
The proportion of social conversation about a brand relative to its competitors within a defined category. Calculated as: (Brand Mentions / Total Category Mentions) x 100. Correlates with market share (Binet & Field research).

**Social Proof**
The psychological phenomenon where people adopt behaviors they observe in others, assuming those behaviors are correct. On social media, social proof manifests as follower counts, engagement numbers, testimonials, and user-generated content. Robert Cialdini identified social proof as one of six principles of persuasion.

**UGC (User-Generated Content)**
Content created by consumers or community members rather than the brand itself. UGC is perceived as more authentic and trustworthy than brand-created content (Stackla research: 79% of people say UGC highly impacts purchasing decisions). Types include reviews, testimonials, photos, videos, and social posts.

## Content & Strategy Terms

**A/B Test (Split Test)**
A controlled experiment comparing two variations of a social element (ad creative, caption, posting time, CTA) to determine which performs better. Statistical significance requires sufficient sample size. Essential for optimizing social advertising and content strategy.

**Atomization (Content Atomization)**
The process of breaking a single large content asset (webinar, blog post, report) into multiple smaller social-native pieces. A one-hour webinar might yield 20+ social posts. Maximizes content ROI and enables platform-specific adaptation.

**Content Calendar**
A strategic document mapping planned content across platforms, dates, content pillars, and formats. Serves as the operational backbone of social media execution. Should balance planned content (70%), timely content (20%), and experimental content (10%).

**Content Flywheel**
A self-reinforcing content system where each piece of content generates data, engagement, and assets that inform and fuel future content. The flywheel accelerates over time as the system accumulates audience insights, repurposable assets, and community momentum.

**Content Pillar**
A core thematic category that anchors a brand's social content strategy. Typically 3-5 pillars that reflect brand values, audience interests, and business objectives. Example pillars: Education, Behind-the-Scenes, Community Spotlight, Product, Industry Insights.

**CTA (Call to Action)**
A directive prompt encouraging the audience to take a specific action (comment, share, click, sign up, purchase). Effective CTAs are specific, urgent, and value-driven. Each piece of social content should have a clear primary CTA.

**Evergreen Content**
Content that remains relevant and valuable over extended periods, not tied to specific dates, trends, or events. Evergreen content can be recycled and repurposed. Contrasts with timely content, which has a limited relevance window.

**Hook**
The opening element of a piece of content (first 1-3 seconds of video, first line of a caption) designed to capture attention and stop the scroll. Effective hooks create curiosity gaps, challenge assumptions, or promise specific value. Critical for engagement in algorithmic feeds.

**STEPPS Framework**
Jonah Berger's framework for creating contagious content. Six principles: Social Currency (makes sharers look good), Triggers (environmental reminders), Emotion (high-arousal emotions), Public (visible behaviors), Practical Value (useful information), Stories (narrative wrappers). Published in *Contagious: Why Things Catch On* (2013).

## Advertising Terms

**Audience Network**
An ad placement extending beyond the core social platform to partner apps and websites. Meta's Audience Network extends Facebook/Instagram ads to third-party apps. Generally provides lower-quality placements at lower costs.

**CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)**
The total cost of acquiring a new customer through social channels. Calculated as: Total Social Spend / Number of New Customers Acquired. Includes ad spend, tool costs, and labor. A critical efficiency metric for paid social.

**Conversion**
A completed desired action by a user (purchase, sign-up, download, form submission). Conversion tracking requires pixel implementation and proper attribution setup. Social media conversions can be direct (last-click) or assisted (part of a multi-touch journey).

**CPC (Cost Per Click)**
The average cost for each click on a social advertisement. Calculated as: Total Spend / Total Clicks. Benchmarks vary by platform, industry, and objective. Meta average CPC: $0.50-$2.00; LinkedIn average CPC: $5.00-$12.00.

**CPM (Cost Per Mille)**
The cost per 1,000 impressions of an advertisement. Calculated as: (Total Spend / Total Impressions) x 1,000. A standard media buying metric. Lower CPM indicates more efficient reach; must be balanced against audience quality and conversion potential.

**Creative Fatigue**
The decline in ad performance that occurs when a target audience has seen the same creative asset too many times, resulting in decreased engagement and increased costs. Monitored through frequency metrics and performance degradation patterns. Requires regular creative refresh.

**Custom Audience**
An ad targeting audience built from a brand's own data -- email lists, website visitors, app users, customer databases, or social engagers. Custom audiences enable highly targeted advertising to warm prospects. Foundation for retargeting and lookalike audience creation.

**EMV (Earned Media Value)**
A metric that assigns a monetary value to organic social engagement by calculating what equivalent paid media exposure would cost. EMV = Impressions x CPM rate (or Engagements x estimated value). Commonly used in influencer marketing. Controversial but widely adopted.

**Frequency**
The average number of times a user has been exposed to an advertisement within a defined period. Optimal frequency varies by objective: awareness campaigns can sustain higher frequency; conversion campaigns suffer from diminishing returns above 3-5x per week.

**Lookalike Audience**
An algorithmically generated audience that resembles a source audience (typically a custom audience of existing customers). The platform identifies users with similar demographics, interests, and behaviors. Available on Meta, LinkedIn, TikTok, and X. Quality depends on source audience size and signal strength.

**Pixel**
A JavaScript code snippet placed on a website that tracks user behavior and enables conversion tracking, retargeting, and audience building. The Meta Pixel, LinkedIn Insight Tag, and TikTok Pixel are platform-specific implementations. Essential for paid social measurement and optimization.

**ROAS (Return on Ad Spend)**
The revenue generated per dollar of advertising spend. Calculated as: Revenue Attributed to Ads / Total Ad Spend. A ROAS of 4:1 means $4 revenue per $1 spent. The primary efficiency metric for performance-oriented social advertising.

**Retargeting (Remarketing)**
Advertising to users who have previously interacted with a brand's website, app, social content, or other digital properties. Retargeting audiences typically convert at 3-5x the rate of cold audiences because they have demonstrated prior interest.

## Influencer Terms

**Ambassador Program**
A long-term influencer partnership where creators represent the brand consistently over months or years, rather than through one-off sponsored posts. Ambassadors typically receive ongoing compensation and deeper brand integration.

**Creator Economy**
The economic ecosystem of independent content creators who monetize their audiences through brand partnerships, platform monetization features, merchandise, subscriptions, and digital products. Estimated at $100B+ market size.

**Influencer Tiers:**
- **Nano-influencers:** 1K-10K followers. Highest engagement rates (4-8%). Hyper-niche audiences. Low cost.
- **Micro-influencers:** 10K-100K followers. Strong engagement (2-4%). Niche authority. Moderate cost.
- **Mid-tier influencers:** 100K-500K followers. Balanced reach and engagement. Growing authority. Higher cost.
- **Macro-influencers:** 500K-1M followers. Significant reach. Lower engagement rates (1-2%). Premium cost.
- **Mega-influencers:** 1M+ followers. Maximum reach. Lowest engagement rates (<1%). Celebrity pricing.

**Whitelisting (Creator Licensing)**
The practice of running paid advertisements through an influencer's social media account, using their identity and audience data for ad targeting. Enables brands to amplify influencer content with paid distribution while maintaining the creator's authentic voice.

## Analytics & Measurement Terms

**Attribution Model**
The method used to assign credit for conversions across multiple marketing touchpoints. Models include: last-click (100% credit to final touch), first-click (100% to first touch), linear (equal distribution), time-decay (recent touches weighted higher), and data-driven (algorithmic assignment).

**Benchmark**
A standard point of reference against which social media performance is measured. Benchmarks can be internal (historical performance), competitive (industry averages), or aspirational (best-in-class). Sprout Social Index and Hootsuite Digital Reports provide annual industry benchmarks.

**KPI (Key Performance Indicator)**
A quantifiable metric used to evaluate success against defined objectives. Social media KPIs should be tied to business objectives, not vanity metrics. Example hierarchy: Business KPI (revenue) -> Marketing KPI (leads) -> Social KPI (click-through rate).

**Social ROI**
The return on investment attributable to social media activities. Calculated as: (Value Generated from Social - Cost of Social) / Cost of Social x 100. Measuring social ROI requires proper attribution, conversion tracking, and value assignment to social touchpoints.

**Vanity Metric**
A metric that appears impressive but does not meaningfully indicate business value or progress toward strategic objectives. Examples: raw follower count, total likes (without context), impression volume (without engagement context). Vanity metrics are not inherently useless but must be contextualized with performance metrics.

## Crisis & Reputation Terms

**Brand Safety**
The practice of ensuring brand content and advertisements do not appear alongside harmful, offensive, or inappropriate content. In social media, brand safety concerns include ad placement contexts, user-generated comment content, and association with controversial creators or topics.

**Crisis Tier Classification:**
- **Tier 1 (Low):** Negative comments, minor complaints, isolated negative mentions. Managed by community team.
- **Tier 2 (Medium):** Trending negative sentiment, viral complaint, minor PR issue with social amplification. Requires social lead involvement.
- **Tier 3 (High):** Full social media crisis -- viral negative content, organized backlash, mainstream media pickup. Requires cross-functional crisis team activation.

**Issue vs. Crisis**
An issue is a concern that has potential to escalate but is currently contained. A crisis is an event that has already escalated and threatens brand reputation, requiring immediate coordinated response. Social media can transform issues into crises within minutes.

**Reputation Recovery**
The strategic process of rebuilding brand perception following a crisis or sustained negative sentiment. Involves listening, acknowledging, taking corrective action, communicating transparently, and demonstrating sustained positive change over time.
