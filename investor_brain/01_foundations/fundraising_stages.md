# Fundraising Stages -- Pre-Seed Through IPO

## Overview

Each fundraising stage has distinct characteristics, expectations, investor profiles,
and success criteria. Misalignment between company reality and stage expectations is
the primary cause of failed fundraising campaigns. This module provides a comprehensive
map of every stage with precise benchmarks drawn from YC data, First Round Capital
research, and NVCA reporting.

---

## Stage Progression Map

```
PRE-SEED → SEED → SERIES A → SERIES B → SERIES C → LATE STAGE → IPO/EXIT
  │         │        │          │          │           │           │
  │         │        │          │          │           │           │
  Idea &    PMF      Repeatable Scaling   Market      Path to    Public

                                Engine

  $1M                                                            (offering)
```

## Pre-Seed Stage

### Definition and Context

Pre-seed is the earliest institutional capital stage. It funds the journey from idea
to initial prototype and early user validation. The term gained prominence circa 2018
as seed rounds grew larger and a gap emerged for very early companies.

### Benchmarks

| Dimension | Pre-Seed Standard |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Raise Amount** | $100K - $1M (median: $500K) |
| **Valuation** | $2M - $8M post-money (or uncapped/capped SAFE) |
| **Instrument** | SAFE (post-money, per YC) or convertible note |
| **Dilution** | 10-20% |
| **Timeline** | 2-8 weeks for angels; 4-12 weeks for funds |
| **Team Size** | 1-3 founders |
| **Revenue** | $0 (pre-revenue acceptable) |
| **Product** | Prototype, MVP, or detailed wireframes |

### What Pre-Seed Investors Evaluate

```
EVALUATION WEIGHT AT PRE-SEED

Team: 50-60%
  - Founder-market fit (why YOU for THIS problem?)
  - Technical capability to build V1
  - Prior startup experience (helpful but not required)
  - Coachability and resilience

Market/Idea: 30-40%
  - Problem clarity and urgency
  - Market size potential (top-down plausibility)
  - Timing thesis (why now?)
  - Initial insight or unfair advantage

Traction: 0-10%
  - Waitlist signups
  - LOIs from potential customers
  - Prototype user feedback
  - NOT revenue (too early to expect)
```

### Pre-Seed Investor Types

| Investor Type | Typical Check | Characteristics |
|---------------|---------------|-----------------|
| Friends & Family | $5K - $50K | Non-professional, relationship-based |
| Angel Investors | $10K - $100K | Individual investors, often ex-founders |
| Angel Syndicates | $50K - $500K | AngelList, organized groups |
| Pre-Seed Funds | $100K - $500K | Precursor, Hustle Fund, Contrary |
| Accelerators | $125K - $500K | YC ($500K), Techstars ($120K), others |

### Pre-Seed Fundraising Strategy

```
OPTIMAL PRE-SEED APPROACH

1. Build a prototype (even if ugly) -- shows execution ability
2. Get 5-10 users/customers using it (even free) -- shows demand
3. Identify 20-30 target angels/funds with thesis match
4. Seek warm introductions (2nd degree connections)
5. Run process in 2-4 week sprints
6. Use post-money SAFEs with $4-6M cap (adjust for market)
7. Close rolling (SAFEs allow this -- no "round" to close)
8. Target 18 months of runway at planned burn

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Over-optimizing valuation at pre-seed (ruins Series Seed dynamics)
- Raising too little (< 12 months runway)
- Raising too much (high expectations, limited progress)
- Using complex instruments (priced round, participating preferred)
- Waiting for product perfection before raising
```

## Seed Stage

### Definition and Context

Seed is the first significant fundraise, funding the search for product-market fit.
Post-2020, seed rounds have bifurcated: "traditional seed" ($1-3M) and "large seed"
($3-6M). The Investor Brain calibrates to the specific sub-stage.

### Benchmarks

| Dimension | Seed Standard |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Raise Amount** | $1M - $4M (median: $2.5M) |
| **Valuation** | $8M - $20M post-money |
| **Instrument** | SAFE, convertible note, or priced round |
| **Dilution** | 15-25% |
| **Timeline** | 4-12 weeks |
| **Team Size** | 3-8 people |
| **Revenue** | $0 - $50K MRR (revenue helpful but not required) |
| **Product** | Live product with early users |

### What Seed Investors Evaluate

```
EVALUATION WEIGHT AT SEED

Team: 40-50%
  - Can this team find PMF?
  - Domain expertise and insight depth
  - Speed of execution (shipped product in X weeks)
  - Ability to recruit early employees

Market: 25-35%
  - TAM > $1B (at minimum)
  - Clear wedge into market
  - Market timing evidence
  - Distribution insight

Product/Traction: 15-25%
  - Working product (not vaporware)
  - Early user engagement signals
  - Qualitative user love (NPS, testimonials)
  - Initial retention data
  - Early revenue is a bonus, not a requirement
```

### Seed Round Structure Considerations

**SAFE vs. Priced Round at Seed:**

```
DECISION FRAMEWORK

Choose SAFE when:
  - Raising < $2M
  - No lead investor insists on priced round
  - Speed is critical
  - Legal costs matter ($5K SAFE vs. $30-50K priced)
  - Rolling close is advantageous

Choose Priced Round when:
  - Raising > $3M
  - Lead investor requires it
  - Setting clear governance is important
  - Company has complex cap table
  - Setting a 409A benchmark is valuable

HYBRID APPROACH:
  - Raise $500K-$1M on SAFEs while finding lead
  - Lead investor prices the round
  - SAFEs convert into the priced round
  - Common and effective strategy
```

## Series A

### Definition and Context

Series A is the pivotal transition from "searching for PMF" to "scaling a proven model."
It is the most competitive fundraising stage because the bar is high (proven metrics)
but the company pool is large (many seed companies compete for fewer A investors).

Per First Round Capital research, only ~20-30% of seed-funded companies raise a Series A.

### Benchmarks

| Dimension | Series A Standard |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Raise Amount** | $5M - $15M (median: $10M) |
| **Valuation** | $30M - $80M post-money |
| **Instrument** | Priced preferred equity (always) |
| **Dilution** | 15-25% (including option pool refresh) |
| **Timeline** | 6-16 weeks (often longer) |
| **Team Size** | 10-30 people |
| **Revenue** | $1M - $3M ARR (SaaS); varies by model |
| **Growth** | 15-30% MoM revenue growth; or strong engagement metrics |

### Series A Readiness Checklist

```
SERIES A READINESS (per YC and First Round frameworks)

MUST-HAVES:
□ Clear evidence of product-market fit
  - Net revenue retention > 100%
  - Strong engagement/retention cohorts
  - Organic growth or efficient paid acquisition
□ $1M+ ARR or equivalent traction for non-SaaS
□ Repeatable customer acquisition (not one-off deals)
□ Identifiable path to $100M+ ARR
□ Team that can execute next 18 months of plan
□ Clear use of funds tied to specific milestones

STRONG-TO-HAVES:
□ Unit economics trending toward LTV/CAC > 3x
□ Gross margins > 60% (SaaS: > 70%)
□ Low churn (< 5% monthly for SMB; < 2% for enterprise)
□ Key hire pipeline identified
□ Board or advisory bench strength

NICE-TO-HAVES:
□ Notable customer logos
□ Industry recognition or press
□ Strategic partnerships
□ IP or technical moat
□ International expansion potential
```

### Series A Process Design

```
SERIES A FUNDRAISING TIMELINE

Weeks -8 to -4: PREPARATION
  - Financial model completed (3-year projections)
  - Data room assembled (see Templates/data_room_checklist.md)
  - Pitch deck created and tested with friendly investors
  - Target investor list finalized (30-50 firms)
  - Warm introduction requests sent

Weeks -4 to 0: SOFT LAUNCH
  - Share deck with 5-10 "practice" investors
  - Iterate on pitch based on feedback
  - Identify objections and prepare responses
  - Refine target list based on practice round feedback

Week 0: LAUNCH
  - Send deck to all target investors within 1-2 weeks
  - Compress the process to create competitive tension
  - Schedule first meetings in tight windows

Weeks 1-4: FIRST MEETINGS
  - 2-4 meetings per day
  - Same pitch, same energy, refine continuously
  - Track engagement signals (who asks for data room access?)
  - Update investors who show interest on process timeline

Weeks 4-6: PARTNER MEETINGS
  - Deep dives with 3-5 interested firms
  - Management presentations
  - Customer/reference calls
  - Market analysis presentations

Weeks 6-8: TERM SHEETS
  - Receive and compare term sheets
  - Negotiate terms (see 04_term_sheets/)
  - Select lead investor
  - Negotiate final terms

Weeks 8-12: CLOSING
  - Due diligence (legal, financial, technical)
  - Definitive document negotiation
  - Board formation
  - Wire and close
```

## Series B

### Definition and Context

Series B funds the scaling of a proven go-to-market engine. The company has demonstrated
PMF and repeatable acquisition; now it needs capital to accelerate growth, expand to
new markets, and build organizational infrastructure.

### Benchmarks

| Dimension | Series B Standard |
|-----------|------------------|
| **Raise Amount** | $15M - $50M (median: $30M) |
| **Valuation** | $80M - $300M post-money |
| **Instrument** | Priced preferred equity |
| **Dilution** | 10-20% |
| **Revenue** | $5M - $20M ARR |
| **Growth** | 100-200% YoY revenue growth |
| **Burn Multiple** | < 2x (net burn / net new ARR) |
| **Team Size** | 30-100 people |
| **Gross Margin** | > 65% (SaaS: > 75%) |

### Series B Evaluation Shift

```
AT SERIES B, EVALUATION SHIFTS TO METRICS

Market: 20-25%
  - Market size validated by revenue traction
  - Competitive dynamics proven manageable
  - Expansion market identified

Metrics: 40-50%
  - ARR growth rate and trajectory
  - Unit economics (proven, not projected)
  - Net revenue retention
  - CAC payback period
  - Gross margin profile
  - Burn efficiency (burn multiple)

Team: 15-20%
  - VP-level hires in place
  - Management team can scale to $50M+ ARR
  - Culture and retention evidence

GTM: 10-15%
  - Multiple acquisition channels proven
  - Sales process documented and repeatable
  - Pipeline coverage and conversion metrics
```

## Series C and Beyond

### Definition

Series C and later rounds fund market expansion, international growth, potential
acquisitions, and preparation for liquidity events. At this stage, the company is
expected to have strong unit economics and a clear path to profitability.

### Benchmarks by Round

| Dimension | Series C | Series D | Series E+ |
|-----------|----------|----------|-----------|
| **Raise** | $50-150M | $100-300M | $200M+ |
| **Valuation** | $300M-$1B | $500M-$3B | $1B+ |
| **ARR** | $20-50M | $50-100M | $100M+ |
| **Growth** | 80-150% YoY | 50-100% YoY | 30-60% YoY |
| **Path to Profit** | Visible | Clear | Near-term |
| **Investors** | Growth funds | Late-stage/crossover | Pre-IPO funds |

### Late-Stage Investor Types

```
LATE-STAGE CAPITAL SOURCES

Growth Equity Funds:
  - General Atlantic, TA Associates, Summit Partners
  - $100M+ checks, board seats, operational support
  - Focus on unit economics and path to profitability

Crossover Funds (Public + Private):
  - Tiger Global, Coatue, D1 Capital
  - Large checks ($50-500M), less governance
  - Price-insensitive in bull markets
  - Pre-IPO positioning strategy

Sovereign Wealth / Mega-LPs:
  - Mubadala, GIC, ADIA
  - Very large checks, long time horizons
  - Strategic considerations beyond financial return

Corporate Strategic:
  - Google Ventures, Salesforce Ventures, Intel Capital
  - Strategic alignment matters
  - Can be acquisition pipeline (buyer or partner)
  - Watch for competitive constraints
```

## Bridge Rounds

### When Bridges Happen

Bridge rounds provide interim financing between major rounds. They are neither
inherently good nor bad -- context determines their strategic value.

```
BRIDGE ROUND SCENARIOS

POSITIVE BRIDGE (strength):
  - Company is performing but not yet at Series X metrics
  - Existing investors offer bridge to reach milestone
  - Terms: SAFE or note at modest premium to last round
  - Signal: Company is on track, just needs more time

NEGATIVE BRIDGE (weakness):
  - Company failed to raise Series X
  - Running out of runway
  - Existing investors provide life support
  - Terms: Often punitive (high discount, low cap)
  - Signal: Company may be struggling

STRATEGIC BRIDGE:
  - Company choosing to delay fundraise for better terms
  - Market timing play (waiting for better conditions)
  - Milestone within reach that would significantly improve terms
  - Smart use of bridge as optionality
```

### Bridge Terms

| Bridge Type | Typical Instrument | Terms |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| Positive | SAFE or note | 10-20% discount, fair cap |
| Negative | Note with warrants | 20-30% discount, low cap, warrants |
| Strategic | SAFE | At or near last round valuation |
| Inside bridge | Note | Pro-rata from existing investors |

## Stage Transition Failure Modes

### The Series A Gap

```
WHY COMPANIES FAIL TO RAISE SERIES A

1. Insufficient traction (40% of failures)
   - Revenue below $1M ARR threshold
   - Growth rate below 10% MoM
   - Engagement metrics not compelling

2. Unclear PMF signal (25%)
   - Revenue exists but churn is high
   - Growth is paid-only (no organic)
   - Small TAM ceiling

3. Team gaps (15%)
   - No technical co-founder
   - No GTM leader
   - Founder conflict

4. Market timing (10%)
   - Raising during market downturn
   - Sector out of favor
   - Regulatory headwinds

5. Process failures (10%)
   - Raised at too-high seed valuation (down round risk)
   - Poor investor targeting
   - Weak pitch execution
   - No competitive tension in process

SURVIVAL STRATEGIES:
  - Extend runway (cut burn, bridge from existing investors)
  - Hit milestone then re-approach
  - Consider smaller "seed extension" round
  - Pivot to stronger market signal
  - Explore revenue-based financing as alternative
```

## Round Sizing Framework

### The 18-24 Month Rule

```
ROUND SIZING FORMULA (YC Standard)

Target Raise = Monthly Burn Rate * (18 to 24 months) + Buffer

EXAMPLE:
Current burn: $100K/month
Planned team growth: +$50K/month burn over 6 months
Average burn over period: ~$150K/month

Minimum raise: $150K * 18 = $2.7M
Optimal raise: $150K * 24 = $3.6M
With 20% buffer: $3.6M * 1.2 = $4.3M

Target: $4M seed round

ADJUSTMENTS:
Bull market: Can raise for 18 months (easy to raise again)
Bear market: Raise for 30+ months (hard to raise again)
Capital-intensive: Add specific capex requirements
Revenue offset: Reduce by projected revenue growth
```

### Milestone-Based Sizing

```
ALTERNATIVE: SIZE TO MILESTONE

Identify the milestone that unlocks next round at higher valuation.

Series A milestone: $1M ARR with 15%+ MoM growth
Current ARR: $100K
Growth rate: 20% MoM
Time to $1M ARR: ~12 months
Cost to get there: ~$2M (team + marketing + ops)
Buffer: 6 months additional runway = $1M

Target seed: $3M

This approach ties fundraising directly to value creation,
which is the most defensible pitch to investors.
```
