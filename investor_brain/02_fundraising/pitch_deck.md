# Pitch Deck -- Structure, Content, and Execution

## Overview

The pitch deck is the founder's primary tool for communicating the investment opportunity.
A great deck does not close a round on its own -- it opens doors, structures conversations,
and provides a reference artifact for investor decision-making. This module covers deck
architecture, slide-by-slide content, storytelling integration, and financial presentation.

Primary references: Sequoia pitch deck template, YC application framework, First Round
Capital "How to Present to Investors", Reid Hoffman LinkedIn Series B deck analysis.

---

## Deck Architecture

### The Two Deck System

Every company needs two versions of the pitch deck:

```
PRESENTATION DECK (used in meetings)
  - 10-15 slides
  - Minimal text, maximum visual impact
  - Designed to support a verbal narrative
  - NO detailed financial tables
  - Builds emotional + intellectual case

READ-AHEAD DECK (sent before/after meetings)
  - 15-25 slides
  - Self-contained narrative (readable without presenter)
  - Includes supporting data, metrics, and context
  - Appendix with detailed financials
  - Builds complete information picture

RULE: Never send the presentation deck as a read-ahead.
It won't make sense without narration. Never present the
read-ahead in person. It's too dense for live presentation.
```

### Slide Sequence -- The Sequoia Framework (Adapted)

```
CANONICAL SLIDE ORDER

1.  TITLE / HOOK
2.  PROBLEM
3.  SOLUTION
4.  WHY NOW
5.  MARKET SIZE
6.  PRODUCT
7.  TRACTION
8.  BUSINESS MODEL
9.  COMPETITION
10. TEAM
11. FINANCIALS
12. THE ASK

OPTIONAL ADDITIONS:
  - Customer testimonials (after product or traction)
  - Go-to-market strategy (after business model)
  - Vision / roadmap (before the ask)
  - Appendix slides (after the ask)
```

## Slide-by-Slide Deep Dive

### Slide 1: Title / Hook

```
PURPOSE: Capture attention. Frame the entire narrative in one sentence.

ELEMENTS:
  - Company name and logo
  - One-line description (the "high concept" pitch)
  - Optional: Hero image or key metric

THE ONE-LINER FORMULA:
  "[Company] is [category] for [target customer] that [key differentiator]"

EXAMPLES:
  "Stripe: Payments infrastructure for the internet"
  "Airbnb: Book rooms with locals rather than hotels"
  "Figma: Collaborative interface design tool for teams"

BAD ONE-LINERS:
  "We're an AI-powered platform leveraging blockchain..." (jargon soup)
  "We're disrupting a $500B market..." (everyone says this)
  "We're like Uber for [X]..." (overused analogy framework)

GOOD ONE-LINERS:
  Specific, concrete, understandable by a non-expert in 3 seconds
  If your mother can't understand it, simplify further
```

### Slide 2: Problem

```
PURPOSE: Establish the pain point so viscerally that the audience
demands a solution before you present one.

STRUCTURE:
  1. State the problem in human terms (not abstract)
  2. Quantify the problem (cost, time, frequency)
  3. Show who suffers (specific persona)
  4. Explain why existing solutions fail

TECHNIQUES:
  - Tell a specific customer story (anecdote → data)
  - Use a "day in the life" scenario
  - Show the cost of the status quo
  - Use contrasting images (before/after)

EXAMPLE FRAMEWORK:
  "[Persona] spends [X hours/dollars] on [painful activity]
   because [root cause]. Current solutions [specific failures].
   This costs the industry [$X billion] annually."

COMMON MISTAKES:
  ✗ Problem too abstract ("communication is broken")
  ✗ Problem too niche (small market signal)
  ✗ Problem is a vitamin, not a painkiller
  ✗ No quantification of pain
  ✗ Problem only exists if you squint
```

### Slide 3: Solution

```
PURPOSE: Present your solution as the inevitable answer to the
problem you just described.

STRUCTURE:
  1. Clear statement of what you do (one sentence)
  2. How it works (3 bullet points maximum)
  3. Key differentiator (why YOUR solution)
  4. Product screenshot or demo preview

THE "MAGIC MOMENT" TECHNIQUE:
  Show the exact moment a user experiences the value
  - Screenshot of the "aha" moment
  - Before/after comparison
  - User quote about the experience

EXAMPLE:
  "Rippling lets you onboard a new employee in 90 seconds.
   One click: payroll, benefits, devices, and app access."
   [Screenshot of the 90-second onboard flow]

RULES:
  - Solution must directly address the stated problem
  - No jargon (if audience needs a glossary, you failed)
  - No feature lists (save for product slide)
  - Focus on outcome, not mechanism
```

### Slide 4: Why Now

```
PURPOSE: Explain the market timing that makes this opportunity
available today but not five years ago.

THIS IS THE SLIDE MOST FOUNDERS SKIP (per Sequoia, it's one of the most important)

WHY NOW CATEGORIES:
  1. Technology enabler (new tech makes this possible)
     "GPT-4 class models now enable real-time document analysis"
  2. Regulatory change (new rules create opportunity)
     "Open banking regulations require API access to bank data"
  3. Behavioral shift (customers changed)
     "Remote work made distributed collaboration essential"
  4. Market structure change (industry reorganization)
     "Consolidation of suppliers created a monopoly problem"
  5. Economic shift (cost/price inflection)
     "Cloud compute costs dropped 90% in 5 years"

FRAMEWORK:
  "[Macro trend] has created [specific opportunity].
   Companies that move now will capture [specific advantage].
   In [X years], this window closes because [barrier to entry rises]."

WHAT INVESTORS HEAR:
  Good "why now" → "This team sees something others don't yet"
  No "why now" → "If this was a good idea, someone would have done it already"
```

### Slide 5: Market Size

```
PURPOSE: Prove the market is large enough to build a venture-scale
business ($1B+ potential).

TAM / SAM / SOM FRAMEWORK:

TAM (Total Addressable Market):
  Total revenue opportunity if you captured 100% of the market
  Method: Top-down industry reports + bottom-up calculation
  Example: "$50B global HR software market"

SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):
  Portion of TAM you can realistically target
  Example: "$15B US mid-market HR software"

SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):
  What you can capture in 3-5 years
  Example: "$500M (3-5% of SAM within 5 years)"

BOTTOM-UP CALCULATION (more credible than top-down):
  Number of target customers: 50,000 companies
  Average contract value: $30,000/year
  SAM = 50,000 * $30,000 = $1.5B

PRESENTATION TIPS:
  - Lead with bottom-up (more credible)
  - Support with top-down (validates scale)
  - Show market growth rate (growing > static)
  - Never use "if we just capture 1% of..." (investor red flag)
  - Show why market is expanding (not just big today)

MINIMUM MARKET SIZE BY STAGE:
  Seed: $1B+ TAM (believable path)
  Series A: $5B+ TAM with clear SAM wedge
  Series B+: $10B+ TAM with demonstrated SAM capture
```

### Slide 6: Product

```
PURPOSE: Show (not tell) what the product does and why users love it.

STRUCTURE:
  - 2-3 product screenshots or demo GIFs
  - Key features mapped to user outcomes (not feature names)
  - Social proof: user quote, NPS score, or engagement metric

SHOWING THE PRODUCT:
  Option A: High-fidelity screenshots with callouts
  Option B: Short product demo video (30-60 sec, embedded)
  Option C: Live demo (ONLY if product is bulletproof)

FEATURE PRESENTATION FRAMEWORK:
  ✗ BAD: "We have AI-powered analytics, real-time dashboards,
          and automated reporting"
  ✓ GOOD: "Managers save 5 hours/week because our system
           automatically surfaces the 3 metrics that matter"

  Feature → Benefit → Outcome → Evidence

PRODUCT EVOLUTION ROADMAP (optional):
  Show where product is going (not just where it is)
  V1: [current] → V2: [near-term] → Vision: [long-term]
  This signals ambition and long-term thinking
```

### Slide 7: Traction

```
PURPOSE: Prove that the market wants what you're building.
This is the most important slide for Series A and beyond.

WHAT CONSTITUTES TRACTION (by stage):

Pre-Seed:
  - Waitlist signups
  - LOIs from potential customers
  - Pilot conversations
  - Prototype user feedback

Seed:
  - Active users and engagement metrics
  - Revenue (even small)
  - Growth rate (MoM)
  - Retention cohorts
  - Notable customer logos

Series A:
  - $1M+ ARR (SaaS standard)
  - 15-30% MoM growth
  - Net revenue retention > 100%
  - LTV/CAC > 3x
  - Multiple acquisition channels

VISUALIZATION:
  - Revenue graph (up and to the right)
  - Cohort retention curves
  - Growth rate acceleration
  - Customer logo wall
  - Key metric call-outs

MOST POWERFUL TRACTION SLIDE:
  Single graph: revenue or key metric over time
  Clear upward trajectory
  Annotation of key inflection points
  One number that captures the story

  "We grew from $0 to $1.5M ARR in 9 months,
   with 20% MoM revenue growth"
```

### Slide 8: Business Model

```
PURPOSE: Show how you make money and why the economics work.

ELEMENTS:
  1. Revenue model (subscription, transaction, usage, etc.)
  2. Pricing structure (tiers, per-seat, per-usage)
  3. Unit economics (CAC, LTV, payback period, gross margin)
  4. Expansion revenue mechanics (upsell, cross-sell)

UNIT ECONOMICS PRESENTATION:

  ┌────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ UNIT ECONOMICS                     │
  │                                    │
  │ CAC:           $500                │
  │ Monthly ARPU:  $200                │
  │ Gross Margin:  80%                 │
  │ Monthly Churn:  3%                 │
  │ LTV:          $5,333               │
  │ LTV/CAC:       10.7x              │
  │ Payback:       3.1 months          │
  │ Net Revenue                        │
  │   Retention:   125%               │
  └────────────────────────────────────┘

  LTV = ARPU * Gross Margin / Monthly Churn
  LTV = $200 * 0.80 / 0.03 = $5,333

BUSINESS MODEL RED FLAGS TO ADDRESS:
  - Negative unit economics → show path to positive
  - Single revenue stream → show expansion plans
  - High concentration risk → show customer diversification
  - Low margins → explain why they improve at scale
```

### Slide 9: Competition

```
PURPOSE: Show market awareness and defensibility.
THIS IS NOT about proving you have no competition.

THE 2x2 MATRIX (standard but effective):
  Plot competitors on two axes that favor you
  You should be in the top-right quadrant

EXAMPLE AXES:
  X: Ease of use (left: complex, right: simple)
  Y: Depth of features (bottom: shallow, top: deep)

  Your company should be in "simple + deep" quadrant
  While competitors cluster in "complex + deep" or "simple + shallow"

ALTERNATIVE: COMPARISON TABLE
  Feature/Capability matrix showing where you win

WHAT INVESTORS ACTUALLY WANT TO KNOW:
  1. You understand the competitive landscape (not naive)
  2. You have a defensible moat (network effects, data, switching costs)
  3. You know why customers choose you over alternatives
  4. Your differentiation is sustainable (not easily copied)

MOAT CATEGORIES:
  - Network effects (more users → more value)
  - Data advantages (proprietary dataset)
  - Switching costs (deep integration)
  - Brand / community (trust and loyalty)
  - Regulatory (licenses, compliance)
  - Technology (patents, unique IP)
  - Cost advantage (structural efficiency)

NEVER SAY:
  "We have no competition" → signals naivete
  "Our competition is spreadsheets" → may be fine at seed, not at A
```

### Slide 10: Team

```
PURPOSE: Prove this is the team that can execute this specific plan.

WHAT TO INCLUDE:
  - Founder photos and titles
  - 1-line bio highlighting relevant experience
  - Key advisor or board member (if impressive)
  - Team size and composition
  - Key hires made or planned

FOUNDER-MARKET FIT NARRATIVE:
  "Why is THIS team uniquely positioned to solve THIS problem?"

  Examples:
  - Domain expertise: "10 years as a CFO" (for fintech)
  - Technical depth: "PhD in ML from MIT" (for AI company)
  - Customer insight: "Was the target customer" (for vertical SaaS)
  - Previous success: "Built and sold X to Y" (for serial founders)

WHAT INVESTORS EVALUATE:
  - Can this team build the product? (technical capability)
  - Can this team sell the product? (GTM capability)
  - Can this team recruit? (talent magnetism)
  - Can this team navigate adversity? (resilience signals)
  - Do the founders complement each other? (team dynamics)

TEAM GAPS:
  It's OK to have gaps -- acknowledge them.
  "We're actively recruiting a VP of Sales to complement
   our strong technical founding team."
  This shows self-awareness, which investors value.
```

### Slide 11: Financials

```
PURPOSE: Show the financial trajectory and how capital deployed
creates value.

FOR SEED STAGE:
  - High-level 3-year projection
  - Revenue trajectory (bottoms-up)
  - Key assumptions stated explicitly
  - Burn rate and runway

FOR SERIES A:
  - Detailed 3-year financial model
  - Revenue build (MRR waterfall)
  - Expense breakdown by category
  - Path to profitability (or next milestone)
  - Sensitivity analysis in appendix

FINANCIAL PROJECTION PRESENTATION:

  Year          Y1         Y2         Y3
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  Revenue      $500K      $2.5M      $8M
  Growth       -          400%       220%
  Gross Margin 65%        72%        78%
  OpEx         $2M        $4M        $7M
  Net Burn     $1.5M      $1.5M      $1M
  Cash Needed  $3M        -          Break-even

RULES FOR FINANCIAL PROJECTIONS:
  ✓ Bottom-up: build from unit economics, not "if we get 1% of market"
  ✓ State all assumptions explicitly
  ✓ Show sensitivity: "if growth is 50% of plan, we still have X runway"
  ✓ Be ambitious but defensible
  ✗ Never show a hockey stick without explaining the inflection
  ✗ Never project profitability in Year 1 of a SaaS business
  ✗ Never ignore competition in projections
```

### Slide 12: The Ask

```
PURPOSE: Clearly state what you need and what you'll do with it.

ELEMENTS:
  1. Amount raising
  2. Use of funds (3-4 buckets)
  3. Key milestones the capital will achieve
  4. Timeline to next raise (optional)

USE OF FUNDS FRAMEWORK:
  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ RAISING: $5M SERIES A                      │
  │                                            │
  │ Engineering (40%):    $2.0M                │
  │   - Hire 4 engineers                       │
  │   - Ship v2 platform                       │
  │                                            │
  │ Sales & Marketing (35%): $1.75M            │
  │   - Hire VP Sales + 3 AEs                  │
  │   - Scale paid acquisition                 │
  │                                            │
  │ Operations (15%):    $0.75M                │
  │   - Customer success team                  │
  │   - Infrastructure scaling                 │
  │                                            │
  │ Buffer (10%):        $0.50M                │
  │   - Working capital                        │
  │                                            │
  │ MILESTONES BY MONTH 18:                    │
  │   → $3M ARR                                │
  │   → 100+ enterprise customers              │
  │   → Positive unit economics                │
  │   → Series B ready                         │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────┘

THE MILESTONE BRIDGE:
  This capital takes us from [current state] to [milestone]
  That milestone positions us for [next fundraise at higher valuation]
```

## Deck Design Principles

### Visual Standards

```
DESIGN RULES:

1. ONE IDEA PER SLIDE
   - If you need two ideas, use two slides
   - Cognitive overload kills attention

2. MAXIMUM 30 WORDS PER SLIDE (presentation deck)
   - Headlines, not paragraphs
   - Bullets, not sentences
   - Data, not descriptions

3. CONSISTENT VISUAL LANGUAGE
   - One font family (two weights maximum)
   - One color palette (3-4 colors)
   - Consistent chart styling
   - Company brand applied throughout

4. WHITE SPACE IS YOUR FRIEND
   - Don't fill every pixel
   - Let key points breathe
   - Focal point should be obvious

5. DATA VISUALIZATION
   - One chart per slide
   - Label axes and data points
   - Use color to highlight the key insight
   - Remove chart junk (gridlines, legends if obvious)

HANDOFF TO DESIGN BRAIN:
  For companies with significant fundraising targets ($5M+),
  engage the Design Brain for professional deck design.
  Content and narrative: Investor Brain
  Visual execution: Design Brain
```

## Common Deck Mistakes

```
TOP 10 PITCH DECK FAILURES (per First Round Capital research)

1. Too many slides (>15 for presentation)
2. All text, no visuals
3. Feature list instead of value proposition
4. Market size from "top down only" (not bottoms-up)
5. No clear business model
6. Team slide buried or missing
7. Financial projections without assumptions
8. Competitive slide says "no competition"
9. The ask is vague ("we're exploring options")
10. No narrative arc (just facts, no story)
```
