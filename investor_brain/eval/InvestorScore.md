# Investor Score -- Quality Enforcement (Authoritative)

This document defines how fundraising and investor relations quality is evaluated.
Every investor-facing deliverable and process must be scored before it is considered ready.

If quality is not measurable, it is not enforced.
If fundraising is not rigorous, capital is left on the table or taken on bad terms.

---

## SCORING RULES (MANDATORY)

Each investor-facing deliverable or process must be scored across the following dimensions.
Score each category from **1 (poor)** to **5 (excellent)**.

### Hard Fail Dimensions
These dimensions are critical. Score <3 = immediate rework required:
- **Financial Story**
- **Term Sheet Literacy**
- **Cap Table Accuracy**

### Passing Criteria
- Average score across all dimensions >= 4.0
- No hard-fail dimension < 3
- No dimension < 2 under any circumstance
- Narrative Quality is NOT a hard fail -- evaluated holistically

### Holistic Note
Narrative Quality is evaluated holistically. A technically sound pitch with a weak story can be improved. A great story with wrong numbers cannot be shipped at all.

## 1. NARRATIVE QUALITY

**Question:**
Does the pitch tell a compelling story with a clear problem-solution arc, an inspiring yet credible vision, and emotional resonance?

### What to Evaluate
- Story arc is clear (problem -> insight -> solution -> traction -> vision)
- Problem is visceral and relatable (not abstract)
- Solution is presented as inevitable given the insight
- Vision is ambitious yet credible (not delusional)
- Founder-market fit is demonstrated (why this team for this problem)
- Competitive narrative is honest (acknowledges reality, shows differentiation)
- Urgency is conveyed (why now, why this moment)
- Emotional hook exists (investors remember stories, not spreadsheets)

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- Compelling story arc that builds naturally; problem makes the room feel pain; solution feels inevitable; vision is bold and credible; founder-market fit is unquestionable; "why now" is urgent; investor leaves the meeting thinking about your company
- **4** -- Strong narrative; clear problem-solution arc; vision is compelling; founder fit evident; good "why now"; memorable but not exceptional
- **3** -- Adequate narrative; story is logical but not emotional; vision is reasonable but not inspiring; founder fit mentioned but not proven; "why now" is weak
- **2** -- Narrative is disjointed; problem and solution disconnected; vision is either too small or unbelievable; no founder fit story; no urgency
- **1** -- No narrative; feature list instead of story; no emotional connection; vision unclear; pitch is forgettable

### Story Arc Framework

```
1. HOOK (10 seconds)
   One sentence that makes the investor lean in.

2. PROBLEM (2 minutes)
   Make the problem visceral. Use a specific story or data point.
   The investor should feel the pain.

3. INSIGHT (1 minute)
   What have you figured out that others have not?
   This is the "aha" moment.

4. SOLUTION (2 minutes)
   Demo or describe the product as the inevitable result of the insight.
   Show, do not tell.

5. TRACTION (2 minutes)
   Prove it works. Numbers, customers, growth, engagement.
   Let the data validate the story.

6. MARKET (1 minute)
   Big enough to matter. Growing. Timing is right.

7. TEAM (1 minute)
   Why this team is uniquely positioned to win.

8. ASK (30 seconds)
   Clear, specific, confident.
```

### Common Failures
- Starting with "We are a platform that..." (boring, no hook)
- Problem statement that is too abstract ("businesses struggle with efficiency")
- Solution that is described as a feature list, not a narrative
- Vision that is either too small (not VC-scale) or absurd (we will own everything)
- No "why now" (if this problem exists for 10 years, why solve it today?)
- Pitch deck with 40+ slides
- Founder tells their resume instead of their story

### Remediation
Score <4 --> Rewrite pitch using story arc framework. Test with 3 people outside the company. Get honest feedback on emotional impact. Iterate.

## 2. FINANCIAL STORY

**Question:**
Are unit economics sound, growth trajectory justified with data, and path to profitability clear and credible?

### What to Evaluate
- Unit economics are clearly defined (CAC, LTV, payback period, gross margin)
- LTV/CAC ratio is healthy (>3x) or path to 3x is shown
- Growth trajectory is justified by data (not aspirational hockey sticks)
- Revenue model is clear and defensible
- Path to profitability is articulated (not "we will figure it out later")
- Burn rate and runway are transparent
- Use of funds is specific and tied to milestones
- Financial projections have stated assumptions that can be tested

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- Unit economics are proven and compelling; LTV/CAC >3x demonstrated; growth backed by cohort data and bottoms-up analysis; revenue model validated; clear path to profitability with milestones; burn rate transparent; use of funds tied to specific outcomes; assumptions documented and testable
- **4** -- Unit economics are solid with minor gaps; LTV/CAC trending to 3x; growth supported by data with reasonable assumptions; revenue model clear; profitability path outlined; burn rate known; use of funds specific
- **3** -- Unit economics are early but directional; LTV/CAC unclear; growth based on top-line numbers without cohort analysis; revenue model exists but untested; profitability path vague; burn rate disclosed; use of funds general
- **2** -- Unit economics not calculated; no LTV/CAC analysis; growth is a hockey stick with no basis; revenue model undefined; no profitability discussion; burn rate unclear; use of funds is "growth"
- **1** -- No financial story; revenue projections are fantasy; no understanding of unit economics; no burn rate awareness; use of funds is a black box

### Financial Model Requirements

| Component | Must Include | Red Flag |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| Revenue model | Per-unit economics, pricing justified | "We will monetize later" |
| Growth projections | Bottom-up model, cohort data | Top-down TAM percentage only |
| Unit economics | CAC, LTV, payback, gross margin | "We do not know our CAC yet" |
| Burn analysis | Monthly burn, runway, milestones before next raise | "We will figure it out" |
| Use of funds | Specific allocation with expected outcomes | "General working capital" |
| Sensitivity analysis | Best/base/worst case | Single scenario only |
| Assumptions | All major assumptions stated and testable | Hidden or unstated assumptions |

### Common Failures
- Projecting $100M revenue in Year 3 with no basis
- Not knowing CAC or how it is calculated
- LTV calculated on assumptions, not actual retention data
- "We just need 1% of the market" (top-down TAM fallacy)
- Burn rate not disclosed or minimized
- No sensitivity analysis (only shows the optimistic case)
- Confusing revenue and bookings
- Ignoring churn in revenue projections

### Remediation
Score <3 --> STOP. Fix financials before any investor meeting. Calculate actual unit economics. Build bottoms-up growth model. Create sensitivity analysis. This is a hard fail dimension.

## 3. MARKET CREDIBILITY

**Question:**
Is the TAM/SAM/SOM defensible, validated bottom-up, and is the competitive landscape honestly represented?

### What to Evaluate
- TAM/SAM/SOM is calculated (not just TAM)
- Bottom-up market sizing validates top-down estimates
- Market is growing (with cited data)
- Competition is acknowledged honestly (not "we have no competition")
- Differentiation is clear and defensible
- Market timing is justified (why now)
- Customer segments are defined and validated
- Barriers to entry are identified (both for you and against competitors)

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- TAM/SAM/SOM all calculated with bottom-up validation; market growth supported by multiple data sources; competition thoroughly analyzed with honest positioning; differentiation is demonstrated (not just claimed); timing argument is compelling; customer segments validated with data; barriers to entry clear
- **4** -- TAM/SAM/SOM present with reasonable methodology; market growth cited; competition analyzed; differentiation articulated; timing reasonable; segments defined; barriers considered
- **3** -- TAM calculated (SAM/SOM weak); market growth assumed; competition mentioned superficially; differentiation is features-based; timing not addressed; segments broad; barriers unclear
- **2** -- Only TAM cited (top-down); no market growth data; competition ignored or dismissed; no real differentiation; no timing argument; no segmentation; no barriers analysis
- **1** -- No market sizing; no competitive analysis; "we have no competition"; undifferentiated; no market context

### Market Sizing Methodology

**Top-Down (validates scale):**
```
TAM = Total industry revenue or total addressable users x average revenue
SAM = TAM filtered by your segment, geography, and reach
SOM = SAM filtered by realistic capture rate in 3-5 years
```

**Bottom-Up (validates reality):**
```
Number of potential customers you can reach
x Realistic conversion rate
x Average revenue per customer
= Bottom-up SOM
```

**Rule:** Bottom-up SOM must be within 2x of top-down SOM. If they diverge wildly, one model is wrong.

### Competition Framework

| Axis | Your Company | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Primary value prop | | | | |
| Target segment | | | | |
| Pricing model | | | | |
| Key weakness | | | | |
| Key strength | | | | |
| Moat/defensibility | | | | |

**Rule:** Never say "we have no competition." The current alternative (even if it is spreadsheets or doing nothing) is the competition.

### Common Failures
- $50B TAM with no SAM or SOM
- Market sizing from a single analyst report without validation
- Claiming no competition (instant credibility loss)
- Feature comparison table that makes you win everything (investors see through this)
- No bottom-up validation of market size
- Ignoring adjacent competition
- Market timing argument is absent

### Remediation
Score <4 --> Recalculate market with both top-down and bottom-up. Conduct honest competitive analysis. Define specific differentiation. Build timing argument.

## 4. PROCESS MANAGEMENT

**Question:**
Is the fundraising pipeline tracked, follow-up maintained, and warm introductions leveraged effectively?

### What to Evaluate
- Investor pipeline tracked in CRM (not email threads)
- Pipeline stages defined (target -> intro -> meeting -> partner meeting -> term sheet -> close)
- Follow-up cadence maintained (no dropped conversations)
- Warm introductions prioritized over cold outreach
- Investor targeting is strategic (right fund, right stage, right thesis)
- Materials personalized per investor (not one-size-fits-all)
- Rejection reasons tracked and analyzed
- Timeline managed proactively (create urgency without desperation)

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- Pipeline in CRM with all stages tracked; follow-up cadence automated; 90%+ meetings from warm intros; investor targeting matched to fund thesis; materials customized per investor; rejection reasons systematically analyzed; timeline creates healthy urgency
- **4** -- Pipeline tracked; follow-up consistent; mostly warm intros; good targeting; some customization; rejections noted; timeline managed
- **3** -- Pipeline exists but not current; follow-up inconsistent; mix of warm and cold; targeting adequate; generic materials; rejections not analyzed; timeline reactive
- **2** -- No pipeline tracking; follow-ups dropped; mostly cold outreach; poor targeting; same deck for everyone; no rejection analysis; no timeline management
- **1** -- No process; spraying and praying; no tracking; no follow-up; cold emails only; no targeting; no learning from rejections

### Pipeline Management Framework

| Stage | Definition | Expected Conversion | Action Required |
|-------|-----------|--------------------|-----------------|
| Target | Identified as potential fit | N/A | Research fund thesis, portfolio, check size |
| Warm Intro | Introduction made | 60% to meeting | Thank introducer, personalize outreach |
| First Meeting | Initial pitch | 40% to follow-up | Send follow-up materials within 24 hours |
| Partner Meeting | Full partnership pitch | 30% to term sheet | Prepare for deep-dive questions |
| Due Diligence | Active evaluation | 50% to term sheet | Respond to all requests within 48 hours |
| Term Sheet | Offer received | 70% to close | Evaluate terms, negotiate, decide |
| Close | Money in bank | 95% | Legal review, sign, wire |

### Common Failures
- No CRM (tracking in head or scattered notes)
- Warm intro requested without context (lazy asks)
- Same pitch deck sent to early-stage and growth-stage investors
- Not researching fund thesis before meeting (wasting time)
- Following up once and giving up
- No urgency creation (fundraise drags on forever)
- Not tracking why investors pass

### Remediation
Score <4 --> Set up pipeline tracking. Map warm intro network. Research each target investor. Create follow-up templates. Analyze rejection patterns.

## 5. TERM SHEET LITERACY

**Question:**
Are all terms understood, founder-friendly vs investor-friendly provisions recognized, and negotiable items identified?

### What to Evaluate
- All economic terms understood (valuation, preference, participation, anti-dilution)
- All control terms understood (board composition, protective provisions, drag-along)
- Founder-friendly vs investor-friendly provisions identified
- Negotiable items identified with priority ranking
- Implications of each term modeled for future rounds
- Legal counsel engaged before signing
- Term sheet compared to market benchmarks
- Impact on existing shareholders modeled

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- Every term understood with implications modeled; founder-friendly vs investor-friendly clearly mapped; negotiable items prioritized; future-round impact modeled; legal counsel engaged; benchmarked against market; existing shareholder impact calculated; negotiation strategy prepared
- **4** -- Major terms understood; founder/investor alignment recognized; key negotiable items identified; some future modeling; legal involved; general benchmarking
- **3** -- Core terms understood; some terms unclear; limited founder/investor analysis; negotiable items not prioritized; minimal future modeling; legal engaged late
- **2** -- Superficial term understanding; cannot distinguish founder-friendly from investor-friendly; no negotiation strategy; no future modeling; legal not engaged
- **1** -- Does not understand term sheet; signs without analysis; no legal counsel; no awareness of term implications

### Critical Terms to Evaluate

| Term | What It Means | Founder Impact | Negotiable? |
|------|--------------|----------------|-------------|
| Pre-money valuation | Company value before investment | Ownership dilution | High |
| Liquidation preference | Who gets paid first in exit | Founder payout in downside scenarios | High |
| Participation | Double-dip on exit proceeds | Reduces founder take in moderate exits | High |
| Anti-dilution | Protection against down rounds | Founder dilution in tough times | Medium |
| Board composition | Who controls the board | Decision authority | High |
| Protective provisions | Investor veto rights | Operational constraints | Medium |
| Option pool | Shares reserved for employees | Pre-money dilution trick | High |
| Drag-along | Force sale rights | Can force founder exit | Low (standard) |
| Pay-to-play | Require follow-on investment | Protects against passive investors | Medium |
| Pro-rata rights | Right to invest in future rounds | Investor maintains ownership | Low |
| Information rights | What you must share | Reporting burden | Low |
| Founder vesting | Founder shares vest over time | Can lose equity if leave | Medium |

### Term Sheet Red Flags

| Red Flag | Why It Matters |
|----------|---------------|
| >1x participating preferred | Severely reduces founder payout |
| Full ratchet anti-dilution | Devastating in down rounds |
| Cumulative dividends | Grows the preference stack silently |
| Investor majority on board at seed/A | Too much control too early |
| Broad protective provisions | Operational handcuffs |
| Option pool > 15% at seed (pre-money) | Hidden valuation haircut |
| No-shop period > 60 days | Eliminates competitive pressure |
| Excessive information rights | Administrative burden |
| Milestone-based tranches with loose milestones | Creates ongoing uncertainty |
| Redemption rights | Forced buyback without your control |

### Common Failures
- Not knowing what "participating preferred" means
- Accepting first term sheet without negotiation
- Not involving legal counsel until signing day
- Focusing only on valuation and ignoring other terms
- Not modeling term impact on future fundraising rounds
- Not understanding how the option pool affects effective valuation
- Signing without reading (yes, this happens)

### Remediation
Score <3 --> STOP. Do not proceed with any term sheet negotiation. Engage experienced startup counsel. Model all terms. Study NVCA model term sheet. This is a hard fail dimension.

## 6. BOARD MANAGEMENT

**Question:**
Are board materials professional, meetings productive, and the investor relationship proactively managed?

### What to Evaluate
- Board materials sent in advance (5+ business days before meeting)
- Materials are concise, honest, and data-rich
- Meeting agenda is structured and time-managed
- Key decisions are framed with options (not surprises)
- Bad news is delivered proactively (not hidden until asked)
- Follow-up items are tracked to completion
- Board member expertise is leveraged between meetings
- Relationship is maintained outside of formal meetings

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- Materials sent 7+ days in advance; concise and visually clear; agenda drives productive discussion; decisions prepared with 2-3 options and recommendation; bad news delivered before good news with plan; all action items tracked and completed; board members consulted individually between meetings; relationship is partnership, not reporting
- **4** -- Materials sent 5+ days in advance; clear and comprehensive; agenda structured; decisions framed; bad news delivered honestly; most action items tracked; some between-meeting engagement
- **3** -- Materials sent 3-5 days in advance; adequate but wordy; agenda loose; some decisions sprung on board; bad news minimized; action items inconsistently tracked; minimal between-meeting engagement
- **2** -- Materials sent <3 days or late; unclear or incomplete; no real agenda; board surprised by decisions; bad news hidden; action items not tracked; no relationship outside meetings
- **1** -- No materials or last-minute; meetings are chaos; no agenda; bad news discovered by board; no follow-through; adversarial relationship

### Board Materials Template

```
1. Executive Summary (1 page)
   - Key highlights and lowlights
   - Decisions needed from the board
   - Help wanted (specific asks)

2. Key Metrics Dashboard (1 page)
   - Revenue/growth metrics
   - Unit economics
   - Runway
   - Key product metrics

3. Financial Update (1-2 pages)
   - P&L summary
   - Cash position and burn
   - Budget vs actuals
   - Runway projection

4. Product Update (1 page)
   - Shipped last quarter
   - In progress
   - Next quarter priorities

5. Team Update (1 page)
   - Headcount changes
   - Key hires/departures
   - Culture/morale

6. Strategic Discussion Topic (1-2 pages)
   - Background and context
   - Options with pros/cons
   - Recommendation
   - Decision request

Appendix: Detailed financials, customer list, pipeline
```

### Common Failures
- Board deck is 60 slides of status updates
- No strategic discussion (just reporting, no value extracted)
- Bad news hidden until investors discover it themselves
- No asks (board cannot help if you do not ask)
- Meeting runs over with no resolution
- Follow-up items from last meeting not addressed

### Remediation
Score <4 --> Restructure board materials using template. Set meeting agenda 1 week in advance. Prepare 1 strategic discussion topic. Track all action items. Have 1:1 with each board member between meetings.

## 7. CAP TABLE ACCURACY

**Question:**
Is the cap table accurate, dilution modeled correctly for future rounds, waterfall analysis current, and ESOP planning thoughtful?

### What to Evaluate
- Cap table is accurate and current (verified by legal)
- All shares, options, warrants, and SAFEs/notes are reflected
- Dilution is modeled for next 2-3 rounds
- Waterfall analysis shows payout at various exit valuations
- ESOP is sized appropriately and planned for future grants
- Convertible instruments are correctly modeled (conversion scenarios)
- Cap table is maintained in a proper tool (not just a spreadsheet)
- All amendments and changes are documented

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- Cap table verified by counsel and current to the day; all instruments reflected correctly; dilution modeled for 3+ future scenarios; waterfall analysis at 5+ exit valuations; ESOP sized with 2-year hiring plan; convertibles modeled with all trigger scenarios; maintained in dedicated tool; all changes documented with board resolutions
- **4** -- Cap table current within 30 days; all major instruments reflected; dilution modeled for next round; waterfall at 3+ exit valuations; ESOP adequate; convertibles modeled; tool or well-maintained spreadsheet; changes documented
- **3** -- Cap table mostly current; some instruments may be missing or unclear; dilution modeled loosely; waterfall at 1-2 exit valuations; ESOP exists but not planned; convertible conversion unclear; spreadsheet; documentation incomplete
- **2** -- Cap table out of date; missing instruments; no dilution modeling; no waterfall; ESOP not planned; convertibles not modeled; messy spreadsheet; poor documentation
- **1** -- No cap table or fundamentally inaccurate; no awareness of dilution; no waterfall; no ESOP planning; convertible instruments lost track of; no tool; no documentation

### Waterfall Analysis Requirements

Model payout at these exit valuations (at minimum):
1. **Downside:** 0.5x invested capital
2. **Breakeven:** 1x invested capital
3. **Moderate:** 3x invested capital
4. **Target:** 10x invested capital
5. **Home run:** 50x invested capital

For each scenario, show:
- Amount to each investor class (by preference seniority)
- Amount to common shareholders
- Amount to ESOP holders
- Effective per-share price for founders
- Whether participation cap is triggered
- Whether conversion is advantageous (preferred vs common)

### Cap Table Red Flags

| Red Flag | Risk |
|----------|------|
| Founder owns < 50% at seed | Control risk, motivation concern |
| ESOP > 20% before Series A | Over-diluted before scaling |
| Outstanding SAFEs/notes not tracked | Surprise dilution at conversion |
| No ESOP refresh planned | Cannot attract talent |
| Cap table in someone's email | Will be wrong |
| 409A not current (> 12 months old) | Legal and tax risk |
| Departed employees with unvested shares not handled | Phantom equity |

### Common Failures
- Not knowing your own ownership percentage accurately
- Forgetting about SAFEs that have not converted yet
- Not modeling dilution before accepting a term sheet
- ESOP pool created without a hiring plan to size it
- Waterfall analysis never run (surprises at exit)
- Using a generic spreadsheet that does not handle preferences correctly
- 409A valuation expired

### Remediation
Score <3 --> STOP. Get cap table verified by counsel immediately. Model all outstanding instruments. Run waterfall analysis. Plan ESOP. This is a hard fail dimension.

## 8. INVESTOR RELATIONS

**Question:**
Are updates consistent, is the founder transparent about bad news, and are asks specific and actionable?

### What to Evaluate
- Monthly or quarterly updates sent consistently
- Updates include both good and bad news
- Metrics are tracked consistently over time (same format)
- Asks are specific and actionable (not "help us with intros")
- Responses to investor questions are timely (< 48 hours)
- Relationship is maintained between updates (not only when you need something)
- Investor expertise is leveraged for advice (not just money)
- Trust is actively built through transparency

### Scoring Guide
- **5** -- Monthly updates sent consistently for 12+ months; balanced good/bad news; consistent metric tracking; specific asks with context; responses within 24 hours; regular between-update engagement; investors proactively offer help; deep trust established through transparency
- **4** -- Monthly or quarterly updates consistent; mostly balanced; metrics tracked; asks are clear; responses within 48 hours; some between-update engagement; investors responsive; good trust
- **3** -- Updates sent but inconsistently; skew toward good news; metrics change format; asks are vague; responses within 1 week; minimal engagement between updates; trust adequate
- **2** -- Updates rare; only good news shared; no consistent metrics; no asks; slow responses; only contact when raising; trust thin
- **1** -- No updates; bad news hidden until discovered; no metrics; only appear when desperate for money; investors feel blindsided; trust broken

### Investor Update Template

```
Subject: [Company] - [Month] Update

TLDR (3 bullets max)
- [Biggest win]
- [Biggest challenge]
- [Key metric]

KEY METRICS
| Metric | This Month | Last Month | Trend |
|--------|------------|------------|-------|
| MRR/ARR | | | |
| Growth Rate | | | |
| Burn Rate | | | |
| Runway | | | |
| Key Product Metric | | | |
| Customers | | | |

WINS
- [What went well]

CHALLENGES
- [What did not go well -- be honest]

ASKS (be specific)
- "Looking for intro to [specific person/company] for [specific reason]"
- "Need advice on [specific decision] -- context: [brief background]"
- "Hiring for [specific role] -- job description attached"

WHAT IS NEXT
- [Top 3 priorities for next month]
```

### Common Failures
- Only sending updates when things are going well
- Updates that read like press releases (all spin, no substance)
- Vague asks ("we need help with sales") instead of specific ("looking for intro to VP Sales at Company X because they match our ICP")
- Metrics that change format or disappear when they decline
- Going silent for months and then appearing with an emergency
- Not leveraging investor expertise (expensive advice you are already paying for)

### Remediation
Score <4 --> Set up monthly update cadence. Use template. Include both wins and challenges. Make asks specific. Respond to investor messages within 48 hours. Schedule 1:1 calls quarterly.

## FINAL INVESTOR SCORE DECISION

**Hard Fail Dimensions (Financial Story, Term Sheet Literacy, Cap Table Accuracy):**
- Score <3 --> **IMMEDIATE REWORK REQUIRED**

**All Dimensions:**
- Average score >= 4.0 --> **INVESTOR-READY**
- Average score < 4.0 --> **NOT READY -- REWORK REQUIRED**

**Narrative Quality:**
- NOT a hard fail
- Evaluated holistically (authentic storytelling, unique angles qualify)
- Score <3 triggers coaching, not automatic failure

Scores must be stated explicitly before final output.

## SCORING CALIBRATION GUIDE

### What a "5" Fundraise Looks Like
- Pitch tells a story that investors cannot stop thinking about
- Unit economics are proven and compelling
- Market sizing is bottom-up validated and credible
- Pipeline is managed like a professional sales process
- Every term in the sheet is understood and negotiated wisely
- Board meetings are productive and relationship-building
- Cap table is airtight and future-modeled
- Investors feel like trusted partners, not ATMs

### What a "3" Fundraise Looks Like
- Pitch is logical but not memorable
- Financials are directional but incomplete
- Market sizing is top-down only
- Pipeline is tracked informally
- Terms are mostly understood
- Board meetings are status updates
- Cap table is probably correct
- Investor relationship is transactional

### What a "1" Fundraise Looks Like
- No pitch narrative, just features
- No financial understanding
- No market analysis
- No process, just random meetings
- Terms not understood
- Board meetings are adversarial or nonexistent
- Cap table is wrong
- Investors are surprised by bad news

---

## ENFORCEMENT RULE

Quality is enforced, not assumed.
Do not justify low scores.
Rework until standards are met.
Fundraising that does not meet the bar does not go to market -- it goes back to preparation.
