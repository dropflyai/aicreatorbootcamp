# Investor Brain -- Benchmark Tests (Authoritative)

These scenarios test the Investor Brain's ability to handle real-world fundraising and investor relations challenges.
Each scenario must be worked through completely. Partial answers are failures.

A benchmark is not a quiz. It is a simulation of actual fundraising pressure.
If the Investor Brain cannot handle these scenarios rigorously, it cannot advise on real capital decisions.

---

## HOW TO USE THESE BENCHMARKS

1. Present each scenario to the Investor Brain
2. Evaluate the response against the criteria listed
3. Score using InvestorScore.md dimensions
4. A passing response must score >= 4.0 average across applicable dimensions
5. Hard fail on any dimension = scenario failed

## SCENARIO 1: Aggressive Term Sheet Analysis

**Prompt:**
"VC offers $5M at $20M pre-money with 1x participating preferred, 2x full ratchet anti-dilution, a 20% option pool (pre-money), and 2 of 5 board seats. They want an answer in 72 hours. Analyze and counter-propose."

**Must Include:**
- Term-by-term analysis with founder impact
- Effective valuation calculation (pre-money minus option pool = effective pre)
- Participating preferred impact at multiple exit scenarios (waterfall analysis)
- Full ratchet anti-dilution danger explained with down-round scenario
- Board composition implications (2 of 5 is fine, but protective provisions matter)
- Specific counter-proposal with justification for each change
- Negotiation strategy (what to concede, what to hold firm)
- Market benchmarking (are these terms standard for stage/round?)
- 72-hour pressure assessment (is this legitimate urgency or pressure tactic?)
- Walk-away criteria (at what terms is this deal not worth taking?)
- Alternative financing options to strengthen negotiating position

**Fail If:**
- Accepts terms as reasonable without analysis
- Does not calculate effective valuation
- Does not run waterfall analysis on participating preferred
- Does not explain full ratchet danger
- Counter-proposal is vague ("negotiate better terms")
- Does not address the time pressure
- No walk-away framework

## SCENARIO 2: Fundraising Process Failure Diagnosis

**Prompt:**
"You are 8 months into fundraising. 40 meetings, 12 second meetings, 0 term sheets. Runway is 6 months. Morale is low. The team is distracted. Diagnose what is wrong and pivot the strategy."

**Must Include:**
- Systematic diagnosis framework (is it the market, the pitch, the process, or the business?)
- Meeting-to-advance conversion analysis (where in the funnel are deals dying?)
- Feedback synthesis (what are investors actually saying in their passes?)
- Pattern recognition (are specific investor types passing? at specific stages?)
- Honest assessment of whether the business is fundable in current state
- Pivot strategy options (change pitch, change target, change terms, change business)
- Bridge financing options to extend runway
- Plan B if fundraising fails (profitability path, acqui-hire, wind down)
- Team communication plan (morale management during crisis)
- Revised timeline with milestones
- Decision framework: when to stop fundraising

**Fail If:**
- Says "keep trying" without changing approach
- Does not analyze where in the funnel deals are dying
- Does not consider that the business itself may need to change
- No Plan B
- No bridge financing consideration
- Ignores team morale and communication

## SCENARIO 3: Board Conflict Resolution

**Prompt:**
"Board member (lead investor) wants you to cut burn 50% immediately. You believe growth investment is critical -- you are seeing product-market fit signals and cutting would kill momentum. Your other board member (co-investor) is neutral. Present your case."

**Must Include:**
- Data-driven case for growth investment (specific PMF signals with evidence)
- Empathy for investor's perspective (acknowledge the concern)
- Financial scenario analysis (what happens if you cut 50% vs maintain vs compromise)
- Compromise proposal with milestones (invest for X months, reassess at Y)
- Risk acknowledgment (what if you are wrong about PMF?)
- Milestone-based framework (under what conditions would you agree to cuts?)
- Coalition building strategy (how to get the neutral board member on your side)
- Communication approach (data first, then narrative, then ask)
- Fallback position if you lose the vote
- Precedent setting awareness (how this decision shapes future board dynamics)

**Fail If:**
- Dismisses investor concern without data
- Only presents the growth case without acknowledging downside
- No compromise option
- No milestone-based proposal
- Does not consider the neutral board member's role
- Emotional argument instead of data-driven
- No fallback position

## SCENARIO 4: Down Round Navigation

**Prompt:**
"Your last round was Series A at $50M post-money. 18 months later, you need to raise again. Growth is 40% YoY (not the 100% promised), burn is $500K/month, runway is 5 months. Best offer is $30M pre-money. Navigate this situation."

**Must Include:**
- Honest assessment of the situation (this is a down round)
- Anti-dilution implications for existing investors and founders
- Options analysis: take the down round, find bridge, cut to profitability
- Existing investor engagement strategy (do they participate? do they block?)
- Employee impact assessment (underwater options, morale, retention)
- Communication plan for all stakeholders (team, investors, customers)
- Term negotiation specific to down rounds (pay-to-play, anti-dilution waiver)
- Option repricing or refresh consideration for employees
- Narrative reframing (how to position the company post-down round)
- Forward plan that makes the down round the last bad news

**Fail If:**
- Pretends this is not a down round
- Does not calculate anti-dilution impact
- Ignores employee morale and option impact
- No stakeholder communication plan
- Does not explore alternatives to the down round
- No forward plan for recovery

## SCENARIO 5: SAFE and Convertible Note Complexity

**Prompt:**
"You raised $2M on SAFEs at various caps: $500K at $6M cap, $800K at $8M cap, $700K at $10M cap (all post-money SAFEs). Now you are raising a priced round. A VC offers $3M at $12M pre-money. Calculate the cap table post-close and explain to the founders what their ownership will be."

**Must Include:**
- Step-by-step SAFE conversion calculation for each tranche
- Share price calculation from the priced round
- Conversion price for each SAFE based on cap
- Post-money ownership table showing every stakeholder
- Founder dilution calculation (before and after)
- Comparison of founder ownership vs what they expected
- Option pool impact (if required by VC)
- Clear explanation that is understandable to non-financial founders
- Visual or tabular representation of the cap table
- Identification of any conversion complications

**Fail If:**
- Math is wrong
- Does not handle multiple cap levels correctly
- Conflates pre-money and post-money SAFE mechanics
- Cannot explain the result in plain language
- Forgets the option pool in the calculation
- Does not show founder dilution clearly

## SCENARIO 6: Investor Due Diligence Preparation

**Prompt:**
"You are about to enter due diligence with a Tier 1 VC. They sent a 50-item due diligence checklist. Your data room is empty. You have 1 week. Prioritize and execute."

**Must Include:**
- Due diligence item categorization (financial, legal, product, team, market)
- Priority ranking (what kills the deal if missing vs nice-to-have)
- Realistic 1-week timeline with daily milestones
- Delegation plan (who prepares what)
- Red flag identification (are there items that could kill the deal?)
- Narrative preparation (how to present challenging items)
- Data room organization structure
- Items that need legal counsel
- Items that can be prepared in parallel
- Communication with the VC (set expectations on timeline)
- Quality standard (complete and accurate, not rushed and wrong)

**Fail If:**
- Tries to do all 50 items with equal priority
- No delegation plan
- Does not identify potential deal-killers
- No communication plan with the VC
- No quality standard (rushes everything)
- Ignores items that need legal preparation

## SCENARIO 7: Multi-Term Sheet Negotiation

**Prompt:**
"You have received 2 term sheets for your Series A. Fund A: $8M at $32M pre, 1x non-participating preferred, 1 board seat, clean terms. Fund B: $10M at $40M pre, 1x participating preferred, 2 board seats, strong protective provisions. Fund B has a better brand name. Compare, decide, and negotiate."

**Must Include:**
- Side-by-side term comparison table
- Economic analysis at multiple exit scenarios (participation matters at mid-range exits)
- Control analysis (board composition + protective provisions)
- Brand value assessment (is Fund B's brand worth the worse terms?)
- Effective valuation comparison (higher valuation with worse terms may be worse deal)
- Negotiation strategy (use competition to improve both offers)
- Recommendation with clear reasoning
- Counter-proposal to preferred fund
- Relationship consideration (which fund is better long-term partner?)
- Speed consideration (can you play them against each other without losing both?)

**Fail If:**
- Picks the higher valuation without analyzing other terms
- Does not run exit scenario analysis
- Ignores protective provisions (these matter enormously)
- Does not use competitive pressure to negotiate
- No consideration of long-term partnership quality
- Decision is based on brand name alone

## SCENARIO 8: Pivot Communication to Investors

**Prompt:**
"After 12 months of building Product A, data shows that a tangential feature (Product B) has 5x more engagement and clear willingness-to-pay signals. You want to pivot the company from A to B. Your seed investors invested in Product A. Communicate the pivot and maintain their confidence."

**Must Include:**
- Data-driven case for the pivot (engagement metrics, revenue signals, customer feedback)
- Honest assessment of Product A (what worked, what did not)
- Product B opportunity sizing (market, competition, differentiation)
- Financial implications (burn, runway, milestones for Product B)
- Risk assessment (what if Product B also does not work?)
- Communication strategy (do not surprise; foreshadow, then present)
- 1:1 conversations before formal announcement
- New milestone framework (what does success look like for Product B?)
- Ask: do they need to re-approve, or is this within founder authority?
- Team implications (can the team execute Product B?)
- Timeline for the pivot

**Fail If:**
- Announces pivot without data
- Does not acknowledge Product A failure
- No financial analysis of the pivot
- Surprises investors (no foreshadowing)
- No new milestone framework
- Ignores the trust dynamic (investors chose Product A)

## SCENARIO 9: Founder Secondary Sale Request

**Prompt:**
"A Series B investor offers the founder the option to sell $1M of their shares in a secondary transaction as part of the round. The founder has been taking below-market salary for 3 years. Is this a good idea? How should it be structured?"

**Must Include:**
- Assessment of appropriateness (founder salary sacrifice justifies some liquidity)
- Signaling analysis (does secondary signal lack of confidence?)
- Market benchmarking (what is normal for secondaries at this stage?)
- Structuring options (who buys, at what price, with what restrictions)
- Tax implications overview (long-term capital gains, 409A considerations)
- Investor reaction management (how existing investors will perceive this)
- Amount calibration (is $1M appropriate given company stage and founder equity?)
- Restrictions and lockup recommendations
- Board approval process
- Communication approach to existing investors
- Alternative liquidity options if secondary is not ideal

**Fail If:**
- Simply says yes or no without analysis
- Does not address the signaling risk
- Ignores tax implications
- No consideration of existing investor reaction
- No market benchmarking
- Does not discuss structuring options

## SCENARIO 10: Investor Relations During Crisis

**Prompt:**
"Your biggest customer (35% of revenue) just churned. You have a board meeting in 2 weeks. Burn rate means you need to raise in 4 months. How do you handle investor communication?"

**Must Include:**
- Immediate communication plan (do not wait for board meeting)
- Honest disclosure framework (what to share, when, how)
- Impact analysis (revenue, growth rate, narrative, fundraising implications)
- Mitigation plan (how you will replace the revenue)
- Customer concentration lesson (never again >20% from one customer)
- Board meeting preparation (present problem + plan, not just problem)
- Fundraising impact assessment (revised narrative, revised timeline)
- Worst-case scenario planning
- Team communication (internal message)
- Customer diversification strategy going forward
- Timeline: what happens in the next 30/60/90 days

**Fail If:**
- Waits until board meeting to disclose
- Minimizes the impact
- No mitigation plan
- Does not address fundraising implications
- No customer concentration strategy going forward
- Presents problem without plan

## SCENARIO 11: Strategic vs Financial Investor Decision

**Prompt:**
"You are raising Series A. A strategic investor (large tech company in your space) offers $6M at $25M pre with co-development partnership. A financial VC offers $8M at $30M pre with clean terms. The strategic partnership could accelerate product but creates competitive entanglement. Evaluate."

**Must Include:**
- Economic comparison (amount, valuation, terms)
- Strategic value assessment framework
- Competitive risk analysis (what if the strategic becomes a competitor?)
- Information rights and data sharing implications
- Impact on future fundraising (do financial VCs invest alongside strategics?)
- Impact on potential acquirers (does strategic investment limit exit options?)
- Partnership terms analysis (co-development scope, IP ownership)
- Board and governance implications
- Customer and market perception
- Recommendation with conditions
- Hybrid option (can you take both? should you?)

**Fail If:**
- Picks based on valuation alone
- Does not analyze competitive risk
- Ignores impact on future fundraising
- Does not consider exit option limitations
- No analysis of partnership terms
- Does not explore hybrid option

## SCENARIO 12: IPO Readiness Assessment

**Prompt:**
"The board asks whether the company should pursue an IPO in 18 months. Revenue is $50M ARR growing 60% YoY. You are profitable at the EBITDA level. 200 employees. Assess readiness."

**Must Include:**
- IPO readiness framework (financial, operational, legal, governance)
- Financial readiness (audit history, SOX compliance prep, financial reporting)
- Operational readiness (scalable systems, internal controls, SOPs)
- Legal readiness (IP clean, litigation risk, regulatory compliance)
- Governance readiness (independent board members, committees, D&O insurance)
- Market conditions assessment (IPO window, comparable companies, investor appetite)
- Alternative liquidity options (direct listing, SPAC, secondary, stay private)
- Timeline with preparation milestones
- Cost estimate for IPO preparation
- Hiring needs (CFO experience, IR function, legal counsel)
- Risk assessment (what could derail the IPO?)
- Recommendation with conditions

**Fail If:**
- Simply says "yes, you are ready" without detailed assessment
- No SOX/compliance discussion
- Does not consider market conditions
- No alternative liquidity analysis
- No cost or timeline estimate
- Ignores governance readiness

## SCENARIO 13: Convertible Note vs Priced Round Decision

**Prompt:**
"You are raising $1.5M. You can either do a priced seed round at $8M pre-money or a convertible note with $10M cap, 20% discount, 2-year maturity. You expect to raise Series A in 12-18 months. Which do you choose and why?"

**Must Include:**
- Economic comparison at various Series A valuations
- Dilution modeling for both scenarios
- Speed and cost comparison (legal fees, time to close)
- Investor preference analysis (who prefers what and why)
- Maturity risk assessment (what if Series A takes longer than 2 years?)
- Control implications (priced round may include board seat; note typically does not)
- Signaling effects (does a priced round signal more confidence?)
- Cap table clarity comparison (priced round is cleaner)
- Recommendation with specific conditions
- Sensitivity analysis (at what Series A valuation does one option clearly beat the other?)

**Fail If:**
- Picks one without comparing both
- No dilution modeling
- Ignores maturity risk on the note
- Does not consider speed/cost difference
- No sensitivity analysis
- Blanket recommendation without conditions

## SCENARIO 14: Investor Ghosting Recovery

**Prompt:**
"A partner at a top VC was very enthusiastic after 3 meetings. Said they would send a term sheet 'this week.' That was 3 weeks ago. Emails are unreturned. Mutual connections say the partner is 'busy.' What do you do?"

**Must Include:**
- Situation assessment (ghosting vs busy vs internal politics vs passing)
- Communication strategy (escalation ladder: email, mutual connection, direct message, phone)
- Specific message templates for each escalation step
- Parallel process maintenance (do not stop other conversations)
- Signal reading (what does the ghost mean for your pipeline?)
- Timeline for moving on (at what point do you consider this a pass?)
- Maintaining the relationship (do not burn bridge even if they passed)
- Lessons learned (were there signals you missed?)
- Pipeline diversification strategy
- Emotional management (do not let one investor's behavior affect your pitch to others)

**Fail If:**
- Keeps emailing the same message repeatedly
- Stops all other fundraising to wait
- Burns the bridge with an angry message
- Does not leverage mutual connections
- No timeline for moving on
- Does not reflect on what might have changed

## SCENARIO 15: Employee Equity Education

**Prompt:**
"Three of your early employees are asking about their equity: 'What are my options actually worth? Should I exercise? What happens if we raise more money? What if we get acquired at $X?' Create an education session that answers their questions honestly without making promises."

**Must Include:**
- Equity 101 explanation (options, strike price, vesting, cliff)
- Fair market value vs liquidation preference vs actual payout
- Exercise decision framework (early exercise benefits, tax implications, 83(b) election)
- Dilution explanation (how future rounds affect their percentage)
- Acquisition payout scenarios (waterfall at various exit valuations)
- Tax implications of exercise (ISO vs NSO, AMT, capital gains)
- Risk acknowledgment (equity may be worth zero)
- What NOT to say (no valuation promises, no timeline promises)
- Legal disclaimers (this is education, not financial advice)
- Resources for further learning (tax advisor, equity calculator)
- Common misconceptions addressed

**Fail If:**
- Implies equity has a guaranteed value
- Does not explain liquidation preferences and their impact on common shares
- Ignores tax implications
- Does not address the possibility that equity is worth zero
- Makes promises about future valuation or exit
- No legal disclaimer

## SCENARIO 16: Venture Debt Evaluation

**Prompt:**
"A venture debt provider offers $3M in debt at 9% interest, 3-year term, with warrants for 0.5% of fully diluted shares. You just raised a $10M Series A and have 18 months of runway. Should you take it?"

**Must Include:**
- Venture debt mechanics explanation (interest, repayment schedule, warrants)
- Cost of capital analysis (total cost including warrants)
- Use case assessment (when venture debt makes sense vs when it does not)
- Runway extension calculation (how many months does $3M add?)
- Risk assessment (what if you cannot repay? covenants, defaults)
- Impact on future fundraising (does debt on balance sheet help or hurt?)
- Comparison to alternative financing (raise more equity, revenue-based financing)
- Warrant dilution calculation (0.5% seems small but compounds)
- Timing assessment (is right after Series A the right time?)
- Terms to negotiate (interest rate, warrant coverage, covenants, prepayment)
- Recommendation with conditions

**Fail If:**
- Says yes because "more money is always good"
- Does not calculate total cost of capital
- Ignores default risk and covenant implications
- Does not compare to alternatives
- No negotiation strategy for terms
- Treats warrants as negligible without calculating dilution

## SCORING BENCHMARKS

Each scenario is scored against applicable InvestorScore.md dimensions.

### Passing Threshold
- Average score >= 4.0 across applicable dimensions
- No hard-fail dimension (Financial Story, Term Sheet Literacy, Cap Table Accuracy) < 3
- Response is complete (all "Must Include" items addressed)

| Level | Score Range | Meaning |

| Expert | 4.5 - 5.0 | Production-ready investor guidance |
| Proficient | 4.0 - 4.4 | Solid with minor gaps |
| Developing | 3.0 - 3.9 | Needs significant improvement |
| Failing | < 3.0 | Cannot be used for real fundraising decisions |
