# Secondary Markets — Pre-IPO Liquidity and Private Share Transactions

## The Secondary Market Ecosystem

Secondary markets enable the buying and selling of shares in private companies before an IPO or acquisition. They exist because venture-backed companies now stay private for 10-15 years on average (up from 4-5 years in the 2000s), creating significant demand for liquidity from early shareholders — founders, employees, and early-stage investors. The secondary market for private company shares has grown to an estimated $100B+ in annual transaction volume, creating both opportunities and complexities for all parties.

---

## 1. Transaction Types

### Direct Secondary Sales

A shareholder sells their shares directly to a buyer (typically an institutional investor, fund, or high-net-worth individual).

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Seller | Employee, founder, angel investor, early VC |
| Buyer | Growth equity fund, secondary fund, family office, HNW individual |
| Size | $100K to $50M+ per transaction |
| Process | Negotiated directly between parties (often through a broker) |
| Timeline | 4-12 weeks from initiation to close |
| Company involvement | Required for ROFR review and transfer approval |

### Company-Sponsored Tender Offers

The company organizes a structured buyback program, often facilitated by a new investor or existing investor.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Organizer | Company, with board approval |
| Buyer | New investor (lead), sometimes existing investors |
| Eligible sellers | Employees meeting criteria (tenure, share quantity) |
| Pricing | Typically set at last round price or board-determined FMV |
| Size | $10M to $500M+ (for late-stage companies) |
| Process | Formal offer with election period, pro-rata allocation if oversubscribed |
| Advantages | Controlled, consistent pricing, broad employee participation |

### Fund-of-Fund / LP Secondary

A limited partner (LP) in a venture fund sells their fund interest to another LP:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Seller | LP wanting liquidity before fund maturation |
| Buyer | Secondary fund (Lexington, Coller, Ardian, HarbourVest) |
| Size | $5M to $1B+ |
| Pricing | Typically 80-95% of NAV (discount for illiquidity) |
| Process | LP interest transfer with GP approval |
| Impact on company | None direct (ownership is at the fund level) |

### SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle) Transactions

A buyer creates an SPV to pool capital from multiple investors to purchase shares:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Structure | LLC or LP that holds shares in the private company |
| Organizer | Broker, fund manager, or platform |
| Minimum investment | $25K-$250K per SPV participant |
| Advantages | Enables smaller investors to participate in private company equity |
| Risks | Additional layer of fees and complexity; limited information rights |

## 2. Pricing Dynamics

### How Are Private Shares Priced?

Private share pricing is fundamentally different from public markets — there is no continuous market-making, limited price transparency, and significant information asymmetry.

**Pricing Reference Points:**

| Reference | Description | Reliability |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Last round price | Price per share from most recent priced round | Moderate (may be 6-18 months old) |
| 409A valuation | Independent fair market value assessment | Low-Moderate (common stock discount applied) |
| Comparable public company multiples | Apply public comps to private company metrics | Moderate |
| Recent secondary transactions | Price from recent comparable private sales | High (if recent and similar context) |
| Broker/platform indicative pricing | Market maker quotes based on supply/demand | Moderate |

### Pricing Discounts and Premiums

| Factor | Impact on Price |
|--------|----------------|
| Time since last round | Longer gap = more uncertainty = wider discount |
| Company performance since last round | Strong performance = premium; weak = discount |
| Shares available (supply) | High supply of sellers = discount |
| Buyer demand | Multiple interested buyers = premium |
| Share class | Common stock typically 10-30% discount to preferred |
| Information access | Less information = higher risk premium |
| Liquidity timeline | No near-term exit path = higher discount |
| Transfer restrictions | More restrictions = higher discount |
| Lock-up period | Post-transfer lock-up = discount |

**Typical Discount to Last Round Price:**

| Scenario | Discount Range |
|----------|---------------|
| Strong company, high demand, recent round | 0-10% |
| Solid company, moderate interest | 10-25% |
| Company between rounds, uncertainty | 20-35% |
| Distressed or down-round risk | 30-60% |
| Employee shares with significant restrictions | 25-40% |

## 3. Right of First Refusal (ROFR)

### What Is ROFR?

The Right of First Refusal gives the company (and sometimes existing investors) the right to purchase shares at the same price and terms offered by an outside buyer. Virtually all venture-backed companies include ROFR provisions in their charter and shareholder agreements.

### ROFR Process

```
Step 1: Seller receives offer from outside buyer
        ↓
Step 2: Seller submits ROFR notice to company
        (includes: buyer identity, price, share count, terms)
        ↓
Step 3: Company has [30-60 days] to exercise ROFR
        ↓
Step 4a: Company exercises → Company buys shares at offered price
Step 4b: Company does not exercise → Assigns to existing investors
        ↓
Step 5a: Investors exercise → Investor buys shares
Step 5b: No one exercises → Transaction with original buyer proceeds
        ↓
Step 6: Board approval required for transfer
```

### ROFR Strategic Considerations

| For the Company | For the Seller | For the Buyer |
|----------------|---------------|--------------|
| ROFR gives control over cap table | ROFR delays transaction by 30-60 days | Buyer may lose the deal if ROFR exercised |
| Can prevent hostile or undesirable shareholders | ROFR may deter buyers (they dislike uncertainty) | Price discovery is constrained |
| Can manage information access | Process is cumbersome; may discourage selling | Must be prepared for ROFR exercise |
| Some companies use ROFR to set pricing floors | Seller cannot freely shop shares | Relationship with company matters |

## 4. Information Asymmetry

### The Fundamental Problem

In public markets, all investors access the same information (SEC filings, earnings calls). In private secondary markets, information is asymmetrically distributed:

| Party | Information Access |
|-------|-------------------|
| Company (board, management) | Full information |
| Existing investors (board members) | Near-full information |
| Selling employees | Limited (know product, culture; may not know full financials) |
| Buying investors (with NDA) | Partial (may receive data room access from company) |
| Buying investors (without NDA) | Minimal (public information, rumor, extrapolation) |
| Platform buyers | Minimal to partial (platform may provide data summaries) |

### Managing Information Asymmetry

**For Buyers:**
- Request data room access from the company (company may or may not cooperate)
- Review publicly available information (press releases, job postings, product launches)
- Interview former employees and customers (within legal bounds)
- Use third-party data providers (PitchBook, CB Insights, Crunchbase, SimilarWeb)
- Price in an information discount (if you cannot get data, pay less)

**For Sellers:**
- Understand what you are allowed to share (insider trading considerations apply)
- Avoid sharing material non-public information (MNPI) that could create legal liability
- Work with the company's legal team on what can be disclosed to buyers

**For Companies:**
- Decide whether to facilitate secondary sales (be proactive, not reactive)
- If facilitating: provide standardized data package to approved buyers
- If restricting: communicate policy clearly to shareholders

## 5. Secondary Market Platforms

### Major Platforms

| Platform | Focus | How It Works | Fees |
|----------|-------|-------------|------|
| Forge Global | Pre-IPO equity | Marketplace matching buyers and sellers; provides data and analytics | 3-5% commission |
| CartaX (Carta) | Pre-IPO equity | Leverages Carta cap table data; targets institutional buyers | Negotiated |
| EquityZen | Pre-IPO equity | Curates investment opportunities for accredited investors via SPVs | 5% seller + 5% buyer |
| Nasdaq Private Market | Tender offers | Administers company-sponsored tender offers and liquidity programs | Negotiated with company |
| SharesPost (acquired by Forge) | Pre-IPO equity | Merged into Forge platform | See Forge |
| Zanbato | Institutional secondary | Targets institutional investors with larger block trades | Negotiated |

### Platform Selection Guide

| Need | Recommended Platform(s) |
|------|------------------------|
| Employee looking to sell shares | Forge, EquityZen (easiest access for individuals) |
| Company running a tender offer | Nasdaq Private Market, Carta |
| Institutional buyer seeking large blocks | Forge, Zanbato, direct broker relationships |
| VC fund selling LP interest | Dedicated secondary fund (Lexington, Coller) |

## 6. Board Approval and Governance

### Board's Role in Secondary Transactions

Most company charters require board approval for any share transfer. The board acts as gatekeeper with several considerations:

| Consideration | Board Perspective |
|--------------|------------------|
| Buyer suitability | Is the buyer an appropriate shareholder? (Not a competitor, not hostile) |
| Pricing | Is the price reasonable? Could it set an unwanted valuation precedent? |
| Information access | What information should the buyer receive? |
| Volume | How many shares are being sold? Will this create signaling problems? |
| Employee morale | Will allowing/denying sales affect employee retention? |
| Cap table complexity | Will the transaction add excessive shareholders or SPVs? |
| Tax and legal implications | Any tax or regulatory concerns? |

### Developing a Secondary Transaction Policy

Proactive companies establish a standing secondary policy:

| Policy Element | Options |
|---------------|---------|
| Frequency | Quarterly windows, annual tender, ad hoc approval |
| Eligible sellers | All shareholders, or employees only after vesting + tenure requirements |
| Maximum sale amount | Unlimited, or cap (e.g., max 25% of vested shares per window) |
| Pricing mechanism | Last round price, 409A, board-determined FMV, or market-based |
| Approved buyer list | Pre-approved institutional buyers, or open to any accredited investor |
| ROFR enforcement | Always exercise, selective exercise, or waive for approved buyers |
| Information policy | Standardized data package for approved buyers |

## 7. Tax and Legal Considerations

### Tax Implications for Sellers

| Seller Situation | Tax Treatment |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Shares held >1 year (long-term) | Long-term capital gains rate (0-20% + 3.8% NIIT) |
| Shares held <1 year (short-term) | Ordinary income rate (up to 37% + 3.8% NIIT) |
| ISO shares with 83(b) election | Capital gains if holding period met |
| NSO shares exercised and sold | Ordinary income on spread at exercise + capital gains on subsequent appreciation |
| QSBS exclusion (Section 1202) | Up to $10M or 10x basis excluded from federal tax if held 5+ years |

### Legal Considerations

- **Securities law compliance:** Private share sales must comply with SEC exemptions (Rule 144, Regulation D)
- **Company transfer restrictions:** Review shareholder agreement, charter, and any lockup agreements
- **MNPI risks:** Sellers with access to material non-public information face insider trading liability
- **State blue sky laws:** Compliance with state securities regulations
- **International considerations:** Non-US sellers or buyers may face additional regulatory requirements
