# IPO Readiness — The Path from Private to Public

## The IPO Decision

An Initial Public Offering is the most complex, consequential, and irreversible corporate transaction a company will undertake. It transforms the company's capital structure, governance, reporting obligations, compensation design, and organizational culture. The 18-24 month preparation timeline is not a suggestion — it is the minimum required to build the infrastructure, processes, and team that public company status demands. Companies that rush to market face painful and expensive corrections post-IPO.

---

## 1. The 18-24 Month IPO Preparation Timeline

### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-6)

**Governance and Board:**
- Reconstitute board for public company requirements (majority independent directors)
- Establish audit committee (fully independent, financial expert requirement)
- Establish compensation committee (fully independent)
- Establish nominating/governance committee
- Adopt public company governance policies (insider trading, code of ethics, whistleblower)
- Engage independent board members with public company experience

**Financial Infrastructure:**
- Engage Big Four or national audit firm (Deloitte, EY, PwC, KPMG, BDO, Grant Thornton)
- Begin auditing 2-3 years of historical financial statements (required for S-1)
- Implement SOX-ready internal controls framework
- Upgrade ERP and financial systems if needed
- Hire or upgrade VP Finance/Controller with public company experience
- Establish revenue recognition policies compliant with ASC 606

**Legal Infrastructure:**
- Engage IPO counsel (Latham, Wilson Sonsini, Cooley, Davis Polk, etc.)
- Clean up corporate structure (eliminate shell entities, resolve IP ownership)
- Review and resolve any pending or threatened litigation
- Ensure all employee IP assignment agreements are in place
- Review customer contracts for any onerous or unusual terms
- Begin preparing the registration statement narrative

### Phase 2: Build-Out (Months 7-12)

**SOX Compliance (Sarbanes-Oxley Act):**
- Section 302: CEO and CFO must certify financial statements
- Section 404: Management assessment of internal controls over financial reporting
- Design and document key financial processes and controls
- Implement control testing procedures
- Remediate any identified material weaknesses

**Financial Reporting:**
- Establish quarterly close process (target: 15-20 business days)
- Build financial reporting package (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow, KPIs)
- Develop management's discussion and analysis (MD&A) capability
- Create earnings release template
- Build investor relations function

**People and Compensation:**
- Redesign compensation for public company (reduce option grants, introduce RSUs)
- Review all executive employment agreements
- Design public company equity plan (submit for shareholder approval)
- Evaluate D&O insurance needs (directors and officers liability)
- Hire VP Investor Relations

### Phase 3: Execution (Months 13-18)

**Registration Statement (S-1/F-1):**
- Draft S-1 registration statement (narrative, financials, risk factors)
- Multiple rounds of SEC comment letters and responses
- Audited financial statements for 2-3 fiscal years + interim periods
- Risk factors section (comprehensive, not boilerplate)
- MD&A section (narrative explaining financial results)
- Business description (competitive landscape, strategy, moat)

**Underwriter Selection:**
- Select lead left bookrunner (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan, etc.)
- Select co-managers and syndicate members
- Negotiate underwriting fee (typically 3.5-7% of gross proceeds)
- Agree on greenshoe (over-allotment option, typically 15%)

**Investor Relations Preparation:**
- Build investor targeting list
- Create investor presentation (the "roadshow deck")
- Prepare management for analyst meetings and investor questions
- Develop guidance framework (will you provide quarterly guidance?)
- Set up IR website section

### Phase 4: Launch (Months 19-24)

**Roadshow (2-3 Weeks):**
- Management presents to institutional investors across major financial centers
- Typically 8-10 meetings per day for 10-14 business days
- CEO, CFO, and possibly COO/CRO present
- Build book of demand (orders from institutional investors)
- Price discovery through investor feedback

**Pricing and Allocation:**
- Price IPO based on book of demand (typically set the evening before trading)
- Allocate shares to institutional investors
- Historically: IPO pop of 10-20% considered successful by bankers
- Founder perspective: Large pop means you left money on the table

**First Day of Trading:**
- Listing on NYSE or NASDAQ
- Opening bell ceremony (marketing event)
- Real-time market monitoring
- Quiet period begins (25-40 days post-IPO — limited public communication)

## 2. Alternative Paths to Public Markets

### Direct Listing

| Feature | Traditional IPO | Direct Listing |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| New shares issued | Yes (primary offering) | No (existing shares only) |
| Capital raised | Yes | Not in initial listing (possible now via SEC rule change) |
| Underwriter role | Bookrunner, roadshow, pricing | Financial advisor only |
| Lockup period | 90-180 days typical | None (all shares tradeable immediately) |
| Price discovery | Book building with institutions | Market-based on first trading day |
| Cost | 3.5-7% underwriting discount | ~1-2% advisory fee |
| Best for | Companies needing capital | Well-known companies with strong brand, no capital need |

**Notable Direct Listings:** Spotify (2018), Slack (2019), Coinbase (2021), Roblox (2021).

### SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company)

| Feature | Traditional IPO | SPAC Merger |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| Timeline | 18-24 months | 3-6 months (SPAC already public) |
| Pricing | Market-based, roadshow | Negotiated with SPAC sponsor |
| Valuation certainty | Unknown until pricing night | Fixed valuation in merger agreement |
| Dilution | Underwriting fee (3.5-7%) | SPAC sponsor promote (20%), warrants |
| Regulatory scrutiny | SEC S-1 review | SEC S-4/proxy review (now more stringent) |
| Best for | Strong companies with clear story | Companies needing speed or certainty |

**SPAC Caution (Post-2022):** SEC has dramatically increased scrutiny of SPAC mergers. Many post-SPAC companies have performed poorly. SPAC as a path to public markets has become less attractive.

### Dual-Class Share Structure

Dual-class structures give founders voting control disproportionate to economic ownership:

| Class | Typical Structure | Example |
|-------|------------------|---------|
| Class A (public) | 1 vote per share | Public investors |
| Class B (founders) | 10 votes per share | Founders, early insiders |

**Arguments For:** Founder maintains long-term vision against activist pressure
**Arguments Against:** Reduces public shareholder influence; governance concerns; some index funds exclude dual-class companies

**Notable Dual-Class:** Google/Alphabet, Meta/Facebook, Snap, Zoom

## 3. IPO Readiness Checklist

### Financial Readiness

- [ ] 2-3 years of audited financial statements (PCAOB-compliant audit)
- [ ] Revenue recognition policies documented and audited (ASC 606)
- [ ] Quarterly close process under 20 business days
- [ ] Financial forecasting capability (rolling 4-quarter model)
- [ ] Key performance metrics defined and tracked
- [ ] SOX 302 and 404 compliance framework implemented
- [ ] Material weaknesses identified and remediated
- [ ] Tax structure reviewed and optimized

### Legal Readiness

- [ ] Corporate structure clean (single operating entity or clear structure)
- [ ] All IP properly assigned and protected
- [ ] Material contracts reviewed for unusual terms
- [ ] Litigation risk assessed and disclosed
- [ ] Regulatory compliance confirmed for all jurisdictions
- [ ] Employee classification and labor compliance verified
- [ ] Environmental/ESG compliance assessed
- [ ] D&O insurance in place

### Governance Readiness

- [ ] Board composition meets exchange listing requirements
- [ ] Audit, compensation, and nominating committees established
- [ ] Committee charters adopted
- [ ] Code of conduct and ethics policy adopted
- [ ] Insider trading policy implemented
- [ ] Related-party transaction policy in place
- [ ] Whistleblower procedures established
- [ ] Board meeting cadence and materials process established

### Operational Readiness

- [ ] Scalable systems for finance, HR, legal, IT
- [ ] Cybersecurity posture assessed and hardened
- [ ] Business continuity and disaster recovery plans
- [ ] Investor relations function staffed
- [ ] Communications team prepared for public scrutiny
- [ ] Executive team experienced with or trained for public company obligations
- [ ] Employee equity transition plan (options to RSUs, lockup communication)

## 4. Post-IPO Obligations

### Ongoing Reporting Requirements

| Filing | Deadline | Content |
|--------|----------|---------|
| 10-K (Annual) | 60 days after fiscal year end | Audited financials, MD&A, risk factors |
| 10-Q (Quarterly) | 40 days after quarter end | Unaudited financials, MD&A update |
| 8-K (Current) | 4 business days after material event | Material events, acquisitions, executive changes |
| Proxy statement (DEF 14A) | Before annual meeting | Board nominees, executive compensation, proposals |
| Section 16 filings (Form 4) | 2 business days after insider trade | Insider stock transactions |

### Quiet Period and Blackout Windows

- **Quiet period:** 25-40 days post-IPO; limited public statements
- **Earnings blackout:** Typically 2-4 weeks before earnings release; insiders cannot trade
- **Regulation FD:** No selective disclosure; material information must be public
