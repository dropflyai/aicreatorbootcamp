# Legal Process -- Due Diligence, Data Room, SPA, and Closing

## Overview

The legal process between term sheet signing and closing is where deals can die or
terms can shift. This module covers due diligence preparation, data room construction,
definitive agreement negotiation, and the closing process. While the Investor Brain
does not provide legal advice, it provides the strategic framework for managing this
process efficiently.

Primary references: NVCA model legal documents, Feld "Venture Deals" (legal chapters),
Yokum Taku (WSGR) startup legal guides, Cooley GO legal toolkits.

Note: All legal document preparation and review should involve qualified startup counsel.
This module provides strategic guidance, not legal advice.

## Due Diligence Overview

### What Due Diligence Covers

```
DUE DILIGENCE CATEGORIES

1. CORPORATE / LEGAL
   - Certificate of incorporation and amendments
   - Bylaws
   - Board minutes and consents
   - Stockholder agreements
   - Cap table and equity records
   - Outstanding litigation or claims
   - IP assignments and ownership
   - Material contracts
   - Regulatory compliance

2. FINANCIAL
   - Financial statements (audited if available)
   - Tax returns (3 years)
   - Revenue recognition policies
   - Accounts receivable/payable aging
   - Burn rate analysis
   - Bank statements
   - Outstanding debt or obligations

3. INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY
   - Patent filings and grants
   - Trademark registrations
   - Copyright registrations
   - Trade secrets and protection measures
   - Open source usage audit
   - IP assignment agreements (all employees/contractors)
   - Freedom to operate analysis

4. HUMAN RESOURCES
   - Employment agreements (key employees)
   - Contractor agreements
   - Employee handbook / policies
   - Option grants and vesting schedules
   - 409A valuations
   - Non-compete/non-solicit agreements
   - Key employee retention plans

5. COMMERCIAL
   - Customer contracts (top 10-20)
   - Revenue concentration analysis
   - Churn and retention data
   - Pipeline and forecasting
   - Vendor contracts
   - Partnership agreements

6. TECHNICAL
   - Architecture documentation
   - Security audit / penetration test results
   - Scalability assessment
   - Technical debt inventory
   - Dependency audit
   - Data privacy compliance (GDPR, CCPA)
   - Disaster recovery plan
```

### Due Diligence Timeline

```
TYPICAL DD TIMELINE (Series A)

Week 1: Document Collection
  - Investor sends DD request list
  - Company assembles documents into data room
  - Priority: Corporate, financial, IP documents

Week 2: Initial Review
  - Investor's legal team reviews corporate structure
  - Financial due diligence begins
  - IP review initiated

Week 3: Deep Dive
  - Customer reference calls
  - Technical assessment (if applicable)
  - Management team interviews
  - Follow-up questions from legal review

Week 4: Findings and Negotiation
  - DD findings shared with company
  - Issues identified and addressed
  - Definitive document drafting begins
  - Final negotiations on any DD-discovered issues

ACCELERATED DD (2-3 weeks):
  Possible when:
  - Data room is pre-built and organized
  - Clean corporate records
  - No significant issues discovered
  - Investor has done preliminary work pre-term sheet

EXTENDED DD (6-8 weeks):
  Happens when:
  - Complex corporate structure
  - International operations
  - Regulatory requirements
  - Issues discovered during DD
  - Late-stage deals with higher stakes
```

### Data Room Best Practices

```
DATA ROOM MANAGEMENT

PLATFORM OPTIONS:
  - DocSend (good for early stage, analytics on viewer behavior)
  - Google Drive (free, adequate for seed)
  - Carta (integrated with cap table)
  - Ansarada (enterprise DD platform, later stage)
  - Datasite (M&A and IPO grade)

SECURITY REQUIREMENTS:
  - Watermarking on sensitive documents
  - View-only access (no download) for initial review
  - Granular permission controls per folder
  - Activity logging (who viewed what, when)
  - NDA required before access

ORGANIZATION PRINCIPLES:
  1. Clear folder structure (as above)
  2. Consistent naming: "[Date]_[Category]_[Document]_[Version]"
  3. Index document at top level listing all contents
  4. Version control (clearly mark latest versions)
  5. Redact appropriately (customer names if under NDA, etc.)

PRE-BUILD YOUR DATA ROOM:
  Do not wait for a term sheet to build the data room.
  Pre-build it during fundraising preparation.
  This accelerates DD by 1-2 weeks and signals professionalism.

UPDATE CONTINUOUSLY:
  Keep the data room current even when not fundraising.
  Monthly financial updates, new contracts, new hires.
  "Data room readiness" is a sign of operational maturity.
```

## Definitive Agreements

### Documents in a Priced Round

```
THE FIVE CORE DOCUMENTS (NVCA Model)

1. STOCK PURCHASE AGREEMENT (SPA)
   The master agreement for the equity purchase.
   Contains: purchase price, representations, warranties,
   conditions to closing, indemnification.

   KEY NEGOTIATION POINTS:
   - Representations and warranties (scope and survival)
   - Conditions to closing (what must happen before wire)
   - Indemnification caps and baskets
   - Bring-down conditions (reps must be true at closing)

2. CERTIFICATE OF INCORPORATION (Amended & Restated)
   Creates the new preferred stock class and its rights.
   Contains: All economic terms (liquidation preference,
   anti-dilution, dividends, conversion rights).

   KEY NEGOTIATION POINTS:
   - Liquidation preference mechanics
   - Anti-dilution formula
   - Conversion rights and conditions
   - Protective provisions
   - Dividend rights

3. INVESTOR RIGHTS AGREEMENT (IRA)
   Grants information, registration, and other rights to investors.
   Contains: Information rights, registration rights, board observer
   rights, ROFR on new issuances, management rights.

   KEY NEGOTIATION POINTS:
   - Information rights scope and frequency
   - Registration rights (demand vs. piggyback)
   - Right of first refusal on new share issuances
   - Lockup provisions
   - Board observer rights

4. VOTING AGREEMENT
   Governs board composition and voting commitments.
   Contains: Board seat allocation, voting commitments,
   drag-along provisions.

   KEY NEGOTIATION POINTS:
   - Board composition and size
   - Drag-along thresholds and conditions
   - Voting commitments for board elections

5. RIGHT OF FIRST REFUSAL AND CO-SALE AGREEMENT (ROFR/Co-Sale)
   Restricts secondary transfers of shares.
   Contains: Company ROFR on share transfers, investor co-sale
   rights, transfer restrictions.

   KEY NEGOTIATION POINTS:
   - ROFR mechanics (company first, then investors)
   - Co-sale scope (which shares, which holders)
   - Exceptions for estate planning, family transfers
```

### Negotiating Definitive Documents

```
DEFINITIVE DOCUMENT NEGOTIATION PROCESS

WEEK 1: FIRST DRAFT
  Lead investor's counsel produces first draft
  (Or company counsel if company has the stronger position)
  Based on: NVCA model documents + term sheet specifics

WEEK 2: COMPANY REVIEW
  Company counsel reviews and produces redline (markup)
  Focus areas:
  - Deviations from term sheet (reject any new terms)
  - Representation scope (narrow where possible)
  - Conditions to closing (minimize discretionary conditions)
  - Protective provision language (match term sheet exactly)

WEEK 3: NEGOTIATION
  Counsel-to-counsel calls to resolve markup
  Founders involved for business term decisions
  Key decision points escalated to principals

WEEK 4: FINAL AND SIGN
  Clean documents circulated
  Board approval (board consent or meeting)
  Stockholder approval (if required by protective provisions)
  Signature pages collected
  Closing checklist completed

COMMON POST-TERM SHEET ISSUES:
  1. "Scope creep" -- investor's counsel adds terms not in term sheet
     RESPONSE: "This wasn't in our term sheet. We should stick to
     what we agreed."
  2. New diligence findings requiring renegotiation
     RESPONSE: Assess materiality; negotiate specific adjustment
  3. Timeline delays from counsel responsiveness
     RESPONSE: Set and enforce deadlines for draft turnarounds
```

## The Closing Process

### Closing Checklist

```
PRE-CLOSING REQUIREMENTS

CORPORATE:
  □ Board approval of financing
  □ Stockholder approval (if required)
  □ Updated Certificate of Incorporation filed with Delaware
  □ Good standing certificates
  □ Secretary's certificate

SECURITIES:
  □ Compliance with securities exemptions (Reg D)
  □ Accredited investor questionnaires from all investors
  □ Blue sky filings (state securities filings)
  □ Form D filing with SEC (within 15 days of first sale)

DOCUMENTATION:
  □ All five core documents executed
  □ All exhibits and schedules attached
  □ Legal opinion (if required)
  □ Management rights letter (for ERISA investors)
  □ Indemnification agreements for new directors

FINANCIAL:
  □ Wire instructions confirmed
  □ Funds received
  □ Stock certificates or book-entry confirmation
  □ Updated cap table reflecting new issuance

POST-CLOSING:
  □ Updated cap table distributed to all stakeholders
  □ Board meeting scheduled (new board composition)
  □ Investor added to information distribution list
  □ Press release / announcement (if agreed)
  □ Form D filed with SEC
  □ Blue sky filings completed
  □ Option grants for new hires (at new 409A)
```

### Simultaneous vs. Sequential Closing

```
CLOSING MECHANICS

SIMULTANEOUS CLOSING (standard for most VC rounds):
  All investors close on the same date.
  All documents signed and all funds wired simultaneously.
  Clean cap table update.

ROLLING CLOSING (common for seed rounds / SAFEs):
  Investors close at different times over a period.
  Lead investor closes first, others follow.
  Typical window: 30-90 days for all investors.

FIRST/FINAL CLOSE:
  First close: Lead investor + major participants
  Final close: Remaining investors join (often 60-90 days later)
  Minimum close: Minimum amount required for first close
  (e.g., $3M of a $5M round must close at first close)

ESCROW:
  Sometimes funds held in escrow until all conditions met.
  Common for large rounds or complex transactions.
  Release triggered by final closing condition satisfaction.
```

## Common Closing Issues and Remedies

```
ISSUES THAT DELAY OR DERAIL CLOSING

1. OUTSTANDING IP ASSIGNMENT
   Former contractor never signed IP assignment.
   REMEDY: Locate contractor, execute assignment,
   or obtain legal opinion on company ownership.
   PREVENTION: Always get IP assignments at engagement start.

2. MISSING CORPORATE RECORDS
   Board minutes not maintained, consent not documented.
   REMEDY: Reconstruct minutes from email records and decisions.
   PREVENTION: Maintain corporate records from Day 1.

3. UNVESTED FOUNDER SHARES WITHOUT SECTION 83(b)
   Founder failed to file 83(b) election within 30 days.
   REMEDY: Cannot be retroactively fixed. Restructure via
   option grant if necessary. Consult tax counsel.
   PREVENTION: File 83(b) within 30 days of share issuance.

4. CONVERTIBLE INSTRUMENT COMPLICATIONS
   SAFE conversion math disputed.
   REMEDY: Model all scenarios, agree on cap table pre-close.
   PREVENTION: Use standard YC SAFEs, maintain cap table model.

5. INVESTOR COLD FEET
   Investor delays or attempts to renegotiate post-term sheet.
   REMEDY: Enforce no-shop expiration.
   Engage backup investors from pipeline.
   PREVENTION: Reference check investors before selecting lead.

6. REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS
   HSR filing (antitrust) or CFIUS review for foreign investors.
   REMEDY: Begin filings immediately; these have mandatory
   waiting periods.
   PREVENTION: Identify regulatory requirements during DD.

7. EXISTING INVESTOR RIGHTS
   Previous investors have information rights, pro-rata rights,
   or consent rights that must be exercised.
   REMEDY: Notify existing investors, obtain consents/waivers.
   PREVENTION: Know your existing obligations before launching round.
```

## Post-Closing Actions

```
AFTER THE WIRE: FIRST 30 DAYS

WEEK 1:
  □ Send welcome/thank you to all investors
  □ Press announcement (coordinate with investors)
  □ Update company cap table (official records)
  □ Issue stock certificates or update Carta
  □ Schedule first board meeting with new members
  □ File Form D with SEC
  □ Begin hiring against fundraise plan

WEEK 2:
  □ First board meeting (new composition)
  □ Approve option pool grants (new 409A pending)
  □ Establish investor update cadence
  □ Set up reporting schedule
  □ Engage 409A valuation firm (for new post-round valuation)

WEEK 3-4:
  □ Send first investor update
  □ Begin executing on use-of-funds plan
  □ Set up formal financial reporting
  □ Establish board meeting schedule (quarterly standard)

ONGOING:
  □ Monthly investor updates
  □ Quarterly board meetings
  □ Annual financial review
  □ Maintain data room for future rounds
  □ Track milestones against fundraise plan
```
