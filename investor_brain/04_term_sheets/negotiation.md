# Negotiation -- Leverage, Tactics, and Multi-Term Sheet Scenarios

## Overview

Term sheet negotiation is where fundraising transitions from storytelling to deal-making.
The negotiation phase determines the economic and governance structure that will govern
the company for years. This module covers leverage assessment, negotiation tactics,
multi-term sheet management, and the psychology of VC deal-making.

Primary references: Feld "Venture Deals" (negotiation chapters), Chris Voss "Never Split
the Difference", Roger Fisher "Getting to Yes", Kupor "Secrets of Sand Hill Road"
(investor perspective on negotiations).

## Leverage Framework

### Understanding Your Leverage Position

```
LEVERAGE ASSESSMENT MATRIX

HIGH LEVERAGE (founder advantage):
  □ Multiple term sheets in hand
  □ Strong and accelerating metrics
  □ Round is oversubscribed
  □ Prominent investor already committed
  □ No time pressure (18+ months runway)
  □ Hot market for your sector/stage

MODERATE LEVERAGE (balanced):
  □ 1-2 term sheets
  □ Solid but not exceptional metrics
  □ Reasonable investor interest
  □ 12-18 months runway
  □ Normal market conditions

LOW LEVERAGE (investor advantage):
  □ No term sheets after extended process
  □ Metrics are weak or declining
  □ < 6 months runway
  □ Market downturn
  □ Previous round at high valuation (down round risk)
  □ Known problems (co-founder departure, customer loss)

LEVERAGE SCORING:
Rate each factor 1-5, sum total:
  25-30: Strong position → negotiate aggressively
  15-24: Moderate position → negotiate selectively
  6-14: Weak position → focus on closing, not optimizing
```

### The Paradox of Options

```
THE BEST TIME TO NEGOTIATE IS WHEN YOU DON'T NEED TO

When you have multiple term sheets:
  - Investors compete for allocation
  - Terms improve automatically
  - You have walk-away power

When you have one term sheet:
  - Take it and negotiate at the margins
  - Don't over-negotiate and lose the deal
  - A good deal closed beats a perfect deal lost

When you have zero term sheets:
  - You have no negotiating position
  - Fix the underlying issue (metrics, pitch, targeting)
  - Consider bridge financing from existing investors

RULE: Generate term sheets BEFORE negotiating terms.
Never negotiate a single term sheet aggressively unless
you genuinely have a walk-away position.
```

## Negotiation Principles

### Principled Negotiation (Fisher/Ury Framework)

```
FOUR PRINCIPLES FROM "GETTING TO YES"

1. SEPARATE THE PEOPLE FROM THE PROBLEM
   The investor is not your adversary; the terms are the subject.
   "We love working with you. We'd like to adjust the
    liquidation preference to align with market standards."

2. FOCUS ON INTERESTS, NOT POSITIONS
   Position: "We want $20M pre-money"
   Interest: "We want to retain enough ownership to stay motivated"

   Their position: "$15M pre-money"
   Their interest: "We need ownership to generate fund-returning returns"

   FIND THE OVERLAP:
   Both want the company to succeed → both want motivated founders
   → both want returns → creative structure can satisfy both

3. GENERATE OPTIONS FOR MUTUAL GAIN
   Instead of splitting the difference:
   "What if we structured it as $18M pre-money with a smaller
    option pool, which effectively gives us both what we need?"

4. INSIST ON OBJECTIVE CRITERIA
   "Let's look at comparable recent rounds in our space:
    [Company A] raised at [X]x, [Company B] at [Y]x.
    Our metrics position us at [Z]x."
```

### The Feld Negotiation Rules

```
BRAD FELD'S NEGOTIATION PRINCIPLES (from "Venture Deals")

1. "THERE ARE ONLY TWO THINGS THAT MATTER: ECONOMICS AND CONTROL"
   Everything else is either a subset of these or noise.
   Focus your negotiation energy on these two dimensions.

2. "DON'T NEGOTIATE TERMS YOU DON'T UNDERSTAND"
   If you don't understand a clause, stop and learn.
   Ask your lawyer. Ask an advisor. Read this brain.
   Never agree to something you don't fully comprehend.

3. "EVERY TERM HAS A COST"
   Investors have a "term budget" -- pushing back on one term
   often means they'll concede another.
   Identify which terms matter most to you and trade the rest.

4. "HAVE A LAWYER WHO KNOWS VC"
   General practice lawyers cost you money on VC deals.
   Use Wilson Sonsini, Cooley, Gunderson, Fenwick, Goodwin.
   They know NVCA docs and can spot non-standard provisions.

5. "TIME KILLS DEALS"
   Extended negotiations erode trust and momentum.
   Once you have a term sheet you like, close quickly.
   Negotiation should take days, not weeks.
```

## Tactical Negotiation Playbook

### Valuation Negotiation

```
VALUATION NEGOTIATION TACTICS

TACTIC 1: ANCHOR HIGH WITH JUSTIFICATION
"Based on our [growth rate / comparables / traction],
we're looking for a [$X]M pre-money valuation."
Always provide the justification immediately after the number.

TACTIC 2: NEVER BE THE FIRST TO NAME A NUMBER (if possible)
"What range are you thinking about?"
"Help me understand how you're thinking about valuation."
Let them anchor, then negotiate from their position.

TACTIC 3: USE THE OPTION POOL AS A LEVER
If investor says $10M pre-money:
"We'd accept $10M pre-money with a 10% option pool
rather than $12M with a 20% pool. The effective
pre-money to existing holders is the same."

TACTIC 4: STRUCTURE OVER PRICE
"We could accept your valuation if you'd agree to
1x non-participating preferred instead of participating.
The valuation gap is offset by cleaner terms."

TACTIC 5: MILESTONE RATCHET (advanced)
"How about $12M pre-money, with a warrant that converts
at $15M if we hit [$X]M ARR within 12 months?
This protects your downside while rewarding our performance."

TACTIC 6: THE WALK-AWAY SIGNAL
"We appreciate the offer. We're going to take a few days
to consider this alongside our other conversations."
(Only use if you genuinely have other options)
```

### Liquidation Preference Negotiation

```
PUSHING BACK ON PARTICIPATING PREFERRED

Investor proposes: 1x participating preferred
Your goal: 1x non-participating preferred

APPROACH 1: MARKET STANDARD ARGUMENT
"In recent comparable rounds we've tracked, 1x non-participating
is standard. [Company A], [Company B], and [Company C]
all closed with non-participating. We'd like to align with
market norms."

APPROACH 2: ALIGNMENT ARGUMENT
"Participating preferred creates misaligned incentives in
moderate exit scenarios. If we're aligned on building for
a large outcome, non-participating gives you identical
economics at exits above [conversion point].

The only scenarios where participating matters are moderate
exits -- and we're not building for a moderate exit."

APPROACH 3: TRADE
"We'd accept a slightly lower valuation in exchange for
non-participating preferred. The economic outcome is similar
for both of us in the upside case."

APPROACH 4: CAP COMPROMISE
"If participating is important to you, let's add a cap.
3x cap means your maximum return through the preference
mechanism is 3x, after which you convert to common.
This protects your downside while keeping incentives aligned."

IF THEY WON'T BUDGE:
Model the actual dollar impact across exit scenarios.
If the difference is small relative to the deal, accept.
If significant, this is a legitimate walk-away point.
```

### Board Composition Negotiation

```
NEGOTIATING BOARD STRUCTURE

FOUNDER GOAL: Maintain effective board control through Series A

PREFERRED STRUCTURE (Series A):
  2 Founder seats + 1 Independent + 1 Investor + 1 Independent = 5 seats
  Founders + Independents = 4 of 5 (if independents are truly independent)

IF INVESTOR WANTS MAJORITY:
"We believe the best outcomes come from founder-led boards
at this stage. We'd propose:
  2 Common seats (founders)
  1 Preferred seat (your partner)
  2 Independent seats (mutually agreed)

This gives you meaningful governance input while allowing us
to operate with the speed that early-stage companies need."

IF INVESTOR INSISTS ON 2 PREFERRED SEATS:
"We'd accept 2 preferred seats if the independent director
is genuinely independent -- someone both of us respect who
brings relevant industry expertise."

INDEPENDENT DIRECTOR NEGOTIATION:
"Independent" should mean:
  - Not affiliated with the investor's firm
  - Not a personal friend of the investor
  - Has relevant industry expertise
  - Both sides have approval rights

WHO MAKES A GOOD INDEPENDENT:
  - Former CEO/founder in your space
  - Industry expert with board experience
  - Someone both sides genuinely respect
```

## Multi-Term Sheet Management

### When You Have Multiple Term Sheets

```
MULTI-TERM SHEET PROTOCOL

STEP 1: COMPARE SYSTEMATICALLY
Use Templates/term_sheet_comparison.md to create a
side-by-side comparison across all material terms.

STEP 2: IDENTIFY YOUR PRIORITY FRAMEWORK
Rank the importance of each dimension:

  Priority 1: Valuation / dilution
  Priority 2: Liquidation preference structure
  Priority 3: Board composition
  Priority 4: Investor value-add and reputation
  Priority 5: Other terms (anti-dilution, etc.)

  NOTE: Priority 4 (investor quality) often matters more
  than Priority 1 (valuation) in the long run.

STEP 3: CROSS-NEGOTIATE (ethically)
"Thank you for the term sheet. We have multiple offers
and are evaluating them this week. Your terms are
competitive, though we'd like to discuss [specific term]
to make this our clear first choice."

Rules:
  ✓ You may disclose that you have other term sheets
  ✓ You may share which firm(s) offered (with their permission)
  ✗ You may NOT fabricate term sheets
  ✗ You may NOT misrepresent other offers
  ✗ You should NOT create an extended bidding war

STEP 4: DECIDE AND COMMIT
Set a deadline for your decision (3-5 business days).
Once you decide, inform all parties immediately:
  - To the winner: "We'd like to move forward. Let's sign."
  - To others: "We've decided to go in a different direction.
    We appreciate your interest and hope to work together
    in the future."

STEP 5: CLOSE QUICKLY
Once you select, move to definitive documents immediately.
Momentum matters. Do not let the selected investor
lose enthusiasm.
```

### Evaluating Beyond Economics

```
TOTAL TERM SHEET VALUE ASSESSMENT

Economics (40% weight):
  - Valuation and dilution
  - Liquidation structure
  - Anti-dilution terms

Governance (20% weight):
  - Board composition
  - Protective provisions
  - Founder control preservation

Investor Quality (30% weight):
  - Track record and reputation
  - Relevant portfolio and network
  - Partner quality (the person, not just the firm)
  - Follow-on investment history
  - Board behavior reputation (reference checks!)

Operational Terms (10% weight):
  - Information rights
  - ROFR/co-sale
  - No-shop duration
  - Closing timeline

EXAMPLE COMPARISON:

Term                  Firm A        Firm B        Firm C
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Pre-money             $20M          $18M          $22M
Liquidation           1x non-part   1x non-part   1x part
Anti-dilution         Broad WA      Broad WA      Full ratchet
Board                 2C/1P/2I     2C/1P/2I      2C/2P/1I
Investor Reputation   Top tier      Mid tier      New fund
Follow-on History     Strong        Moderate      Unknown
Partner Fit           Excellent     Good          Average

ANALYSIS:
  Firm C: Highest valuation but participating preferred +
          full ratchet + investor board majority + new fund
          → Worst deal despite best headline number

  Firm A: Moderate valuation, clean terms, best partner
          → Best overall deal despite not highest valuation

  Firm B: Lowest valuation, clean terms, decent fit
          → Negotiate up or use as leverage with Firm A
```

## Negotiation Psychology

### Reading Investor Signals

```
INVESTOR NEGOTIATION SIGNALS

STRONG INTEREST SIGNALS:
  - Quick response to communications (same day)
  - Introduces you to portfolio founders
  - Asks detailed operational questions (not just financial)
  - Partner calls you directly (not through associate)
  - Invites you to partner meeting quickly
  - Talks about "how we'd work together" (not "if")
  - Provides references proactively
  - Discusses board dynamics and value-add specifics

LUKEWARM SIGNALS:
  - Delayed responses (3-5 days)
  - Asks for "more data" without specifics
  - Passes you to associate for follow-up
  - "Let us discuss internally and get back to you"
  - Vague timeline ("sometime in the next few weeks")

NEGATIVE SIGNALS:
  - Radio silence after meeting
  - "We need to see more traction"
  - "Interesting, but not right now"
  - Cancels or reschedules meetings
  - Sends associate to do reference calls (not partner)
```

### Maintaining Relationship Through Negotiation

```
PRESERVING THE LONG-TERM RELATIONSHIP

This investor will be on your board for 7-10 years.
How you negotiate sets the tone for the relationship.

PRINCIPLES:
1. Be firm on substance, warm on relationship
   "I really want to work with you. Here's what I need
    for the terms to work."

2. Never bluff
   If you say you have other term sheets, you must have them.
   If you say you'll walk away, you must be willing to.

3. Show your work
   "Here's why I think 1x non-participating is right:
    [data, comparables, logic]"
   Not: "I just want non-participating."

4. Win gracefully
   When you get a concession, don't gloat.
   "Thank you. This structure works well for both of us."

5. Lose gracefully
   When you concede a point, do so clearly.
   "I understand your position. We can work with that."

6. Express genuine enthusiasm
   "I'm excited about working together. Let's get this done."
```

## Common Negotiation Mistakes

```
MISTAKES THAT COST FOUNDERS

1. OVER-NEGOTIATING VALUATION ON A STRONG TERM SHEET
   Getting an extra $2M pre-money at the cost of investor
   goodwill is rarely worth it. Pick your battles.

2. UNDER-NEGOTIATING TERMS ON A HIGH-VALUATION OFFER
   High valuation with terrible terms (participating, ratchet)
   is worse than moderate valuation with clean terms.

3. NEGOTIATING WITHOUT UNDERSTANDING
   Agreeing to terms you don't understand because you don't
   want to appear unsophisticated. Always ask.

4. LETTING YOUR LAWYER NEGOTIATE EVERYTHING
   Lawyers should handle legal details. Founders should handle
   business terms. Don't abdicate the relationship to lawyers.

5. TAKING TOO LONG
   Extended negotiations signal indecisiveness and erode trust.
   The best negotiations conclude within 5-10 business days.

6. NEGOTIATING VIA EMAIL
   Complex negotiations should happen on calls or in person.
   Email creates misunderstandings and removes nuance.
   Use email to confirm agreements, not to negotiate them.

7. FORGETTING THAT TERMS COMPOUND
   A term that seems minor today (e.g., cumulative dividends)
   can have massive impact over 7 years.
   Model every term across time before accepting.

8. NOT HAVING A WALK-AWAY POINT
   Define your walk-away before entering negotiation.
   If you don't know when to walk away, you'll either
   over-concede or under-negotiate.
```
