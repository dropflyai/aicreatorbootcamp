# Competitive Intelligence — Gathering, Battle Cards, and Ethical CI

## The Role of Competitive Intelligence in Sales

Competitive intelligence (CI) is the systematic collection, analysis, and application
of information about competitors and the competitive environment. In sales, CI serves
one purpose: to help sellers win deals against specific competitors by understanding
their strengths, weaknesses, strategies, and likely moves.

The distinction between competitive intelligence and competitive information is
critical. Information is raw data (competitor raised prices, launched a feature, hired
a VP Sales). Intelligence is analyzed, contextualized, and actionable (competitor's
price increase creates a window for displacement in their mid-market segment because
their customers are cost-sensitive and their new VP Sales is focused on enterprise).

## CI Gathering Framework

### Source Categories

CI sources exist on a spectrum from public (openly available) to private (proprietary
or confidential). Ethical CI operates exclusively in the public and observable zones.

#### Tier 1: Public Sources (Open, Freely Available)

| Source | What It Reveals | Access Method |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| Competitor Website | Positioning, pricing, features, case studies | Direct browsing, Wayback Machine for changes |
| Job Postings | Strategic priorities, technology stack, team structure | LinkedIn Jobs, Glassdoor, Indeed |
| Press Releases | Funding, partnerships, product launches, executive hires | PR Newswire, Business Wire, Google Alerts |
| SEC Filings (public companies) | Revenue, growth rate, customer count, strategy | SEC EDGAR, annual reports, 10-K/10-Q |
| Patent Filings | R&D direction, future product capabilities | USPTO, Google Patents |
| Social Media | Messaging, culture, customer sentiment | LinkedIn, Twitter/X, YouTube |
| Industry Analyst Reports | Market position, competitive ranking, strengths/weaknesses | Gartner, Forrester, G2, TrustRadius |
| Review Sites | Customer satisfaction, feature gaps, support quality | G2, TrustRadius, Capterra, Reddit |

#### Tier 2: Observable Sources (Requires Active Collection)

| Source | What It Reveals | Collection Method |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| Win/Loss Interviews | Why buyers chose us or the competitor | Structured post-decision interviews |
| Customer Feedback | What existing customers say about competitors | CS team debriefs, NPS comments |
| Sales Team Intel | What reps hear in deals against competitors | CRM competitor fields, Slack channels |
| Conference Presentations | Strategy, product direction, market vision | Attend or watch recorded sessions |
| Technology Analysis | Product architecture, integrations, performance | Trial accounts, API documentation |
| Partner/Channel Intel | Competitor's partner strategy, deal dynamics | Partner feedback sessions |

#### Tier 3: Network Sources (Relationship-Based)

| Source | What It Reveals | Access Method |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| Industry Experts | Market dynamics, competitive trends | Advisory relationships, expert networks (GLG, AlphaSights) |
| Former Competitor Employees | Internal culture, strategy, product roadmap | Ethical interviews (no trade secrets) |
| Shared Customers | Head-to-head comparison from user perspective | Customer advisory board, QBR discussions |
| Analyst Relationships | Unpublished insights, market predictions | Analyst briefings and inquiries |

### CI Collection Cadence

| Activity | Frequency | Owner | Output |
|----------|-----------|-------|--------|
| Competitor website monitoring | Weekly | CI team / Marketing | Change log |
| Win/loss analysis | Monthly | Enablement + CI | Win/loss report |
| Review site monitoring | Weekly | CI team | Sentiment analysis |
| Sales team intel aggregation | Bi-weekly | Enablement | Intel digest |
| Analyst briefing | Quarterly | Product Marketing | Analyst insight summary |
| Deep competitive analysis | Quarterly | CI team | Competitive landscape report |
| Battle card update | Monthly | CI + Product Marketing | Updated battle cards |

## Battle Cards

### What Is a Battle Card?

A battle card is a concise, actionable document that equips a sales rep to compete
against a specific competitor in a deal. It is NOT a feature comparison matrix. It
is a strategic guide that tells a rep how to position, what to say, what questions
to ask, and how to handle competitor-specific objections.

### Battle Card Template

**Section 1: Competitor Overview (1/2 page)**
```
Company: [Competitor Name]
Founded: [Year] | Headquarters: [Location]
Funding/Revenue: [Most recent known]
Employees: [Approximate count]
Target Market: [Their ICP — how it differs from ours]
Key Customers: [Known logos]
Recent News: [Last 90 days — funding, launches, hires, losses]
```

**Section 2: Positioning Statement (2-3 sentences)**
How does the competitor position themselves? What is their core message?
What is their claimed differentiator?

**Section 3: Our Strengths Against Them (Top 3-5)**

| Our Strength | Why It Matters | Proof Point |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| [Capability 1] | [Business impact for buyer] | [Customer quote, data, or case study] |
| [Capability 2] | [Business impact for buyer] | [Customer quote, data, or case study] |
| [Capability 3] | [Business impact for buyer] | [Customer quote, data, or case study] |

**Section 4: Their Strengths Against Us (Top 3-5)**

| Their Strength | Our Counter-Narrative | Evidence |
|---------------|---------------------|----------|
| [Their capability] | [How we address or reframe this] | [Proof point] |
| [Their capability] | [How we address or reframe this] | [Proof point] |

**Section 5: Trap Questions**
Questions designed to expose competitor weaknesses (see below).

**Section 6: Objection Handling**
Specific scripts for when the buyer raises competitor-specific objections.

**Section 7: Land Mines**
Topics or criteria that, if introduced early, make it harder for the competitor to win.

**Section 8: Competitive Pricing Intelligence**
Known pricing model, typical discount ranges, and pricing strategy.

## Trap Questions

### What Are Trap Questions?

Trap questions are diagnostic questions that, when asked by the buyer, expose a
competitor's weakness. The seller does not attack the competitor — the seller
educates the buyer on what questions to ask during THEIR evaluation, which naturally
surfaces competitor gaps.

### Trap Question Design Framework

**Step 1:** Identify a specific competitor weakness
**Step 2:** Formulate a question the buyer should ask ANY vendor
**Step 3:** The question is fair and legitimate (not misleading)
**Step 4:** Our answer to the question is strong; the competitor's is weak

### Trap Question Examples

**When Competitor Has Poor Scalability:**
"One thing I would recommend asking any vendor you evaluate is: 'Can you share
performance benchmarks at 10x your current data volume? And can I speak with a
customer who has scaled to that level?' Understanding how the platform performs
under growth is critical because migration costs are significant if you outgrow
the tool."

**When Competitor Requires Heavy Professional Services:**
"An important question for any vendor: 'What percentage of your customers are
fully self-sufficient within 90 days of deployment, without professional services?'
Time to value without dependency on vendor services is a key indicator of product
maturity."

**When Competitor Has High Churn:**
"I always recommend asking: 'What is your net revenue retention rate?' and 'What
is your gross logo churn rate?' These numbers tell you how happy existing customers
are better than any case study."

**When Competitor Locks In Data:**
"A critical question for your evaluation: 'If we ever decide to leave, how do we
export our data? What format? What is the timeline? Is there a cost?' Data
portability is a sign of vendor confidence in their product."

### Trap Question Ethics

Trap questions must be:
- **Legitimate:** The question is one a buyer SHOULD ask any vendor
- **Fair:** The question is not misleading or based on false premises
- **Honest:** If the buyer asks us the same question, we answer truthfully
- **Non-disparaging:** You never name the competitor when suggesting the question

Trap questions that violate these principles are manipulative, not strategic, and
will damage your credibility if detected.

## Ethical CI Boundaries

### What Is Ethical CI?

The Society of Competitive Intelligence Professionals (SCIP, now Strategic and
Competitive Intelligence Professionals) provides the industry standard for CI ethics.

### Ethical CI Principles

1. **Identify yourself honestly.** Never misrepresent who you are or who you work
   for when gathering information.

2. **Respect confidentiality.** Never solicit, accept, or use trade secrets or
   proprietary information from competitor employees.

3. **Comply with all laws.** No trespassing, no electronic surveillance, no
   hacking, no bribery, no theft of proprietary documents.

4. **Respect sources.** Do not pressure sources to share information they are
   uncomfortable sharing.

5. **Use public information ethically.** Just because information is public does
   not mean all uses are ethical.

### Specific Ethical Lines

| Ethical | Unethical |
|---------|-----------|
| Reading competitor's public documentation | Accessing competitor's private systems |
| Asking a customer about their experience with a competitor | Posing as a customer to gather competitor pricing |
| Analyzing competitor job postings for strategic signals | Hiring competitor employees solely to extract trade secrets |
| Attending competitor's public webinars | Recording competitor's private meetings |
| Reviewing competitor trial/freemium product | Reverse engineering competitor code in violation of EULA |
| Asking general questions to former competitor employees | Asking for specific roadmap details under NDA |
| Monitoring competitor social media posts | Creating fake accounts to infiltrate competitor communities |

### Gray Areas and How to Navigate

**Scenario: A competitor employee applies to your company.**
Ethical: Interview normally, assess their skills and culture fit.
Unethical: Interview specifically to extract competitive information.

**Scenario: A customer shares their competitor's proposal with you.**
Ethical: Decline to review it and focus on your own value proposition.
Borderline: Review it and adjust your proposal accordingly.
Unethical: Share it with others in your organization or use it to disparage.

**Scenario: Competitor's pricing appears on a public forum.**
Ethical: Note the information as market intelligence.
Unethical: Use it to create misleading comparisons or share without context.

## CI Distribution and Activation

### Making CI Actionable

CI that lives in a document nobody reads is worthless. Distribution mechanisms:

| Channel | Content | Frequency |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| Battle Cards | Full competitive playbooks | Updated monthly |
| Slack/Teams Alerts | Breaking competitor news | Real-time |
| Weekly CI Digest | Summary of competitive developments | Weekly email |
| Sales Kickoff | Deep competitive strategy sessions | Annual |
| Deal-Level Support | Custom CI for specific opportunities | On-demand |
| Competitive Win Stories | How we won against [competitor] | Monthly |

### CI Feedback Loop

The most valuable CI comes from the sales team itself. Create a structured feedback
mechanism:

1. **CRM Competitor Field:** Required on every opportunity (multi-select for multiple
   competitors)
2. **Win/Loss Competitor Debrief:** Structured 15-minute interview after every
   competitive deal
3. **Slack CI Channel:** Open channel for reps to share what they hear in real-time
4. **Quarterly CI Review:** Cross-functional meeting (Sales, Product, Marketing) to
   analyze competitive trends and adjust strategy

---

**Competitive intelligence is the discipline of seeing the battlefield clearly.
It does not win deals by itself — it enables sellers to make informed strategic
decisions about how to position, differentiate, and win.**
