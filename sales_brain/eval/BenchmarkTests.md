# Sales Benchmark Tests -- Scenario-Based Evaluation

Every sales scenario below must be evaluated with a structured response.
These benchmarks test real-world sales acumen, not textbook answers.
Model responses show the standard. Your responses must meet or exceed.

---

## HOW TO USE BENCHMARK TESTS

1. Read the scenario completely before formulating a response
2. Draft your response independently
3. Compare against the model response
4. Score each response dimension: Strategy (1-5), Execution (1-5), Risk Mitigation (1-5)
5. Average must be >= 4.0 to pass

## SCENARIO 1: PROCUREMENT DISCOUNT DEMAND

**Situation:**
You are in final negotiation for a $120K annual deal. Your pricing is fair and validated by your champion. Procurement joins the call and demands a 30% discount, citing a competitor's lower price. Your champion is on the call but silent.

**Context:**
- Your list price: $120K/year
- Competitor price (claimed): $84K/year
- Your solution has 3 capabilities the competitor lacks
- Champion previously told you the competitor is "not as strong"
- Procurement's tone is aggressive but professional

**Challenge:**
Walk through your exact response, word by word.

### Model Response

**Step 1: Acknowledge without conceding.**
"Thank you for sharing that. I appreciate the transparency, and I want to make sure we find a structure that works for everyone."

**Step 2: Reframe from price to value.**
"Before we discuss numbers, I want to make sure we are evaluating the same scope. From our conversations with [Champion Name], there are three capabilities that were identified as critical to your success: [Capability 1], [Capability 2], and [Capability 3]. Can you confirm those are still priorities?"

**Step 3: Expose the comparison gap.**
"Those capabilities are not available in the alternative you mentioned. So when we compare pricing, we need to compare on equal scope. If we removed those capabilities from our proposal, our price would actually be below the number you cited. But based on [Champion]'s feedback, removing them would compromise the business outcome you are trying to achieve."

**Step 4: Offer a structured alternative (not a discount).**
"What I can do is offer a phased implementation that spreads the investment differently -- for example, a 2-year commitment at $110K/year, which gives you budget predictability while reflecting the long-term partnership. Would that be worth exploring?"

**Step 5: Re-engage the champion.**
"[Champion Name], does this align with how you see the path forward?"

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Did NOT immediately offer a discount
- Reframed the conversation to value
- Exposed the false comparison
- Offered a non-price concession (term commitment for reduced annual)
- Re-engaged the champion to shift power dynamics

## SCENARIO 2: CHAMPION GOES DARK

**Situation:**
A $75K deal has been in Stage 3 (Evaluation) for 3 weeks. Your champion, who was highly engaged, has not responded to 2 emails and 1 voicemail over the past 10 days. Last known status: they were presenting internally to the VP of Operations.

**Context:**
- Champion: Director of IT
- EB: VP of Operations (never met)
- Last meeting was a successful technical demo
- Champion said "I'll present this to my VP next week" -- that was 3 weeks ago
- You have no other contacts in the account

**Challenge:**
Create a 5-step recovery plan with exact actions and timelines.

### Model Response

**Day 1: Multi-channel outreach to champion (not desperate)**
- Send a brief, value-added email (not "just checking in"):
  "Hi [Name], I came across [relevant industry article/data point] that connects to the [specific pain point] we discussed. Thought it might be useful for your internal conversations. Happy to discuss if helpful."
- Call. Leave a 30-second voicemail referencing the email.
- Send a LinkedIn message: casual, professional, referencing shared context.

**Day 2-3: Activate alternative contacts**
- Identify 2-3 other stakeholders from discovery notes, LinkedIn, and org chart research.
- Reach out to a peer of the champion (e.g., another Director in IT or a technical lead who attended the demo): "Hi [Name], I enjoyed our conversation during the demo. I had a quick question about [technical detail] -- do you have 10 minutes this week?"
- Goal: Gather intel on what is happening internally without going over the champion's head.

**Day 4-5: Leverage your executive sponsor**
- Brief your VP of Sales or CRO on the situation.
- Propose an executive-to-executive outreach: your VP to their VP of Operations.
- Frame it as a "strategic alignment" conversation, not a deal rescue.
- Prepare a one-page executive briefing for your exec to use.

**Day 5-7: Create urgency without pressure**
- If champion responds: schedule next step immediately. Ask directly, "What happened when you presented to [VP]? How can I help?"
- If champion still dark: Send a "break-up" style email: "Hi [Name], I want to be respectful of your time. If the timing has changed or priorities have shifted, I completely understand. Just let me know so I can plan accordingly on our end."

**Day 7-10: Restage the deal**
- If no response after 10 days total silence: move deal to Stage 2 (re-qualify).
- Document all attempts in CRM.
- Set a 30-day nurture cadence.
- Conduct an internal debrief: what could have been done differently to multi-thread earlier?

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Did NOT send a desperate "just checking in" email
- Activated alternative contacts without going over champion's head
- Engaged executive sponsor appropriately
- Created urgency without damaging the relationship
- Had a clear restaging plan if recovery fails

## SCENARIO 3: CFO SURPRISE APPEARANCE

**Situation:**
You are on a scheduled call with your champion (VP of Marketing) and the Director of Marketing to discuss implementation timeline. 2 minutes into the call, the CFO joins unexpectedly. She immediately asks: "I have 10 minutes. Can someone tell me what the ROI is on this?"

**Context:**
- Deal value: $200K/year
- You have an ROI model but it hasn't been validated with the CFO yet
- Champion has been positioning internally but hasn't shared your ROI deck
- CFO is known to be data-driven and skeptical of marketing spend

**Challenge:**
What do you say in the first 60 seconds? How do you handle the next 10 minutes?

### Model Response

**First 60 seconds:**
"[CFO Name], great to have you here. I appreciate your time. Let me give you the 2-minute version, then I would love your perspective.

Your team identified that [specific problem] is costing approximately $[X] annually in [lost revenue / wasted spend / inefficiency]. Our solution addresses this by [specific mechanism], and based on the numbers [Champion Name] and I have validated, we are projecting a [X]x return within the first 12 months.

But I want to make sure those numbers hold up to your scrutiny. Can I walk you through the three key assumptions in our model?"

**Minutes 2-5: Walk the assumptions**
- Present exactly 3 assumptions (not 10). Keep it tight.
- For each: state the assumption, state the source (ideally the prospect's own data), state the sensitivity (what happens if the assumption is off by 20%).
- Example: "Assumption one: your team currently spends 15 hours per week on [task], based on what [Director] shared with us. If that is actually 12 hours or 18 hours, the ROI shifts from [X]x to [Y]x. Still positive in all scenarios."

**Minutes 5-8: Invite the CFO's challenge**
- "What assumptions would you challenge or adjust?"
- Let the CFO poke at the model. Every challenge they raise that you address increases credibility.
- If they raise a number you don't have: "That is a great point. I want to get that right rather than guess. Can we schedule 20 minutes this week to walk through the full model with your data?"

**Minutes 8-10: Anchor next steps**
- "Based on what we've discussed, would it be helpful for me to send you a one-page financial summary you can review on your own time? Then we could do a 30-minute deep dive with [Champion] to finalize the business case."
- Goal: get a second meeting with the CFO. That is the win.

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Did NOT panic or revert to a product pitch
- Led with business impact in the first 60 seconds
- Limited to 3 key assumptions (not a data dump)
- Showed sensitivity analysis (what if assumptions are wrong)
- Turned the surprise into an opportunity for deeper engagement
- Secured a next step with the CFO

## SCENARIO 4: BUDGET CUT MID-DEAL

**Situation:**
Your $150K deal is at Stage 4 (Proposal). The champion calls and says: "Bad news. We just had a company-wide budget freeze. All new spending over $50K needs CEO approval. I still want to do this, but I don't know how."

**Context:**
- Original budget holder: VP of Operations (your EB)
- CEO: Never engaged, no relationship
- Champion is genuine -- they really want the solution
- The problem you solve is real and costing them ~$400K/year
- Your minimum viable deal: $80K (reduced scope)

**Challenge:**
Develop a strategy to save this deal.

### Model Response

**Phase 1: Validate and quantify (Day 1)**
- "I appreciate you telling me directly. Let me ask a few questions so we can figure this out together."
- Confirm: Is the freeze permanent or temporary? Is it all spending or discretionary? Are there exceptions for projects with clear ROI?
- Get the exact new approval process in writing.
- Key question: "If we could show the CEO that this investment saves $400K per year, would that be enough to get an exception?"

**Phase 2: Restructure the deal (Day 1-3)**
- Option A: Phased approach. Start with $45K (below threshold) for Phase 1 that delivers quick ROI. Phase 2 expands after proving value.
- Option B: Multi-year commitment at reduced annual. $130K/year for 2 years instead of $150K/year for 1 year. Total contract value increases, annual decreases.
- Option C: ROI-linked structure. Lower upfront with performance milestones that trigger additional investment.
- Present all three options to champion. Let them advise which fits their internal reality.

**Phase 3: Build the CEO business case (Day 3-5)**
- Create a one-page executive summary: Problem ($400K annual cost), Solution (our platform), Investment ($X based on chosen option), ROI (Xx return), Timeline (value in 90 days).
- Coach the champion on how to present it: "Here is exactly what to say to your CEO..."
- Offer to have your CEO/CRO call their CEO: peer-to-peer conversation.

**Phase 4: Activate executive alignment (Day 5-7)**
- Your executive reaches out to their CEO: "I understand [Company] is being thoughtful about investments right now. We work with [similar companies] who faced the same budget scrutiny, and this is how they justified the investment..."
- This is not a hard sell. It is a credibility-building conversation.

**Phase 5: Accelerate timeline (Day 7-14)**
- If restructured deal gets traction: move fast. Budget freezes can tighten further.
- Get signatures before end of the fiscal period if possible.
- If CEO says no: graceful exit with relationship preserved. Set a 90-day re-engagement plan.

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Did NOT accept "budget freeze" at face value without probing
- Offered multiple restructuring options
- Built a CEO-level business case
- Activated executive-to-executive engagement
- Had a fallback plan if the deal cannot be saved

## SCENARIO 5: MULTI-VENDOR BAKE-OFF

**Situation:**
Your prospect announces they are doing a formal evaluation of 3 vendors (including you). They will score each vendor on a matrix they have created. The evaluation will take 4 weeks. They want a standardized demo, pricing in a specific format, and references.

**Context:**
- You are Vendor B (not the incumbent, not the cheapest)
- Vendor A is the incumbent (lower price, known limitations)
- Vendor C is a startup (flashy features, unproven at scale)
- Your champion is on the evaluation committee but not the decision-maker
- The evaluation matrix was designed by procurement

**Challenge:**
How do you win a bake-off you did not design?

### Model Response

**Strategy 1: Influence the criteria (Week 1)**
- Work with your champion to understand every criterion on the matrix.
- Identify criteria where you are strongest. Coach champion to weight those higher.
- Identify criteria that are missing but should matter (e.g., total cost of ownership, implementation risk, scalability). Suggest they be added.
- Key phrase for champion: "I want to make sure we are evaluating on the things that will actually determine success, not just features."

**Strategy 2: Control your demo (Week 1-2)**
- Do the standardized demo as requested, but also request a "custom scenario" slot.
- In the custom slot, show the 2-3 use cases where you dominate and competitors cannot follow.
- Plant questions for your champion to ask during competitor demos that expose their weaknesses.

**Strategy 3: Weaponize references (Week 2-3)**
- Do not just provide references. Curate them strategically.
- Choose references that match the prospect's industry, size, and use case exactly.
- Brief your references: "They will probably ask about X. Here is what would be most helpful to share..."
- If possible, arrange a peer conversation (their VP talks to your customer's VP).

**Strategy 4: Build consensus beyond the committee (Week 2-3)**
- Identify users who will actually use the product daily. Get them excited.
- Find the "silent influencer" -- the person not on the committee but whose opinion carries weight.
- Create grassroots support so the committee hears from multiple directions.

**Strategy 5: Reframe the decision (Week 3-4)**
- Before the final decision meeting, send a "decision framework" one-pager to your champion:
  "When evaluating vendors, the top 3 factors that determine long-term success are [your strengths]. Here is why, based on data from [analyst report / case study]."
- This reframes the decision from the procurement matrix to your narrative.

**Strategy 6: Handle pricing strategically**
- Price on value, not on their format. Include an ROI model alongside the formatted pricing.
- If they demand apples-to-apples pricing, include a "total cost of ownership" analysis that factors in implementation, training, ongoing support, and switching costs.
- Make it clear what they get for the price difference.

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Did NOT passively accept the evaluation framework
- Influenced criteria through the champion
- Set competitive traps for the other vendors
- Built support beyond the evaluation committee
- Reframed the decision narrative
- Handled pricing strategically with value framing

## SCENARIO 6: INBOUND LEAD -- QUICK QUALIFICATION

**Situation:**
An inbound lead comes in from a demo request form. The contact is a "Marketing Coordinator" at a 50-person company. They wrote: "We are looking at tools like yours to help with our marketing automation."

**Context:**
- Your average deal size is $80K
- Your ICP is 200-2000 employee companies
- Marketing Coordinators are typically not decision-makers
- This company might be too small

**Challenge:**
Do you pursue this lead? If yes, how? If no, what do you do instead?

### Model Response

**Step 1: Quick ICP assessment (5 minutes)**
- Company size: 50 employees (below ICP of 200-2000). Flag as potential disqualification.
- But check: Are they funded? Growing rapidly? Revenue per employee high?
- Check LinkedIn: Is the company hiring aggressively? Recently raised funding?
- Decision: If no growth signals, this is likely not a fit.

**Step 2: If pursuing -- qualify fast, do not invest heavy**
- Send a short, friendly response with 3 qualifying questions:
  1. "What is driving the need for marketing automation right now?"
  2. "How large is your marketing team currently?"
  3. "Do you have a budget and timeline in mind?"
- Based on responses, decide in 24 hours whether to invest more time.

**Step 3: If disqualifying -- do it gracefully**
- "Thanks for your interest! Based on what you shared, our solution is typically best suited for companies with larger marketing teams. I would recommend looking at [Alternative A] or [Alternative B] which might be a better fit for your current stage. If things change as you grow, we would love to reconnect."
- Log in CRM with nurture tag. Set a 6-month check-in.

**Step 4: Extract value regardless**
- Even if disqualified: ask how they heard about you (attribution data).
- Ask if they know others in their network who might benefit (referral).
- Add to marketing nurture sequence for future re-engagement.

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Did NOT spend hours on a likely unqualified lead
- Made a data-driven qualification decision
- Disqualified gracefully with alternatives
- Extracted referral and attribution value

## SCENARIO 7: LEGAL REDLINE NIGHTMARE

**Situation:**
Your $180K deal is in final contract review. The customer's legal team returns the contract with 47 redlines, including changes to limitation of liability, indemnification, data processing, SLA terms, and a demand to remove your arbitration clause.

**Context:**
- Deal was supposed to close this week (end of quarter)
- Customer's legal team has a reputation for being aggressive
- Your champion wants to close but has no authority over legal
- Your legal team says 30 of the 47 redlines are acceptable, 10 need discussion, and 7 are non-starters

**Challenge:**
How do you close this deal this quarter?

### Model Response

**Hour 1-4: Triage and strategy**
- Categorize all 47 redlines into three buckets with your legal team:
  - Accept (30): Sign off immediately. Show willingness to move.
  - Discuss (10): Prepare alternative language that addresses their concern while protecting your interests.
  - Non-starters (7): Prepare business justification for why these cannot change, with examples of how other enterprise customers accepted these terms.
- Prepare a single document showing all 47 items with your response to each.

**Hour 4-8: Champion alignment**
- Call your champion: "Here is where we are. We have accepted 30 of 47 changes immediately. We have alternative language for 10 more. There are 7 items we need to discuss further. Can you help me understand your legal team's priorities among those 7?"
- Ask: "Who on your legal team has the authority to make final calls? Can we get a direct conversation scheduled?"

**Day 2: Direct legal-to-legal call**
- Schedule a 1-hour call between your legal counsel and their legal counsel.
- Sales should be on the call but let legal lead the legal discussion.
- Pre-brief your legal: "The priority is closing this quarter. Be flexible on language, firm on substance."
- Go through the 10 "discuss" items first (build momentum with agreements) before tackling the 7 non-starters.

**Day 2-3: Escalation if needed**
- If their legal will not move on critical items: escalate to business sponsors on both sides.
- Frame it as: "Legal has done great work narrowing down to [X] items. We need business leadership to weigh in on the risk tolerance for these final points."
- Your CRO to their VP: "We want to get this done. Here is what we need to move forward."

**Day 3-4: Creative solutions for non-starters**
- For each non-starter, prepare a creative alternative:
  - Can not remove arbitration? Offer a choice-of-venue clause.
  - Limitation of liability too low? Offer a higher cap tied to contract value.
  - SLA terms too aggressive? Offer a ramp-up period with full SLAs after 90 days.
- Show flexibility in structure while protecting substance.

**Day 4-5: Final push**
- If 5+ of the 7 are resolved, push for signature.
- If 2-3 remain: propose a "letter of intent" or "commercial agreement" that locks in the deal for revenue recognition, with a 30-day window to finalize legal terms.
- Document everything. Get executive approval for any creative structures.

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Triaged immediately instead of panicking
- Showed flexibility by accepting 30 redlines up front
- Engaged legal-to-legal directly
- Had creative alternatives for non-starters
- Had a backup plan for revenue recognition if full contract is delayed

## SCENARIO 8: UPSELL INTO EXISTING ACCOUNT

**Situation:**
A current customer ($60K ARR) has been using your product for 1 year. Usage metrics show they are only using 40% of features. Their renewal is in 3 months. You have been asked to upsell them to a $120K tier.

**Challenge:**
How do you upsell a customer who is not fully using what they already have?

### Model Response

**Step 1: Diagnose the usage gap first**
- Do NOT pitch the upsell yet. First understand why usage is at 40%.
- Possible reasons: poor onboarding, wrong users trained, features do not match workflow, champion left, competing tools in use.
- Schedule a "health check" call: "We want to make sure you are getting maximum value before your renewal. Can we review your usage together?"

**Step 2: Fix the value gap**
- Based on diagnosis, create a 30-day "value acceleration" plan.
- Get usage from 40% to 70%+ before discussing expansion.
- This builds credibility and eliminates the "why would we buy more when we are not using what we have" objection.

**Step 3: Expand from proven value**
- Once usage improves, identify the specific use cases where expanded features would multiply value.
- Frame the upsell around business outcomes, not features: "You are now saving $[X] annually with core features. The advanced tier would address [additional pain point] that [specific stakeholder] raised, adding another $[Y] in savings."

**Step 4: Multi-thread the expansion**
- Your current champion may not have authority for the expansion budget.
- Identify who benefits from the additional capabilities. Get them involved.
- Create a business case that includes both the current value (proven) and projected additional value.

**Scoring Criteria:**
- Did NOT pitch the upsell to an underutilizing customer
- Diagnosed the usage gap first
- Built value before asking for more money
- Created a business case grounded in proven results

## SCENARIO 9: PROSPECT ASKS "WHAT MAKES YOU DIFFERENT?"

**Situation:**
In the first 5 minutes of a discovery call, the prospect asks: "So, what makes you different from [Competitor X]?"

**Challenge:**
Respond without turning discovery into a pitch.

### Model Response

"Great question, and I want to give you a real answer, not a marketing one. The honest truth is that what makes us different depends entirely on what matters most to you. For some of our customers, it is [Strength A]. For others, it is [Strength B]. But I would rather understand your situation first so I can tell you specifically where we add value that others do not.

Can I ask -- what prompted you to take this meeting today? What is the problem you are trying to solve?"

**Why this works:**
- Acknowledges the question respectfully
- Does not trash the competitor
- Pivots back to discovery
- Shows confidence (not defensive)
- Demonstrates customer-centricity

## SCENARIO 10: END-OF-QUARTER PRESSURE

**Situation:**
It is the last week of Q4. You are $200K short of quota. You have one deal at $180K in Stage 4 and one deal at $50K in Stage 3. Your manager is pressing hard.

**Challenge:**
What is your plan for the week? What will you do and what will you NOT do?

### Model Response

**What I WILL do:**
- $180K deal: Call champion. Be direct. "Where are we? What is left to close? What can I do to help this week?" Map every remaining step with dates. Remove every obstacle personally.
- $50K deal: Assess honestly. Is it closable in 5 days? If yes, go all-in on compressing timeline. If not, do not force it -- forcing creates bad deals.
- Check pipeline for any other deals that could pull forward legitimately.
- Communicate transparently with my manager: "Here is where each deal stands. Here is what I need help with."

**What I will NOT do:**
- Will not offer unnecessary discounts to pull deals forward
- Will not pressure a Stage 3 deal to skip qualification
- Will not promise implementation timelines we cannot deliver
- Will not misrepresent deal stage to make the forecast look better
- Will not sacrifice Q1 pipeline by pulling deals forward artificially

**Why this matters:**
Desperation closes destroy trust. A deal closed with a bad discount or broken promise costs more than a missed quarter.

## SCENARIO 11: INBOUND RFP -- DO WE RESPOND?

**Situation:**
You receive a 40-page RFP from a Fortune 500 company. The deal could be worth $500K+. You have no existing relationship in the account. The RFP deadline is 3 weeks away.

**Challenge:**
Do you respond? What is your decision framework?

### Model Response

**Decision Framework (Answer 5 questions):**

1. **Did we help write this RFP?** No. This is a cold RFP. That means someone else likely shaped the requirements.
2. **Can we win on merit alone?** Review the requirements. If >70% align with our strengths, maybe. If <50%, no.
3. **Can we access the decision-maker before the deadline?** If the RFP prohibits vendor contact, we are flying blind. Disadvantaged.
4. **Do we have references in their industry?** If yes, we have credibility. If not, we are a risk to them.
5. **What is the opportunity cost?** 3 weeks of team time (sales, SE, legal) on a low-probability deal vs. working qualified pipeline.

**Decision: Usually NO for cold RFPs.**
- Win rates on cold RFPs are typically 5-10%.
- If we respond, we are column fodder (making the incumbent's required "3 bids" look legitimate).

**If we DO respond:**
- Condition: We must get a meeting with the business stakeholder (not just procurement) within Week 1.
- If meeting is declined: do not respond.
- If meeting is granted: customize response heavily around their specific pain, not generic capabilities.

## SCENARIO 12: CUSTOMER THREATENS TO CHURN

**Situation:**
Your largest customer ($300K ARR) calls to say they are evaluating competitors for their renewal in 60 days. They cite "lack of innovation" and "better pricing elsewhere."

**Challenge:**
Save this account. Walk through your 60-day plan.

### Model Response

**Week 1: Emergency discovery**
- Schedule an in-person or video meeting with all key stakeholders. Not just your champion -- the EB and end users too.
- Ask: "Help me understand specifically what 'lack of innovation' means for your team. What capabilities do you need that you feel we have not delivered?"
- Ask: "What pricing have you seen, and what scope does it include?" (Competitors often quote lower for less.)
- Document every concern in writing. Share it back to them: "Here is what I heard. Did I get it right?"

**Week 2-3: Action plan**
- For each concern, prepare a response: product roadmap items, workarounds, services, pricing adjustments.
- Engage your product team: Can any requested features be fast-tracked?
- Engage your executive sponsor: CRO or CEO calls their exec. "You are our most important customer. Here is what we are doing."
- Create a written "Customer Success Plan" with specific commitments and dates.

**Week 4-6: Execute and prove**
- Deliver on at least one commitment tangibly (beta access, feature preview, dedicated support).
- Weekly check-ins with champion. "Is this addressing your concerns?"
- If pricing is genuinely uncompetitive: restructure the deal. Multi-year, expanded scope, volume discount -- but only with reciprocal commitment.

**Week 7-8: Close the renewal**
- Present renewal proposal with value summary: "Here is what you achieved this year. Here is what we are committing to next year."
- Get signatures. Do not let it drag past the 60-day window.

## SCENARIO 13: PROSPECT SAYS "SEND ME PRICING"

**Situation:**
First email exchange. Prospect writes: "This looks interesting. Can you send me your pricing?"

**Challenge:**
Respond without losing the deal and without sending a price sheet into a vacuum.

### Model Response

"Happy to share pricing details. Our pricing depends on a few factors like team size, use cases, and implementation scope, so I want to make sure I give you numbers that are actually relevant to your situation.

Can we grab 15 minutes this week? I will come prepared with pricing options tailored to what you are looking for. What does [Day/Time] look like?"

**If they insist on pricing via email:**
"Totally understand. Here is a high-level range: our customers typically invest between $[Low] and $[High] annually depending on scope. To give you a precise number, I would need to understand [2 specific things]. Happy to do that over a quick call or email exchange -- whichever you prefer."

## SCENARIO 14: DEAL LOST TO COMPETITOR -- POST-MORTEM

**Situation:**
You lost a $100K deal to Competitor X after 3 months of work. The prospect chose the competitor despite your champion's support.

**Challenge:**
Conduct a thorough post-mortem. What do you analyze?

### Model Response

**Post-Mortem Framework:**

1. **When did we actually lose?** Identify the moment the deal shifted. Was it after a specific demo? A competitor meeting? An internal meeting we were not in?
2. **Who made the final decision?** Was it our champion? The EB? A committee? Someone we never met?
3. **What criteria did they ultimately use?** Were they the criteria we thought, or did they change?
4. **Where were we single-threaded?** Did we rely too heavily on one contact?
5. **What did the competitor do that we did not?** Better demo? Lower price? Executive engagement? Relationship?
6. **What would we do differently?** Be specific. Not "better discovery" but "we should have asked X in the first meeting."
7. **Customer exit interview:** Request a 15-minute call with the prospect. "No hard feelings. I just want to learn." Most will agree. The intel is invaluable.

## SCENARIO 15: INTERNAL CHAMPION FIGHT

**Situation:**
Two departments at a prospect company both want your product but want different configurations. The VP of Sales wants Configuration A ($100K). The VP of Marketing wants Configuration B ($80K). They are fighting internally and your deal is stuck.

**Challenge:**
Unstick this deal without picking a side.

### Model Response

**Step 1: Understand both positions**
- Meet with each VP separately. Understand their specific requirements and why they differ.
- Document the overlap (where they agree) and the gaps (where they diverge).

**Step 2: Create a unified proposal**
- Design a Configuration C that addresses both needs.
- Show each VP: "Here is how your requirements are met within this structure."
- Price it appropriately (likely $110-130K given expanded scope).

**Step 3: Facilitate the internal conversation**
- Offer to lead a joint meeting: "I have spoken to both teams and I think there is a way to meet everyone's needs with a single implementation. Would it be helpful if I walked through the options with both teams present?"
- You become the mediator, not a participant in the fight.

**Step 4: Escalate if needed**
- If they cannot agree, find their common boss (likely the COO or CEO).
- Through your exec sponsor, engage at that level: "Both teams see value. There is an opportunity to consolidate and save. Would it help if we presented a unified business case?"

## SCORING SUMMARY

Each scenario response is scored on three dimensions:

| Dimension | Weight | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Strategy | 40% | Quality of the approach and thinking |
| Execution | 35% | Specificity and actionability of the plan |
| Risk Mitigation | 25% | How well risks are identified and addressed |

**Pass:** Average >= 4.0 across all dimensions
**Conditional Pass:** Average 3.0-3.9 with no dimension below 3
**Fail:** Average < 3.0 or any dimension below 2
