# Channel Strategy — Direct vs. Indirect, Partner Types, and Economics

## The Channel Decision

The channel strategy decision — how you bring your product to market through
intermediaries — is one of the most consequential and least reversible decisions in
go-to-market design. A direct-only motion limits reach but maximizes control. A
channel-first motion maximizes reach but introduces dependency, margin compression,
and relationship complexity. Most mature B2B SaaS companies operate a hybrid model
that leverages both direct and indirect motions for different segments.

The fundamental question is not "should we have channel partners?" but "for which
segments, motions, and use cases does indirect distribution create net positive
value compared to direct?"

---

## Direct vs. Indirect: Decision Framework

### Direct Selling

**When Direct is Optimal:**
- Product requires deep technical discovery and configuration
- ACV is high enough to justify dedicated seller cost ($50K+)
- Buyer expects vendor-direct relationship (regulated industries, enterprise)
- Product-market fit is still being refined (direct provides feedback loop)
- Competitive advantage is in the sales experience itself

**Direct Selling Economics:**
```
Revenue per Rep:     $800K ACV annual (fully ramped)
Fully Loaded Cost:   $250K (OTE + benefits + tools + management overhead)
Sales Efficiency:    3.2x revenue per dollar of sales cost
Control:             Full (messaging, pricing, customer experience)
Feedback Cycle:      Immediate (direct customer contact)
Scalability:         Limited by hiring capacity and ramp time
```

### Indirect (Channel) Selling

**When Channel is Optimal:**
- Product is standardized with limited configuration needs
- Market is geographically dispersed (partner provides local presence)
- Buyer prefers purchasing through trusted advisors (consultants, MSPs, VARs)
- Market is fragmented and too expensive to cover with direct sales
- Product is complementary to partner offerings (creates bundling opportunity)
- Self-serve or low-touch ACV makes direct selling uneconomical

**Channel Selling Economics:**
```
Revenue per Partner: Varies dramatically ($50K - $5M annual)
Cost per Partner:    $50-150K (partner management, enablement, support)
Channel Margin:      15-40% given to partner (reduces your margin)
Control:             Partial (partner controls customer relationship)
Feedback Cycle:      Delayed (information filtered through partner)
Scalability:         High (leverage existing partner relationships)
```

### Hybrid Model (Recommended for Most B2B SaaS)

| Segment | Motion | Rationale |
|---------|--------|-----------|
| Enterprise ($100K+ ACV) | Direct (with partner overlay) | Complexity requires direct engagement |
| Mid-Market ($25K-$100K ACV) | Direct + Channel (co-sell) | Partner provides reach, you provide depth |
| SMB (<$25K ACV) | Channel-led or self-serve | Direct sales uneconomical at this ACV |
| New Geographies | Channel-first | Partner provides local expertise and relationships |
| Vertical-Specific | Channel with vertical specialist | Partner has domain expertise you lack |

## Partner Types

### Technology Partners (ISVs)

**Definition:** Companies whose products integrate with yours to create combined value.
**Value Exchange:** Technology integration creates mutual customer value and referral flow.
**Examples:** CRM + Marketing Automation, Data Warehouse + BI Tool, Cloud Platform + SaaS App.

**Partnership Tiers:**
| Tier | Integration Depth | Go-to-Market | Investment |
|------|------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Integration | API connection, basic data flow | Listing on marketplace | Low |
| Technology Alliance | Deep integration, shared data model | Joint marketing, co-sell | Medium |
| Strategic Technology | Embedded/native integration | Joint roadmap, shared engineering | High |

### Resellers and VARs (Value-Added Resellers)

**Definition:** Companies that resell your product, often bundled with their own
services (implementation, customization, training).
**Value Exchange:** They earn margin on the resale; you gain reach and service capacity.

**Reseller Economics:**
```
List Price:              $100K
Reseller Discount:       25-40% ($25-40K margin for partner)
Your Net Revenue:        $60-75K
Partner's Revenue:       $25-40K product margin + $50-100K services revenue
Customer Pays:           $100K (or higher with services)
```

### System Integrators (SIs)

**Definition:** Consulting firms that implement complex technology solutions for
enterprise customers (Accenture, Deloitte, KPMG, and boutique firms).
**Value Exchange:** They recommend and implement your product within larger
transformation projects; you provide technology that enhances their project scope.

**SI Partnership Dynamics:**
- SIs do not resell — they recommend and implement
- Their loyalty is to the customer's outcome, not your product
- Revenue comes from services ($150-500/hour billing rates)
- Their recommendation carries enormous weight with enterprise buyers
- They expect deep product knowledge and responsive partner support

### Managed Service Providers (MSPs)

**Definition:** Companies that operate technology on behalf of customers, typically
for SMB and mid-market segments.
**Value Exchange:** They bundle your product into their managed service; you gain
distribution without direct customer management.

### Consulting and Advisory Partners

**Definition:** Strategy consultants, fractional executives, and advisory firms
that recommend technology to their clients.
**Value Exchange:** Referral fees or influence-based relationship; they recommend
your product during strategic engagements.

### Marketplace Partners

**Definition:** Cloud marketplace listings (AWS, Azure, GCP, Salesforce AppExchange).
**Value Exchange:** Access to marketplace buyers, procurement simplification, and
cloud commitment burn-down.

## Channel Economics

### Partner Compensation Models

| Model | Structure | When to Use |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Reseller Discount | Partner buys at discount, resells at list | Transactional, product-led |
| Referral Fee | Partner refers, you sell, partner gets 10-20% of first-year ACV | Light-touch partnerships |
| Revenue Share | Partner and you split ongoing revenue (70/30, 80/20) | Deep, ongoing partnerships |
| Influence Fee | Flat fee or percentage for deals partner influenced | Advisory and SI partnerships |
| MDF (Market Development Funds) | You fund partner's marketing activities | Demand generation partnerships |

### Channel Unit Economics

```
Direct Sale:
  Revenue: $100K ACV
  Cost of Sale: $25K (rep + support)
  Gross Margin on Sale: 75%

Channel Sale (Reseller):
  Revenue: $75K net (after 25% partner margin)
  Cost of Sale: $8K (partner manager allocation)
  Gross Margin on Sale: 89% of net revenue
  BUT: Absolute margin is $67K vs $75K direct

Channel Sale (Referral):
  Revenue: $85K net (after 15% referral fee)
  Cost of Sale: $5K (partner management)
  Gross Margin on Sale: 94% of net revenue
  Absolute margin: $80K (higher than direct due to lower cost of sale)
```

**When Channel is Economically Superior:**
Channel sales are economically superior when the cost of direct acquisition (sales
rep cost, marketing cost, travel) exceeds the channel margin. This is typically
true for:
- Low-ACV deals (<$25K) where direct rep cost exceeds margin
- Geographically remote markets where travel costs are prohibitive
- Vertical markets where partner's domain expertise shortens sales cycles

## Channel Conflict Management

### Types of Channel Conflict

| Conflict Type | Description | Prevention |
|--------------|-------------|-----------|
| Direct vs. Channel | Your direct team competes with your partners | Deal registration, segment rules |
| Channel vs. Channel | Two partners compete for the same deal | Territory assignment, first-to-register |
| Pricing Conflict | Partner undercuts your direct pricing | MAP (Minimum Advertised Price) policy |
| Service Conflict | Partner provides poor service, damages your brand | Partner certification, SLA requirements |
| Poaching | Partner or you steal each other's customers | Non-compete clauses, relationship boundaries |

### Deal Registration System

Deal registration is the primary mechanism for preventing channel conflict:

**How It Works:**
1. Partner identifies an opportunity and registers it in your partner portal
2. Registration includes: account name, contact, estimated ACV, expected close date
3. Your team reviews within 24-48 hours (accept, reject, or flag as duplicate)
4. If accepted, partner has exclusive rights for a defined period (60-90 days)
5. If partner does not progress the deal within the period, registration expires
6. Registered deals receive higher margin or enhanced support

**Registration Rules:**
- First to register wins (if two partners register the same account)
- Registration does not guarantee margin — deal must progress
- Registration does not override existing direct relationships
- Expired registrations can be re-registered or converted to direct
- Disputes escalated to channel chief within 48 hours

## Building a Channel Program

### Phase 1: Foundation (Months 1-6)

**Objective:** Prove channel viability with 3-5 pilot partners

1. Define ideal partner profile (ICP for partners)
2. Recruit 3-5 pilot partners in different categories
3. Build minimum viable partner enablement (training, content, demo environment)
4. Create deal registration process (can be spreadsheet initially)
5. Assign dedicated partner manager
6. Measure: partner-sourced pipeline, first partner-closed deal

### Phase 2: Formalization (Months 6-12)

**Objective:** Build repeatable partner program

1. Develop partner portal (training, content, deal registration, reporting)
2. Create tiered partner program (Silver/Gold/Platinum or equivalent)
3. Build partner certification curriculum
4. Formalize partner compensation (margin schedule, referral fees)
5. Develop co-marketing playbook (joint webinars, co-branded content)
6. Measure: partner contribution to pipeline (target 15-25%)

### Phase 3: Scale (Months 12-24)

**Objective:** Channel becomes a significant revenue contributor

1. Hire dedicated channel team (channel AEs, partner marketing, partner ops)
2. Scale to 20-50 active partners
3. Launch marketplace listings (AWS, Azure, AppExchange)
4. Develop SI and consulting relationships for enterprise
5. Implement partner analytics and optimization
6. Measure: partner contribution to revenue (target 20-40%)

## Partner Program Metrics

| Metric | Formula | Target |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Partner-Sourced Pipeline | Pipeline where partner identified the opportunity | 20-30% of total |
| Partner-Influenced Pipeline | Pipeline where partner was involved but did not source | 15-25% of total |
| Partner-Closed Revenue | Revenue closed by partners | 15-30% of total |
| Partner Activation Rate | Partners with revenue / Total partners | >40% |
| Average Revenue per Active Partner | Total partner revenue / Active partners | Growing trend |
| Partner Satisfaction (PSAT) | Survey score (1-10) | >7.5 |
| Time to First Deal | Days from partner activation to first closed deal | <180 days |
| Deal Registration Volume | Registrations submitted per month | Growing trend |
| Co-Sell Win Rate | Win rate on co-sell deals vs. partner-only deals | Co-sell > solo |

---

**Channel strategy is not outsourcing sales. It is extending your market reach
through strategic relationships that create mutual value. Partners are not
alternatives to direct sales — they are force multipliers.**
