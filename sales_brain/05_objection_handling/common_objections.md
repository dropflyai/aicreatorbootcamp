# Common Objections — Top 25 Objections with Response Scripts

## How to Use This Module

Each objection below includes: the verbatim objection, the typical root cause, the
diagnostic question to identify the true concern, and a scripted response framework.
Scripts are starting points — they must be adapted to your product, market, and buyer
context. Memorizing scripts without understanding the psychology behind them produces
robotic, ineffective responses.

The response format follows the LAER model: Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond.

---

## PRICE OBJECTIONS

### Objection 1: "It is too expensive"

**Root Cause:** Value not established, budget mismatch, or anchoring to competitor price.

**Diagnostic:** "When you say too expensive, help me understand: too expensive compared
to what? Your budget, a competitor's price, or the expected return?"

**Response Framework:**
"I hear you, and price matters — it should. Let me share some context. Our customers
typically see [specific metric] improvement within [timeframe]. For a company your
size, that translates to approximately [$value] annually. So the question becomes:
is [$price] a reasonable investment to generate [$value] in return? Let us walk
through the numbers together to see if the math works for your specific situation."

**If compared to a competitor:**
"[Competitor] is a solid product at a lower price point. The difference in investment
typically reflects [specific differentiator]. Our customers who evaluated both found
that the [differentiator] saved them [quantified value] that more than offset the
price difference. Would it help to connect you with one of those customers?"

### Objection 2: "We do not have budget for this"

**Root Cause:** Genuine budget constraint, or budget exists but is allocated elsewhere.

**Diagnostic:** "Is this a timing issue — budget is not available now but could be in
a future cycle? Or is this project not budgeted at all?"

**Response:**
"I understand — budget cycles are real constraints. Let me ask: if we could demonstrate
that this investment pays for itself within [timeframe], would that change the
conversation? Sometimes the ROI case is strong enough to justify reallocation or
off-cycle budget request. Would it be helpful if I built a business case you could
present to your CFO?"

### Objection 3: "Can you do better on price?"

**Root Cause:** Procurement conditioning or genuine price sensitivity.

**Diagnostic:** "Absolutely, I want to find the right structure. To do that, help me
understand: are we in the right ballpark, or is there a specific number you need to
hit? And what would a yes look like if we can get there on price?"

**Response:**
"I appreciate the directness. Our pricing reflects the value we deliver, and we want
to make sure the structure works for both of us. Here are some options: we could
adjust the scope to match your budget, we could structure payments differently
(annual vs. multi-year), or we could start with a smaller deployment and expand as
you see value. Which of those would be most helpful to explore?"

## STATUS QUO OBJECTIONS

### Objection 4: "We are happy with our current solution"

**Root Cause:** Loss aversion, switching cost perception, or genuine satisfaction.

**Diagnostic:** "That is great to hear — a working solution is valuable. Out of
curiosity, if you could change one thing about your current setup, what would it be?"

**Response:**
"Most of our customers were happy with their previous solution too — until they saw
what was possible. I am not suggesting your current approach is broken. What I am
suggesting is that there may be a significant gap between 'working fine' and
'performing optimally.' [Company X] thought their solution was fine until they
realized they were losing [quantified cost]. Would it be worth 30 minutes to explore
whether a similar gap exists for you?"

### Objection 5: "We just signed a contract with [competitor]"

**Root Cause:** Recent decision, committed investment.

**Diagnostic:** "Congratulations on making a decision — that process is never easy.
When does your contract come up for renewal? And how will you measure success?"

**Response:**
"I respect that. Let me suggest something low-pressure: let us stay in touch over
the next [contract term]. I would love to share our product updates and customer
results periodically. That way, when renewal comes up, you have a clear comparison
point. Would a quarterly check-in be too much to ask?"

### Objection 6: "We are going to build it in-house"

**Root Cause:** Control preference, engineering culture, or underestimation of effort.

**Diagnostic:** "That makes sense for some organizations. Help me understand: have you
scoped the engineering effort? And how does this project compete for resources against
your core product roadmap?"

**Response:**
"Building in-house gives you maximum control — that is a real benefit. The question
our customers typically face is opportunity cost. Your engineering team could spend
[estimated months] building and maintaining this, or they could spend that time on
your core product differentiators. Our platform took [X years] and [$Y million] to
build with a dedicated team. The total cost of ownership for build-vs-buy usually
favors buy at 3-5x less over three years when you include maintenance, iteration,
and talent allocation. Want me to put together a TCO comparison?"

## TIMING OBJECTIONS

### Objection 7: "Not the right time"

**Root Cause:** Competing priorities, fiscal calendar, or insufficient urgency.

**Diagnostic:** "I completely understand — timing matters. Help me understand: is there
a specific event or milestone you are waiting for? Or is this more about bandwidth
and competing priorities right now?"

**Response:**
"Timing is important, and I would never push you into something that does not make
sense right now. That said, let me share something I see frequently: the cost of
waiting. Every month you operate without [solution], you are incurring approximately
[$cost] in [lost productivity/risk/missed revenue]. Over the next [typical delay
period], that adds up to [$total]. Is there a way to start small now and expand
when timing is better?"

### Objection 8: "We need to think about it"

**Root Cause:** Unresolved concerns, decision process not mapped, or polite rejection.

**Diagnostic:** "Of course — this is an important decision. Can I ask: what specific
aspects are you going to be thinking about? I want to make sure I have given you
everything you need to make a great decision."

**Response:**
"I want to support your decision process, not rush it. The most common things people
think about are [ROI, implementation, internal alignment]. Which of those, if any,
resonates? And would it help if I provided [specific resource] to make the evaluation
easier? Also, can we schedule a follow-up for [specific date] so I can answer any
questions that come up during your review?"

### Objection 9: "Send me some information"

**Root Cause:** Polite dismissal, genuine interest but low priority, or information
gathering for internal justification.

**Diagnostic:** "Happy to — I want to send you exactly what is relevant. What specific
questions are you trying to answer? That way I can tailor what I send rather than
burying you in generic materials."

**Response:**
"Absolutely. Rather than sending a generic deck, let me put together a brief summary
tailored to your situation — specifically around [problem discussed] and how we have
helped similar companies. I will include a one-page ROI estimate and a relevant case
study. I will send that by [day] and follow up on [day + 2] to see if it raises any
questions. Does that work?"

### Objection 10: "Call me back next quarter"

**Root Cause:** Current quarter is committed, fiscal year planning, or delay tactic.

**Diagnostic:** "I will absolutely follow up then. So I can be prepared: what changes
next quarter that makes this a better conversation? And is there anything we can do
now to set the groundwork so we are ready to move quickly when the time is right?"

**Response:**
"Happy to reconnect then. To make the most of our time next quarter, would it make
sense to do a brief technical evaluation now — no commitment, just getting your team
familiar with the platform? That way, when budget opens up, you are not starting from
scratch on a 6-week evaluation. We could do a 30-minute technical overview next week."

## AUTHORITY OBJECTIONS

### Objection 11: "I need to check with my boss"

**Root Cause:** Single-threaded deal, EB not engaged, or contact lacks authority.

**Diagnostic:** "Of course — what does your boss care about most when evaluating
something like this? And would it be helpful if I joined that conversation to answer
any technical or ROI questions directly?"

**Response:**
"I want to make sure your boss has everything they need to evaluate this properly.
In my experience, executives at that level care most about [ROI, risk, strategic
alignment]. Would it help if I prepared a one-page executive summary focused on those
areas? And I would be happy to join the meeting — not to sell, but to answer questions
firsthand. When are you planning to discuss this with them?"

### Objection 12: "The committee needs to review this"

**Root Cause:** Consensus-driven culture, formal procurement process.

**Diagnostic:** "Understood — can you walk me through the committee process? Who sits
on it, what criteria do they evaluate, and when do they next meet?"

**Response:**
"Buying committees are thorough, and that is a good thing. Let me help you be
successful in that meeting. I can provide: a one-page executive summary, a technical
architecture overview, an ROI analysis, and reference contacts in your industry.
Would it also be helpful for me to prepare answers to the top 5 questions committees
typically ask about solutions like ours? And I am happy to attend or be on standby
during the review."

## COMPETITION OBJECTIONS

### Objection 13: "[Competitor] has feature X that you do not"

**Root Cause:** Feature-level comparison, possibly seeded by competitor.

**Diagnostic:** "Good question — how important is [feature X] to solving the specific
problem we discussed? And have you seen it in action, or is this based on their
marketing materials?"

**Response:**
"You are right — [competitor] does have [feature X], and we do not in the same way.
Here is our approach to that need: [alternative approach]. The reason we chose this
path is [rationale]. What our customers in similar situations have found is that
[outcome]. The question I would encourage you to evaluate is: does [feature X] solve
the business problem better, or does our approach to [broader outcome] deliver more
value? Would a side-by-side evaluation on your actual use case help clarify?"

### Objection 14: "[Competitor] is cheaper"

**Root Cause:** Price shopping, procurement leverage, or genuine budget constraint.

**Diagnostic:** "Help me understand the comparison: are you looking at the same scope,
the same implementation, and the same ongoing support? Sometimes the headline price
does not tell the full story."

**Response:**
"Price is an important factor, and I want to make sure you are comparing apples to
apples. Our customers who evaluated [competitor] found that the total cost of
ownership — including implementation, customization, support, and expansion —
actually favored us because [specific reasons]. Let me build a 3-year TCO comparison
that includes all the hidden costs. Would that be useful?"

### Objection 15: "[Competitor] is the industry leader"

**Root Cause:** Risk aversion, social proof seeking, "nobody gets fired for buying IBM."

**Diagnostic:** "They are well-known — what specifically about their market position
appeals to you? Is it the brand, the feature set, or something else?"

**Response:**
"[Competitor] is established, and there is comfort in that. The trade-off our
customers often find is that market leaders optimize for breadth, not depth. They
serve everyone, which means they may not serve your specific needs as well. We are
focused exclusively on [segment/use case], and that focus means [specific advantage].
[Customer X], who evaluated both, chose us because [reason]. Can I connect you?"

## INTERNAL OBJECTIONS

### Objection 16: "Our team will not adopt this"

**Root Cause:** Change management concern, prior failed implementations.

**Response:**
"That is the most important concern in any software decision — adoption makes or breaks
the ROI. Here is how we address it: [onboarding program, training, customer success
support]. More importantly, our adoption rates are [X%] within 90 days. We also offer
a phased rollout starting with your most enthusiastic team members as internal
champions. Would it help to discuss our change management playbook?"

### Objection 17: "We had a bad experience with a similar product"

**Root Cause:** Prior implementation failure creating skepticism.

**Response:**
"I am sorry to hear that — a bad implementation wastes time and erodes trust. May I
ask what specifically went wrong? [Listen]. That is exactly why we [specific
differentiator in implementation/support]. We also offer [guarantee/SLA/pilot] so
you can validate before fully committing."

### Objection 18: "IT/Security will never approve this"

**Root Cause:** Procurement and compliance barriers, or excuse.

**Response:**
"Security and IT approval is essential, and we take it seriously. We are SOC 2 Type II
certified, GDPR compliant, and we have a pre-completed security questionnaire ready.
Would it help if our security team connected directly with yours? We have gone through
this process with [similar companies] and typically complete security review in [X weeks]."

## STALL OBJECTIONS

### Objection 19: "Let me do more research"

**Response:**
"Absolutely — informed decisions are the best decisions. What specific areas are you
researching? I may be able to point you to relevant resources: [analyst reports,
comparison guides, customer interviews]. Can we schedule a call for [date] to discuss
what you find?"

### Objection 20: "We are in a freeze right now"

**Response:**
"I understand — freezes happen. Two questions: is the freeze on new vendors, new
spending, or both? And when do you expect it to lift? In the meantime, would it
make sense to complete a technical evaluation so you are ready to move quickly once
the freeze ends?"

### Objection 21: "We need to see a reference first"

**Response:**
"Absolutely — references are important. Let me connect you with [company] who is in
your industry and uses our product for a similar use case. Would you prefer a call,
or would a written case study work as a starting point?"

## EDGE CASE OBJECTIONS

### Objection 22: "Your company is too small/new"

**Response:**
"Fair concern. Here are the facts: we have [$X] in funding with [Y] years of runway,
[Z] employees, and [N] customers including [notable logos]. Our net revenue retention
is [%], meaning customers are not just staying — they are expanding. Would it help
to meet our CEO or CTO to get a sense of the team and vision?"

### Objection 23: "I was burned by a startup before"

**Response:**
"That is a legitimate concern, and I do not take it lightly. Here is what makes us
different: [specific stability indicators]. We also offer [data portability/contract
protection/escrow] so your data and investment are protected regardless of what
happens. What specific protections would make you comfortable?"

### Objection 24: "We are being acquired/reorganizing"

**Response:**
"Congratulations — or condolences, depending on the day. Transitions create
uncertainty, but they also create opportunities to establish new standards. Would it
make sense to position this as the solution the combined entity adopts? Or would you
prefer to revisit after the dust settles? Either way, I am here."

### Objection 25: "Just send me the pricing"

**Response:**
"Happy to share pricing — I want to be transparent. Before I do, can I ask: what
would make this a 'good price' for you? I want to make sure I am proposing the right
package for your needs, not just the cheapest option. A 5-minute conversation about
your requirements will help me put together something that actually makes sense for
your situation."

## Objection Response Quality Checklist

For every objection response, verify:
- [ ] Did I acknowledge the concern genuinely (not dismissively)?
- [ ] Did I diagnose the root cause (not just the surface statement)?
- [ ] Did I provide evidence (data, case study, proof point)?
- [ ] Did I advance the conversation (propose a next step)?
- [ ] Did I avoid being defensive or argumentative?
- [ ] Did I verify the concern was resolved before moving on?

---

**The best objection handlers are not smooth talkers. They are deep listeners who
make buyers feel heard, understood, and supported in their decision process.**
