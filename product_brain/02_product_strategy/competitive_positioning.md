# Competitive Positioning

## What This Enables

A systematic approach to defining how your product is perceived relative to alternatives in the market. Positioning determines which category customers place you in, which competitors they compare you to, and which value dimensions they evaluate you on. Done well, positioning makes your product the obvious choice for your target segment. Done poorly, it forces you to compete on price.

---

## The Core Insight

April Dunford (Obviously Awesome, 2019): "Positioning is the act of deliberately defining how you are the best at something that a defined market cares a lot about." Positioning is not messaging (that comes after). It is not branding (that comes after). Positioning is the strategic foundation that messaging and branding are built upon. Most products fail at positioning not because they lack value, but because they position against the wrong alternatives, target the wrong segment, or compete on the wrong dimensions.

---

## The Five Components of Positioning (Dunford Framework)

### Component 1: Competitive Alternatives

**Question:** What would customers do if your product did not exist?

This is NOT "who are your competitors?" It is: what is the customer's status quo? What are they doing TODAY to solve this problem?

| Alternative Type | Example | Implication |
|-----------------|---------|-------------|
| Direct competitor | Another product in your category | Compete on features, price, or experience |
| Indirect competitor | Different category, same job | Compete on approach/paradigm |
| Manual process | Spreadsheets, email, pen and paper | Compete on efficiency and automation |
| Do nothing | Customer tolerates the pain | Compete on awareness and urgency |

**Key insight:** Your biggest competitor is often "do nothing" or "use a spreadsheet." If you position against a direct competitor but your customers are actually using spreadsheets, your messaging will miss.

### Component 2: Unique Attributes

**Question:** What do you have that the alternatives lack?

These must be objective, verifiable capabilities — not subjective claims like "best" or "easiest."

| Attribute Type | Example | Strength |
|---------------|---------|----------|
| Feature | "Real-time collaboration" | Moderate (competitors can copy) |
| Technology | "On-device AI processing" | Strong (hard to replicate) |
| Data/Network | "10M user dataset for recommendations" | Very strong (network effect) |
| Integration | "Native Salesforce integration" | Moderate (partner-dependent) |
| Business model | "Usage-based pricing, no per-seat fee" | Strong (structural advantage) |
| Team/expertise | "Founded by ex-Stripe payments engineers" | Moderate (credibility, not defensible) |

### Component 3: Value (to Customer)

**Question:** What value do those unique attributes deliver to customers?

Attributes are features. Value is what those features enable. Customers buy value, not attributes.

**Attribute -> Value mapping:**
- "Real-time collaboration" -> "Teams make decisions faster without waiting for email"
- "On-device AI processing" -> "Your data never leaves your device — complete privacy"
- "Usage-based pricing" -> "You only pay for what you use — no shelfware"

### Component 4: Target Customer Segment

**Question:** Who cares the most about the value you deliver?

Not "who could use this?" but "who would be MOST disappointed if this did not exist?"

**Segment characteristics to define:**
- Company size / individual profile
- Industry or vertical
- Current pain level (how badly they need this)
- Technical sophistication
- Budget authority
- Buying process

**The tighter the segment, the stronger the positioning.** "Marketing teams at B2B SaaS companies with 50-200 employees" is far more actionable than "businesses."

### Component 5: Market Category

**Question:** What market category do you belong in?

The category sets customer expectations for features, pricing, competitors, and buying process.

| Strategy | When to Use | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Enter existing category** | Category is well-understood, you have clear differentiation | "Project management for engineering teams" |
| **Create subcategory** | Existing category is too broad, you have a specific angle | "Revenue operations platform" (vs generic CRM) |
| **Create new category** | No existing category fits, you are defining a new space | "Product analytics" (Amplitude), "Conversational AI" |

## Category Creation

### When to Create a Category

Create a new category when:
1. Customers struggle to describe your product using existing categories
2. Your product spans multiple existing categories
3. Existing category positioning puts you against larger, established competitors
4. Your approach is fundamentally different from existing solutions

### Category Creation Framework

| Step | Action | Output |
|------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Define the problem in a new way | Problem framing document |
| 2 | Name the category | Category name (2-3 words, memorable) |
| 3 | Define category criteria | What makes a product belong in this category? |
| 4 | Position yourself as the category leader | First-mover advantage in the category you defined |
| 5 | Educate the market | Content, thought leadership, analyst briefings |
| 6 | Get validators | Analysts (Gartner, Forrester), customers, media |

### Category Creation Risks

- **Market education cost:** You must teach customers the category exists before you can sell
- **Premature category creation:** If the category does not resonate, you are alone in a market of one
- **Category hijacking:** A larger competitor adopts your category name and claims leadership
- **Confusion:** If the category is not intuitive, customers default to an existing category

**Rule of thumb:** Category creation is worth the cost only if positioning within existing categories forces you to compete on dimensions where you cannot win.

## Positioning Against Competition

### Competitive Positioning Matrix

Map competitors on the two dimensions your target segment cares about most:

```
                    HIGH [Dimension 1: e.g., Ease of Use]
                         │
                         │  ┌─────────┐
                         │  │  YOUR    │
                         │  │ PRODUCT  │
                         │  └─────────┘
                         │
    LOW [Dimension 2] ───┼─────────────── HIGH [Dimension 2]
     e.g., Enterprise    │                e.g., Enterprise
     Features            │     ┌─────────┐  Features
                         │     │Competitor│
                         │     │    A     │
                         │     └─────────┘
                         │
                    LOW [Dimension 1]
```

**Dimension selection is the most important positioning decision.** Choose dimensions where:
1. You can credibly claim leadership or strong position
2. Your target segment deeply cares about these dimensions
3. Competitors are weak on at least one dimension

### Moat Building

Positioning is temporary. Moats make it durable.

| Moat Type | Mechanism | Durability | Example |
|-----------|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Network effects** | Product gets better with more users | Very high | Slack (team adoption), Figma (collaboration) |
| **Switching costs** | Painful to leave (data, integrations, workflows) | High | Salesforce (CRM data), Notion (team knowledge base) |
| **Data advantage** | Proprietary data improves product | High | Google (search index), Waze (traffic data) |
| **Brand** | Trust and recognition | Moderate | Apple (premium), Stripe (developer trust) |
| **Economies of scale** | Cost advantage from volume | Moderate | AWS (infrastructure scale) |
| **Regulatory/IP** | Patents, licenses, compliance | High (but temporary) | Pharmaceutical patents |

### Competitive Response Framework

When a competitor makes a move:

| Competitor Action | Analysis | Response Options |
|------------------|----------|-----------------|
| Copies your feature | How quickly can they close the gap? | Accelerate innovation, deepen moat |
| Undercuts on price | Can they sustain it? What is their strategy? | Compete on value, not price; strengthen positioning |
| Enters your segment | How credible are they? | Double down on segment specialization |
| Creates new category | Does the category resonate with your customers? | Evaluate: join, ignore, or counter-position |
| Acquires a key player | What capability do they gain? | Assess: does this change competitive dynamics? |

## Positioning Process

### Step-by-Step

1. **List competitive alternatives** (what customers do today without your product)
2. **Identify unique attributes** (objective, verifiable capabilities you have that alternatives lack)
3. **Map attributes to value** (what customer outcomes do these attributes enable?)
4. **Identify best-fit customers** (who cares most about this value?)
5. **Choose market category** (existing, subcategory, or new)
6. **Write positioning statement** (see template below)
7. **Validate with customers** (do they recognize themselves? Does the positioning resonate?)
8. **Test in market** (landing page tests, messaging experiments, sales conversations)

### Positioning Statement Template

```
For [target customer segment]
Who [statement of need or opportunity]
[Product name] is a [market category]
That [key benefit / compelling reason to buy]
Unlike [competitive alternative]
Our product [primary differentiation]
```

### Validation Protocol

Position is not validated until:
1. Target customers spontaneously describe your product using your positioning language
2. Sales conversations are shorter (customers "get it" faster)
3. Win rate against positioned competitors increases
4. Inbound leads match your target segment profile

## Failure Modes

| Failure Mode | Description | Remedy |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| Positioning by committee | Too many stakeholders dilute the positioning | Single PM or product leader owns positioning |
| Positioning for everyone | Trying to appeal to all segments | Narrower segment = stronger positioning |
| Feature-based positioning | Positioning on features instead of value | Always translate features to customer outcomes |
| Static positioning | Never updating positioning as market changes | Quarterly positioning review |
| Competitor-reactive positioning | Changing positioning every time a competitor moves | Strategy-driven positioning; respond to market, not competitors |
| Category confusion | Customers do not understand what category you are in | Test category placement with customer interviews |

---

## The Operator's Framework

1. **Audit current positioning:** How do customers and prospects describe your product today?
2. **Apply Dunford's five components:** Alternatives, attributes, value, segment, category
3. **Build competitive positioning matrix:** Choose your two axes carefully
4. **Write positioning statement:** Use the template
5. **Validate:** Customer interviews, sales feedback, landing page experiments
6. **Communicate:** Ensure all teams (sales, marketing, support) use consistent positioning
7. **Review quarterly:** Positioning degrades as markets shift

---

## Summary

Competitive positioning is the strategic foundation of how your product is perceived in the market. April Dunford's five-component framework (competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value, target segment, market category) provides a systematic approach to positioning that replaces gut feel with evidence-based analysis. The choice of market category — existing, subcategory, or new — is one of the highest-leverage product decisions. Moats (network effects, switching costs, data advantages) make positioning durable. Positioning is not permanent — it must be validated with customers and reviewed quarterly as markets evolve. Strong positioning makes marketing easier, sales shorter, and product strategy clearer. Weak positioning forces competition on price, which is a race to the bottom.
